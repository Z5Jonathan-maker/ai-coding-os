# 24-reconstitution

From lyophilized powder to a perfectly prepared vial — every step, every detail, every mistake to avoid.

Reconstitution is the most common failure point in peptide use. Get this wrong and nothing downstream will work — no matter how expensive your compounds are.

Peptides are fragile molecular fragments. They must be handled with care from the moment you open the packaging. Follow these steps precisely every single time.

You need: lyophilized peptide vial, bacteriostatic water (bac water), alcohol swabs, a 29-gauge insulin syringe for drawing/injecting, and a clean flat surface. Have everything laid out before you start.

Use an alcohol swab to wipe the rubber stopper on **both** the peptide vial and the bacteriostatic water vial. Let them air dry for 10 seconds. This prevents bacterial contamination from entering your solution.

Using a fresh insulin syringe, draw exactly **1 mL (100 units on the syringe)** of bacteriostatic water. The standard ratio is 1 mL per vial. This makes dosing math simple and consistent across all your compounds.

Insert the needle through the rubber stopper and **aim for the SIDE of the vial**. Let the water trickle down the glass wall slowly. Do NOT blast it directly onto the powder. Depress the plunger with slow, steady pressure. The water should slide down like a gentle stream, not a firehose.

Aim the needle bevel toward the glass wall. The water will follow the glass surface down to the powder. Direct impact on the lyophilized cake can denature fragile peptide bonds. Gentle is the only speed that matters here.

Peptides are molecular fragments — they are fragile. Shaking creates turbulence that can shear peptide bonds and destroy the compound. **"Treat it like a newborn."** No vigorous motion. No flicking. No vortex mixing. Patience.

Place the vial between your palms and roll it back and forth slowly. Let the water dissolve the powder naturally. Some peptides dissolve in 30 seconds; others take a few minutes. If small particles remain, set the vial down for 5 minutes and try again. **Never force dissolution with shaking.**

Use a fresh 29-gauge insulin syringe. Insert through the cleaned stopper, invert the vial, and draw your calculated dose (see dosing math on next page). Remove air bubbles by gently tapping the syringe and pushing tiny amounts back in.

Once reconstituted, the vial goes straight into the fridge (36-46°F / 2-8°C). **Never freeze reconstituted peptides.** Never leave them at room temperature. With bacteriostatic water, shelf life is up to **30 days refrigerated**. Mark the reconstitution date on the vial.

The formula is simple. Once you know the vial size and how much water you added, everything follows:

Vial Size (mcg) ÷ Water Added (units) = mcg per unit

1 mL = 100 units on an insulin syringe. Each tick mark = 1 unit.

| State | Storage | Shelf Life | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lyophilized (powder) | Refrigerator or freezer | 12-24 months | Freezer extends life; fridge is fine for months |
| Reconstituted (bac water) | Refrigerator ONLY | Up to 30 days | Benzyl alcohol preserves solution |
| Reconstituted (sterile water) | Refrigerator ONLY | 24 hours max | No preservative — bacteria colonize fast |
| Room temperature | NEVER | Degradation begins | Heat and light destroy peptide structures |
| Frozen (after reconstitution) | NEVER | Destroyed | Ice crystals shatter peptide bonds |

| Mistake | What Goes Wrong | Correct Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Shaking the vial | Shears peptide bonds; denatures the compound | Roll gently between palms |
| Blasting water onto powder | Mechanical damage to fragile fragments | Aim for side of vial, let it trickle |
| Using tap water | Bacteria, chlorine, minerals contaminate solution | Bacteriostatic water only |
| Leaving at room temp | Accelerated degradation, bacterial growth | Refrigerate immediately after recon |
| Freezing reconstituted vial | Ice crystals destroy peptide structure | Fridge only — 36-46°F |
| Not swabbing stopper | Bacteria introduced into sterile solution | Alcohol swab every time, every vial |
| Reusing syringes | Contamination and dull needle = tissue damage | Fresh syringe every draw |
| Wrong dosing math | Underdosing (no results) or overdosing (side effects) | Calculate mcg/unit before every draw |
