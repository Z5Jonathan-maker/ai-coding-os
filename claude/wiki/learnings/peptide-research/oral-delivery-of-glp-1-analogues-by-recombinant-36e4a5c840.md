Summary Background The oral delivery of native glucagon like peptide-1 (GLP-1) using recombinant probiotics has shown hypoglycaemic effects and glucose tolerance improvement in diabetic mice. However, the pharmacological mechanisms remain incompletely understood. This study aimed to clarify whether GLP-1 analogues cross the intestinal mucosal barrier and exert systemic hypoglycaemic effects. Methods The recombinant Lactococcus lactis ( L. lactis ) was constructed for oral delivery of long-duration GLP-1 (LGLP) or exendin-4 (EX4). The hypoglycemic effects in vivo were investigated in db/db mice. The pancreatic islet structure and DNA replication were studied with BrdU-labelled double immunofluorescence. The peptide absorptions in vitro and in vivo were traced with tetramethylrhodamine (TMR) labelled LGLP or EX4. The Ussing Chamber test with rat intestinal mucosa and the absorption test in the ligated loops of intestinal tube were used to verify the absorption of GLP-1 analogues. Findings The supernatant of recombinant L. lactis expressing LGLP or EX4 stimulated proliferation and insulin secretion in rat insulinoma cell INS-1 in vitro. The LGLP-recombinant L. lactis significantly reduced fasting blood glucose, improved oral glucose tolerance, especially increased the number and size of pancreatic islets after oral administration for 4 weeks in diabetic mice. TMR-labelled LGLP or EX4 peptides were internalized by intestinal epithelial cells in vitro. LGLP and EX4 peptides crossed the rat intestinal mucosa and entered the opposite chamber in a receptor-mediated endocytosis manner in the Ussing Chamber test. Following injection into the ligated intestinal loops, a small amount of TMR-labelled LGLP or EX4 was observed in the lamina propria of intestinal villi, pancreas and other tissues through mucosal absorption. Interpretation Oral delivery of GLP-1 analogues by recombinant L. lactis restored the structure of pancreatic islets and exerted systemic hypoglycaemic effects through receptor-mediated intestinal mucosa absorption. Research in context Evidence before this study The native full-length form of GLP-1 (1–37) orally delivered using recombinant probiotics were observed to ameliorate hyperglycaemia, improve islet sensitivity, and promote β-cell proliferation in diabetic animals. Exendin-4, a long duration analogue of GLP-1, promoted β-cell proliferation and insulin secretion in vitro when expressed by recombinant Lactococcus lactis . Conversely, the active form GLP-1 (7–37) expressing recombinant L. lactis had neither hypoglycemic effect nor β-cell proliferation in vitro or in vivo. Semaglutide, a GLP-1 analogue, co-formulated with the absorption enhancer sodium N-(8-[2-hydroxybenzoyl] amino) caprylate (SNAC) was absorbed through the gastric mucosa and enter the circulation. However, it is still uncertain whether GLP-1 analogues cross the intestinal mucosa and act on the pancreatic islets to exert their biological effects. The pharmacological mechanism by which GLP-1 analogue oral delivery reprograms certain intestinal cells into insulin-secreting cells, along with the hypothesis of oral GLP-1 analogue improving hyperglycaemia through the gut–pancreas–liver axis by modulating intestinal flora, does not adequately explain how oral GLP-1 analogues promote β-cell proliferation. Added value of this study We established a inducible food-grade L. lactis expression system for the expression of long-duration GLP-1 analogue (LGLP) and exendin-4 (EX4) in the intestinal lumen. The LGLP and EX4 peptides expressed by recombinant L. lactis have been confirmed to have biological activities that stimulate beta-cell proliferation and insulin secretion in vitro. We also proved that oral delivery of LGLP- recombinant L. lactis had good hypoglycemic effects, and especially restored the structure of pancreatic islets in diabetes mice. Further investigation revealed that both LGLP and EX4 peptides were internalized by intestinal epithelial cells, crossed the intestinal mucosa and reached the liver, pancreas and other tissues in a GLP-1 receptor dependent manner. Collectively, this study elucidate a novel pharmacological mechanism by which orally delivered GLP-1 analogues are absorbed into the systemic circulation through receptor-mediated endocytosis in the intestinal mucosa. These GLP-1 analogues could exert direct effects on islet cells, facilitating the restoration of islet structure and ultimately achieving a systemic hypoglycemic effect. Implications of all the available evidence Orally delivered GLP-1 analogues have been demonstrated to exert systemic hypoglycemic effects through receptor-mediated intestinal mucosal absorption. We hypothesize that other peptides with rich receptors on the intestinal epithelium can also be absorbed by the intestinal mucosa, which is suitable for oral delivery. However, whether GLP-1-like peptide can maintain its intact and active structure after absorption through intestinal mucosa needs to be further verified. Moreover, the pharmacokinetics of GLP-1 analogues after oral delivery also needs to be further elucidated. Introduction Glucagon like peptide-1 (GLP-1) is a peptide hormone secreted from enteroendocrine L-cells and pancreatic α-cells in response to food intake. 1 , 2 GLP-1 maintains blood glucose homeostasis throug multiple biological functions. It prevents pancreatic β-cells apoptosis, stimulates β-cell proliferation and induces insulin secretion in a glucose-dependent manner. GLP-1 also plays an important role in energy balance and body weight by delaying gastric emptying, increasing satiety, reducing food absorption and enhancing energy expenditure. 3 , 4 , 5 , 6 , 7 GLP-1 exerts its biological function mainly by acting on the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R), a G-protein-coupled receptor. GLP-1R is highly expressed in various tissues involved in carbohydrate metabolism, including pancreas islet, intestinal epithelia, liver, hypothalamus and brainstem. 8 , 9 , 10 Unfortunately, GLP-1 secretion is impaired in type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM). It was considered to play an important role in the pathogenesis of diabetes. 11 GLP-1R agonists are the most attractive drugs for diabetes treatment. However, the active form of GLP-1 (7–36/37) has an extremely short half-life (several minutes) in vivo since the native GLP-1 is easily inactivated by dipeptidyl peptidase 4 (DPP-IV) which cuts the dipeptide (Ala7-Glu8) at the N-terminus of GLP-1. To overcome this challenge, several GLP-1 analogues with extended duration of action have been developed. 12 , 13 However, GLP-1 analogue injection is not well suitable for the long-term use of chronic diabetes due to the inconvenience of administration. Furthermore, injection is easy to cause complications such as allergy, infection and local irritation. Moreover, the exorbitant price is another barrier preventing the widespread clinical utilization of GLP-1 analogue injection. Less than 5% of patients worldwide can afford these peptide drugs. 14 Therefore, novel production mode should be urgently developed to reduce the costs of peptide drug. Intestinal probiotics can colonize and grow in the gastrointestinal tract, and have a long history of safe consumption. They are considered the suitable host for recombinant protein expression and oral delivery. 15 Peptide drug orally delivered by recombinant probiotics will greatly reduce the production costs for the needless of purification. The native full-length form of GLP-1 (1–37) has been expressed for oral delivery with probiotics, such as L. lactis , Lactobacillus plantarum , or Clostridium butyricum . Oral delivery of GLP-1 (1–37) recombinant bacteria ameliorated hyperglycemia and improved glucose tolerace in diabetic mice, rats, or monkey. 16 , 17 , 18 , 19 , 20 Surprisingly, GLP-1 (1–37) recombinant L. lactis reprogrammed intestinal cells into glucose-responsive insulin-secreting cells. 17 Oral delivery of GLP-1 (1–37) recombinant L. plantarum was also been observed to promote β-cell proliferation in diabetic mice, and even to improve blood pressure in the rat models of spontaneous hypertension. The pharmacological mechanism of recombinant L. plantarum was considered to act on the islets through the gut–pancreas–liver axis by modulating the intestinal microbiota. 18 , 19 However, the recombinant L. lactis of active form GLP-1 (7–37) had neither the reprogramming effects nor islet repair effects in vitro or in vivo. 16 , 17 Interestingly, recombinant L. lactis expressing exendin-4, a long duration GLP-1 analogue originated from Gila Monster , promoted β-cell proliferation and insulin secretion in vitro. 21 We infer that the reason why the recombinant L. lactis of active form GLP-1 (7–37) does not recover islet structure is that the GLP-1 (7–37) is too easily degraded. GLP-1 analogues with extended duration, such as exendin-4, may be able to reach pancreatic tissue and promote β-cell proliferation directly through intestinal mucosa absorption. However, it is uncertain whether GLP-1 analogue peptides cross the intestinal mucosa and then enter the circulation, thereby acting on pancreatic islets to exert their biological effects. In this study, long-duration GLP-1 (LGLP) or exendin-4 (EX4) was expressed and secreted within the intestinal tract by recombinant L. lactis based on the food-grade NICE (Nisin Controlled Expression) system. The hypoglycaemic effects and islet-restoring effects of the GLP-1 analogues after oral delivery were investigated in db/db mice. The mechanism of peptides absorption through the intestinal mucosa was also explored. The food-grade vectors pLN-LGLP, pLN-EX4 and the empty control vector pLN were obtained by excising the ampicillin resistance gene with restriction endonuclease SalⅠ (Cat# ER1011, ThermoFisher Scientific, USA) ( Fig. 1 a). 22 Fig. 1 Vector construction and the expression of recombinant L. lactis in vitro. The recombinant plasmids pLN-LGLP and pLN-EX4 were transformed into L. lactis NZ3900 (Cat# VS-ELS03900-01, MoBiTec GmbH, Germany) by electroporation at 2.5 kv, 25 μF and 200 Ω with a Gene Pulser and Pulse Controller apparatus (Bio-Rad, USA) as described previously. 22 The transformed bacteria were cultured anaerobically in the selective Elliker medium (EM) plates (containing 0.5% lactose, 0.5 g/L l -ascorbic acid, and 0.004% bromocresolpurple) at 30 °C for 48 h. Gene expression induction in vitro The expression induction of LGLP and EX4 in the recombinant L. lactis has been described in a previous work. 22 Briefly, the recombinant bacteria were cultured in the selective EM broth until OD 600nm reached approximately 0.8. Then, 2.0 ng/ml nisin (Cat# 1414-45-5, Sigma–Aldrich, Merck, USA) was added to induce the target gene expression. The supernatants and pellets were collected at 12 h, 24 h and 36 h of induction. The levels of LGLP and EX4 in the supernatants and pellet lysates were detected using Western blotting and ELISA. Islet cell proliferation and insulin secretion To assess the effect of LGLP and EX4 from the transformed L. lactis on islet cell proliferation, the rat insulinoma INS-1 cells (RRID: CVCL_VK12, a gift from the Institute of Metabolic Diseases, Nanjing Medical University, derived from Millipore, Merck, USA) were incubated in the RPMI-1640 medium (Cat# 11875093, ThermoFisher Scientific, USA) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS), 2.0 mmol/L l -glutamine, 50.0 μmol/L mercaptoethanol, and 1.0 mmol/L sodium pyruvate at 37 °C in a humidified incubator with 5% CO 2 . INS-1 cells were seeded and grown in a 96-well plate for 12 h, with a density of 5 × 10 3 cells per well. The transformed L. lactis of LL-LGLP, LL-EX4, and LL-Vect were inoculated in the RPMI-1640 basal medium. LGLP and EX4 expression were induced with 2.0 ng/ml nisin for 12 h. The concentrations of LGLP and EX4 peptides in the supernatants were quantified and diluted to 250 pg/ml. The supernatant PH value was adjusted to 7.3 before treatment. INS-1 cells were treated with 20 μl, 60 μl and 100 μl supernatants to reach the final concentration of the LGLP or EX4 peptide to 50 pg/ml, 150 pg/ml, and 250 pg/ml, respectively. The supernatant of LL-Vect was used as the negative control. After treatment for 24 h and 48 h, the Cell Counting Kit-8 (Cat# CK04-3000, Dojindo Laboratories, Japan) was added and incubated sequentially at 37 °C for 3 h. The absorbance was measured at 450 nm using a microplate reader (Bio-Rad Technologies, USA). The percentage of living cells was calculated and compared with the blank control group. The experiments were conducted in duplicate at least three times. Oral administration of recombinant bacteria in diabetic mice Mice db/db (BKS.Cg-Dock7 m+/+ Lepr db /J) (RRID:IMSR_TAC:DB, Jackson Laboratory, USA) and the wild type mice aged 8 weeks, weighing 18–25 g were fed a normal chow diet. The db/db mice with a body weight exceeding 30 g and fasting blood glucose levels more than 12 mmol were selected and randomly divided into 4 groups, with an equal number of males and females in each group. The negative control group (LL-Vect) group received equivalent amounts of recombinant LL-Vect containing 20 ng/ml nisin, while the blank control group (Saline) was treated with saline. Wild-type mice (WT group) received no treatment. After 4 weeks of treatment, all animals were fasted for 15 h and sacrificed. Results Selection and identification of recombinant L. lactis The recombinant expression plasmids bearing secretory signaling peptide SPK1 and LGLP (7G8-36) or exendin-4 (EX4) gene were constructed ( Fig. 1 a). The L. lactis NZ3900 transformants were obtained through electroporation with the recombinant plasmids pLN-LGLP and pLN-EX4. The transformants of the empty vector pLN were also obtained as the negative control. The clones of L. lactis transformants turned yellow in selective Elliker medium (EM) plates containing bromocresol purple ( Fig. 1 b). The yellow positive clones were expanded in selective EM broth, and the plasmids were extracted for DNA sequencing. The correct transformants with plasmids pLN-LGLP, pLN-EX4, and pLN were referenced as LL-LGLP, LL-EX4, and LL-Vect, respectively. Discussion The use of probiotics as living carriers for GLP-1 analogues not only facilitates oral administration, but also offers significant cost advantages over injections, making them particularly suitable for diabetes treatment in the long term. 16 , 17 Previous studies have shown that recombinant bacteria expressing native GLP-1 (1–37) or long-duration active form of GLP-1 analogue promote β-cell proliferation and ameliorate hyperglycemia in diabetic mice. 17 , 18 Our previous studies have also shown that recombinant Bifidobacterium longum expressing oxyntomodulin, an intestinal peptide hormone, induces systemic weight reduction and lipid-lowering effects. 25 Additionally, thymosin a1-expressing B. longum stimulated lymphocyte proliferation, and thymus and systemic lymph node hyperplasia. 26 However, the pharmacological mechanism by which these recombinant probiotics exert their systemic biological effects remain incompletely understood. Two hypoglycaemic mechanisms have been proposed for orally delivered GLP-1 analogues. One is that GLP-1 analogue reprograms intestinal epithelial cells into insulin-secreting cells. Another is that GLP-1 analogue acts on pancreatic cells through the gut–liver axis by modulating intestinal flora. However, none of the mentioned mechanisms fully explain how oral GLP-1 analogue promotes β-islet cell proliferation. In this study, we established an inducible expression system for GLP-1 analogues expressing within the intestinal tract based on the NICE system. LGLP and EX4 expression were induced in the intestinal tract with the inducer nisin after oral administration of recombinant L. lactis . Nisin is widely used in food products as a natural preservative. It has strong chemical stability in vitro and in the digestive tract. 27 Therefore, nisin is highly suitable as an inducer for exogenous gene expression in the gut. 28 The hypoglycaemic effect and islet-restoring capacity of GLP-1 analogue expressing within the intestinal lumen basing on the NICE system were explored in vitro and in vivo. Our results showed that LGLP expressed by recombinant L. lactis had a faster promoting effect on pancreatic islet cell proliferation and insulin secretion in vitro compared to that of EX4. EX4 only showed good biological activity after 48 h long-term treatment. The above results suggest that the receptor affinity of LGLP may be higher than that of EX4 in mammalian, which possibly due to the fact that EX4 originates from Gila Monster . We also demonstrated that recombinant L. lactis -mediated LGLP expression in the intestinal tract exhibited robust hypoglycaemic effects in db/db mice. The body weight was slightly reduced after 4 weeks of feeding with LGLP-recombinant L. lactis . The fasting blood glucose levels began to decrease after 2 weeks of treatment. The blood glucose levels were close to that of wild type (WT) mice after 4 weeks of oral treatment. The results of the OGTT also indicated that the insulin sensitivity of the diabetic mice was significantly increased after LGLP-recombinant L. lactis treatment. Notably, the effect of LGLP recombinant L. lactis on improving insulin sensitivity was even better than that of liraglutide i.p. collectively, our results showed that the LGLP-recombinant L. lactis lowered blood glucose and improved insulin sensitivity. GLP-1 promoted pancreatic β-cell proliferation while preventing hyperglycaemia-induced β-cell apoptosis. 29 , 30 There is a certain degree of pancreatic islet structural damage and functional degradation in the middle and late stages of type 2 diabetes. 31 The number and size of pancreatic islets in the diabetic mice were indeed reduced in our immunohistochemical results. The LGLP-expressing recombinant L. lactis significantly increased the number and volume of pancreatic islets by oral administration. Furthermore, oral LGLP-recombinant L. lactis significantly stimulated β-cell proliferation (genomic DNA replication). The above results are consistent with previous studies of GLP-1 analogues injection. 29 , 32 Our results indicate that the LGLP peptide expressing in the intestinal lumen may cross the intestinal mucosal barrier and enter the circulation to exert its systemic effects on islet cells. 16 , 18 Decades ago, it was observed that bovine serum protein (BSA) and horseradish peroxidase (HRP) were intactly absorbed by the intestinal mucosa in rats. 33 Green fluorescent protein (GFP) or soluble CD80 fused with cholera toxin B (CTB) crossed the intestinal mucosa and reach the circulation by receptor-mediated endocytosis. 22 , 34 Most notably, GLP-1 could pass through the integral monolayer of Madin–Darby canine kidney cells (MDCK) in vitro. Orally delivered GLP-1 by recombinant L. Lactis has shown a good hypoglycaemic effects and was believed to be absorbed by the intestinal epithelium. 16 Two possible pathways were speculated to contribute to the transport of GLP-1. One of which was the opening of the tight junctions of the monolayer by recombinant L. lactis , and the another was the concentrated deposition of GLP-1 peptides on the apical surface of the monolayer. 16 However, the mechanism by which GLP-1 crosses the intestinal mucosa remains unclear. It is necessary to investigate mechanisms contributing to the passage of GLP-1 analogues through the intestinal mucosal barrier. This study confirmed that GLP-1R is highly expressed in intestinal epithelial cells, pancreatic islets, liver, and kidney using immunofluorescence and western blotting. We also analyzed the absorption of TMR-labeled GLP-1 analogues by intestinal epithelial cells in vitro and in vivo. Endocytosis of LGLP-TMR or EX4-TMR was observed in intestinal epithelial cell NCM460 after 30 min treatment. The amount of internalized LGLP-TMR or EX4-TMR was significantly reduced when pre-treatment with GLP-1R inhibitory peptide EX9. Similarly, the red fluorescence of LGLP-TMR was observed in the lamina propria of intestinal villi, as well as in the liver, kidneys, and pancreas after LGLP-TMR injection in the ligated ileum loops. Even small amounts of LGLP-TMR were observed in the brain. After pre-injection with EX9, the amount of LGLP-TMR was significantly reduced in the lamina propria. These results are consistent with those of previous studies. 16 Perhaps most TMR-labeled peptides that reached the liver, pancreas, and other tissues may be the degraded and inactive peptides. However, at least some of them are intact and active LGLP peptides. These results suggest that LGLP oral delivery not only acts on the GLP-1R receptor on the local intestine, but also exert systemic hypoglycaemic effects through receptor-mediated endocytosis to cross the intestinal barrier. This conclusion is corroborated by the increase in DNA replication of β-cells and the recovery of pancreatic islet structure after the oral administration of LGLP-recombinant L. lactis . GLP-1 plays an important role in regulating lipid metabolism. 35 GLP-1 analogues like liraglutide and exenatide improved lipid metabolism in several animal models and in human diseases. 36 Recently, semaglutide was approved by the USA Food and Drug Administration (FDA) as a weight loss drug. 37 , 38 Our data shown that the TC, TG, LDL, and VLDL levels were decreased after 4 weeks of oral treatment with LGLP- L. lactis . In addition, the serum levels of adiponectin were significantly increased after treatment with LGLP L. lactis . The available data strongly support that oral delivery of LGLP by recombinant L. lactis improves lipid metabolism. Oil red O staining showed that the size of oil particles in the liver was significantly decreased after oral delivery of LGLP-recombinant L. Lactis , which was smaller than that of liraglutide treatment group. These results demonstrated that oral delivery of LGLP-recombinant L. lactis ameliorated lipid metabolism and fatty liver. The beneficial effects of orally delivered LGLP-recombinant L. lactis were even better than those of liraglutide injection. There were still some limitations in this work. Whether GLP-1 analogues absorbed through the intestinal mucosa after oral delivery are intact and have biological activities in the circulation and tissues remains unclear. The pharmacokinetics of GLP-1 analogues in vivo and in the intestine also remain unclear. It also unclear whether polypeptides with few or no receptors on intestinal epithelial cells can be non-specifically endocytosed into the circulation by the intestinal mucosa. In the future, the peptide fingerprinting method based on mass spectrometry should be employed to confirm that the intact peptides can reach the systemic circulation and target tissues through the intestinal mucosa after oral delivery. Therefore, the pharmacokinetics of GLP-1 analogues after oral delivery should also be thoroughly elucidated. In conclusion, we demonstrated that LGLP production by recombinant L. lactis in intestinal tract significantly reduced blood glucose levels, stimulated the proliferation of islet cells, and restored the structure and function of islets in diabetic mice. We also revealed the pharmacological mechanisms by which orally delivered LGLP exerts systemic biological effects through receptor-mediated endocytosis. The genetically engineered probiotic is a promising approach to restoring islet function for diabetes treatment.
