# Mitochondria-targeted delivery strategies for age-related diseases

Abstract Aging is a complex progress accompanied with the progressive deterioration of physiological functions and a marked elevation in mortality risk. Among prominent aging theories, the free radical theory and the mitochondrial dysfunction hypothesis have gained significant attention. Thus, targeted delivery of therapeutic drug to mitochondria might be able to alleviate the mitochondrial dysfunction induced by reactive oxygen species. This review summarizes the possible molecular mechanisms between mitochondrial dysfunction and aging progression. Especially, the recent breakthroughs of mitochondrial-targeted delivery platforms for therapeutics against aging progress. Innovative strategies, including small molecular modification, mitochondrial targeting functional peptide guided delivery and some other strategies are discussed. Their translational potential in anti-aging interventions is evaluated. We anticipate that mitochondria-targeted anti-aging therapeutics will soon enter clinical translation, offering potential solutions to address current age-related challenges. Introduction Understanding the fundamental mechanisms of human aging to ultimately decelerate the progression remains one of the most significant biomedical challenges currently.( Li et al., 2021 ; Olshansky, 2016 ) Age is one of the foremost correlation factors for the majority of prevalent chronic diseases.( Tchkonia and Kirkland, 2018 ; Martel et al., 2020 ; Kennedy et al., 2014 ) Although our comprehension of aging processes remains incomplete, it is widely accepted that aging might originate from molecular-level damage, culminating in cellular, tissue, and ultimately organ dysfunction.( Li et al., 2023 ) This intrinsic aging process is conceptualized as forming a "tapestry" upon which age-related pathologies might manifest.( Huang et al., 2022 ) Conversely, an opposing hypothesis posits that aging merely represents the terminal outcome of accumulated chronic diseases or that aging and chronic diseases are parallel yet independent processes.( Li et al., 2015 ) But regardless of how, cellular senescence has emerged as a hallmark of both age-related diseases.( Calcinotto et al., 2019 ; Wang et al., 2022 ; Yuan et al., 2020 ) Cellular senescence constitutes a response to severe damage or stress, characterized by irreversible cell cycle arrest and the hypersecretion of fibrotic, mitogenic and inflammatory factors. The above-mentioned features collectively termed as the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP).( Sikora et al., 2021 ; Almalki and Almujri, 2025 ) While the SASP plays critical physiological roles, it is reversible, as senescent cells are typically eliminated via immune surveillance under normal conditions.( Lujambio, 2016 ) However, with advancing age, inefficient clearance leads to accumulation of senescent cells, which disrupt tissue homeostasis by degrading stem cell niches and impairing regenerative capacity.( Brunet et al., 2023 ) Although still speculative, evidence suggests that the SASP, rather than the mere physical presence of senescent cells, may drive the primary pathological consequences of senescence.( Wang et al., 2024 ) Despite progress in elucidating SASP and senescence, substantial gaps remain in translating senescence-targeted strategies into clinically viable anti-aging therapies.( Paez-Ribes et al., 2019 ; Wyles et al., 2022 ) SASP and mitochondrial dysfunction engage in a tight bidirectional regulatory crosstalk, with their interaction driving cellular senescence and the pathogenesis of associated diseases. Therefore, mitochondrial dysfunction has been increasingly recognized as a central player in age-related pathologies, including neurodegenerative disorders, cardiovascular diseases, and metabolic syndromes.( Zhang et al., 2023a ; Srivastava, 2017 ) Central to this dysfunction lies the overproduction of reactive oxygen species (ROS) within mitochondria, which acts as both a causative agent and amplifier of organellar deterioration.( Zia et al., 2022 ; Maldonado et al., 2023 ) The particular complexes within the electron transport chain (ETC) serve as the primary source of mitochondrial ROS through incomplete electron transfer during oxidative phosphorylation.( Okoye et al., 2023 ) These free radicals initiate a cascade of molecular damage by oxidizing mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA), membrane phospho-lipids, and respiratory chain proteins.( Vetica et al., 2020 ) Notably, the vulnerability of mtDNA to ROS is exacerbated by its proximity to ETC-generated oxidants, deficiency of histone protection, and insufficient DNA repair mechanisms compared with nuclear DNA.( Gureev et al., 2025 ; Akhmedov and Marín-García, 2015 ) Such oxidative damage impairs triphosadenine (ATP) synthesis efficiency, reduces membrane potential (ΔΨm), and triggers mitochondrial permeability transition pore (mPTP) opening. These events collectively induce cellular bioenergetic collapse and apoptosis.( Cui et al., 2021 ; Phadwal et al., 2021 ) In addition, mitophagy, the selective removal of damaged or dysfunctional mitochondria, is a core mechanism for maintaining cellular homeostasis. Its function declines with age, leading to the accumulation of defective mitochondria, which in turn exacerbates oxidative stress, chronic inflammation, and cellular senescence. This process is a key driver of multiple age-related pathological changes. Recent research indicates that enhancing mitophagy through pharmacological or genetic means has emerged as a promising strategy for intervening in aging and associated diseases. For instance, in the field of neurodegeneration, the drug Remimazolam has been shown to alleviate neuronal damage and cognitive decline in Alzheimer's disease models by activating PTEN-induced putative kinase 1 (PINK1) /Parkin pathway-mediated mitophagy.( Zhang et al., 2025a ) In the skeletal system, an energy metabolism-targeted nanomedicine successfully cleared dysfunctional mitochondria in aged mesenchymal stem cells by promoting mitochondrial fission and mitophagy, reversing the osteoporotic phenotype in aged mice.( Chen et al., 2025 ) Similarly, in an osteoarthritis model, overexpressing PINK1 to restore mitophagic function in chondrocytes effectively mitigated cellular senescence and tissue degeneration.( Dagda et al., 2009 ) Collectively, this evidence underscores that targeting and enhancing the mitophagy pathway can directly address the cellular underpinnings of aging, offering novel and promising avenues for treating a range of age-related diseases. According to the above summarized mechanisms, ROS-induced mtDNA mutations generate defective ETC subunits through error-prone replication, creating a self-perpetuating cycle: dysfunctional complexes produce more ROS→escalating oxidative damage→further ETC impairment.( Romesberg and Van Houten, 2022 ) This "ROS-mtDNA vicious cycle" hypothesis posits that cumulative mitochondrial damage establishes a biochemical threshold beyond which tissues cannot maintain homeostasis, ultimately manifesting as age-related organ failure.( Liskova et al., 2021 ) Paradoxically, while transient ROS bursts serve as redox signaling modulators, chronic oxidative stress overwhelms endogenous antioxidant systems (e.g., glutathione, superoxide dismutase), shifting the balance toward pathological senescence.( Jomova et al., 2023 ) Meanwhile, recent studies further implicated that ROS-mediated cardiolipin peroxidation and the induction of cytochrome c release is a critical step linking mitochondrial decay to inflammaging and pyro ptosis.( Miao et al., 2023 ) Besides ROS, emerging evidence suggest synergistic interactions with calcium dysregulation, mitophagy defects, and nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD + ) depletion might collectively orchestrate mitochondrial demise in aging.( Rottenberg, 2023 ) Small-molecule compounds and protein/peptide are widely used across diverse pathologies, with some showing significant therapeutic potential in mitigating age-related conditions.( Baig et al., 2018 ; Liu et al., 2022 ) However, direct administration of these drugs faces significant pharmacological challenges, including intrinsic instability, poor bioavailability, and suboptimal targeting efficiency.( Nie et al., 2021 ; Boddu et al., 2023 ) For example, the Apostichopus japonicus protein hydrolysate demonstrates potent in vitro antioxidant activity capable of retarding aging progression. However, it could not specifically accumulate in mitochondria which generate most ROS.( Guo et al., 2020 ) More typically, sirtuin homolog (SIRT1) can only ameliorate chronic obstructive pulmonary disease via reducing lung inflammation, when it is delivered into nucleus.( Yanagisawa et al., 2018 ; Zhao and Wu, 2021 ) Therefore, more advanced subcellular protein/peptide delivery is urgently required for the treatment of age-related diseases. These limitations arise fundamentally because the ultimate therapeutic targets of most anti-aging interventions reside within subcellular organelles, particularly mitochondria.( Phua et al., 2024 ) For instance, the NAD + precursor nicotinamide riboside (NR), a widely investigated longevity compound, requires precise delivery to mitochondrial matrix to activate sirtuin deacetylases. However, the effectiveness of NR is greatly compromised by rapid cytosolic degradation and insufficient organellar accumulation in aged cells.( Poljšak et al., 2023 ) Similarly, the antioxidant coenzyme Q10, despite nano-encapsulation strategies, exhibits limited efficacy due to the inability of penetrating mitochondrial inner membrane where its electron transport function is critically needed.( Fišar and Hroudová, 2024 ) Thus, how to directly deliver drugs to mitochondria remains a critical question. Mitochondria is a multi-layer organelle which possesses the outer membrane, inner membrane, intermembrane space and mitochondrial matrix.( Liang et al., 2024 ) Thus, it is difficult for small molecule or protein/peptide therapeutics to directly enter the mitochondria and neutralize the mitochondrial dysfunction. In current mitochondrial targeting tactics, the modification with strongly positive charge lipophilic groups like triphenylphosphonium (TPP) and mitochondrial targeting functional peptide (MTFP) has received lots of attention. TPP can be attracted to the lipid featured and highly negatively charged surface of the membrane.( Batheja et al., 2024 ) MTFP can interact with mitochondrial component and integrated into the matrix or membrane.( Mossmann et al., 2012 ; Backes and Herrmann, 2017 ) Some other strategies are also applied to enhance mitochondrial penetration.( Su et al., 2024 ) Using these strategies can significantly improve the bioavailability and efficacy of drugs, providing a more effective therapeutic approach for aging-related conditions.( Liu et al., 2024 ) For instance, TPP-conjugated nanoparticles have been developed to deliver anti-aging compounds such as resveratrol, overcoming the limitations of free drugs and enhancing their therapeutic effects.( Jiang et al., 2022 ) In summary, mitochondrial dysfunction is not only closely linked to aging-related diseases but also engages in a reciprocal promotion with them, driven by mitochondrial metabolic disorders. In this context, mitochondrial targeting strategies emerge as a precise approach to break this positive feedback loop, thereby combating these conditions effectively.( Atayik and Çakatay, 2022 ) During the process of aging, the possible mechanisms involved with mitochondria are summarized. In addition, this review also delves deeply into the mitochondria targeting strategy. In this review, the related literatures in medical research databases including PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, and ScienceDirect were analyzed. We further elucidate the mitochondrial targeted drug delivery strategies for age-relate disease management and look forward to future developments in this domain. 2. Mitochondrion dysfunction and aging disease: Mitochondrial dysfunction exerts pleiotropic impacts across multiple biological processes of aging.( Lima et al., 2022 ) It is crucial to retain healthy and robust mitochondrial metabolic function for realizing long-term health.( Guo et al., 2023 ; Qin et al., 2024 ) Both the genetic stability of mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) and nuclear DNA underpins the energy-supplying capacity of an organism's tissues.( Tkaczyk-Wlizło et al., 2022 ) In contrast to the mitosis of the nuclear genome, mtDNA replicates independently of the cell cycle, and its proneness to mutation is exacerbated by the low efficiency of its repair system, leading to the gradual accumulation of mutated mtDNA copies within cells over time.( Potapenko et al., 2024 ; Powers et al., 2017 ) When an organism enters the later stages of life, the mutations in both nuclear DNA and mtDNA might surpass a critical threshold.( Pereira et al., 2021 ) These detrimental outcomes act as a driving force behind the age-related diseases.( Elorza and J.P., 2021 ) Besides, disturbances in the mtDNA epigenome are also associated with human diseases.( Donato et al., 2024 ) As a key epigenetic modification, mtDNA methylation is inherently prone to epigenetic alterations due to its susceptibility to environmental disturbances, endogenous metabolites, and other factors throughout the life cycle.( Sharma et al., 2019 ) Research has revealed that individuals with decreased methylation in the mtDNA D-loop region exhibit a higher propensity for developing amyotrophic lateral sclerosis and Parkinson's disease, whereas reduced methylation of the mitochondrial NADH dehydrogenase subunit 1 (MT-ND1) correlates with an increased risk of Alzheimer's disease.( Stoccoro et al., 2018 ; Stoccoro et al., 2017 ) While mitochondrial genome instability and aberrant epigenetic regulation can induce mitochondrial dysfunction, the latter conversely exacerbates all the process. Ultimately, this reciprocal interplay drives the transformation of normal mitochondria toward senescent alterations in morphology, physiology, dynamics, and function. It has been reported that reduced mitochondrial membrane potential, increased proton leakage, elevated ROS production is observed in senescent cells.( Walter et al., 2019 ) The cellular fatty acid oxidation further decreases, along with disrupted mitochondrial metabolism.( Lee et al., 2021 ) Understanding the molecular mechanisms of mitochondrial dysfunction is crucial for cognizing the development of aging-related diseases and might provide insights into potential therapeutic strategies.( Moradi Vastegani et al., 2023 ; Martini and Passos, 2023 ) Mitochondrial dysfunction serves as a cornerstone in the pathogenesis of aging-related diseases, driven by progressive deterioration of energy metabolism and inter-organellar communication.( Palikaras et al., 2017 ; Guo et al., 2022 ) The hallmark features of senescent mitochondria include diminished respiratory capacity, reduced mitochondrial membrane potential (MMP), and compensatory expansion of mitochondrial mass-a phenomenon observed both in vitro and in aged tissues.( Boengler et al., 2017 ; Escrig-Larena et al., 2023 ) These defects manifest through three primary mechanisms: 2.1. Bioenergetic collapse via ATP- AMP axis dysregulation Healthy mitochondria maintain cellular energy homeostasis through the tricarboxylic acid cycle (TCA) and oxidative phosphorylation, generating ATP to power critical processes.( Cai and Jeong, 2020 ) Aging-associated declines in ATP synthesis disrupt the adenosine monophosphate (AMP)/ATP ratio, impairing AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) signaling-a master regulator of mitochondrial dynamics, autophagy, and metabolic adaptation.( Sharma et al., 2023 ; Zhang et al., 2024 ; Hu et al., 2024 ) For instance, in age-related sarcopenia, skeletal muscle exhibits lower ATP levels compared to young controls, leading to AMPK inactivation and subsequent accumulation of dysfunctional mitochondria.( Zhang et al., 2022a ) This energy crisis exacerbates proteostasis failure and muscle atrophy through insufficient phosphorylation of autophagy initiators like Unc-51-like kinase 1(ULK1).( Henriksson, 2022 ) 2.2. NAD + depletion-mediated epigenetic derangement As a pivotal cofactor for sirtuin deacetylases, NAD + links mitochondrial metabolic status to nuclear epigenetic regulation.( Perico et al., 2021 ) Age-dependent NAD + decline cripples SIRT1/3 activity, permitting hyperacetylation of peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma coactivator 1-alpha (PGC-1α)-a nuclear-encoded mitochondrial biogenesis regulator.( Nørremølle et al., 2016 ) Results from mouse models demonstrated that PGC-1α acetylation reduced mitochondrial DNA copy number by accelerating age-related cognitive decline through neuronal bioenergetic failure.( Sun et al., 2021 ) 2.3. ROS-triggered inflammaging cascade Chronic overproduction of mitochondrial ROS (increase in aged endothelial cells) induces peroxidation of cardiolipin-a phospholipid critical for mitochondrial cristae architecture.( Yoo et al., 2025 ) This structural damage promotes cytochrome c leakage and NOD-like receptor family pyrin domain containing 3 (NLRP3) inflammasome activation, generating higher interleukin-1 β (IL-1 β) levels in elderly subjects.( Bao et al., 2024 ) Simultaneously, oxidized mtDNA fragments escape impaired mitophagy, acting as damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) to sustain inflammation-a key driver of atherosclerosis and Alzheimer's disease.( Zhao et al., 2018 ; Geto et al., 2020 ) These interconnected mechanisms create self-reinforcing cycles: ATP deficiency suppresses NAD + regeneration enzymes (e.g., nicotinamide mononucleotide adenylyl transferase (NMNAT)), while ROS overproduction oxidizes NADH pools, collectively collapsing mitochondrial-nuclear cross-talk ( Reuter, 2022 ). Therapeutic strategies aiming to break these cycles-such as mitochondrially targeted NAD + precursors or catalase-loaded nanoparticles-show promise in preclinical models of age-related neurodegeneration and cardiovascular disease.( Singhal et al., 2013 ; Rotllan et al., 2021 ) Peptide/protein drugs are effective weapons and protect human from various disease. Lots of antibody drugs have been developed against tumor.( Lin et al., 2005 ) In addition, there are peptide/protein designed for age-related diseases including osteoporosis.( Wu et al., 2022 ) Considering the close correlation between mitochondrial dysfunction and aging, the targeted delivery of drugs to mitochondria provides a promising paradigm against age-related diseases. 3. Mitochondrial drug delivery systems for age-related disease therapy Current advancements in anti-aging pharmacology emphasize organelle-specific interventions to counteract age-related cellular deterioration.( Petkovic et al., 2021 ) For instance, mitochondria-targeted ubiquinone (MitoQ) selectively accumulates in mitochondria through its TPP moiety, neutralizing ROS at complex I of the electron transport chain-a critical strategy to prevent age-associated mitochondrial membrane potential collapse.( Pokrzywinski et al., 2016 ; Shi et al., 2024 ) Resveratrol, a polyphenolic compound, activates SIRT1 via AMPK-dependent NAD + elevation, enhancing mitochondrial biogenesis through deacetylation and simultaneously suppressing NF-κB-mediated inflammaging in the cytoplasm.( Ungurianu et al., 2023 ) Moreover, by redirecting pharmaceutical agents to mitochondria instead of their original cytoplasmic targets through subcellular targeting strategies, the therapeutic mechanism could be fundamentally transformed, potentially unlocking novel anti-aging properties. For instance, recent studies demonstrated that when metformin, a conventional AMPK activator in the cytoplasm, was specifically delivered to mitochondria through TPP conjugation, it not only preserved its glucose-regulating capacity but avoid the regulation of chromosome epigenetics.( Zhang et al., 2025b ) This mitochondrial-targeted version showed superior effects in improving cellular energy metabolism and avoiding side effects compared to its non-targeted counterpart.( Saheera et al., 2020 ) In the field of treating age-related diseases, compared with small-molecular therapeutics, protein/peptide-based therapies offer unique advantages such as high specificity, low toxicity and multifunctionality.( Liu et al., 2022 ; Liu et al., 2018 ) However, the efficacy of protein/peptide-based therapies are greatly hampered due to the lack of sub-organelle accumulation. Thus, specific targeting protein/peptide to mitochondria might further elevate the efficacy and reduce possible side effects.( Theivendren et al., 2025 ) Mitochondria feature a double-membrane architecture, consisting of the outer mitochondrial membrane, inner mitochondrial membrane, intermembrane space, and mitochondrial matrix.( Sisalli et al., 2020 ) Due to the low permeability of the mitochondrial membrane, extra modifications are needed for protein/peptide-based therapeutics.( Sun et al., 2023 ) Furthermore, targeted delivery to certain parts of the mitochondria might be one of the critical factors in enhancing the efficacy of anti-aging drugs.( Chen et al., 2022 ) Based on current tactics, high-positive-charge lipophilic groups like TPP and MTFP are deeply researched.( Xia et al., 2024a ) In addition, some innovative mitochondrial targeting strategies which have not been applied for are also discussed ( Fig. 1 ). TPP modification Compared with other membrane systems, a characteristic negative membrane potential around -150 to -180 mV is observed for the inner mitochondrial membrane.( Yatchenko and Ben-Shachar, 2021 ) This phenomenon arises from the proton translocation mechanism associated with electron transport chain in mitochondrial membranes. The resultant proton displacement creates a quantifiable proton concentration gradient, with the matrix compartment maintaining lower proton availability than the extramitochondrial environment.( Berry et al., 2018 ) TPP is a commonly used mitochondrial-targeting unit. The molecular structure of TPP comprises a central phosphorus atom coordinated with three phenyl groups. The cationic nature of TPP enables its mitochondrial targeting via the electrostatic attraction. The mitochondrial membrane potential drives preferential accumulation of TPP derivatives, establishing a pronounced concentration gradient between mitochondria and cytoplasmic compartments to achieve organelle-specific targeting.( Cheng et al., 2023 ) Although TPP is widely used for mitochondrial targeting. For example, in antitumor therapy, biodegradable silica nanoparticles (BS-NPs) were chemically modified with cell-penetrating polydisulfides (CPD) to achieve efficient intracellular cargo delivery. Subsequent surface modification of BS-NPs with TPP ultimately enabled successful delivery of the antibody MTCO2 into mitochondria. However, to the best of our knowledge, there are no reported cases of protein/peptide delivery using TPP for age-related disease. This might be attributed to that TPP modification might change the conformation or binding activity of potential protein/peptide drugs. Therefore, we speculate that cleavable linkers between peptide/protein and TPP might be involved to enable responsive drug release in mitochondria in the future.( Kim et al., 2021 ) However, mitochondrial-targeted small-molecular delivery systems based on TPP strategy for anti-aging therapy are classic and effective.( Oppedisano et al., 2020 ) Summarizing the latest studies might provide inspiration for engineering TPP modified protein/peptide delivery systems. Injury caused by cerebral ischemia-reperfusion is one of the main causes of death and disability in elder people. The underlying pathological mechanism is closely related to mitochondrial damage mediated by ROS. Therefore, intracellular delivery of antioxidants is an important therapeutic strategy against cerebral ischemia-reperfusion damage. However, there are many drawbacks in current neuroprotective potential antioxidants. Poor water solubility, low oral bioavailability and difficulty in penetrating the blood-brain barrier all limit the actual clinical application. To overcome the above problems, researchers have developed targeted nanocapsules by encapsulating the quercetin (Qc) with PLGA for oral delivery. To realize mitochondrial targeting, the TPP was directly mixed with PLGA polymer. Swarupa Ghosh et.al proposed that the lipophilic matching between PLGA and TPP enabled uniform dispersion during the oil phase mixing stage. And subsequent emulsification and solidification formed a stable composite matrix structure. In in vitro experiment, the JC-1 staining assay results showed that the nanocapsules with TPP modification better maintained mitochondrial membrane potential compared with free Qc and nanocapsules without TPP modification, evidencing by high intensity of yellow fluorescence. Quantitative estimation of the membrane potential further confirmed that the TPP modified nanocapsules can protect the integrity and stability of the mitochondrial membrane. ( Ghosh et al., 2017 ) Mass spectrometry analysis showed that the Qc content in mitochondria of the QC nanocapsules group was significantly increased compared with that of the TPP-free control group (0.974±0.05 μg/mg protein) and the free Qc group (0.638±0.08 μg/mg protein), reaching 1.632±0.06 μg/mg protein. Furthermore, cerebral edema in the N1QC nanocapsule group was significantly improved (p&lt;0.001). Although directly encapsulating TPP and antioxidant drugs together can significantly enhance the therapeutic efficacy, the concerns and difficulties due to the non-covalent mixing still hinders the further application in treating cerebral ischemia-related diseases.( Wang et al., 2020 ) TPP is a lipophilic cationic molecule, which can easily enter cells via passive diffusion or non-specific endocytosis. In contrast, other small molecules or nanoparticles are usually taken up by non-specific endocytosis through the endosome-lysosome pathway. Therefore, the non-covalent binding TPP and therapeutic agent cannot guarantee the precise mitochondrial delivery due to the distinct intracellular transfer of TPP and other agents. Moreover, the acidic environment and hydrolytic enzymes in lysosomes will degrade the therapeutic agents, preventing it from reaching mitochondria. Thus, there is an urgent need to develop carriers that simultaneously possess the blood-brain barrier penetration ability, mitochondrial targeting, and lysosomes escape. To address this, Xiaoyu Xia designed a dual-delivery lycopene (LYC) nanoparticle system. Firstly, a recombinant human H-ferritin (rHuHF) was selected as the carrier. Subsequently, LYC was dissolved and the pH was ad-justed to induce the self-assembly of rHuHF nanocages which further encapsulated LYC. Lastly, TPP-NHS ester was conjugated to the amino groups of rHuHF, yielding dual-delivery TPP-rHuHF-LYC nanoparticles (NPs). In both mitochondrial membrane potential and mitochondrial superoxide anion indicator assays revealed that the TPP-rHuHF-LYC nanoparticle group preserved mitochondrial functional integrity. The excessive superoxide anions within mitochondria were effectively scavenged and the mitochondrial damage induced by oxidative stress was alleviated, compared with the oxidative damage model group. Furthermore, quantitative analysis of mitochondrial permeability transition pore (mPTP) closure showed that the high-concentration (200 nM) TPP-rHuHF-LYC treatment group exhibited a significantly higher mPTP protection level (93%) than the low- (50 nM, 76%) and medium-concentration (100 nM, 80%) groups, which was close to the normal control group. The reported results indicated that the nanoparticles can inhibit excessive mPTP opening, prevent leakage of mitochondrial contents, and avoid mitochondrial dysfunction and cell apoptosis. Finally, the results of mitochondrial ATP and mtDNA content assays demonstrated that the nanoparticles improved mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation function, promoted energy synthesis, and protected mitochondrial genomic stability. Notably, in the comparison between TPP-rHuHF-LYC and rHuHF-LYC, it can be observed that the rHuHF-LYC without TPP modification exhibit strong fluorescent overlapping signals with the lysosomes of PC12 cells, indicating that they are captured by lysosomes; while the TPP-modified TPP-rHuHF-LYC nanoparticles are almost separated from the lysosomal signals, demonstrating that they successfully achieve lysosomal escape and avoid degradation by lysosomes. In the nesting experiment for evaluating daily neurological function, the TPP-rHuHF-LYC group achieved the highest nesting score, which was almost comparable to that of the normal control group and significantly higher than those of the D-gal group and the TPP-rHuHF group. Mitochondrial Targeting Functional Peptide MTFPs are a class of short peptides, that specifically accumulate in mitochondria. MTFPs, whether engineered or naturally occurring can be used to deliver therapeutic agents, probes, or functional molecules to mitochondria.( Nam et al., 2018 ) Some MTFPs can even participate in modulating mitochondrial functions.( Szeto, 2008 ) Generally, MTFPs originate from diverse species and characterized with multifunctional properties. The underlying mechanisms of MTFPs can be categorized into: 1) Positively charged amino acids (e.g., arginine, lysine) mediate electrostatic interactions with the negatively charged inner mitochondrial membrane. 2) Amphipathic α-helical structures enable membrane penetration across both plasma and mitochondrial bilayers. These structural characteristics ensure accurate mitochondrial targeting and functional integration.( Mason et al., 2014 ; Zhan et al., 2019 ) SS-31, a classic mitochondria-targeting tetrapeptide, demonstrates dual functionality by localizing to mitochondria and restoring their activity.( Szeto, 2008 ) Via electrostatic and hydrophobic interactions, SS-31 can interplay with cardiolipin.( Ye et al., 2024 ; Szeto, 2014 ; Sathappa et al., 2017 ) SS-31 further eliminates ROS through its dimethyltyrosine moiety and suppresses low-density lipoprotein oxidation.( Cho et al., 2007 ; Hao et al., 2015 ) It preserves mitochondrial integrity by mitigating oxidative damage and blocking ROS-induced dysfunction and cell death, demonstrating therapeutic efficacy in models with impaired ATP synthesis and elevated ROS levels.( Li et al., 2024b ; Peng et al., 2021 ) As mentioned previously, ischemic diseases become increasingly prevalent with age, featuring a narrow therapeutic window and generally poor prognosis. Other possible therapeutic strategies The current strategy about mitochondrial-targeted delivery to overcome age-related diseases is limited. However, the strategies applied in other conditions can be broadened for anti-aging therapies. Understanding these targeting mechanisms and effects may provide promising solutions. Herein, we enumerate several applications from antitumor therapy. Owing to the intrinsic strong electronegative of mitochondria, functionalization with cationic functional groups or positively charged peptides enables efficient organelle-specific targeting.( Li et al., 2024c ) Such charge-based interactions promote spatiotemporally precise mitochondrial delivery of therapeutic cargo by exploiting intrinsic bioelectrical properties, thereby optimizing drug delivery accuracy and improving efficacy in managing pathologies involving mitochondrial dysfunction.( Tang et al., 2020 ) This mechanism demonstrates the potential of leveraging electrostatic forces to refine therapeutic strategies at the subcellular level.( Shueng et al., 2022 ) 2-(Dimethylamino)ethyl methacrylate (DEA) is a small hydrophilic molecule that carries a strong positive charge due to its tertiary amine group. DEA achieves selective mitochondrial targeting by interacting with the negative mitochondrial membrane potential. This enables strong electrostatic interactions, facilitating its effective subcellular localization. Despite this advantage, the application in aging-related diseases remains underexplored. However, the potential of DEA in anticancer therapy targeting has attracted significant attention. A no-table example is the poly(2-(N-oxide-N,N-dimethylamino) ethyl methacrylate)-block-poly (2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate) (OPDMA-HEMA) for celastrol delivery developed by Yu Geng’s research group. The cationic OPDMA segment interacted with mitochondrial membrane potential to promote celastrol accumulation, achieving 90% colocalization with mitochondria in B16F10 cells as confirmed by confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM) and MetaMorph analysis.( Geng et al., 2022 ) Based on the similar strategy, Qiuyi Li et.al developed a P-DEA-CPT conjugate, which comprises an N-(2-hydroxypropyl)methacrylamide (HPMA) copolymer carrier functionalized with a 13% molar ratio of DEA. The conjugated DEA endowed optimal mitochondrial targeting and the camptothecin (CPT) was grafted on the copolymer via azelaic acid linkers. This design realized high CPT loading (∼10 wt%) and a positively charged surface (zeta potential: 19.75 ± 0.21 mV). The positively charged surface further enhanced electrostatic interactions with mitochondria. To validate mitochondrial targeting, 4T1 cells were incubated with FITC-labeled HPMA copolymers (P-DEA-FITC), followed by the isolation of mitochondria for flow cytometry analysis. The P-DEA (13% molar ratio)-FITC exhibited a remarkable 24-fold increase in mitochondrial fluorescence intensity compared to the non-modified P-FITC control. Future prospect and conclusion Due to the close relationship between mitochondrial function and the development of age-related diseases, research on mitochondrial-targeted drug delivery systems has gradually become a hot spot. By modifying the targeting groups on the surface of drugs or carriers, it becomes possible to regulate organelle function or achieve efficient targeted drug delivery. Although the mitochondria-targeted drug delivery systems (DDS) hold great promise in combating age-related disease, the limitations and translational challenges still remain, especially regarding safety, specificity, and long-term applicability. The safety profile of mitochondria-targeted DDS remains a translational barrier that requires rigorous evaluation. First, cytotoxicity of targeting moieties and carriers shall not be overlooked. For instance, TPP exhibits dose-dependent cytotoxicity by disrupting mitochondrial membrane integrity. Although TPP-conjugated nanoparticles have demonstrated biocompatibility in short-term studies, the long-term accumulation of TPP in non-target tissues (e.g., liver and kidneys) might induce mitochondrial dysfunction in healthy cells, leading to organ damage. In addition, the specificity of mitochondria-targeted DDS is determined by targeting moieties and their interaction with mitochondrial components. However, the mitochondrial heterogeneity across tissues and cell types and the dynamic changes in mitochondrial phenotypes during aging might misdirect the mitochondria-targeted DDS. For example. Cardiac mitochondria have higher cardiolipin content than neuronal mitochondria, which may affect the binding affinity of MTFPs like SS-31. Additionally, within aged tissues, senescent cells coexist with quiescent and proliferative cells, each with distinct mitochondrial characteristics. Regarding the long-term applicability, anti-aging interventions require long-term and repeated administration to delay or reverse age-related decline. Non-invasive administration is preferred for anti-aging treatment. Long-term toxicity might occur due to carrier accumulation. Non-biodegradable carriers might accumulate in mitochondria over months of administration. More biodegradable and biocompatible materials are necessary.” Moreover, there are distinctions between general disease models and truly aging-driven pathologies. Briefly, in general disease models (e.g., H2O2-induced cell injury or MCAO-induced stroke) typically involve acute, single-hit mitochondrial damage (e.g., rapid ROS burst or membrane collapse). In contrast, aging-driven pathologies (e.g., osteoarthritis), intervertebral disc degeneration are characterized by chronic, multifactorial mitochondrial dysfunction—including mtDNA mutations, impaired mitophagy and inflammaging. Another critical difference is the presence of comorbidities and physiological decline in aging. Aged patients often suffer from multiple age-related diseases and their mitochondria damaged by cumulative toxins. General disease models rarely recapitulate this complexity and might lead to overestimation of DDS efficacy in preclinical studies. In near future, artificial intelligence can also be used to enable precision engineering of mitochondrial-targeted delivery systems to combat aging pathologies. By analyzing mitochondrial proteomics and metabolomics data, AI algorithms can pin-point senescence-associated biomarkers, such as mtDNA mutations, for targeted intervention. By simulating du-al-membrane penetration capabilities and calculating optimal surface charge for cristae-specific accumulation, the mitochondrion import rate can be further improved. Thus, artificial intelligence assisted system design is a promising way to revolutionize mitochondrion targeting for aging-related management. In summary, mitochondria-targeted therapies hold promise for addressing aging-related decline. Aging, linked to mitochondrial dysfunction caused by ROS and mitochondrial DNA damage, requires precise strategies to restore mitochondrial function. Recent advances, such as small-molecule modifications, MTFP-guided delivery systems and some other strategies might be used to enable targeted drug delivery to mitochondria, effectively reducing oxidative stress and improving energy production. Research on these approaches highlights their potential to slow aging processes while avoiding side effects. With improved safety and scalable methods, mitochondrial-targeted treatments are advancing toward clinical use, offering new ways to combat age-related diseases and promote healthier aging.
