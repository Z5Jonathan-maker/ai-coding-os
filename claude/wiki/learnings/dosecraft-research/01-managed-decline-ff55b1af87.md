# 01-managed-decline

Why Modern Medicine Is Designed to Manage Your Symptoms, Not Fix You

The modern healthcare system doesn't cure disease. It manages it. Every symptom gets a prescription. Every prescription has side effects that require another prescription. You're not getting better — you're getting more medicated.

This is the cycle that leading clinical practitioners have identified as the central failure of conventional medicine. You present with a symptom. You leave with a pill. That pill creates a new symptom. You return. You leave with another pill. The only entity that profits from this cycle is the pharmaceutical industry.

The patient gets sicker. The prescriptions multiply. The root cause — the actual biological failure driving the symptoms — is **never addressed**.

A 2020 JAMA Internal Medicine study revealed that polypharmacy — the concurrent use of five or more medications — in adults over 53 produces **cascading adverse effects** that compound exponentially. Each additional medication doesn't add risk linearly; it multiplies it. Drug interactions become unpredictable. Organ systems that were functioning are destabilized by treatments meant for other organ systems.

Source: JAMA Internal Medicine, 2020 — Polypharmacy and Adverse Drug Events in Older Adults

Takes five or more prescription medications daily. Not because they're sicker than previous generations — but because the system is designed to add prescriptions, not remove them.

Two of the most commonly prescribed medications in the world illustrate the managed decline model perfectly. Each "solution" introduces a new biological problem — which then requires its own intervention.

**Prescribed for:** High cholesterol / cardiovascular risk reduction

**What it actually does:** Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase — the enzyme responsible for cholesterol synthesis. But that same pathway produces **Coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10)**, a critical cofactor for mitochondrial energy production.

Clinical Multiple meta-analyses confirm statin-induced CoQ10 depletion and associated myopathy.

**Prescribed for:** Type 2 diabetes / blood sugar management

**What it actually does:** Metformin reduces hepatic glucose output and improves insulin sensitivity. But it simultaneously **depletes Vitamin B12** and impairs its absorption in the ileum.

Clinical Long-term metformin use associated with B12 deficiency in 10-30% of patients (BMJ, 2010).

This isn't about blame. It's about incentive structures. When the business model depends on repeat customers, prevention is bad for the bottom line.

A patient who is cured generates exactly **zero future revenue**. A patient who is chronically managed generates revenue every single month — doctor visits, prescriptions, lab work, specialist referrals, surgical interventions. The system doesn't fail when people stay sick. **The system works exactly as designed.**

Leading practitioners with decades of clinical experience have observed this pattern thousands of times: patients arrive on 8-12 medications, each prescribed to manage the side effects of another. The root cause — the actual biological failure — has never been investigated.

Expert Observation from 25+ years of clinical practice in sports medicine and longevity.
