# 26-oral-vs-injectable

The definitive case for why oral peptides do not work — and the narrow list of compounds actually designed for oral use.

This chapter could save you thousands of dollars. If you are taking oral BPC-157, oral TB-500, or oral anything that is a peptide — you are throwing money away.

This is not a matter of dose optimization or enteric coating. The human digestive system is specifically designed to annihilate peptide chains. Here is why.

Your stomach and small intestine are loaded with proteases — pepsin, trypsin, chymotrypsin — whose entire function is to break peptide bonds into individual amino acids. They evolved specifically to destroy peptide chains. They are extremely efficient at it.

Hydrochloric acid denatures protein structures on contact. Peptide tertiary and secondary structures unfold, exposing bonds to enzymatic cleavage. BPC-157 is 15 amino acids — it never survives this environment intact.

Even if fragments survive the stomach and intestines, they hit the liver before reaching systemic circulation. The liver's cytochrome P450 enzymes metabolize and clear what is left. Bioavailability after all three barriers: effectively **zero**.

Yes, BPC-157 is derived from a protective protein found in gastric juice. But "derived from" does not mean "can be delivered through." Insulin is produced by the pancreas — that does not mean you can drink insulin and expect it to work. The delivery system matters. BPC-157 must bypass the digestive system entirely to maintain its 15-amino-acid structure.

Expert Clinical Dedicated podcast episodes addressing this topic: #168, #258, #307 — all reaching the same conclusion. No clinical trial has demonstrated meaningful systemic bioavailability of oral peptides.

Peptides are chains of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. The human digestive system is purpose-built to break peptide bonds — that is its job. The process is thorough and fast:

The result: a 15-amino-acid peptide like BPC-157 is reduced to individual amino acids — the biological equivalent of feeding a book into a shredder and expecting it to still be readable.

There is exactly one situation where oral BPC-157 has a defensible use case: **direct gastrointestinal conditions** — where the target tissue is the gut lining itself.

BPC-157 is derived from Body Protection Compound found in gastric juice. When taken orally for GI-specific conditions (IBS, leaky gut, gastric ulcers, intestinal inflammation), the peptide contacts the target tissue directly before being degraded. It does not need to survive into systemic circulation because the therapeutic target is the gut wall itself.

| Condition | Route | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Leaky gut / intestinal permeability | Oral (capsule or liquid) | Direct contact with intestinal epithelium before degradation |
| Gastric ulcers | Oral (empty stomach) | BPC-157 contacts ulcer site in stomach lining |
| IBS / Crohn's (GI symptoms) | Oral + SubQ (dual approach) | Oral for local GI effect; SubQ for systemic anti-inflammatory |
| Joint pain, tendon injury, brain fog | SubQ ONLY | Systemic target — oral will never reach these tissues intact |

**Critical distinction:** Oral BPC-157 for GI conditions is a local delivery strategy, NOT evidence that oral peptides achieve systemic bioavailability. The two claims are entirely different.

Intranasal delivery bypasses the GI tract entirely by absorbing peptides through the nasal mucosa — a thin, highly vascularized membrane that allows direct access to the bloodstream and, in some cases, the brain via the olfactory nerve pathway.
