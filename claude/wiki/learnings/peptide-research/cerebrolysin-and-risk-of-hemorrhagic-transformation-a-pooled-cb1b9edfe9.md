# Cerebrolysin and Risk of Hemorrhagic Transformation: A Pooled Analysis of Recent Studies

Given the growing body of data from randomized controlled trials and observational cohorts, we performed a focused quantitative synthesis of studies published between 2021 and 2025 that evaluated the safety of Cerebrolysin with respect to intracranial bleeding events. Because Cerebrolysin is often used with reperfusion therapies that have a bleeding risk, it is important to balance the trade-off between its positive neuroprotective effects and the cost of increased hemorrhagic complications. Additionally, the neurotrophic and anti-inflammatory effects of Cerebrolysin 1 may help preserve the integrity of blood–brain barrier and reduce matrix-mediated vascular damage, offering a biological rationale for a potential reduction in hemorrhagic transformation (HT). A literature search of PubMed, Scopus, and Web of Science was conducted from their inceptions to June 2025, following PRISMA 2020 guidelines. 2 We included randomized controlled trials and observational cohort reports on hemorrhagic outcomes in acute ischemic stroke patients treated with Cerebrolysin. Nonhuman studies, reviews, case reports, abstracts, and studies that did not produce hemorrhagic outcome data were excluded. Quality assessment was performed using the ROB2 tool ( https://methods.cochrane.org/bias/resources/rob-2-revised-cochrane-risk-bias-tool-randomized-trials ). The search strategy is presented in detail in Supplementary Table 1 (in the online-only Data Supplement). Our analyses identified four eligible studies conducted in Egypt, Russia, Croatia, and Poland that collectively enrolled 635 patients. 3 4 5 6 The study designs included both randomized and observational cohorts, and comprised 274 patients who received Cerebrolysin and 361 patients who served as controls. The mean patient age ranged from 59.3 to 76.0 years in the Cerebrolysin arm and from 61.8 to 77.0 years in the control arm. The proportion of males varied between 41.3% and 60.9% in the intervention arm and 47.6% to 60.0% in the control arm. The study characteristics for the included trials and the risk of bias are presented in Supplementary Table 2 (in the online-only Data Supplement). Symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage (sICH) was the main safety endpoint, and was examined in all four included studies. 3 4 5 6 The pooled odds ratio (OR) derived using a Hartung–Knapp REML random-effects model was 0.195 (95% confidence interval [CI]=0.031 to 1.214), which equated to a log OR of -1.638, suggesting that the risk of sICH was lower when taking Cerebrolysin, but with no statistically significant differences. The studies exhibited moderate heterogeneity (I 2 =57.5%). The leave-one-out analyses yielded ORs ranging from 0.162 to 0.330, with no single study substantially influencing the pooled estimate. Fig. 1 represents a more-detailed assessment of this outcome. The secondary outcome of any HT was assessed in three studies (n=535). 3 4 5 The pooled OR was 0.341 (95% CI=0.065–1.788, log OR=-1.076), again indicating a trend toward lower odds of HT in the Cerebrolysin arms as seen in Fig. 1 . However, this finding was also not statistically significant, with substantial heterogeneity observed (I 2 =66.6%). Sensitivity analyses demonstrated that the directionality of the effect was stable, but with persistent variability across the studies. The funnel plot for sICH did not exhibit any major asymmetry, although formal publication bias testing was not feasible due to the small number of studies (k&lt;10), as seen in the Supplementary Table 2 (in the online-only Data Supplement). Overall, the evidence supports Cerebrolysin being linked to a reduced risk of HT after an acute ischemic stroke; however, the effect estimates are imprecise and not significant, which is probably due to the small number of trials, small samples, and methodological variations. Most importantly, the consistency of the effect direction across individual studies is reassuring and highlights the need for larger, adequately powered randomized controlled trials to accurately determine the safety profile of Cerebrolysin. Given the clinical importance of sustaining an overall clinical benefit for neuroprotection while considering the hemorrhagic safety in acute stroke, future studies should prioritize standardizing the definitions of hemorrhagic outcomes, adequately stratifying study analyses according to reperfusion strategies, and applying follow-up measures over longer time periods. While the current evidence supports cautious optimism, it does not allow definitive conclusions to be drawn about the neuroprotective effects of Cerebrolysin against hemorrhagic complications.
