Abstract Aims To determine whether adults with type 2 diabetes (T2D) treated with retatrutide report greater changes in self‐reported appetite, dietary restraint, and disinhibition compared to placebo or dulaglutide and to examine associations with weight change. Materials and Methods These pre‐specified exploratory analyses examined changes from baseline in Appetite Visual Analogue Scale (VAS) and Eating Inventory (EI) scores after 24 and 36 weeks of once‐weekly treatment with placebo, dulaglutide 1.5 mg, or retatrutide 0.5, 4, 8, or 12 mg in 275 adults with T2D. Changes from baseline with retatrutide were compared to those with placebo and dulaglutide. Post‐hoc correlations between changes in body weight and Appetite VAS/EI scores were also examined. Results Compared with placebo, participants who received retatrutide ≥4 mg reported greater reductions from baseline in overall appetite, hunger, and prospective food consumption (l at Week 24 (all p &lt;0.05)). Differences versus dulaglutide were less consistent. Improvements from baseline versus placebo in EI Perceived Hunger and Disinhibition were greater in participants who received retatrutide 8 and 12 mg at Weeks 24 and 36. Dietary Restraint increased from baseline versus placebo only in participants who received retatrutide 12 mg at Week 36 (all p &lt;0.05). Reductions in Perceived Hunger and Disinhibition, and increases in Dietary Restraint were significantly correlated with reductions in body weight at Week 36 ( r = 0.28, r = 0.36, and r = 0.31, respectively). Conclusions Perceived hunger and tendency to overeat (disinhibition) were reduced with higher doses of retatrutide, compared with placebo. Greater weight reduction was associated with decreased perceived hunger and disinhibition and increased dietary restraint. Plain Language Summary What Is the Context and Purpose of this Research Study? Retatrutide is a new treatment currently being developed for people with obesity and people with type 2 diabetes. This study compared the changes in appetite and eating behaviours of adults with T2D who were treated with retatrutide, dulaglutide (an alternative treatment), or placebo (i.e., no treatment). We examined changes in feelings of hunger, satisfaction and fullness, the desire to eat certain types of foods (e.g., sweet, salty or fatty foods), the tendency to overeat (e.g., when stressed), and conscious efforts to limit food intake. We also examined the potential link between changes in appetite or eating behaviours and changes in weight during retatrutide treatment. What Was Done? Adults with type 2 diabetes who participated in this trial completed two questionnaires that measured their eating habits at several time points during the 36‐week period in which they received treatment. The questionnaire responses of people who received retatrutide each week (1–4 different doses) were compared with the responses of people who received no treatment (i.e., placebo) and of people who received dulaglutide treatment. Any potential links between the amount of weight reduction people experienced while receiving treatment during the study and their questionnaire responses were also examined. What Were the Main Results? This study showed that adults with type 2 diabetes who received higher doses of retatrutide reported being less likely to feel hungry or overeat compared to those who received no treatment (i.e., placebo) or those who received dulaglutide. On average, people who experienced greater weight reduction also reported being less hungry and less likely to overeat. What Is the Originality and Relevance of this Study? This is the first study to assess changes in appetite and eating behaviours of adults with type 2 diabetes who received retatrutide. Diabetes Obes Metab . 2025 ; 27 ( 12 ): 6988 ‐ 6998 . doi: 10.1111/dom.70097 40916752 PMC12587234 1 INTRODUCTION Retatrutide is a novel synthetic molecule with agonist action at glucose‐dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP), glucagon‐like peptide‐1 (GLP‐1) and glucagon receptors that is currently under development for weight management in people with obesity and the treatment of type 2 diabetes (T2D). 1 In a Phase 2 placebo‐controlled trial ( NCT04867785 ) in adults with T2D, retatrutide showed substantial improvement in the primary endpoint at Week 36 with glycated haemoglobin (HbA1c) reduction ranging from 0.54% to 2.16% across the 0.5–12 mg dose groups. These differences were significantly different compared with placebo ( p &lt;0.01 for retatrutide doses ≥4 mg) and dulaglutide ( p &lt;0.001 for retatrutide 12 mg). 2 Body weight was also reduced at Week 36 (ranging from 3.3 to 17.2 kg), with the largest decrease seen in the retatrutide 12 mg group ( p &lt;0.05 vs. placebo and p &lt;0.01 vs. dulaglutide for retatrutide ≥4 mg) after 36 weeks of treatment in adults with T2D. 2 Similar results were seen in another Phase 2 study of retatrutide, with additional improvements in blood pressure and lipid metabolism in adults with obesity. 3 In these studies, the safety profile of retatrutide was consistent with GLP‐1 receptor agonists and dual GIP/GLP‐1 receptor agonists. 2 , 3 Evidence suggests that beyond glycaemic control and weight reduction, benefits of GLP‐1 treatment may include reducing risk factors for cardiometabolic disease and improving quality of life. 4 , 5 , 6 , 7 GLP‐1 receptor agonists have also been shown to decrease energy intake via actions in the hypothalamus and brainstem and to slow gastric emptying rate. 8 GIP receptor agonism enhances the effects of GLP‐1 receptor agonism to reduce food intake and improves insulin sensitivity and lipid storage in adipose tissue, 9 , 10 while glucagon agonism reduces appetite, increases energy expenditure, and delays gastrointestinal transit. 1 , 11 Activation of all three receptors may contribute to the weight reduction observed with retatrutide, but it is unclear if changes in appetite, eating attitudes, and eating behaviours are a key mechanism behind retatrutide's clinical results. Thus, the objective of this study was to explore changes in self‐reported appetite (e.g., hunger, satiety, and fullness), desire to eat certain types of foods, dietary restraint, and disinhibition in adults with T2D who received retatrutide, dulaglutide (a GLP‐1 receptor agonist), or placebo in the aforementioned Phase 2 study over 36 weeks. This analysis also examined the association between change in these constructs and change in weight during retatrutide treatment. 2 MATERIALS AND METHODS 2.1 Design This was a randomised, double‐blind, placebo‐ and active comparator‐controlled, parallel‐group, Phase 2 trial conducted in 42 research and healthcare centres in the United States (US). The study is registered at ClinicalTrials.gov ( NCT04867785 ) and full methodological details have been published. 2 In summary, participants were randomised in equal numbers to once‐weekly subcutaneous injections of placebo, dulaglutide 1.5 mg or retatrutide 0.5, 4, 8 or 12 mg (escalated from 2 mg starting dose) maintenance doses for 36 weeks. The retatrutide 4 and 8 mg groups included participants on two dose escalation strategies: 4 mg with no escalation, 4 mg with starting dose 2, 8 mg with starting dose 2 and 8 mg with starting dose 4 mg. Treatment groups were stratified for baseline HbA1c (≤8.5% or &gt;8.5%) and body mass index (BMI, &lt;30 kg/m 2 or ≥30 kg/m 2 ). As previously reported, the primary objective was to assess the superiority of once‐weekly retatrutide (0.5, 4, 8 or 12 mg) relative to placebo for change from baseline in HbA1c (absolute %) at Week 24, though participants were followed for a total of 36 weeks of treatment. 2 The aim of the pre‐specified exploratory analyses reported herein was to test for a change in appetite and food intake‐related constructs with retatrutide treatment (0.5, 4, 8, or 12 mg) relative to placebo or dulaglutide. 2.2 Participants Participants were adults (aged 18–75 years) with T2D, HbA1c 7.0%–10.5%, stable body weight and BMI 25–50 kg/m 2 . Eligible participants had inadequate glycaemic control with diet and exercise alone, or with a stable dose of metformin (≥1000 mg once daily), for ≥3 months before screening. Ethics approval was obtained from the local ethics committees at each site by Advarra, and the study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and the Council for International Organisations of Medical Sciences International Ethical Guidelines and Good Clinical Practice Guidelines. All participants provided written informed consent before study entry. 3 APPETITE, EATING ATTITUDE. AND EATING BEHAVIOUR OUTCOMES 3.1 Appetite Visual Analogue Scale ( VAS ) Appetite VAS results were collected in a fasting state in the morning at baseline and Weeks 4, 8, 12, 16, 24, and 36, and consisted of eight questions, each scored on a straight line from 0 to 100 (Supplementary Table 1 ). 12 Participants placed a mark on the line that corresponded to their current feeling for each appetite sensation, and that was used to generate the score on the 0–100 scale. The first four questions were ratings of hunger, satiety, fullness, and prospective food consumption (PFC; how much food a person thinks they could eat). Higher VAS scores for satiety and fullness point to more satisfaction and feelings of fullness, respectively, whereas lower VAS scores for hunger and PFC indicate less hunger and ability or desire to eat, respectively. The overall Appetite VAS score is calculated as the average of the four individual scores: (satiety + fullness + [100‐PFC] + [100‐hunger])/4. A higher overall Appetite VAS score indicates less appetite, and a lower score indicates more appetite. 12 , 13 The last four questions were ratings of desire to eat foods that are sweet, salty, savoury, and fatty. Higher VAS scores for all four desire‐related questions reflect less desire to eat the food. 3.2 Eating inventory The Eating Inventory (Supplementary Table 1 ), previously known as the Three‐Factor Eating Questionnaire, was administered at baseline and Weeks 24 and 36. The instrument assesses the following three cognitive constructs that can affect eating behaviour and body weight: Disinhibition or the tendency to overeat in response to internal or external stimuli (e.g. when presented with desired foods or when stressed); Perceived Hunger or the subjective sense of hunger; and Dietary Restraint or the cognitive intent and ability to restrict food intake (Supplementary Table 1 ). 14 Lower Disinhibition and Perceived Hunger scores denote a lesser tendency to overeat and a lower predisposition to hunger, respectively, whereas a higher Dietary Restraint score denotes more restricted eating. 4 STATISTICAL ANALYSES A study sample size of 300 participants was estimated to provide at least 99% power for the primary objective, the results of which have been disclosed. 3 Results for the two retatrutide 4 mg groups and the two 8 mg groups were pooled separately for the current analyses. Baseline demographic and clinical characteristics were summarised descriptively: mean (standard deviation [SD]) for continuous data and n (%) for categorical data. Summary statistics for pooled treatments were calculated based on observed data without linear contrasts. The efficacy analysis set included data from all randomised subjects, with data excluded after treatment discontinuation and for subjects who discontinued study treatment due to inadvertent enrolment. Only patients included in the efficacy analysis set and with a non‐missing baseline value of the response variable were included in these analyses. Baseline values were analysed by treatment group by applying an analysis of variance model using type III sum of squares. Least squares mean (LSM) changes from baseline in Appetite VAS and Eating Inventory scores were evaluated within each group at Weeks 4, 8, 12, 16, 24, and 36, and at Weeks 24 and 36, respectively, using a mixed model for repeated measures adjusted for baseline HbA1c and BMI group (Variable = treatment*time + baseline HbA1c group [≤8.5%, &gt;8.5%]*time + baseline BMI group [&lt;30 kg/m 2 , ≥30 kg/m 2 ]*time + baseline*time). LSM changes in Appetite VAS and Eating Inventory from baseline with retatrutide were compared to those with placebo and dulaglutide at Weeks 4, 8, 12, 16, 24, and 36, and Weeks 24 and 36, respectively. Dulaglutide was not compared to placebo in this study. Model estimates for pooled treatments (retatrutide 4 and 8 mg groups) were estimated via a linear contrast that averages the estimates from individual treatment arms. P values were not corrected for multiple comparisons. Pearson correlation analyses between change from baseline in body weight and changes from baseline in Appetite VAS and Eating Inventory at Week 36 were conducted using data for patients included in the efficacy analysis set with a non‐missing baseline value of the response variable. 5 RESULTS This study was conducted between 13 May 2021 and 27 October 2022. Of 534 individuals screened for eligibility, 281 adults with T2D were randomised to a study treatment (randomised population). Excluding inadvertently enrolled individuals ( n = 6), the efficacy analysis set included 275 participants and their data prior to early treatment discontinuation or initiation of rescue therapy. VAS, visual analogue scale. 6 DISCUSSION To better understand the mechanisms associated with weight reduction during retatrutide treatment, two self‐report measures, the Appetite VAS and the Eating Inventory, were used to test for change in appetite, eating attitudes, and eating behaviours in adults with T2D receiving a range of retatrutide doses. The overall Appetite VAS results demonstrate that there were greater reductions in appetite from baseline to Week 24 for retatrutide dosages ≥4 mg compared with placebo. However, results from the individual VAS items were less consistent. Perceived Hunger and Disinhibition, measured with the Eating Inventory, were improved (reduced scores) compared to placebo with higher doses of retatrutide at Weeks 24 and 36. However, greater increases in Dietary Restraint were seen only in the retatrutide 12 mg group compared to the placebo group at Week 36. Importantly, and as expected, greater weight reduction was associated with improved Eating Inventory scores, specifically decreased Perceived Hunger and Disinhibition scores and increased Dietary Restraint scores. Weight reduction did not correlate with changes in the Appetite VAS scores. Together, these results suggest that cognitive‐behavioural constructs of eating behaviours (e.g., Disinhibition and Dietary Restraint) may better reflect changes in appetitive and inhibitory control of eating with retatrutide than measures of appetite assessed with the VAS. Improved eating behaviours, such as overeating and perceived hunger, were dose‐dependent and seen with higher doses of retatrutide compared to placebo or dulaglutide in adults with T2D at Week 36. Conscious restriction of food intake increased more in the retatrutide 12 mg group than in the placebo group at Week 36. The weight reduction effects of GLP‐1 receptor agonism, 2 , 15 , 16 , 17 , 18 , 19 , 20 , 21 , 22 and more recently GIP/GLP‐1 receptor agonism, 18 , 19 are well‐recognised. Selective GLP‐1 receptor agonists approved for the treatment of type 2 diabetes and obesity have been shown to reduce weight, reduce energy intake, decrease cravings for some high‐energy foods, and modify activation of key reward‐related brain areas in response to food‐related stimuli. 13 , 23 , 24 , 25 , 26 , 27 , 28 , 29 Furthermore, Phase 1 mechanism of action studies of tirzepatide, a dual GIP and GLP‐1 receptor agonist, approved for the treatment of type 2 diabetes and obesity, have shown reductions in weight; reduced energy intake with no effect on energy expenditure; reduced appetite, food cravings, food preferences, sensitivity to foods in the environment, and disinhibition, without an effect on dietary restraint compared with placebo in people with obesity. 15 , 30 Similarly, tirzepatide was associated with reductions in weight, fat mass, reduced energy intake, and reduced appetite in a Phase 1 mechanism of action study in patients with type 2 diabetes. 31 Reductions in food intake and appetite did not differ significantly between people with T2D receiving tirzepatide or semaglutide (a GLP‐1 receptor agonist) 31 whereas reductions in appetite and energy intake were significantly greater with tirzepatide compared with liraglutide (another GLP‐1 receptor agonist) in people with obesity. 15 Retatrutide is a triple agonist, targeting the glucagon receptor, in addition to the GLP‐1 and GIP receptors. 1 While GIP is an incretin hormone that enhances meal‐stimulated insulin secretion and modulates glucagon secretion in a glucose‐dependent manner to support postprandial lipid clearance and regulate food intake, 32 , 33 , 34 glucagon increases hepatic glucose output and has been shown to delay gastrointestinal transit, stimulate insulin secretion in certain circumstances, and reduce appetite and body weight. 9 , 35 Glucagon has been shown to reduce calorie intake when co‐infused with GLP‐1 in healthy participants. 36 In a separate study, 3 days of continuous glucagon infusion lowered energy intake on the third day but did not alter energy expenditure. 37 Importantly, glucagon modulates the metabolism of amino acids, and the integrated action of GLP‐1, GIP, and glucagon is necessary for the proper disposal of glucose, lipids, and amino acids. 38 , 39 , 40 There are no clinical data on the simultaneous effect of GIP, GLP‐1, and glucagon receptor agonism on appetite and eating constructs. However, limited data in preclinical species have demonstrated that triple receptor agonists reduce calorie intake. 1 , 41 It is unclear if retatrutide modulates energy expenditure or attenuates metabolic adaptation in humans, though retatrutide has been found to maintain energy expenditure during weight loss in animal models, and this may be due to its effects on the glucagon receptor. 1 Should retatrutide affect energy expenditure in humans, it could not only help to explain weight reduction but also might influence retatrutide's effects on appetite‐related constructs. For example, in humans, increasing total energy expenditure via exercise has little effect on appetite, or it may suppress appetite in the short term; however, in the long term, appetite tends to increase, as does energy intake. 42 In the current trial, dietary restraint increased in the retatrutide 12 mg group at Week 36 compared to placebo; however, participants in the 12 mg dose group had the longest titration period and may not therefore have experienced the full benefits of treatment until late in the trial. It is possible that activation of glucagon receptors drives further weight reduction. Additional studies with adequate controls in larger samples are required to explore dietary restraint and other constructs of eating behaviour in people with T2D treated with retatrutide. Retatrutide's effect on energy expenditure, calorie consumption, and usage with dietary lifestyle interventions is currently being investigated in an ongoing study in people with obesity ( NCT06313528 ). 43 The primary and main secondary findings of the current Phase 2 trial showed clinically meaningful improvements in glycaemic control and robust reductions in body weight in adults with T2D treated with retatrutide. 2 These body weight reductions exceeded those previously achieved over similar periods of time by adults with T2D treated with other incretin‐based therapies, such as dulaglutide, semaglutide, and tirzepatide. 2 , 16 , 17 , 18 , 19 , 20 , 21 , 22 The current findings suggest that modification of eating behaviours with retatrutide may be an important mechanism for weight reduction in these adults with T2D. However, the full mechanisms of action underlying retatrutide's weight reduction efficacy are not yet wholly understood, and individuals who experience significant appetite suppression may require counselling and monitoring to ensure optimal medical and nutritional outcomes. 7 LIMITATIONS This Phase 2 study had several limitations, including the small sample size and limited follow‐up period of 36 weeks. Furthermore, these were either pre‐specified or post hoc exploratory analyses with no adjustments for multiple comparisons. The results are therefore preliminary and can only be regarded as hypothesis‐generating. In addition, the impact of retatrutide treatment on patients' appetite profiles and behaviours may not have been fully captured, as it was limited to the scope of constructs measured in this trial. Dietary records of calorie intake were not collected during the study; therefore, it was difficult to establish correlations with individual Appetite VAS scores and body weight change. Further research is required to determine whether the measures used in this study adequately reflect patients' experiences given the high amount of weight reduction seen with retatrutide. 8 CONCLUSION This study suggests that retatrutide may improve correlates of eating, such as perceived hunger and tendency to overeat, compared to placebo or dulaglutide in adults with T2D. Furthermore, greater weight reduction with higher doses of retatrutide was associated with decreased perceived hunger and disinhibition, as well as increased dietary restraint. These results, in conjunction with improved glycaemic control and body weight reduction demonstrated in this Phase 2 study, suggest enhanced modification of eating behaviours with retatrutide, potentially due to its triple receptor agonist action. Additional studies are required to fully understand retatrutide's mechanism of action.
