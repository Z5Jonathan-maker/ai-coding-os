## Why Accurate Peptide Dosing Matters

Peptides are amino acid chains that operate within narrow effective ranges. Unlike many supplements where "more is roughly fine," peptides follow dose-response curves where:

- **Too little** produces no measurable effect
- **The effective range** triggers the intended biological response
- **Too much** can cause diminishing returns, unwanted side effects, or receptor desensitization

A peptide calculator standardizes this process so that every injection delivers exactly the intended microgram (mcg) or milligram (mg) dose.

### The Three Variables You Must Know

Every peptide dose calculation requires three inputs:

| Variable | What It Means | Example |
|----------|---------------|---------|
| **Peptide amount in vial** | Total milligrams of lyophilized powder | 5 mg BPC-157 |
| **Reconstitution volume** | Milliliters of bacteriostatic water added | 2 mL BAC water |
| **Desired dose** | Micrograms or milligrams per injection | 250 mcg per dose |

From these three values, every other number — concentration per mL, units on the syringe, doses per vial — can be derived.

## How to Calculate Peptide Concentration After Reconstitution

The foundational formula for peptide dosing is:

**Concentration (mcg/mL) = Total Peptide (mcg) / Reconstitution Volume (mL)**

### Step-by-Step Example

Suppose you have a 5 mg vial of BPC-157 and you add 2 mL of bacteriostatic water:

1. **Convert mg to mcg:** 5 mg = 5,000 mcg
2. **Divide by volume:** 5,000 mcg / 2 mL = 2,500 mcg/mL
3. **Result:** Each milliliter of solution contains 2,500 mcg of BPC-157

This concentration is your reference point for every subsequent calculation.

### Common Reconstitution Ratios

|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| 5 mg | 1 mL | 5,000 mcg/mL |
| 5 mg | 2 mL | 2,500 mcg/mL |
| 5 mg | 2.5 mL | 2,000 mcg/mL |

| 10 mg | 5 mL | 2,000 mcg/mL |

**Pro tip:** Many experienced practitioners recommend using 2 mL of BAC water per 5 mg vial because it creates round numbers that are easier to measure on a standard insulin syringe.

## How to Calculate Volume Per Dose

Once you know your concentration, calculate how much liquid to draw for each injection:

**Volume per dose (mL) = Desired dose (mcg) / Concentration (mcg/mL)**

### Example

You want 250 mcg of BPC-157 from a solution at 2,500 mcg/mL:

250 mcg / 2,500 mcg/mL = **0.1 mL per dose**

On a 1 mL (100-unit) insulin syringe, 0.1 mL = 10 units (tick marks).

## Body Weight-Adjusted Dosing

Some peptide protocols specify doses in micrograms per kilogram of body weight (mcg/kg). This approach accounts for individual size differences and is standard in clinical research.

### How to Calculate Weight-Based Doses

**Total dose (mcg) = Body weight (kg) x Dose per kg (mcg/kg)**

### Example

A protocol calls for 5 mcg/kg of a peptide. You weigh 180 lbs:

1. **Convert lbs to kg:** 180 / 2.205 = 81.6 kg
2. **Calculate dose:** 81.6 kg x 5 mcg/kg = 408 mcg
3. **Round to nearest practical unit:** 400 mcg or 410 mcg

## How to Calculate Doses Per Vial

Knowing how many doses a vial contains helps with cost planning and ordering:

**Doses per vial = Total peptide (mcg) / Dose per injection (mcg)**

### Example

A 5 mg (5,000 mcg) vial of TB-500 at 750 mcg per dose:

5,000 / 750 = **6.67 doses per vial** (round down to 6 usable doses, accounting for dead volume in the syringe)

## How to Use a Peptide Dosage Calculator: Step-by-Step

Here is the complete process from vial to syringe:

### Step 1: Gather Your Supplies

- Lyophilized peptide vial (check mg amount on label)
- Bacteriostatic water (BAC water)
- Insulin syringes (29-31 gauge, 1 mL)
- Alcohol swabs

### Step 2: Decide Your Reconstitution Volume

Choose how much BAC water to add. Standard ranges are 1-3 mL per vial. More water means lower concentration and easier measurement of small doses.

### Step 3: Calculate Concentration

Use the formula: Total mcg / mL of BAC water = mcg per mL

### Step 4: Determine Your Dose

Based on your protocol, identify your target mcg per injection. If using weight-based dosing, multiply your body weight in kg by the protocol's mcg/kg specification.

### Step 5: Calculate Syringe Volume

Divide your desired dose by concentration to get mL. Multiply by 100 to convert to insulin syringe units.

### Step 6: Verify and Double-Check

Before every injection, verify your math. A quick sanity check: if your dose is 10% of the vial's total content, you should be drawing 10% of the total reconstituted volume.

## Common Peptide Dosing Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Confusing mg and mcg

1 mg = 1,000 mcg. Mixing these units creates 1,000x dosing errors. Always confirm which unit your protocol uses.

### Mistake 2: Forgetting Dead Volume

Syringes retain approximately 0.05-0.1 mL of solution that cannot be injected. Over a full vial, this can cost you 1-2 doses. Account for this when calculating doses per vial.

### Mistake 3: Using the Wrong Syringe Scale

Insulin syringes come in 30-unit, 50-unit, and 100-unit variants. The tick marks represent different volumes on each. Always verify which syringe you are using before measuring.

### Mistake 4: Not Adjusting for Body Weight

Flat doses (e.g., "take 250 mcg") work for some peptides, but weight-based protocols exist for a reason. A 130-lb person and a 230-lb person may need very different doses for the same compound.

### Mistake 5: Ignoring Concentration After Reconstitution

The same peptide vial reconstituted with 1 mL versus 3 mL produces dramatically different concentrations. Always recalculate if you change your water volume.

## Advanced Dosing Concepts

### Titration Protocols

Leading practitioners recommend starting at 50-75% of the target dose for the first week, then increasing to full dose. This approach helps identify individual sensitivity and reduces the likelihood of side effects.

A typical titration schedule:

| Week | Percentage of Target Dose |
|------|--------------------------|
| Week 1 | 50% |
| Week 2 | 75% |
| Week 3+ | 100% |

### Frequency Considerations

Dosing frequency affects total weekly exposure. Two common approaches:

- **Daily dosing:** Lower per-dose amount, steadier blood levels
- **Twice-daily dosing:** Used for peptides with short half-lives (e.g., BPC-157 at ~4 hours)

**Total weekly dose = Per-injection dose x Number of injections per week**

### Splitting Doses for Injection Site Targeting

Some protocols call for splitting the daily dose between multiple injection sites — for example, injecting half the dose near the injury site and half subcutaneously in the abdomen. The total dose remains the same; only the distribution changes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I calculate peptide dosage from a vial?

Divide the total peptide content (in mcg) by the volume of bacteriostatic water you added (in mL) to get your concentration. Then divide your desired dose (in mcg) by that concentration to determine how much liquid to draw into your syringe. For a 5 mg vial reconstituted with 2 mL of BAC water, each 0.1 mL (10 units on a 100-unit syringe) contains 250 mcg.

### What is the best reconstitution ratio for peptides?

Most practitioners recommend 2 mL of bacteriostatic water per 5 mg vial, creating a concentration of 2,500 mcg/mL. This ratio produces easy-to-measure volumes on standard insulin syringes. However, for very low doses (under 100 mcg), using more water (e.g., 3 mL) provides more precise measurement.

### How do I convert peptide mg to mcg?

Multiply milligrams by 1,000. For example, 5 mg = 5,000 mcg. This conversion is essential because most peptide doses are specified in micrograms (mcg), while vials are labeled in milligrams (mg).

### Do I need to adjust peptide dose for body weight?

It depends on the peptide and protocol. Some compounds like BPC-157 use flat doses (e.g., 250-500 mcg regardless of weight), while others use weight-based dosing (e.g., 5-10 mcg/kg). Always check the specific protocol recommendations for the compound you are using.

### How many doses are in a peptide vial?

Divide the total peptide content by your dose per injection. A 5 mg (5,000 mcg) vial at 250 mcg per dose yields 20 doses. Account for 1-2 doses of loss due to syringe dead volume and reconstitution transfer.

### What syringe should I use for peptide injections?

Standard 1 mL (100-unit) insulin syringes with 29-31 gauge needles are the most common choice for subcutaneous peptide injections. For very small doses, a 0.5 mL (50-unit) syringe provides finer graduation marks and more precise measurement.

### Can I use a peptide calculator for multiple peptides at once?

Yes. When running a stack of multiple peptides, calculate each compound independently using its own vial size, reconstitution volume, and dose. The [DoseCraft Calculator](https://dosecraftapp.com/calculator) supports multi-peptide calculations in a single session.

### What happens if I miscalculate my peptide dose?

A small overcalculation (10-20% above target) is unlikely to cause serious issues for most research peptides but may increase side effects. A significant underdose will simply produce reduced or no results. If you suspect a major dosing error, recalculate from scratch and verify your reconstitution volume.
