# cycling-calendar

When to Start, Stop, and Restart Every Compound — the definitive scheduling reference from leading practitioners.

Know exactly which peptides need breaks, which ones run forever, and how to schedule an entire year of optimized cycling — all in one visual reference.

Not all peptides are created equal. Some compounds cause receptor downregulation with chronic use, while others can be run indefinitely without diminishing returns. Understanding the difference is the key to long-term optimization.

When certain receptors are chronically stimulated, your body reduces their number or sensitivity — a protective mechanism called downregulation. The result: the same dose produces weaker effects over time. Cycling allows receptors to reset, restoring full sensitivity when you resume the compound.

However, not every compound triggers this response. Peptides that work through structural repair pathways (like VEGF upregulation or collagen synthesis) often maintain efficacy indefinitely because they are supporting natural biological processes, not overstimulating receptors.

No receptor desensitization. No tolerance buildup. These compounds work through repair and maintenance pathways that do not downregulate with chronic use.

Require scheduled breaks to prevent receptor downregulation, tolerance, or cumulative toxicity risk. On/off periods are mandatory for sustained efficacy.

How you introduce compounds matters as much as which ones you choose. These rules prevent confusion, ensure accurate tracking, and maximize your ability to attribute effects to specific peptides.

Stagger introductions by at least 2 weeks between new compounds. If you start five peptides on Day 1, you have no idea which one is responsible for any effect — positive or negative. Sequential introduction is the only way to build reliable data about your individual response.

BPC-157 is the ideal first compound — it has the broadest safety profile, no cycling requirement, and establishes a systemic repair baseline. Run it solo for 2 weeks before adding your next compound. This gives you a clean reading on its individual effects.

Log daily metrics (energy, pain, sleep, mood) with each new introduction. After 2 weeks of stable data, add the next compound. This creates a staircase of evidence — you know exactly what each peptide contributes to your protocol.

You'll see this protocol everywhere — 5 days on, 2 days off. The truth: it depends entirely on the specific peptide and its receptor dynamics. "Forever" compounds like BPC-157 gain nothing from weekend breaks. Cycled compounds need their full off-period, not micro-breaks. Follow the compound-specific protocol from the master table, not a one-size-fits-all rule.

| Week | Action | Why |

| Week 3-4 | Add TB-500 | Loading phase — synergy with BPC-157 for tissue repair |
| Week 5-6 | Add MOTS-c or KPV | Metabolic or immune layer — choose based on primary goal |
| Week 7-8 | Add remaining compounds | By now you have clean data on each prior compound's contribution |

Track daily: compound, dose, injection site, time, subjective effects (energy, sleep quality, pain 1-10, mood). Weekly: photos of injection sites, body composition changes, bloodwork markers. Without this data, you cannot optimize — you can only hope.
