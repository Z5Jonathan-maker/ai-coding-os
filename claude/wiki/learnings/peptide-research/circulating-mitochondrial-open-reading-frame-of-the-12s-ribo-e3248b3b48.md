Abstract Background To date, the role of MOTS‐c (mitochondrial open reading frame of the 12S ribosomal RNA type‐c) in acute coronary syndrome remains largely unknown. We measured circulating MOTS‐c levels, markers of oxidative stress, and blood biochemical parameters in patients with acute coronary syndrome and examined their relationship with major adverse cardiac events (MACE). Methods A total of 400 subjects were recruited and divided into 3 groups: normal controls, unstable angina, and acute myocardial infarction, based on the clinical data and angiography results. Serum MOTS‐c and thiobarbituric acid reactive substances were measured upon initial admission. Hospitalization data, major adverse cardiac events, and a follow‐up duration of 18 months were recorded. Results The serum levels of MOTS‐c and thiobarbituric acid reactive substances were higher in patients with acute coronary syndrome and there was a positive correlation between MOTS‐c and thiobarbituric acid reactive substances. In addition, MOTS‐c levels showed a high sensitivity of 0.890 (cutoff value, 326.65 [95% CI, 253.41–631.84]; area under the curve, 0.739 [95% CI, 0.647–0.832], P &lt;0.001) in predicting the occurrence of unstable angina and acute myocardial infarction in the general population. Our data also showed that MOTS‐c/thiobarbituric acid reactive substances levels could be used to predict major adverse cardiac events in the group with acute myocardial infarction, with a sensitivity of 0.800 and specificity of 0.667 (cutoff value, 48.26 ng/umol [95% CI, 45.43–90.16 ng/umol]; area under the curve, 0.718 [95% CI, 0.598–0.839], P =0.003). In vitro studies demonstrated that oxidative stress induces MOTS‐c levels and MOTS‐c treatment reduces hypoxia‐induced oxidative stress through activating antioxidants. Conclusions The circulating MOTS‐c is associated with an increased risk of acute coronary syndrome and the imbalance between oxidative stress and circulating MOTS‐c may play a role in predicting major adverse cardiac events in patients with acute myocardial infarction. This study is among the first to explore the role of circulating MOTS‐c (mitochondrial open reading frame of the 12S ribosomal RNA type‐c) levels in patients with acute coronary syndrome, including unstable angina and acute myocardial infarction. It demonstrates a positive correlation between MOTS‐c and oxidative stress markers (thiobarbituric acid reactive substances), suggesting that MOTS‐c may be upregulated in response to oxidative stress. The balance between MOTS‐c and oxidative stress may be a critical determinant of major adverse cardiac events in severe acute coronary syndrome such as acute myocardial infarction. What Are the Clinical Implications? MOTS‐c could serve as a novel biomarker for early detection and risk stratification in patients with acute coronary syndrome, potentially aiding in timely diagnosis and intervention. The MOTS‐c/thiobarbituric acid reactive substances ratio may help to predict the likelihood of major adverse cardiac events in patients with acute myocardial infarction, offering a tool for personalized prognosis and management. Acute coronary syndrome (ACS) stands as the leading cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide. 1 , 2 Coronary artery bypass grafting and percutaneous coronary intervention have emerged as effective procedures in mitigating the morbidity and mortality associated with ACS, because these interventions play a crucial role in promptly alleviating blockages within the infarct‐related artery and restoring optimal myocardial perfusion. 3 However, a considerable number of patients persist in experiencing recurrent ischemic events despite the implementation of guideline‐directed antithrombotic therapy and a lower residual cardiovascular risk. 4 Oxidative stress and inflammation have gained recognition as 2 pivotal risk factors strongly associated with the instability of atherosclerotic plaques. 5 , 6 MDPs (mitochondrial‐derived peptides) are small bioactive peptides encoded by short open‐reading frames within mitochondrial DNA. Thus far, a total of 8 MDPs have been identified, all of which play critical roles as metabolic regulators. 7 These peptides have garnered considerable attention as novel biomarkers for cardiovascular and metabolic disorders. 8 We have shown that circulating humanin levels are lower in patients with coronary artery disease (CAD), and the low levels of humanin could be used as a prognostic biomarker for major cardiac events in humans. 9 Among the known MDPs, MOTS‐c (mitochondrial open reading frame of the 12S ribosomal RNA type‐c) has consistently demonstrated metabolic‐protective properties through various mechanisms, including antioxidative stress, anti‐inflammatory, and antiapoptotic effects. 8 , 10 MOTS‐c has shown potential in preventing insulin resistance, ameliorating diabetes, 11 and regulating a broad range of genes in response to glucose restriction. 12 In a recent study involving patients with stable CAD, MOTS‐c levels were found to be inversely correlated with the severity and complexity of CAD. However, few studies have explored the alterations in MOTS‐c levels during episodes of severe ischemia and hypoxia following the onset of ACS. Moreover, the relationship between MOTS‐C regulation in response to oxidative stress and its impact on prognosis remains largely unexplored. In our current study, we assessed the circulating levels of MOTS‐c, along with markers associated with oxidative stress and inflammation, in individuals diagnosed with ACS. We aimed to examine the potential relationship between these parameters and the occurrence of major adverse cardiac events (MACE) after percutaneous coronary intervention during longitudinal follow‐up. (The median follow‐up is 18 months with the interquartile range 18 and 19 months.) METHODS The data and materials used to support the findings of the study are available from the corresponding author upon request. Human Subjects This case–control study used blood samples collected from Chinese participants. In this study, we recruited a total of 400 subjects (age 30–82) presenting with acute chest pain and undergoing coronary angiography at the Chest Pain Center of the First Hospital of Jilin University between July and October 2021. Based on the clinical data and angiography results, the participants were categorized into 3 groups: normal control (37 cases), unstable angina (UA) (178 cases), and acute myocardial infarction (AMI) (141 cases). The normal group had no coronary atherosclerotic plaque, and the main diagnoses for these patients included hypertension (18 cases), diabetes (1 case), and myocardial bridge (18 cases). According to the 2020 European Society of Cardiology Guidelines for the management of ACS, 13 UA was defined as myocardial ischemia at rest or on minimal exertion in the absence of acute cardiomyocyte injury/necrosis. Among the participants with UA, coronary angiography revealed at least 1 vessel with a stenosis &gt;50%. AMI was characterized by severe chest pain, troponin elevation, and evidence of vascular stenosis or blockage on coronary angiography. We excluded 44 subjects from the analysis due to severe infection (6 cases), tumors (12 cases), diabetic ketosis (6 cases), uremia (7 cases), and multiple organ failure (9 cases). All participants provided written informed consent before their participation. The study protocol was approved by the Institutional Review Board of the First Hospital of Jilin University (Approval No. AF‐IRB‐032‐06, AF‐IRB‐029‐06), and the study was registered with the Chinese Clinical Trials Register (registration number ChiCTR2300068294). During coronary angiography, blood samples were collected from the catheter. All samples were processed within 4 hours, followed by centrifugation at 1370 g for 10 minutes. The supernatant was collected and frozen at −80 °C for further use. In addition, various data were obtained from the patients’ medical records, including age, sex, height, weight, history of hypertension, history of diabetes, history of hyperlipidemia, history of chronic kidney disease, levels of triglyceride, total cholesterol, low‐density lipoprotein cholesterol, high‐density lipoprotein cholesterol, fasting blood sugar (FBS), glutamic pyruvic transaminase, glutamic pyruvic aminotransferase, creatinine, urea nitrogen, uric acid, potassium, sodium and hemoglobin, white blood cell (WBC) counts, lymphocyte counts, neutrophil granulocyte counts, monocyte counts, platelet counts, ejection fraction (EF), end‐diastolic diameter of the left ventricle, lesion vessel information, and the number of stents. These data were collected to assess the relationship between the circulating levels of MOTS‐c, markers associated with oxidative stress and inflammation, and the occurrence of MACE after percutaneous coronary intervention during longitudinal follow‐up. Measurement of MOTS‐c Serum MOTS‐c levels were determined using Human MOTS‐c ELISA kit (cat number: CEX132Hu) purchased from Cloud‐Clone Corp (Wuhan, China). Human specimens of blood samples were obtained from the Department of Biobank, Division of Clinical Research, The first hospital of Jilin University. Thiobarbituric Acid Reactive Substances Assay Oxidative stress was measured using thiobarbituric acid reactive substances (TBARS) assay kit from Cayman Chemical (Ann Arbor, MI) to evaluate the levels of lipid peroxidation product, malondialdehyde, in serum. Clinical Follow‐Up MACE, including rehospitalization, heart failure, stroke, and mortality, were gathered from hospital records, and telephone interviews conducted at 3‐month intervals following discharge. This follow‐up procedure continued until the occurrence of death or until March 31, 2023, whichever came first. In cases where patients were lost to telephone follow‐up, missing mortality data were obtained from the population registry bureau to ensure complete data collection. The median duration of the follow‐up period was 18 months (interquartile range, 18–19 months), during which researchers systematically monitored and recorded the occurrence of any major adverse cardiac events to evaluate the impact of the study variables. Cell Culture Rat cardiomyoblasts, H 9 C 2 , were obtained from the Cell Bank of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (Shanghai, China). Cells were maintained in 90% DMEM (Biosharp, Hefei, Anhui, China) supplemented with 10% FBS (Hyclone, Logan, UT, USA) and 1% penicillin–streptomycin (Solarbio, Beijing, China) in an incubator with 5% CO 2 at 37 °C. The medium was changed every 2 days. The hypoxia was induced using CoCl 2 , a well‐known chemical hypoxia mimetic agent, which can mimic hypoxia/ischemic conditions by causing inactivation of hydroxylase enzymes and stabilization of HIF‐1α (hypoxia‐inducible factor‐1 alpha). Cells were treated with different concentrations of CoCl 2 (200, 400, 600, 800, and 1000 μmol/L; Sigma Aldrich; Merck KGaA) for different periods (6, 12, 24, and 48 hours). Optimal dose and time point were determined based on the results. To investigate the protective effects of MOTS‐C on CoCl 2 injury, H 9 C 2 cells were pretreated with MOTS‐C (0, 10, 20 and 50 μmol/L) (sequence: Met‐Arg‐Trp‐Gln‐Glu‐Met‐Gly‐Tyr‐Ile‐Phe‐Tyr‐Pro‐Arg‐Lys‐Leu‐Arg, Genscript; Nanjing, Jiangsu, China) for 24 hours before the treatment of CoCl 2 . Cell Counting Kit‐8 Assay Cell viability was assessed using a Cell Counting Kit‐8 kit (Apexbio, Boston, MA) in 96‐well plates according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Cell viability was present as percentage calculated by comparing the value of the treated group to the control group. Antioxidant Activity Assays H 9 C 2 cells (2×10 5 ) were seeded into 24‐well plates and treated with MOTS‐c (10, 20, and 50 μmol/L) for 24 hours followed by 600 μmol/L CoCl 2 for 24 hours. SOD (superoxide dismutase), catalase, and glutathione assay kits (Cayman, MI, USA) were used to detect the intracellular activity of SOD, and catalase and the production of glutathione. Statistical Analysis Continuous variables following a normal distribution were described as mean±SD, and categorical data were reported as percentages and raw numbers. Continuous variable statistical comparisons between groups were performed using the t test and 1‐way ANOVA if normally distributed, and the chi‐square or Fisher’s exact test was used for categorical data. Multivariable logistic regression was used to identify statistically significant risk factors for prognosis; models were adjusted for age and sex after including variables with a P value &lt;0.05 in the univariate analysis. Variables that showed significant changes were included in the multivariable Cox regression analysis to identify risk factors for MACE, with results reported as adjusted odds ratios (ORs) by age and sex, along with corresponding CIs and P values to indicate their statistical significance. The predictive value of the model for MACE was assessed using the receiver operating characteristic curve. Correlation analysis was conducted using the Pearson test, and survival analysis was performed using the Kaplan–Meier curve. All statistical analyses were conducted using SPSS 23.0 software (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA) and a 2‐tailed P &lt;0.05 was considered statistically significant. RESULTS General Characteristics of Participants The general characteristics of the participants are summarized in Table 1 . A higher proportion of subjects in the groups with UA and AMI were male compared with control group. No statistically significant differences were observed among the 3 groups in terms of age, body mass index, or histories of hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia, and chronic kidney disease. Participants in the groups with UA and AMI exhibited significantly elevated levels of urea acid compared with the control group. Subjects in the group with AMI demonstrated higher levels of total cholesterol, low‐density lipoprotein cholesterol, high‐density lipoprotein cholesterol, FBS, liver enzymes, and WBC compared with both the control group and group with UA. These findings suggest a greater degree of lipid dysregulation and systemic inflammation in the group with AMI. Oxidative stress, assessed via serum TBARS, was also elevated in both groups with UA and AMI compared with the control group (9.87±3.95 versus 8.29±2.67 μmol/mL, P &lt;0.05 and 12.43±5.18 versus 8.29±2.67 μmol/mL, P &lt;0.01 for AMI versus control). MOTS‐c Levels Are Significantly Associated With a Higher Risk of ACS As shown in Table 4 , MOTS‐c levels were significantly associated with an increased risk of UA (OR, 1.003 [95% CI, 1.001–1.006]; P =0.011). Male sex was also identified as a risk factor for UA (OR, 12.552 [95% CI, 3.108–50.698], P &lt;0.001). Notably, circulating MOTS‐c levels demonstrated the highest area under the curve (AUC) of 0.715 (95% CI, 0.613–0.818; P &lt;0.001) for predicting UA in the general population, with a cutoff value of 327.15 ng/mL, sensitivity of 0.899, and specificity of 0.514 (Table 5A ; Figure 2A ). As presented in Table 4 , elevated MOTS‐c levels were significantly associated with a higher risk of AMI (OR, 1.005 versus controls [95% CI, 1.002–1.007]; P &lt;0.001). Male sex (OR, 14.807 [95% CI, 3.233–67.820]; P =0.001) and higher FBS (OR, 1.643 [95% CI, 1.033–2.613]; P =0.036) were also identified as independent risk factors for AMI. DISCUSSION The data presented here strongly support the hypothesis that MOTS‐c plays a critical role in cardiovascular diseases. Specifically, our findings demonstrate that both the groups with UA and AMI exhibit elevated levels of MOTS‐c and oxidative stress in the serum compared with the control group. MOTS‐c levels are positively correlated with various clinical factors, including total cholesterol, FBS, glutamic pyruvic transaminase, glutamic pyruvic aminotransferase, history of diabetes, history of hyperlipidemia and TBARS. In addition, our data suggest that MOTS‐c is significantly associated with an increased risk of ACS, and the MOTS‐c/TBARS ratio may serve as a prognostic indicator for patients with AMI. The in vitro data demonstrate that increased levels of MOTS‐c could be an autonomous response to elevated oxidative stress; and the increased MOTS‐c levels mitigate oxidative stress through the activation of antioxidants. These findings suggest that this feedback loop between oxidative stress and MOTS‐c may determine the oxidative stress status, which may potentially affect the prognosis of ACS. In consistent with previous studies, we found that the onset of ACS is associated with male sex, elevated uric acid levels, high cholesterol, and increased FBS; and ACS is also associated with increased serum levels of TBARS, a reliable marker of oxidative stress. 6 , 14 , 15 , 16 Patients with AMI showed significantly elevated liver enzyme levels and WBC counts, along with reduced EF, indicating a heightened inflammatory state and impaired cardiac function in the group with AMI, likely due to myocardial cell necrosis. 17 , 18 UA and AMI are both conditions related to reduced blood flow to the heart but differ in severity and the extent of damage in the heart. Our data suggest that with the progression of ACS severity, there is a significant increase in the levels of both oxidative stress and MOTS‐c. Although oxidative stress is a well‐established contributor to ACS, its specific relationship to disease severity is still under investigation. Consistent with our previous findings, 9 we found the levels of oxidative stress in the serum are positively correlated with the progression of ACS. The protective role of MOTS‐c in cardiovascular disease through its antioxidative, anti‐inflammatory and antiapoptotic properties has been found in recent years. 19 Here, we showed not only an increase in circulating MOTS‐c levels in ACS but also a correlation between MOTS‐c levels and the severity of cardiac conditions. Future studies should be focused on investigating the levels of MOTS‐c in other cardiovascular diseases and the therapeutic effects of MOTS‐c in ACS. The precise mechanisms driving the development of ACS are still not fully understood. A growing body of evidence supports the role of oxidative stress and inflammation in destabilizing atherosclerotic plaques, leading to ACS. 20 MOTS‐c, a recently identified peptide encoded within the mitochondrial 12S ribosomal RNA gene, can translocate to the nucleus during oxidative stress and directly regulate adaptive nuclear gene expression to promote cellular homeostasis. 7 , 12 Our study establishes a direct, linear correlation between MOTS‐c levels and oxidative stress. Both TBARS and MOTS‐c levels were significantly elevated in patients with ACS and diabetes, indicating that MOTS‐c may have an impact on gene regulation in response to glucose restriction, 12 possibly involving the regulation of oxidative stress. Recent research has indicated that MOTS‐c could protect against H 2 O 2 ‐induced inflammation and oxidative stress in H 9 C 2 cells by inhibiting NF‐κB (nuclear factor kappa B) and activating the Nrf2/ARE pathways. 21 It has also been shown that MOTS‐c prevents the development of heart failure under pressure overload conditions in mice, and MOTS‐c protects H 9 C 2 cells from hypoxia induced cell death in vitro in different cells. 22 These led us to hypothesize that the elevation of MOTS‐c could be a cell autonomous response from increased oxidative stress in patients with ACS, and the elevated MOTS‐c may help to lower oxidative stress. Indeed, we found CoCl 2 ‐induced oxidative stress leads to the increased production of MOTS‐c and exogenously added MOTS‐c reduces oxidative stress through the induction of antioxidants in H 9 C 2 cells. These indicate that this self‐regulatory circle between MOTS‐c and oxidative stress determines the oxidative stress status in patients with ACS. However, we found while reducing oxidative stress and activating antioxidants, MOTS‐c failed to protect CoCl 2 ‐induced cell death in H 9 C 2 cells. We think that this inconsistency in the cell viability is possibly due to the cytotoxicity induced by CoCl 2 is too strong in our study. The in vitro experiments used supraphysiologic concentrations of CoCl 2 to simulate hypoxic conditions, which may limit the biological relevance of the observed effects. Furthermore, although MOTS‐c treatment showed some beneficial effects, a definitive rescue effect was not demonstrated. These findings should be interpreted as exploratory and supportive of clinical data, rather than as mechanistic proof. Future studies using physiologically relevant models and dosing are needed to clarify the underlying mechanisms. Of note, our study showed that after adjusting for age, sex, and history of diabetes, a decreased MOTS‐c/TBARS ratio serves as a significant risk factor for MACE in patients treated with percutaneous coronary intervention for AMI during an 18‐month follow‐up. By using receiver operating characteristic curve analysis, we demonstrate that a cutoff value of 48.26 ng/μmol of MOTS‐c/TBARS ratio can predict the incidence of MACE in the group with AMI with an 80% sensitivity and 66.7% specificity within the subsequent 18 months. This finding is clinically relevant, as oxidative stress contributes to adverse cardiac events following reperfusion therapy in ACS. Previous studies have shown that elevated levels of oxidative stress markers—such as plasma advanced oxidation protein products and oxidized low‐density lipoprotein—are positively correlated with increased MACE incidence in patients with AMI who underwent primary coronary stenting after a 6‐month follow‐up. 23 , 24 The dynamic interplay between oxidative stress and MOTS‐c levels in patients with ACS appears to determine the overall oxidative stress status. Patients with higher MOTS‐c production may experience lower oxidative stress levels and, consequently, have a better prognosis compared with those with lower MOTS‐c levels. As expected, our study revealed a significant positive correlation between MOTS‐c levels and total cholesterol. Notably, patients with AMI with hyperlipidemia exhibited higher circulating MOTS‐c levels, which may be attributed to elevated oxidative stress in this subgroup. We hypothesize that the increase in MOTS‐c may represent a positive feedback mechanism in response to dyslipidemia. This is supported by previous findings indicating that MOTS‐c influences fatty acid metabolism through activation of AMPK (AMP‐activated protein kinase), a key regulator of cellular energy homeostasis. 25 Acting as an adaptive transcriptional cofactor during metabolic stress, MOTS‐c may play a role in various cellular contexts characterized by disrupted energy balance, including oxidative stress, inflammation, and immune responses. 7 , 25 , 26 , 27 Our study also identified a significant association between circulating MOTS‐c levels and the risk of ACS. Specifically, we determined that a MOTS‐c cutoff value of 326.65 ng/mL predicted ACS occurrence with a high sensitivity of 89.0%. Furthermore, a higher cutoff value of 750.63 ng/mL effectively distinguished AMI from UA, yielding a specificity of 79.8%. These findings highlight the potential utility of circulating MOTS‐c as a biomarker for early ACS detection and for differentiating between ACS subtypes. Interestingly, a recent study reported lower MOTS‐c levels in patients with stable CAD. 28 This discrepancy may reflect differences in patient populations and oxidative stress profiles between ACS and stable CAD. Previous research has shown that, even in the absence of significant differences in angiographic stenosis, patients with unstable angina exhibit markedly higher levels of reactive oxygen species and plasma thioredoxin compared with those with stable angina. 29 These findings suggest that the clinical manifestations of coronary artery disease may be more closely linked to oxidative stress activity than to the degree of coronary stenosis. A notable distinction was observed in the prevalence of diabetes between individuals with non‐MACE and MACE in both the group with UA and the group with AMI. Patients who experienced a cardiovascular event displayed relatively elevated FBS levels, although no statistical difference was observed. Previous studies have consistently demonstrated that in the context of ACS, not only diabetes but also other hyperglycemic states contribute to worse prognoses, both in the short‐term and long‐term outcomes. 16 Given that patients with diabetes face a higher risk of mortality compared with patients without diabetes, recent trials investigating the efficacy of SGLT2 (sodium‐glucose cotransporter‐2) inhibitors and GLP‐1 (glucagon‐like peptide 1) analogues have demonstrated improved cardiovascular outcomes in patients with type 2 diabetes who are at a high risk for cardiovascular events. 30 , 31 , 32 These findings highlight the potential of these medications in reducing cardiovascular risks in individuals with type 2 diabetes, thereby showing that therapeutic interventions have a greater impact in terms of benefits for these patients as well. Our previous study demonstrated that reduced circulating levels of humanin, another mitochondria‐associated peptide, can serve as a prognostic biomarker capable of distinguishing patients with UA who later develop MACE from those who do not, even after adjusting for age and other confounding factors. 9 Circulating humanin has been proposed as a marker of mitochondrial health and function. 33 Notably, Daniela et al. reported that lower serum humanin levels were associated with reduced left ventricular EF and impaired peak global longitudinal strain, suggesting a potential link between humanin and cardiac function. 34 Collectively, these findings suggest that following the onset of ACS, circulating humanin levels decline in parallel with increasing myocardial injury and oxidative stress. 35 , 36 , 37 Consequently, the presence or absence of sufficient circulating MOTS‐c may reflect an individual’s capacity to regulate internal physiological imbalances, 12 ultimately contributing to divergent clinical outcomes. 8 , 11 Our study has several limitations. First, circulating MOTS‐c levels were measured just once at the onset of ACS, and dynamic changes in MOTS‐c were not measured in the subsequent follow‐up. Therefore, we were not able to obtain the dynamic changes between MOTS‐c and oxidative stress over time, thus limiting the understanding of its potential role in ACS progression or prognosis. Second, we had a limited number of subjects in the group with AMI with no real healthy control subjects, which may cause inaccurate estimates of risk and the cut‐point of MOTS‐c/TBARS. This could have an impact on the generalizability of our findings, particularly in terms of the biomarker’s diagnostic performance. Thus, a larger cohort study with healthy controls should be performed in the future to validate the current findings. Third, no animal experiments were performed to confirm whether the increase in MOTS‐c after the onset of ACS was attributed to a decrease in humanin due to damaged myocardium. Finally, although in vitro CoCl 2 induced hypoxia model is valuable for understanding the basic mechanisms of hypoxia and testing potential treatments, we realize the limitations when translating findings to clinical ischemia. Clinical ischemia involves complex and dynamic changes in oxygen levels, and it occurs in a multifaceted environment where interactions between various cell types, extracellular matrix components, and systemic factors play a crucial role. Conclusions In conclusion, our study demonstrated that higher circulating levels of MOTS‐c in patients with ACS, including UA and AMI, and MOTS‐c is positively correlated with oxidative stress. Although MOTS‐c exhibited moderate discriminatory performance (AUC, 0.70–0.77), these values indicate limited diagnostic accuracy in a clinical context. As such, our findings should be viewed as preliminary and hypothesis generating rather than definitive evidence of clinical applicability. Further validation in larger, independent cohorts is necessary to determine the robustness and potential utility of MOTS‐c as a prognostic biomarker. In vitro data showed that the increased MOTS‐c levels could be a consequence of increased oxidative stress under hypoxic conditions, and increased MOTS‐c levels reduce oxidative stress through the activation of antioxidants. Our study identified MOTS‐c/TBARS as a potential prognostic marker for predicting the subsequent development of MACE in patients with AMI. Considering previous studies have shown a cardioprotective effect of MOTS‐c, we think that MOTS‐c may serve as a potential biomarker for ACS.
