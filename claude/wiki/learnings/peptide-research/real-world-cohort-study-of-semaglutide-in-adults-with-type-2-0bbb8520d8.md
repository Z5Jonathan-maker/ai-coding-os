Introduction The aim of this study was to evaluate changes in clinical parameters among adults with type 2 diabetes (T2D) after initiating subcutaneous semaglutide in Chinese clinical practice. Methods SCHOLAR was a retrospective cohort study using data from the Tianjin regional electronic health records database, including adults with T2D who initiated once-weekly semaglutide (full analysis set [FAS]). Endpoints included change in glycated hemoglobin (HbA 1c ) and proportion with HbA 1c &lt; 7.0% at month 6 (primary)/12 (secondary); lipids, medication classes, and safety (exploratory endpoints). Results Among 26,859 individuals (FAS), mean ± SD age was 57 ± 13 years, and duration of T2D was 4 ± 3 years. Individuals with HbA 1c measurements at baseline and month 6 ( n = 3055; baseline HbA 1c 7.65%) and 12 ( n = 2897; baseline HbA 1c 7.75%) were included in the HbA 1c analyses. Mean change (95% CI) in HbA 1c was − 0.65%-points (− 0.71, − 0.59) at month 6, and − 0.47%-points (− 0.53, − 0.41) at month 12 with semaglutide (both p &lt; 0.0001). The proportion of individuals with HbA 1c &lt; 7.0% was 40.4%, 57.9%, and 49.8% at baseline, month 6, and month 12, respectively. Additionally, for individuals with HbA 1c ≥ 7.0% at baseline (mean HbA 1c 8.59%), mean HbA 1c change (95% CI) at month 6 was − 1.17%-points (− 1.25, − 1.08) ( p &lt; 0.0001). Total cholesterol, low-density lipoprotein cholesterol, and triglycerides significantly decreased after 12 months with semaglutide ( p &lt; 0.05). Among individuals with continued semaglutide use, mean daily insulin dose, and number of combined oral antidiabetic, antihypertensive, and lipid-lowering medication classes reduced after 6/12 months (all p &lt; 0.05). In the FAS, constipation, diarrhea, and abdominal discomfort were the most frequently reported adverse events (1.73%, 0.91%, and 0.89%, respectively). Conclusions Initiating once-weekly semaglutide was associated with significant improvements versus baseline in glycemic control and lipids in people with T2D from Tianjin, China. Safety of semaglutide was similar to the established safety profile per adverse events assessment. Clinical Trials Registration NCT06351748 . Introduction Globally, China has the largest number of adults (aged 20–79 years) living with diabetes [ 1 ]. Chinese guidelines for the treatment of type 2 diabetes (T2D) define a comprehensive treatment strategy, including glycemic control, management of blood pressure, lipid levels, body weight, and lifestyle modifications [ 2 , 3 ]. Achieving glycated hemoglobin (HbA 1c ) &lt; 7.0% has been associated with a significant reduction in the risk of diabetes-related complications [ 4 , 5 ]. Guidelines by the Chinese Society of Diabetes support the use of glucagon-like peptide 1 receptor agonists (GLP-1RAs) as a second-line treatment option after metformin and as a first-line treatment option for people with T2D and atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) or at high risk of ASCVD or with chronic kidney disease (CKD) [ 3 ]. Semaglutide is a GLP-1 analog, developed for treatment of T2D as a once-weekly subcutaneous formulation [ 6 ]. Semaglutide 0.5 mg and 1.0 mg were first approved by the US Food and Drug Administration in December 2017 [ 6 ], and approved in China by the National Medical Products Administration in April 2021 for the treatment of adults with T2D and to reduce the risk of major adverse cardiovascular events in people with T2D and cardiovascular disease (CVD) [ 7 ]. Semaglutide 2.0 mg has not been approved in China. The efficacy and safety of once-weekly subcutaneous semaglutide 0.5 and 1.0 mg versus placebo or active comparators have been documented in the global SUSTAIN phase 3 clinical trial program in people with T2D [ 8 – 19 ]. In SUSTAIN China, once-weekly semaglutide was superior to once-daily sitagliptin 100 mg in reducing mean HbA 1c (0.5 mg, − 1.4%-points; 1.0 mg, − 1.7%-points; sitagliptin, − 0.9%-points) and mean body weight (0.5 mg, − 2.9 kg; 1.0 mg, − 4.2 kg; sitagliptin, − 0.4 kg) after 30 weeks (all p &lt; 0.0001) [ 20 ]. The real-world effectiveness of once-weekly semaglutide in people with T2D has been investigated across 10 countries in Europe and Canada (SURE program), which consistently showed improvements in glycemic control with semaglutide [ 21 – 24 ]; however, clinical practice can vary significantly across countries as a result of differences in population demographics (including ethnicity), prescribing habits, healthcare infrastructure, economic conditions, insurance coverage, and national treatment guidelines or policies, and there is limited evidence on the real-world use of semaglutide in China. This retrospective cohort study investigated the real-world use and impact of once-weekly subcutaneous semaglutide in individuals with T2D in Tianjin, China, using data from the Tianjin regional electronic health records (rEHR) database. This study provides insight into the effect of semaglutide on HbA 1c over 6 and 12 months. Methods Study Design The real-world Study of CHina Ozempic cLinicAl pRactice in individuals with T2D (SCHOLAR) was a retrospective cohort study conducted in Tianjin, China (ClinicalTrials.gov; NCT06351748 ). The study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and the Guidelines for Good Pharmacoepidemiology Practices. Patient consent was not required as the analyses used retrospective de-identified data without access to personal identifying information. The study protocol and the application for a waiver to informed consent were approved by the independent ethics committee and institutional review board. We can confirm that we, the authors, have permission to use the Tianjin Healthcare and Medical Big Data Platform to access and use the data for this study. Data Sources De-identified medical information was extracted from the Tianjin Healthcare and Medical Big Data Platform, which is a city-level rEHR database with data updated every 3–6 months. Tianjin represents a large region in Northern China, with a population of approximately 13.6 million [ 25 ]. Tianjin joined the Cities Changing Diabetes program in 2014, joining more than 40 cities worldwide in a program to tackle T2D [ 25 , 26 ]. The platform includes electronic medical records from 82 hospitals (43 tertiary hospitals and 39 secondary hospitals) in Tianjin. Of note, specific semaglutide dose information could not be obtained from the rEHR database because of limitations in how dosing is recorded. Individuals Eligible individuals were ≥ 18 years at the index date (the date that each individual first initiated once-weekly semaglutide), diagnosed with T2D prior to or on the index date, and had initiated once-weekly subcutaneous semaglutide for the first time within the patient identification period (January 1, 2022 to February 28, 2023, according to the records within the database). T2D diagnoses were confirmed on the basis of records of inpatient diagnoses, prescriptions of oral antidiabetic medications, presence of diabetes-related complications, and levels of fasting plasma glucose and 2-h postprandial glucose. Individuals were excluded if they were diagnosed with type 1 diabetes prior to or on the index date, or if they were pregnant or had participated in clinical trials within 36 months prior to or on the index date. Individuals were not excluded if they had been previously treated with GLP-1RAs other than semaglutide during the baseline period. Study Procedures The study period was January 1, 2019 to November 30, 2023 (Supplementary Fig. S1 ). Individuals were identified for inclusion if they were prescribed once-weekly subcutaneous semaglutide for the first time during the patient identification period (January 1, 2022 to February 28, 2023, according to the records within the database). The index date was defined as the date that each individual was first prescribed once-weekly semaglutide. The study period included a baseline period, which was 3, 6, 12, or 36 months before the index date, depending on the specified analysis, the patient identification period, and an observation period, defined as the period from the index date to the earliest occurrence of the last available database record, death, type 1 diabetes diagnosis, pregnancy, initiation in another clinical trial, or the end of study. Baseline characteristics were obtained at the following time intervals: HbA 1c , body weight, body mass index (BMI), vital signs, laboratory test results, and antidiabetic (except for GLP-1RAs) and non-antidiabetic medication use within 3 months prior to or on the index date; GLP-1RA use within 6 months prior to the index date; and comorbidities within 36 months prior to or on the index date. Data management and quality control procedures are described in the Supplementary Methods. Endpoints The primary endpoints were change in HbA 1c from baseline to month 6 and the proportion of individuals with HbA 1c &lt; 7.0% at month 6. Secondary endpoints were change in HbA 1c from baseline to month 12, the proportion of individuals with HbA 1c &lt; 7.0% at month 12, and change in body weight from baseline to month 6 and 12. A ± 3-month assessment window was applicable to each time point, as assessments were not scheduled at exactly 6 and 12 months as a result of the real-world nature of the study. Individuals who had HbA 1c measurements at both baseline and month 6 or 12 were included in the primary or secondary analyses, respectively. A post hoc sensitivity analysis for change in HbA 1c from baseline to month 12 was conducted that extended the assessment window to ± 6 months and the baseline time window from 3 months prior to the index date 6 months. Exploratory endpoints included change in daily insulin doses from baseline to month 6 and 12; change in the number of oral antidiabetic medications, antihypertensive medications, and lipid-lowering agent classes from baseline to month 6 and 12; change in lipid levels from baseline to month 12; and the safety profile of once-weekly semaglutide based on pre-selected adverse events (AEs) during the observation period (Supplementary Methods). A post hoc exploratory analysis was also conducted to evaluate selected gastrointestinal AEs (constipation, diarrhea, and nausea) in individuals who were receiving α-glucosidase inhibitors at baseline. A subgroup analysis was conducted, which included change in HbA 1c from baseline to month 6 by different baseline characteristic subgroups (age, sex, duration of diabetes, HbA 1c , BMI, medications, estimated glomerular filtration rate, and comorbidities). The main analyses were conducted among individuals who initiated semaglutide regardless of whether and when treatment was discontinued. Additional analyses were conducted among individuals with continuous once-weekly semaglutide treatment at month 6 or 12. Continuous treatment was defined as a lack of discontinuation in once-weekly semaglutide for 6 or 12 months, respectively, during the observation period (post-index date). Discontinuation was defined as a failure to refill once-weekly semaglutide within the permissible gap period of 45 days after the depletion of the previous supply. The discontinuation date was defined as the end date of the last prescription. The assessment windows relative to the index date for endpoints are shown in Supplementary Fig. S1 . Statistical Analysis The study planned to include all eligible individuals who met the inclusion criteria from the Tianjin Healthcare and Medical Big Data Platform. Sample size calculations are described in the Supplementary Methods. Conversion of original data from the rEHRs to analyzable data, and all data analyses, were performed using SAS ® software v9.4 and R v4.3.3. The Observational Medical Outcomes Partnership Common Data Model methodology was utilized during the data conversion process to standardize the structure and content of observational data and enable efficient analyses. As the study is retrospective with no comparator group, all endpoints are exploratory. The full analysis set (FAS) included all individuals. For each endpoint, a subpopulation from the FAS was used, and included those with an available measurement of the endpoint at both baseline and the specified assessment window (i.e., month 6 or 12 ± 3 months). The primary analysis set included those with available HbA 1c measurements at baseline and month 6. Baseline continuous and categorical endpoints were summarized using descriptive statistics. A paired t test was applied to compare the baseline and post-index values for all continuous primary and secondary endpoints (including change in HbA 1c ), and the endpoints of change in daily insulin doses and lipid profiles. A Wilcoxon rank-sum test was applied to compare baseline and post-index values for the change in the number of oral antidiabetic medications, antihypertensive medications, and lipid-lowering agent classes. Analysis of variance was used to compare change in HbA 1c across different subgroups. Missing data were not imputed. Results Patients and Baseline Characteristics A total of 34,238 individuals first received once-weekly semaglutide during the patient identification period. Among these, 26,859 (78.4%) met the full inclusion criteria and were included in the FAS, and 3055 individuals had an available HbA 1c measurement at baseline and month 6, and so were included in the primary analysis set. In the FAS, 57.3% of individuals were male, and the mean ± standard deviation (SD) age was 57.4 ± 13.1 years, duration of T2D was 4.01 ± 3.3 years, mean HbA 1c was 7.96 ± 1.79%, and body weight was 72.8 ± 12.5 kg. Secondary Endpoints Among 2897 (10.8%) individuals with an available HbA 1c measurement at baseline and 12 months post-index, there was a significant reduction in HbA 1c after 12 months of semaglutide treatment versus baseline. The mean change from baseline (95% CI) was − 0.47%-points (− 0.53, − 0.41) ( p &lt; 0.0001), and 49.8% ( n = 1443) of individuals had an HbA 1c &lt; 7.0% at month 12. Among those with continuous semaglutide treatment at month 12 and an HbA 1c measurement at month 6 and 12, the mean change from baseline (95% CI) was − 0.65%-points (− 0.82, − 0.47) ( p &lt; 0.0001), and 58.9% ( n = 185 of 314) had an HbA 1c &lt; 7.0% at month 12. To assess the robustness of our findings, a sensitivity analysis on change in HbA 1c at 12 months was conducted using an expanded baseline window of 6 months, which increased the sample size from 2897 to 5055 individuals. Change in HbA 1c from baseline after 12 months of semaglutide treatment was − 0.43% (95% CI − 0.48, − 0.39). Only 396 individuals had an available body weight measurement at baseline and month 6, representing 1.5% of individuals in the FAS. As such, there were insufficient data points to conduct a meaningful analysis and these data have not been included. Exploratory Endpoints Among individuals with insulin use at baseline and who continued semaglutide treatment for 6 months ( n = 1686; mean [SD] baseline daily dose 50.49 units [28.05]) and 12 months ( n = 484; mean [SD] baseline daily dose 48.75 units [26.23]), the mean daily insulin dose was significantly reduced at month 6 and 12 versus baseline (Fig. 2 ). Discussion In this real-world study in adults with T2D in Tianjin, China, significant reductions in HbA 1c were observed after 6 and 12 months of initiating semaglutide, and a large proportion of individuals achieved treatment targets of &lt; 7.0%. We observed a reduction in the use of daily insulin and the number of combined medication classes, as well as significant improvements in lipids at 12 months. HbA 1c reductions after 6 months were generally consistent with other retrospective real-world studies of once-weekly semaglutide in non-Asian countries, such as the SURE program, as well as real-world studies that only included individuals with baseline HbA 1c ≥ 7.0% [ 21 – 24 , 27 ]. However, although the results were similar, there were notable differences between the results of a pooled analysis of four SURE studies and our current study in terms of overall efficacy, with HbA 1c reductions of − 1.1% versus − 0.65%, respectively [ 28 ]. This may reflect differences in the population characteristics at baseline, such as higher mean HbA 1c in the SURE analysis compared with this current analysis (8.1% vs. 7.7%, respectively) and a lower proportion of participants with HbA 1c &lt; 7.0% (19.1% vs. 40.4%, respectively). It was also not possible to confirm the dose of semaglutide that participants were taking, so it is possible that participants were taking a lower semaglutide dose in the current study versus the SURE analysis. Findings were broadly aligned with those of a smaller single-center study conducted in Shanghai, China [ 29 ]. As expected, greater HbA 1c reductions of 1.4%-points and 1.7%-points were observed in the SUSTAIN China trial of once-weekly semaglutide 0.5 mg and 1.0 mg, respectively, over 30 weeks [ 20 ]. This is consistent with the higher efficacy typically seen in randomized controlled trials due to stricter inclusion criteria, better adherence, and structured AE management. In SCHOLAR, baseline HbA 1c was lower and eligibility criteria broader than SUSTAIN China, with no specific requirement for baseline HbA 1c and prior medication use [ 20 ]. SUSTAIN China required HbA 1c ≥ 7.0% to 10.5% and metformin monotherapy [ 20 ]. Notably, in our study, the magnitude of HbA 1c reductions was higher in individuals with baseline HbA 1c ≥ 7.0% (− 1.17%-points), suggesting superior glycemic control in individuals with uncontrolled baseline HbA 1c . Glycemic outcomes were consistent across baseline BMI and sex subgroups. Similarly, other post hoc analyses of once-weekly semaglutide in different populations did not report sex-related differences in HbA 1c reductions [ 30 , 31 ]. The extent of HbA 1c reductions differed by baseline medications, with the highest efficacy seen in those receiving oral antidiabetic medication only, followed by those receiving insulin (monotherapy or combination regimens). The smallest reduction was in individuals receiving oral antidiabetic medication plus a GLP-1RA at baseline. This was expected, as participants switching from another GLP-1RA therapy may already have derived some of the benefits of this drug class. HbA 1c was still reduced in individuals switching from another GLP-1RA to semaglutide, albeit to a lesser extent than those who were GLP-1RA-naïve at baseline, suggesting that the previous GLP-1RA was suboptimal in duration, dose, or adherence, or that semaglutide provided additional benefit due to its greater efficacy relative to other GLP-1RAs. This aligns with a real-world effectiveness analysis, REALISE-DM, which reported further reductions in HbA 1c after switching from liraglutide or dulaglutide to semaglutide [ 32 ]. Interestingly, at baseline, there was a subgroup of individuals (33.5%) with baseline HbA 1c &lt; 7.0%, which raises the question of why they were receiving semaglutide. Additional analyses verified T2D diagnoses in 99% of the FAS and confirmed that most individuals in the subgroup had GLP-1RA or insulin use within 6 months prior to baseline, and a comorbidity of overweight/obesity, CVD, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, or CKD, suggesting treatment may have been related to these conditions. In the current study, significant reductions in lipids, including total cholesterol, LDL-cholesterol, and triglycerides, were seen after 6 and 12 months. HDL-cholesterol significantly increased by month 6. These findings are consistent with the cardiometabolic benefits of semaglutide in people with T2D at high cardiovascular risk reported in SUSTAIN 6 [ 13 ]. Over 50% of individuals in our study were receiving α-glucosidase inhibitors at baseline for management of postprandial blood glucose. This aligns with typical usage in China, where carbohydrate intake is typically higher than Western diets [ 33 ]. Although semaglutide has not been evaluated in combination with α-glucosidase inhibitors in clinical trials, treatment with GLP-1RAs has been associated with improved postprandial glucose dysmetabolism, and therefore coadministration could lead to additive effects [ 34 ]. Evaluation of these potential benefits was beyond the scope of our analyses and warrants future investigation. The safety profile of semaglutide was as expected for the GLP-1RA class. Gastrointestinal disorders were most frequent, primarily constipation, diarrhea, and abdominal discomfort. Incidence of gastrointestinal AEs was not substantially increased in individuals who were receiving α-glucosidase inhibitors at baseline. Overall, safety findings were similar to the established safety profile of semaglutide and consistent with real-world data from the SURE program [ 35 ]. SCHOLAR has strengths and limitations. Data were extracted from a large city-level database in Tianjin, which has a population of approximately 13.6 million, and included rEHRs from 82 hospitals, providing a large sample size and variation. Individual patient records could be linked across multiple hospitals in the city via a generated study ID. There was a large difference in the number of individuals included in the FAS and individual analysis sets, mostly due to missing data, as ≥ 2 measurements (one at baseline and one at 6 or 12 months) were required for each endpoint. However, baseline characteristics were generally similar between the FAS and the primary analysis set, and a sensitivity analysis for change in HbA 1c at month 12 with an expanded sample size was consistent with the main analysis, supporting the generalizability of the results to the general T2D population. As this was a descriptive and exploratory study rather than comparative study to assess changes in selected clinical parameters following initiation of semaglutide in routine clinical practice, information on comparator treatments was not collected, limiting our ability to draw causal inferences; additionally, while analysis of variance was used to compare different subgroups, no adjustments were made to the p values. As such, our findings should be interpreted with caution. Of consideration, only prescription records could be analyzed, so it was not possible to determine whether individuals filled the prescriptions or adhered to the prescribed treatment. Additionally, specific details for the semaglutide dose could not be obtained from the rEHRs because of limitations in how dosing information is recorded and variability in device use. Missing data or misclassification of study variables was possible, as data were from rEHRs collected for healthcare delivery rather than research. Body weight analysis was not feasible because of the limited number of individuals who had these data included in their rEHR, which may reflect that body weight is not consistently documented in diabetes departments in China. Non-clinical factors that may impact drug switching (e.g., insurance coverage) were not captured in this analysis; however, our study was initiated after addition of semaglutide to the National Reimbursement Drug List to minimize the impact of reimbursement-driven switching. The study findings should be generalized to the nationwide population with caution, considering the geographic representativeness. This is true for many studies, and the broad eligibility criteria ensured that a wide variation, reflective of the real-world practice in Tianjin, China, was covered. A wider challenge in real-world registry studies is that patients who undergo further clinical investigations, such as HbA 1c measurements, may have more severe disease, which may impact the overall response seen in this population. Conclusions In individuals who initiated semaglutide, clinically significant improvements in glycemic control were observed after 6 months. Additionally, improvements were observed in lipid levels and there was a reduction in mean daily insulin dose and the number of combined medications. SCHOLAR provides real-world insight into the use and impact of once-weekly semaglutide in adults with T2D in China.
