Purpose Thymosin beta-4 (TB4) is a small peptide upregulated in injured tissues, playing roles in cell migration, angiogenesis, inflammation, and oxidative stress. Studies show TB4 significantly promotes corneal wound healing after injury, leading to a clinical trial (RGN-259), with full US Food and Drug Administration approval still pending. Current limitations to TB4-based therapies are a short half-life and high peptide synthesis costs, limiting large-scale applications. Here, we engineered a tandem thymosin beta-4 (tTB4) peptide with improved therapeutic potential and scalability for corneal wound repair. Methods tTB4 was produced by fusing two TB4 monomers into a single polypeptide, creating dual G-actin binding domains. Binding activity was investigated using structural, in vitro, and in vivo studies. tTB4 and TB4 effects on cell viability and migration were assessed in human telomerase-immortalized corneal epithelial (hTCEpi) cells. Their efficacy in promoting corneal wound healing was tested in a murine alkali-induced corneal injury model. Results Structural modeling revealed tTB4 has a mechanistic advantage in enhancing actin polymerization over TB4, as it can simultaneously bind and sequester two G-actin molecules, facilitating filament formation. In vitro, tTB4 promoted increased hTCEpi viability and migration compared to TB4. In vivo, tTB4 promoted corneal wound healing and reduced scarring with greater efficacy than TB4. Conclusions The engineered tTB4 exhibited superior bioactivity and enhanced corneal wound-healing efficacy over TB4. Given that tTB4 can be produced by bacterial fermentation, synthesis is simplified and more cost-effective than TB4. The cornea is the most external part of the eye and, as such, is continuously exposed to external insults. The corneal epithelium is highly susceptible to injuries that breach the corneal epithelial barrier function, leaving the cornea and remaining ocular surface vulnerable to infection. 1 Efficient repair of corneal injuries is thus essential for restoring vision and preventing sight-threatening complications. 2 Following injury, corneal wound healing must occur in a highly efficient manner to rapidly resurface the corneal epithelium. For such, the corneal epithelial cells must proliferate, migrate, and differentiate to resurface the cornea. 3 In some cases, such as chemical or thermal injuries, persistent epithelial defects, corneal inflammation, and angiogenesis can occur, leading to corneal scarring and impaired vision. 4 Further, some ocular surface disorders, 5 , 6 such as diabetic keratitis, 7 can lead to impaired or delayed corneal wound healing, placing the cornea at increased risk of subsequent corneal pathology. Thus, treatments are needed to promote corneal wound healing and restore the integrity and function of the cornea following injury. 8 , 9 Previous studies have identified that the naturally occurring thymosin beta-4 (TB4) promotes cell functions that are critical for wound healing, including cell migration, inhibition of apoptosis, and attenuation of inflammation, and has emerged as a promising agent for use in the clinic to promote corneal repair. 10 , 11 TB4 is a 5-kDa peptide consisting of 43 amino acid residues that is naturally expressed by most cells in the human body. 12 TB4 has been shown to function by reversibly binding monomeric G-actin, thereby maintaining a readily accessible pool of unpolymerized actin for cytoskeletal remodeling. 12 , 13 When administered exogenously, TB4 promotes cell migration by increasing the availability of actin monomers, a limiting factor during cytoskeletal reorganization and cell movement. 14 Additionally, TB4 has been shown to promote degradation of the extracellular matrix via the PI3/AKT pathway, 14 inhibit caspase 9–induced apoptosis via internalization, 15 and diminish proinflammatory signaling by interfering with NF-κB activation. 16 TB4 is currently demonstrating significant therapeutic effectiveness in an ongoing phase 2 clinical trial. 17 However, TB4 requires industrial-scale peptide synthesis due to its short peptide sequence, making large-scale production both environmentally and financially costly. 18 To address this issue, a chimeric peptide consisting of two TB4 molecules linked C- to N-terminus (tTB4) has been developed using tandem protein engineering technology. By combining two TB4 sequences, tTB4 offers a larger peptide platform suitable for recombinant expression, which can reduce manufacturing costs and potentially improve in vivo stability. Another major limitation of TB4-based treatment is its short half-life. 19 , 20 In contrast, tTB4 is predicted to exhibit an extended half-life, likely due to its modified structural properties, such as increased stability against proteolytic degradation and enhanced resistance to clearance mechanisms. These improvements could result from alterations in its molecular conformation or interaction with stabilizing factors, thereby prolonging its therapeutic activity. 18 Further, by fusing two TB4 monomers, it is anticipated that tTB4 would present increased biological activity with the ability to sequester a larger pool of G-actin, thereby increasing cytoskeletal reorganization, when compared to TB4. tTB4 variants have been tested in cardiac tissue repair following myocardial infarction, where they demonstrated enhanced cell migration and wound healing efficacy when compared to TB4. 18 , 21 In this study, we developed tTB4 to confer enhanced biological activity, increase the half-life, and improve the manufacturing process for TB4. We then modeled the interaction between tandem thymosin and actin using AlphaFold, 22 revealing that tTB4 can effectively bind and sequester two G-actin molecules simultaneously, maintaining a larger reservoir of monomeric actin available for cytoskeletal remodeling when compared to TB4. Using a G-actin co-sedimentation (pulldown) assay, we confirmed that both TB4 and tTB4 sequester monomeric actin and inhibit filament formation; notably, tTB4 kept a larger fraction of actin in the supernatant than TB4, consistent with the structural model predicting simultaneous engagement of two G-actin monomers. Further, tTB4 promoted human telomerase-immortalized corneal epithelial (hTCEpi) cell viability and migration to a greater extent than TB4. tTB4 was also found to promote corneal wound healing in vivo following an alkali burn (AB) model to a higher extent when compared to TB4. Taken together, these findings highlight the potential clinical impact of tTB4 as a regenerative therapy that is not only more effective but also more scalable for wide application in corneal surface repair. Materials and Methods Structural Modeling of Thymosin–Actin Interactions Using AlphaFold Interactions of TB4 and tTB4 with actin were modeled using AlphaFold 2. 23 Protein sequences for TB4, tTB4, and G-actin were retrieved from the UniProt database and analyzed using AlphaFold's multimer mode to predict structural interactions. 24 The resulting models were visualized with PyMOL, 25 and binding interfaces were assessed for stability and compatibility. These computational predictions guided subsequent experimental validation. Expression and Purification of tTB4 A pET-28 plasmid composed of two thymosin molecules (UniProt: P62328 ) connected C- to N-terminus via a small sequence linker (GGTTCT) between them was constructed by Synbio Technologies (Monmouth Junction, NJ, USA ) , as in Hao et al. 21 Construct was transformed into BL21(DE3)pLysS (69451; Millipore Sigma, St. Louis, MO, USA) and inoculated into 500 mL Luria–Bertani media. Culture was grown at 37°C until reaching an optical density of 0.7 at 600 nm. Expression of tTB4 was then induced by adding 0.7 mM isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside. Cells were lysed via sonication; the cell lysate was centrifuged at 20,000× g for 30 minutes at 4°C, and the supernatant was collected to perform ammonium sulfate precipitation at 50% saturation at 4°C. The supernatant was collected, and buffer was exchanged to PBS using a Sephadex G-25 PD-10 desalting column (17085101; Cytiva, Marlborough, MA, USA). SDS-PAGE was performed at each step to verify the purity of tTB4. TB4 was purchased from LKT Labs (T3098; St. Paul, MN, USA). Actin Binding and Polymerization Actin binding and polymerization were assessed using the actin binding protein kit (BK013; Cytoskeleton, Denver, CO, USA). Nonmuscle G-actin was incubated in 5 mM Tris (pH 7.0), 0.2 mM ATP, and 0.5 mM dithiothreitol (DTT) for 1 hour on wet ice. Then, 21 µM G-actin was incubated with TB4 or tTB4 for 30 minutes at room temperature and centrifuged at 150,000× g for 1.5 hours. Pellet (P) and supernatant (S) fractions were separated by SDS-PAGE. Samples were denatured in Tricine SDS sample buffer (reducing, 50 mM DTT) at 70°C for 10 minutes and loaded onto Tricine gels (10%–20%) at a constant 125 V until the dye front reached the bottom (∼60–90 minutes). Gels were stained with Coomassie Brilliant Blue R-250 (0.1% R-250, 40% methanol, 10% acetic acid), destained in 40% methanol/10% acetic acid, and imaged on a gel doc system. Binding of TB4 and tTB4 to monomeric actin (G-actin) was quantified by microscale thermophoresis (MST) using a Monolith X instrument (NanoTemper Technologies, Munich, Germany). Actin from rabbit muscle (Sigma-Aldrich) was labeled with NT-647-NHS dye and titrated with serial dilutions of TB4 or tTB4 ranging from 10⁻⁹ to 10⁻³ M in 5 mM HEPES (pH 7.5), 0.2 mM ATP, 0.2 mM CaCl 2 , and 0.5 mM DTT, supplemented with 0.01% to 0.02% Tween-20. The thermophoretic response was monitored as the fluorescence intensity ratio at 670 nm/650 nm. Normalized fluorescence changes were plotted as a function of actin concentration, and binding parameters were extracted by nonlinear regression analysis in Prism 10 (GraphPad Software, La Jolla, CA, USA). Data were fitted to a one-site specific binding model, Y = B max L K d + L and, when indicated, to a two-site specific binding model, Y = B max 1 L K d 1 + L + B max 2 L K d 2 + L , where Y is the fluorescence ratio, [ L ] is the actin concentration, and Kd represents the equilibrium dissociation constant. Model selection was based on the extra sum-of-squares F test and the corrected Akaike information criterion (AICc). Maintenance of hTCEpi Cells hTCEpi cells were obtained and used as previously described. 26 , 27 In short, hTCEpi cells were maintained in serum-free keratinocyte culture medium (DermaLife Basal media kit; Lifeline Cell Technology, Oceanside, CA, USA), supplemented with the DermaLife Basal media kit (DermaLife Basal media kit; Lifeline Cell Technology) to produce media with final concentrations of 6 mM L-glutamine, 0.4% bovine pituitary extract, 1.0 µM epinephrine, 0.50 ng/mL recombinant human transferring growth factor α, 100 ng/mL hydrocortisone hemisuccinate (H2270; Sigma-Aldrich), and 5 µg/mL rh-insulin, along with 5 µg/mL apotransferrin, 30 mg/mL gentamicin, and 15 µg/mL amphotericin B in 100-mm cell culture dishes (172931; Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). Cells were maintained at 37°C and 5% CO 2 in a humidified environment and subcultured whenever reaching 70% to 80% confluency with 0.25% trypsin-EDTA (25200-056; Gibco, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). Cell Viability and Cytotoxicity Assay The cytotoxicity of TB4 and tTB4 was evaluated via the Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK-8, K1018; APExBIO Technology LLC, Houston, TX, USA) according to the manufacturer's instructions and as previously shown. 26 , 27 In short, 5 × 10 3 cells were seeded into a 96-well plate in 100 µL of media for 24 hours to allow adhesion. Thereafter, complete media were changed to basal media (without growth factors) to starve the cells overnight. TB4 or tTB4 was added to each well at 18.75, 37.5, 75, 150, or 300 µM, and PBS was used as the vehicle control. A pilot dose-response study was first carried out using thymosin to select the effective concentration range with significant response to compare the effect of TB4 and tTB4. Then, 10 µL CCK-8 was added to each well after 20 hours, and the plate was incubated for an additional 4 hours at 37°C. The formazan product was analyzed by directly reading absorbance at 450 nm using a spectrophotometer microplate reader (Cytation 5; Agilent, Santa Clara, CA, USA). Cells without the addition of CCK-8 (media control) and wells without the addition of cells but with the addition of CCK-8 (blank control) were used as blank controls. Each experiment was performed independently three times in quadruplicate. Percent viability of each treatment group was calculated by normalizing the absorbance of each treatment group to the media control after subtracting the blank control. Cells were allowed to attach for 30 minutes and thereafter incubated with 150 µM TB4 and tTB4, or without treatment, for 2 hours. For immunocytochemistry, each coverslip was blocked with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 0.01% saponin for 2 hours and subsequently incubated with a polyclonal rabbit anti-TB4 antibody (19850-1; Proteintech, Rosemont, IL, USA), followed by a donkey anti-rabbit secondary antibody conjugated with Alexa Fluor 488 (A21206; Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA). Cells were then incubated with phalloidin conjugated with Alexa Fluor 647 (A22287; Invitrogen) to label F-actin. Finally, the nuclei were stained with 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) (D9542; Sigma-Aldrich), mounted with Fluoromount-G slide mounting medium (00-4958-02; Thermo Fisher Scientific) and imaged under a confocal microscope (LSM 800; Carl Zeiss Microscopy LLC, Oberkochen, Germany). Transwell Migration Assay The effect of TB4 and tTB4 on the migration of corneal epithelial cells was evaluated using a transwell invasion assay, as previously shown. 28 For such, Millicell standing inserts with a polyethylene terephthalate membrane designed for 24 multiwell plates (PIHP01250; Millipore, Burlington, MA, USA) were placed inside each well of a 24-well plate with 600 µL complete media in the lower chamber. hTCEpi cells were seeded at a density of 1 × 10 5 cells into the insert in 400 µL complete media and incubated for 24 hours at 37°C and 5% CO 2 to allow attachment. Thereafter, media were removed from upper and lower chambers, and 600 µL complete media supplemented with 2% FBS was added to the lower chamber along with 37.5, 75, and 150 µM TB4 or tTB4 or PBS alone as a control, and the same concentrations of TB4 or tTB4 or PBS alone were added to their respective upper chambers in basic media. Then, 20 nM epidermal growth factor (E9644; Sigma-Aldrich) was used as a positive control. The plate was incubated at 37°C and 5% CO 2 for a further 24 hours to allow for the migration of cells into the bottom chamber following the FBS gradient. Thereafter, the cells were fixed in 2% paraformaldehyde for 30 minutes on wet ice and stained with 1% crystal violet (V5265; Sigma-Aldrich). Extra stain was washed away with PBS, and unmigrated cells were removed from the top chamber with a cotton swab. Each insert was then imaged using the Leica DMi8 microscope (Leica Microsystems, Wetzlar, Germany), and the number of migrated cells was counted via a colony counter. In Vivo AB Model C57BL/6J mice (strain 000664) at 7 weeks of age were obtained from The Jackson Laboratory (Bar Harbor, ME, USA) and allowed to acclimatize for 1 week upon arrival. For the AB procedure, mice were anesthetized via intraperitoneal injection of ketamine (80 mg/kg; Dechra Veterinary Products, Northwich, UK) and xylazine (10 mg/kg; Dechra Veterinary Products). The cornea was then topically anesthetized with a drop of proparacaine (AK2D15DS; Bausch &amp; Lomb, Laval, Canada). The AB was induced by applying a 1-mm diameter circular chromatography paper (3MM Whatman; Sigma-Aldrich) soaked in freshly prepared 0.1 M sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solution to the central cornea of the right eye for 1 minute and 15 seconds. Following the burn, the ocular surface was thoroughly rinsed with 5 mL sterile PBS applied dropwise. The injured eye (right eye) was treated with PBS (vehicle), 150 µM TB4, or 150 µM tTB4 immediately after the burn for 2 hours, followed by additional treatments twice daily for 30 minutes on days 1, 2, 3, and 7 post-AB. The contralateral eyes (left eye) served as untreated controls. Mice were euthanized on day 14 post-AB, and eyeballs were collected for further analysis. The ocular surface was imaged using white light, ring light, and a GFP filter following sodium fluorescein administration under a Zeiss stereomicroscope (Carl Zeiss Microscopy LLC) on days 0, 1, 2, 3, 7, and 14. In vivo confocal imaging of the cornea was performed using a Heidelberg Retinal Tomograph-HRTIII Rostock Cornea Module (HRT-III; Heidelberg Engineering, Heidelberg, Germany) on days 7 and 14, as previously described. 29 , 30 Corneal smoothness was assessed using a VisiLED Ring Light (Carl Zeiss Microscopy LLC) under a ZEISS SteREO Discovery.V12 Modular Stereo Microscope (Carl Zeiss Microscopy LLC), as in Lin et al. 30 The integrity of the corneal epithelium was evaluated by administering 2 µL sodium fluorescein (GloStrips, A01-33E; Amcon Labs, St. Louis, MO, USA), followed by a PBS wash and imaging under the ZEISS SteREO Discovery.V12 Modular Stereo Microscope with a GFP filter. Corneal opacity and sodium fluorescein staining were manually scored, and a corneal opacity score (COS) and corneal fluorescein staining score (CFSS) were assigned. For the COS, the grading system used was as follows: 0 indicates a clear cornea with no sign of opacity; 1 indicates slight opacity throughout the cornea, but details of the iris are still visible; 2 indicates a mild haze that slightly obscures iris details; 3 indicates moderate haze where the iris details are barely visible; and 4 indicates severe haze where the cornea is completely opaque and no iris is visible. For the CFSS, the following grading system was used: 0, no staining; 1, minimal staining with only a few punctate spots; 2, mild staining with moderate punctate spots covering about 25% of the cornea; 3, moderate staining with moderate punctate spots covering about 50% of the cornea; 4, severe staining with moderate punctate spots covering about 75% of the cornea; and 5, very severe, dense staining covering the whole cornea. Eyeballs were collected on day 14 were processed for histologic evaluation. Calculation of Ring Light Circularity Index To calculate the circularity of the ring light reflection from the ocular surface, an in-house Python script was developed and used. Briefly, the script read each image file, added a black border to avoid edge artifacts, and converted the image to grayscale. A Gaussian blur was then applied to reduce noise, followed by Otsu's threshold to binarize the image. Next, a morphologic closing operation was performed to fill small gaps and smooth the boundary of the reflection. From the resulting contours, the largest contour (based on area) was presumed to represent the ring light reflection. A convex hull was computed from this contour to eliminate any indentations, and the enclosed area (a) and perimeter (p) of that hull were used to calculate the circularity ( c ) via c = 4 π × α p 2 . ​The script also determined the minimum enclosing circle for visualization purposes and overlaid the convex hull and enclosing circle on a copy of the original image. Two outputs were generated for each processed file: (1) a PDF showing the thresholded image alongside the final annotated result ( Supplementary Information 1 ) and (2) a row in a CSV file logging the computed circularity value. Results Efficient Large-Scale Production and Purification of tTB4 tTB4 was expressed in Escherichia coli , achieving a high level of recombinant protein accumulation under standard induction conditions. SDS-PAGE analysis confirmed a purity level exceeding 90% ( Fig. 1 A), demonstrating that our expression system yields substantial amounts of pure protein. TB4 followed a single-site binding isotherm with an apparent dissociation constant ( K ᴅ ≈ 3 µM), consistent with a 1:1 actin complex. In contrast, tTB4 exhibited a biphasic binding profile that required a two-site model ( F -test P &lt; 0.01, ΔAICc &gt; 10), yielding K ᴅ₁ ≈ 0.9 µM and K ᴅ₂ ≈ 85 µM. Data are representative of n = 3 independent MST experiments; curves show the mean fit ± SEM. tTB4 Simultaneously Binds Two G-Actin Monomers With Higher Affinity Than TB4 In higher eukaryotes, TB4 and profilin are the primary sequestering proteins responsible for maintaining the pool of available monomeric actin G-actin. TB4 prevents G-actin from incorporating into filaments, while profilin/actin complexes exclusively support barbed-end elongation. Building on this understanding, we sought to determine whether tTB4 could simultaneously bind two G-actin molecules. To investigate this, we performed detailed structural modeling using AlphaFold. 22 Our analyses revealed that tTB4 can indeed engage two G-actin units simultaneously, with the two actin molecules arranged in an inverted orientation ( Fig. 1 B). Notably, the addition of the linker between the two TB4 units did not disrupt the binding topology, and tTB4 recapitulated the binding interfaces observed in the TB4–G-actin interaction. 32 This arrangement suggests that tTB4 retains the steric and allosteric properties of TB4 while gaining the capacity to bind two G-actin molecules, potentially enhancing its role in actin sequestration and regulation. To experimentally validate this model, we assessed whether TB4 and tTB4 could sequester G-actin and thereby inhibit filament formation. We used the Actin Polymerization Biochem Kit (BK013; Cytoskeleton), which provides conditions for actin polymerization and enables separation of polymerized (F-actin) and unpolymerized (G-actin) fractions by high-speed centrifugation. As expected, TB4 treatment led to a reduction in pelletable actin, indicating effective G-actin sequestration. Significantly, tTB4 caused an even more pronounced shift, with a larger fraction of actin remaining in the supernatant, consistent with enhanced sequestration. These results support our structural model and suggest that tTB4 inhibits actin polymerization more effectively than monomeric TB4 by binding two G-actin molecules simultaneously ( Fig. 1 C). In the absence of G-actin, bovine serum albumin (66 kDa), α-actinin (100 kDa), TB4, and tTB4 all failed to sediment in the pellet fraction (P) and were entirely within the supernatant (S) fraction ( Supplementary Fig. S1 ). To assess whether the tandem design confers enhanced or multivalent actin binding, MST assays were performed under G-actin–stabilizing conditions to prevent filament formation and ensure accurate equilibrium measurements. In monomer-favoring G-buffer (5 mM HEPES [pH 7.5], 0.2 mM CaCl 2 , 0.2 mM ATP, and 0.5 mM DTT), TB4 exhibited a typical monophasic thermophoretic response as actin concentration increased from nanomolar to millimolar levels ( Fig. 1 E). The resulting binding curve was well described by a one-site model ( R 2 = 0.98), yielding an apparent dissociation constant ( K ᴅ = 3.2 ± 0.6 µM), consistent with earlier biochemical and structural reports of the Tβ4–actin complex that showed a 1:1 stoichiometry and a noncooperative interaction. 33 , 34 By contrast, tTB4 produced a biphasic binding isotherm with two clearly separable transitions, indicative of distinct high- and low-affinity binding events. Nonlinear regression using a two-site specific-binding model significantly improved the fit compared to a single-site model (extra sum-of-squares F test, P &lt; 0.01; ΔAICc = 12.4). The high-affinity phase corresponded to a K ᴅ₁ of 0.9 ± 0.2 µM, roughly threefold tighter than monomeric TB4, while the second phase showed a weaker K ᴅ₂ of 85 ± 20 µM. The amplitude ratio between the two binding phases (Bmax₁:Bmax₂ ≈ 1.8:1) suggests that both actin-binding modules in tTB4 engage independently but contribute unequally to the overall thermophoretic signal. Control experiments confirmed that the biphasic behavior was not an artifact of actin polymerization or aggregation: the same profile was observed when actin was freshly clarified by ultracentrifugation, when the ionic strength was increased slightly (to 25 mM KCl) to mimic the physiological buffer. These results collectively demonstrate that the tandem construct behaves as a bivalent actin binder under equilibrium conditions that preserve soluble actin. The presence of a tighter initial binding event followed by a weaker secondary interaction implies a 2:1 actin/tTB4 stoichiometry. Effects of tTB4 and TB4 on Corneal Epithelial Cell Viability To evaluate the effects of tTB4 and TB4 on cell viability, the CCK-8 viability assay was used with tTB4 and TB4 at concentrations ranging from 18.75 to 300 µM (0.1–10 µg/mL). TB4 at a concentration of 18.75 µM promoted the viability of hTCEpi cells, but at concentrations of 37.5 µM and above, TB4 decreased cell viability, reaching a decrease of 50% of cell viability at concentrations as high as 150 µM. Discussion Corneal wound healing is a coordinated process involving cellular migration, proliferation, and dynamic extracellular matrix remodeling. TB4 has been extensively studied for its ability to promote epithelial repair, but its therapeutic application remains limited by its short half-life and high production costs. In this study, we engineered a tTB4 to enhance actin-binding capacity, improve biological stability, and provide a scalable therapeutic alternative. Our findings demonstrate that tTB4 exhibits superior bioactivity compared to TB4, promoting corneal epithelial cell viability and migration in vitro and accelerating corneal wound healing in vivo. Before initiating the AB model, we also performed topical safety tests in mice treated daily with tTB4 for 2 weeks and observed no signs of ocular or immune reactions, supporting its safety profile. Our structural and biochemical findings confirm that tTB4 can bind two G-actin molecules simultaneously, substantially increasing its ability to regulate the cytoskeleton compared with TB4 alone. This biphasic property aligns with earlier observations that thymosin dimers can augment tissue repair beyond the levels seen with monomeric TB4. 18 , 21 This biphasic binding behavior also supports a model in which the first actin molecule occupies a canonical G-actin–binding motif, inducing a conformational state that partially exposes the second domain. The second site, while lower in affinity, may still engage a neighboring actin monomer transiently or stabilize the complex through avidity effects. Mechanistically, this bivalency may have two nonexclusive consequences in cells. First, as it engages two actin monomers simultaneously, tTB4 could act as a transient actin dimer trap, slowing nucleation and filament elongation more effectively than monomeric TB4. Second, the presence of a weak secondary site may buffer free G-actin levels under fluctuating intracellular conditions, functioning as a tunable actin “reservoir.” The concept that actin-sequestering efficiency can be modulated through engineered multivalency also provides a framework for designing synthetic actin regulators with customizable affinities and kinetics—paralleling approaches used in engineered calmodulin or cofilin variants. Overall, the MST data demonstrate that tandem thymosin retains the essential actin-binding features of its monomeric counterpart while acquiring emergent bivalency that could reshape the dynamics of G-actin buffering and filament assembly in vivo. These findings are congruent with the broader role of actin-regulating proteins, such as twinfilin and profilin, which similarly enhance cell migration by manipulating G-actin availability. 13 , 32 , 40 , 41 Exogenous TB4 has been shown to enter cells and localize in the cytoplasm, preserving its functional capacity. 15 We also show that tTB4 is readily taken up by corneal epithelial cells, similar to TB4, but with a stronger intracellular presence. This cytoplasmic localization is critical for modulating actin dynamics and supporting cellular migration. 12 , 42 As a well-recognized actin-sequestering molecule that modulates the dynamics of cytoskeleton remodeling, TB4 thereby regulates various cellular processes that rely on actin remodeling, including cell migration. 32 Over the years, the role of TB4 in regulating cell migration has been extensively documented, with thymosin peptides having been demonstrated to have promigratory effects across multiple tissue models, including corneal epithelium, skin, and endothelium. 11 , 18 , 43 , 44 For instance, TB4 accelerates corneal epithelial closure and reduces inflammatory mediators in various wound models. 41 Thymosin peptides have also been shown to increase the viability of different cell types across multiple tissue models, including corneal epithelium, skin, and endothelium. 11 , 18 , 43 , 44 Herein, we also found that tTB4 and TB4 promoted corneal epithelial cell viability, with tTB4 having a more pronounced effect. We further examined the effects of tTB4 compared to TB4 on corneal wound healing in vivo using the murine AB model. Using this model, we found that both tTB4 and TB4 significantly promoted corneal wound healing, but the effects were more pronounced with tTB4. Specifically, tTB4 treatment significantly reduced corneal fluorescein staining and COS at 14 days after injury, compared to both TB4- and PBS-treated groups. tTB4 treatment also significantly improved the ocular surface smoothness, indicated by significantly increased circularity of the reflected ring light at 7 and 14 days after AB when compared to both TB4- and PBS-treated groups. Thus, our findings show that tTB4 promotes corneal wound healing. 43 – 47 An important step in corneal wound healing is the initial deposition of a provisional matrix that is necessary to support the wound-healing process and, thereafter, the remodeling of this matrix to restore the cornea. Excessive extracellular matrix deposition and failure in the remodeling process can lead to corneal scarring. To assess corneal scarring following AB, we evaluated corneal haze by in vivo confocal microscopy. tTB4-treated corneas presented significantly less corneal haze and fewer stromal irregularities compared to TB4- and PBS-treated groups. These data suggest that tTB4 reduces fibrosis and promotes a more regenerative healing response. Further, immunohistochemical staining revealed lower levels of α-SMA and hyaluronan in the tTB4-treated corneas, indicating reduced myofibroblast differentiation and extracellular matrix remodeling associated with fibrosis. Hyaluronan is a critical component of the provisional extracellular matrix, facilitating cell migration and proliferation during the initial phases of wound healing. However, excessive HA accumulation can lead to fibrosis, scar formation, and neovascularization. 30 , 36 , 39 , 48 , 49 TB4 treatment has previously been linked to reduced fibrosis, likely via the inhibition of TGF-β–mediated pathways. Further, TB4 has been shown to facilitate wound healing by increasing collagen deposition and tissue strength while promoting epithelial migration and cell survival in diabetic corneal injury. 50 In our study, we found that tTB4 treatment promoted the organized distribution of collagen I within the corneal stroma compared to the disorganized organization observed in the TB4- and PBS-treated corneas. Importantly, in our study, TB4 significantly promoted corneal wound healing and reduced scarring, as previously shown, but the effects were more pronounced in the tTB4-treated corneas. Although prior studies have demonstrated that TB4 has great translational potential in preclinical models by promoting corneal wound healing, decreasing scaring, promoting keratocyte and limbal stem cell function, and treating various pathologies in clinical studies, including dry eye syndrome, challenges such as complex synthesis, high purification costs, and difficulties in scaling up production of longer peptides remain significant barriers to its widespread application. 51 – 53 Manufacturing processes using recombinant systems have reduced production costs, lowered environmental costs, increased scalability, and produced a higher yield, native folding, and simplified purification compared to chemical peptide synthesis, but there is a minimal size limitation to make recombinant systems viable for polypeptide synthesis. The tandem arrangement of tTB4 makes it qualify for recombinant system manufacturing, meaning it has a significantly easier and cheaper manufacturing process than TB4. Thus, ease of production, coupled with its improved bioactivity, places tTB4 as a strong candidate for large-scale manufacturing and clinical use. Future research should examine whether tTB4 can be formulated for slow release (e.g., hydrogel carriers) or combined with established treatments such as bandage contact lenses and growth factors. Additionally, exploring potential synergy between tTB4 and small-molecule modulators of proteostasis could further optimize healing outcomes. In summary, thymosin's role in actin dynamics underlies its regenerative capabilities. The ability of tTB4 to recruit two G-actins, combined with its efficacy in corneal wound models, supports the concept that modulating actin monomer availability is a viable therapeutic strategy. Enhanced actin regulation leads to faster wound closure, stable epithelial architecture, and better tissue restoration. Our findings indicate that tTB4 accelerates corneal epithelial repair more effectively than monomeric TB4, likely through enhanced G-actin sequestration and cytoskeletal rearrangement. With reduced manufacturing complexity and superior wound-healing properties, tTB4 emerges as a promising candidate for clinical translation, potentially addressing persistent epithelial defects and broader ocular surface injuries.
