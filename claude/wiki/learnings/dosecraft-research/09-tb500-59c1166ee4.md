TB-500's mechanism centers on one protein: actin. Understanding how actin drives tissue repair explains why TB-500 is so effective across seemingly unrelated injuries — from torn muscles to cardiac fibrosis to chronic tendinopathy.

Actin is one of the most abundant proteins in eukaryotic cells. It forms the cytoskeleton — the internal scaffolding that gives cells their shape, enables movement, and provides the structural framework for tissue architecture. There are two forms:

Individual actin monomers in their soluble form. TB-500 binds G-actin with high affinity, sequestering it and controlling the rate at which it polymerizes into filaments. This regulation prevents chaotic, disorganized scar tissue and promotes ordered tissue architecture.

Polymerized actin filaments that form the cytoskeleton and contractile apparatus. TB-500 promotes controlled F-actin assembly, enabling cells to extend lamellipodia (cellular "feet") that allow migration toward damaged tissue. This is the fundamental mechanism of wound healing.

TB-500 upregulates actin polymerization at the leading edge of cells, enabling fibroblasts, endothelial cells, and keratinocytes to migrate toward the injury site. Without this migration signal, repair cells remain stationary.

Actin filaments serve as the template for extracellular matrix deposition. TB-500 ensures collagen fibers are laid down in organized, parallel arrays — functional tissue rather than disorganized scar.

Endothelial cells require actin-driven migration to form new blood vessels. TB-500 promotes sprouting angiogenesis — new capillary formation from existing vessels — ensuring repaired tissue has adequate blood supply.
