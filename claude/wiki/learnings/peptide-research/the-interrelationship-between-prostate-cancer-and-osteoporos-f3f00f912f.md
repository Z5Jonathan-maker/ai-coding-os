Abstract Osteoporosis is frequently regarded as a complication associated with androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) in patients with prostate cancer. Nevertheless, a notably higher incidence of osteoporosis has been observed in prostate cancer patients even in the absence of ADT, when compared to age-matched healthy individuals. This phenomenon is likely attributable to the synergistic effects of aging, chronic inflammation, and tumor-related factors, which collectively contribute to accelerated bone loss in this population. Accumulating evidence has demonstrated that signaling pathways such as RANKL/RANK/OPG, Wnt, CXCL12/CXCR4, TGF-β, and IGF-1, play critical roles in both prostate cancer and osteoporosis. These pathways may provide potential targets for the concurrent treatment of the two diseases. Despite this, existing reviews have provided only limited examination of the overlapping molecular mechanisms underpinning both diseases. This review aims to elucidate the interplay between osteoporosis and prostate cancer, offering a comprehensive analysis of the shared signaling pathways implicated in the progression of both conditions. In addition, prevailing treatment strategies for the prevention of osteoporosis in prostate cancer patients are evaluated, and the potential for the development of dual-action pharmacological agents targeting the RANKL/RANK/OPG, Wnt, and CXCL12/CXCR4 pathways is discussed. These emerging approaches may yield more effective therapeutic modalities for individuals with prostate cancer. Introduction Osteoporosis and its associated fractures have become a prominent global public health issue. The global prevalence of osteoporosis has been reported as 23.1% in women and 11.7% in men, with an upward trend increasingly evident among individuals diagnosed with cancer [ 1 , 2 ] . Prostate cancer constitutes the second most commonly diagnosed malignancy in men, with tumor progression largely reliant on androgen presence. Androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) is regarded as the primary treatment modality for advanced prostate cancer and is also employed as an adjuvant approach in cases of early high-risk prostate cancer. Evidence suggests that individuals undergoing ADT exhibit a 28% reduction in bone mineral density (BMD) compared to age-matched control groups over a 10-year period, with the incidence of osteoporosis reaching 81% [ 3 ] . Osteoporosis frequently contributes to skeletal-related events (SRE), such as pathological fractures, severe pain, and bone instability. Among prostate cancer patients, the overall fracture risk is elevated by 1.9-fold, accompanied by a 9% increase in absolute fracture risk [ 4 , 5 ] . Pathological fractures are linked to more than a 20% rise in mortality risk in this population [ 5 ] . As a result, osteoporosis has been extensively recognized as a significant complication in the treatment of prostate cancer. However, patients with prostate cancer exhibit increased susceptibility to osteoporosis even in the absence of ADT. A study conducted by Hussain et al [ 4 ] revealed that 42% of individuals with advanced prostate cancer presented with osteoporosis prior to the initiation of ADT, a prevalence significantly higher than that observed in age-matched control groups. Signaling pathways such as RANKL/RANK/OPG, Wnt, and CXCL12/CXCR4 are involved in both prostate cancer progression and bone metabolic disorders (e.g., osteoporosis). Factors such as TGF-β and IGF-1 are also important factors that cannot be ignored. This suggests that osteoporosis should not be regarded merely as a consequence of prostate cancer treatment. Rather, it may be a condition potentially linked to prostate cancer through overlapping molecular mechanisms, thereby offering possible therapeutic targets for concurrent management of both diseases. The RANKL, Wnt, CXCL12/CXCR4, TGF-β, and IGF-1 pathways are key pathways that have attracted considerable attention in various diseases, including prostate cancer and osteoporosis. ADT is indispensable in the treatment of prostate cancer. As a result, many reviews have been published on them. However, existing reviews typically focus only on prostate cancer or osteoporosis as individual diseases, or cover only partial pathways, failing to fully explore the crosstalk between these pathways in both prostate cancer and osteoporosis [ 3 , 6 , 7 ] . This review aims to fill this gap by providing a comprehensive analysis of the mechanistic connections between osteoporosis and prostate cancer, with an in-depth discussion of the common signaling pathways involved. We discuss the potential benefits, clinical limitations, safety issues, and clinical translation challenges of these targets from the perspective of both diseases, and evaluate the feasibility of dual-target therapies. Furthermore, we summarize key points from current clinical guidelines regarding skeletal health management for prostate cancer patients. Therefore, this review not only seeks to provide a comprehensive overview of the potential molecular connections between prostate cancer and osteoporosis but also summarizes existing therapeutic strategies, explores potential therapeutic targets, and provides valuable references and guidance for researchers in this field. Osteoporosis Osteoporosis can be categorized into two primary types according to etiology: primary osteoporosis and secondary osteoporosis [ 9 ] . Primary osteoporosis predominantly comprises postmenopausal osteoporosis, senile osteoporosis, and idiopathic osteoporosis. In contrast, secondary osteoporosis arises from a more intricate set of causes, including endocrine disorders (e.g., hyperthyroidism), connective tissue diseases (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis), and pharmacologically induced osteoporosis. Drug-induced osteoporosis is commonly attributed to prolonged administration of agents that disrupt calcium-phosphorus metabolism or impair bone remodeling. Frequently implicated medications include long-term glucocorticoid therapy and osteoporosis secondary to ADT in prostate cancer patients [ 10 ] . Under physiological conditions, bone metabolism is maintained as a dynamic equilibrium governed primarily by osteoclast-mediated bone resorption and osteoblast-mediated bone formation [ 11 ] . A disturbance of this homeostatic process may lead to the onset of osteoporosis. A common method for diagnosing osteoporosis is dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DXA) [ 12 ] . DXA scans can accurately measure BMD at sites such as the spine, hip, and forearm. Due to its simplicity, non-invasiveness, and ability to provide quantitative data, it is widely considered the gold standard for diagnosing osteoporosis [ 13 ] . In clinical research, the Fracture Risk Assessment Tool (FRAX) is also extensively used. The FRAX model combines a patient’s clinical characteristics (such as age, sex, weight, and previous fracture history) with bone mineral density data to assess the risk of fractures occurring over the next 10 years [ 14 ] . Furthermore, by measuring bone resorption and formation biomarkers, physicians and researchers can gain dynamic information about bone metabolism [ 15 , 16 ] . This helps to identify osteoporosis risk earlier, particularly in cases of mild bone loss that may not be detectable by DXA. HIGHLIGHTS Shared pathways : The RANKL, Wnt, CXCL12/CXCR4, TGF-β, and IGF-1 pathways are key in both prostate cancer and osteoporosis, making them potential therapeutic targets for managing both conditions. Literature gap addressed : Unlike previous reviews that focus solely on either prostate cancer or osteoporosis, this review uniquely provides an integrated perspective on the mechanistic overlap between the two diseases. Dual-disease analysis : This review explores both conditions simultaneously, emphasizing the role of shared molecular mechanisms in developing effective dual-purpose therapeutic strategies. Therapeutic potential and limitations : This review discusses both the therapeutic potential and safety challenges of targeting shared pathways, and assesses whether dual-target therapies are feasible. Future directions and research implications : This review serves as a valuable resource for guiding future research and improving patient management strategies in both diseases. Guideline summary : This review summarizes key recommendations from current clinical guidelines on skeletal health management in prostate cancer patients, providing practical value for clinicians. The dysregulation of the RANKL/RANK/OPG pathway is regarded as one of the central molecular mechanisms contributing to the development of osteoporosis. RANKL, secreted by osteoblasts, binds to RANK receptors on the surface of osteoclast precursor cells, thereby facilitating the differentiation, activation, and survival of osteoclasts and ultimately enhancing bone resorption [ 17 ] . OPG mitigates bone resorption by binding to RANKL and preventing its interaction with RANK receptors on osteoclast precursor cells, thereby suppressing osteoclast activity [ 18 ] (Fig. 1 ). Moreover, the Wnt signaling pathway and the CXCL12/CXCR4 axis have also been identified as significant regulators of bone metabolism. Activation of the Wnt/β-catenin pathway induces the differentiation of mesenchymal stem cells into osteoblasts by upregulating osteoblast-specific genes (e.g., Runx2 and Osterix) [ 19 ] . The CXCL12/CXCR4 pathway has been implicated in promoting the proliferation, migration, and osteogenic differentiation of bone marrow mesenchymal stem cells (BMSCs) [ 20 ] . TGF-β and IGF-1 also associated with osteogenesis. TGF-β promotes osteogenic differentiation by activating the Smad2/3 signaling pathway. Specifically, TGF-β promotes osteoblasts to release OPG and inhibit RANKL expression [ 21 ] . In addition, TGF-β promotes β-catenin stability, while β-catenin enhances Smad-mediated transcription, together driving osteoblast differentiation and bone matrix mineralization [ 22 , 23 ] . However, Liu et al [ 24 ] revealed that the synergistic interaction between TGF-β and Wnt signaling can also act on non-osteoblastic cells within bone tissue, leading to an upregulation of RANKL expression, which in turn facilitates the differentiation and activation of osteoclasts. IGF-1 promotes the differentiation of MSCs into osteoblasts by activating mTOR through the PI3K-Akt pathway [ 25 ] . Figure 1. The RANK/RANKL/OPG signaling axis in bone homeostasis. Osteoblasts release RANKL and OPG. RANKL binds to the RANK receptor on the surface of osteoclast precursor cells, thereby promoting osteoclastogenesis and activation. Osteoporosis and prostate cancer The influence of prostate cancer on bone metabolism is primarily reflected in alterations in hormonal levels and the occurrence of bone metastasis. First, both the treatment and progression of prostate cancer have been shown to alter systemic hormone levels, frequently increasing the risk of bone loss. Among therapeutic approaches, ADT exerts the most pronounced effect on bone metabolism. ADT substantially suppresses testosterone levels either surgically (e.g., bilateral orchiectomy) or pharmacologically (e.g., luteinizing hormone-releasing hormone analogues or antagonists), thereby inhibiting the androgen receptor (AR) signaling pathway and suppressing prostate cancer cell proliferation [ 26 ] . The consequent decline in testosterone leads to reduced estrogen levels generated via aromatization. Estrogen deficiency, in turn, results in downregulated OPG expression and elevated RANKL levels, thereby promoting osteoclast differentiation while impairing osteoblast activity [ 27 ] . Clinical studies have demonstrated that patients undergoing ADT experience an annual decrease in lumbar spine and hip BMD of approximately 2–10%. Following long-term therapy, BMD levels in these patients are reported to be 6.5–28% lower than those in age-matched, untreated individuals [ 3 , 28 ] . The rate of bone loss is most rapid during the initial phase of treatment. Although it subsequently decelerates, the loss remains progressive [ 29 ] . Approximately 50% of patients receiving ADT develop osteoporosis within 5 years, and this prevalence increases to 81% after 10 years [ 3 ] . Second, bone metastasis represents a frequent complication of prostate cancer, with over 80% of patients with advanced disease developing skeletal involvement [ 30 ] . Prostate cancer-associated bone metastases exhibit mixed lesion characteristics, wherein osteoblastic and osteolytic processes coexist within metastatic foci [ 31 ] . This leads to a state of elevated bone turnover. In metastatic sites, the interaction between prostate cancer cells and the bone matrix establishes a self-perpetuating “vicious cycle” of prostate cancer bone metastasis [ 32 ] (Fig. 2 ). Upon migration to bone tissue, prostate cancer cells secrete various factors (e.g., PTHrP, PGE2) that induce osteoblasts to express RANKL. In concrete terms, PTHrP activates RANKL expression through dual signaling pathways involving Gαs and Gαq, which respectively depend on the cAMP–PKA–CREB and PLC–PKC–AP-1 signaling cascades. RANKL functions as a “soil factor” in bone metastasis by recruiting RANK-expressing prostate cancer cells to colonize bone tissue [ 33 ] . RANKL further engages RANK receptors on osteoclast precursors, promoting osteoclastogenesis, intensifying bone resorption, and accelerating bone degradation. The resorption process subsequently releases growth factors embedded within the bone matrix, such as TGF-β1 and IGF-1 [ 34 ] . IGF-1 enhances prostate cancer cell proliferation, survival, and adaptability within the bone microenvironment by activating the MAPK and PI3K-AKT-mTOR signaling pathways, thereby contributing to the progression of bone metastasis [ 34 , 35 ] . Following receptor binding on prostate cancer cells, TGF-β1 initiates activation of the SMAD2/3 complex, which translocates into the nucleus to regulate downstream gene transcription, thereby promoting cancer cell proliferation, survival, and invasiveness [ 36 ] . Concurrently, RANKL has been shown to significantly upregulate MMP-1 expression in prostate cancer cells such as PC-3 [ 37 ] . MMP-1, a collagenase, degrades type I collagen – the primary structural component of the bone matrix – thereby compromising the physical barrier of the bone microenvironment and facilitating prostate cancer cell migration and invasion [ 38 , 39 ] . Collectively, these molecular events intensify prostate cancer bone metastasis, reinforcing a pathological cycle that fosters both tumor progression and bone destruction. Figure 2. However, even in the absence of bone metastasis and ADT, the incidence of osteoporosis among prostate cancer patients remains markedly elevated [ 3 ] . Studies have shown that 42% of men with prostate cancer exhibited osteoporosis prior to the initiation of treatment, in contrast to 27% in age-matched control groups [ 4 ] . Research conducted by Mistry et al [ 40 ] revealed that 45–61% of men presented with at least one grade 2 vertebral fracture before the administration of ADT. Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that prostate cancer cells are capable of secreting specific factors, including RANKL, IL-6, and PTHrP, which interfere with normal bone metabolism and consequently induce bone loss [ 41 , 42 ] . Therefore, the influence of prostate cancer on bone metabolism is multifactorial, and the relationship between prostate cancer and osteoporosis cannot be regarded merely as a treatment-induced causal association. Conversely, these conditions may be interconnected through overlapping molecular mechanisms. A comprehensive understanding of these shared mechanisms may facilitate the development of more effective therapeutic strategies for prostate cancer patients, thereby reducing the risk of osteoporosis and enhancing overall quality of life. Shared molecular pathways between prostate cancer and osteoporosis RANKL/RANK/OPG signaling pathway RANKL, a member of the tumor necrosis factor superfamily 11, exists in both membrane-bound and soluble forms, being expressed by T cells, osteoblasts, tumor cells, and other cell types [ 43 ] . RANK, classified under the tumor necrosis factor receptor superfamily member 11A, is predominantly expressed in osteoclast precursors, mature osteoclasts, dendritic cells, and certain tumor cells [ 44 ] . OPG serves as a decoy receptor that competitively inhibits the interaction between RANKL and RANK. A reduction in OPG levels or an elevated RANKL/RANK ratio facilitates increased osteoclast activity, thereby accelerating bone resorption and ultimately contributing to the progression of osteoporosis [ 45 ] . RANKL is markedly expressed in prostate cancer tissues [ 46 ] . Elevated RANKL expression has been correlated with prostate cancer progression, a heightened risk of bone metastasis, and reduced survival rates [ 47 ] . Upon binding of RANKL to its receptor RANK on the cellular membrane, downstream activation of IκB kinase α (IKKα) is initiated, involving its phosphorylation and subsequent translocation into the nucleus. Once translocated, the activated IKKα engages with the promoter region of the human Maspin gene, resulting in transcriptional repression [ 48 ] . Maspin, a serine protease inhibitor, suppresses prostate cancer cell migration and invasion; its loss compromises adhesion to the basement membrane and facilitates the induction of epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) [ 49 ] . Moreover, the RANKL-RANK signaling axis activates the NF-κB pathway via the IKK complex, which includes IKKα and associated subunits. This activated NF-κB modulates transcription factors such as Snail and Twist, suppresses epithelial markers like E-cadherin, and enhances mesenchymal markers including vimentin and N-cadherin, collectively promoting EMT [ 50 ] . EMT induces a reduction in intercellular adhesion among prostate cancer cells and augments their motility [ 51 ] . The concurrent influence of EMT and Maspin dysregulation accelerates the detachment of prostate cancer cells from the primary tumor, facilitates intravasation, and promotes colonization of distant organs, including the bone. Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway The Wnt signaling pathway preserves tissue homeostasis by orchestrating stem cell self-renewal and cellular differentiation processes [ 52 ] . The canonical pathway stabilizes β-catenin to activate transcription of growth-related genes, while the non-canonical pathway regulates cell polarity and calcium signaling independently of β-catenin [ 53 ] (Fig. 3 ). The Wnt signaling pathway is fundamentally involved in the pathogenesis and therapeutic modulation of osteoporosis. Activation of the Wnt/β-catenin signaling cascade facilitates the differentiation of BMSCs into osteoblasts while concurrently suppressing their adipogenic differentiation [ 54 ] . In addition, Wnt signaling enhances the maturation and survival of osteoblasts and indirectly attenuates osteoclast activity through the upregulation of OPG expression and suppression of RANKL production [ 55 ] . However, the study conducted by Maeda et al [ 56 ] indicated that specific non-canonical Wnt pathways, such as the Wnt5a/ROR2 axis, may stimulate RANK expression in osteoclast precursor cells via activation of the JNK signaling pathway, thereby contributing to bone resorption. Collectively, the Wnt signaling pathway predominantly acts to suppress osteoclastogenesis and facilitate osteogenesis. In prostate cancer, activation of the Wnt pathway has been associated with elevated Gleason scores, increased prostate-specific antigen (PSA) levels, earlier disease onset (diagnosed before age 50), and a heightened risk of recurrence following radical prostatectomy [ 57 , 58 ] . Canonical Wnt signaling supports the self-renewal capacity of prostate cancer stem cells by inducing the expression of downstream target genes such as LGR5, CD44, and NANOG [ 59 ] . Activation of the canonical pathway results in the nuclear accumulation of β-catenin, which forms complexes with the AR to promote the transcription of AR target genes (e.g., PSA), thereby enhancing prostate cancer cell resistance to ADT [ 60 ] . Non-canonical Wnt pathways, including Wnt5A, are activated through FZD2 and ROR2 receptors and trigger the Ca 2 ⁺/CaMKII signaling axis, leading to cytoskeletal reorganization and increased migratory capacity of prostate cancer cells [ 61 , 62 ] . Concurrently, Wnt5A may act synergistically with the TGF-β signaling cascade to upregulate EMT markers such as N-cadherin and Vimentin via the SMAD pathway, thereby facilitating EMT progression and promoting prostate cancer metastasis [ 34 , 63 ] . In addition, non-canonical Wnt signaling (e.g., Wnt11) can regulate prostate cancer cell growth independently of AR signaling, enhancing cell survival and resistance to therapeutic interventions following castration [ 59 ] . The study by Vela et al [ 62 ] demonstrated that expression of the non-canonical Wnt target gene PITX2 was 2252-fold higher in bone metastatic lesions than in normal prostate tissue, suggesting a potential role of non-canonical Wnt signaling in driving bone metastasis. The Wnt and RANKL/RANK/OPG pathways can form a positive feedback loop within bone metastatic lesions. Aberrant activation of the Wnt signaling pathway in prostate cancer cells can facilitate the osteogenic differentiation of BMSCs, which subsequently results in elevated secretion of RANKL [ 64 ] . This increase in RANKL further amplifies Wnt signaling in prostate cancer cells, thereby enhancing their invasiveness and metastatic potential, and accelerating the progression of bone metastasis [ 47 ] . In summary, although Wnt pathway activation contributes to osteogenesis in bone metabolism, it also facilitates the proliferation and metastatic dissemination of prostate cancer cells. CXCL12/CXCR4 Chemokines are signaling molecules responsible for regulating cellular migration and adhesion. CXCL12, also referred to as SDF-1, functions as a homeostatic CXC chemokine [ 65 ] . The signaling axis constituted by CXCL12 and its receptor CXCR4 has been implicated in a wide range of physiological and pathological processes, including bone metabolism, embryonic development, tissue repair, angiogenesis, tumor progression, and cellular proliferation [ 66 , 67 ] (Fig. 4 ). Figure 4. Schematic representation of the CXCR12/CXCR4 signaling pathway. Upon CXCL12 binding, CXCR4 activates G protein–dependent signaling pathways including PI3K/AKT, Ras/RAF/ERK, PLC/Ca 2+ /PKC, and Rho GTPases (Rac, Rho, Cdc42), regulating cell survival, migration, and chemotaxis. It also inhibits adenylate cyclase (AC) to reduce cAMP. CXCR4 can additionally signal via G protein–independent pathways such as JAK/STAT and GRK/β-arrestin. This figure was drawn by Figdraw. The CXCL12/CXCR4 axis exerts multifaceted regulatory effects on bone metabolism. This axis facilitates the proliferation, migration, and osteogenic differentiation of BMSCs by activating downstream signaling cascades such as PI3K/Akt and MAPK pathways [ 66 ] . With advancing age, expression levels of CXCL12 and its receptors, including CXCR4, in muscle and bone tissues are reduced, leading to impaired migratory capacity and diminished osteogenic differentiation potential of BMSCs, thereby compromising bone formation and repair processes [ 68 ] . Conversely, circulating CXCL12 levels have been shown to increase progressively with aging and are associated with the recruitment of osteoclasts [ 69 , 70 ] . Studies have shown that CXCL12 establishes a concentration gradient that directs the migration of CXCR4-expressing osteoclast precursors toward specific sites poised for bone resorption [ 71 ] . The CXCL12/CXCR4 signaling axis also augments RANK expression in osteoclast precursors, thereby increasing their responsiveness to RANKL stimulation [ 72 ] . Furthermore, CXCR4 signaling can activate critical molecules such as NF-κB and ERK, which intersect with the downstream pathways of RANKL. These signals promote the differentiation and activation of osteoclasts. A cohort study involving 1536 elderly individuals revealed a negative correlation between plasma CXCL12 levels and hip BMD [ 69 ] . Moreover, plasma CXCL12 levels were found to be positively correlated with TNF-α and CTX-1 expression [ 70 ] . TNF-α has been demonstrated to promote osteoclastogenesis under both in vitro and in vivo conditions [ 73 ] . CTX-1 serves as a sensitive biochemical marker of bone turnover, and elevated serum CTX-1 levels indicate enhanced bone resorption in individuals presenting with vertebral or hip fractures [ 74 ] . In conclusion, increased plasma CXCL12 concentrations are strongly associated with elevated osteoclast activity, although the precise mechanisms involved remain incompletely understood. In prostate cancer, elevated CXCR4 protein expression has been significantly associated with tumor metastasis, though no correlation has been observed with Gleason score or T staging [ 51 ] . The CXCL12/CXCR4 axis has been found to upregulate the expression of migration-associated proteins, such as MMP-9, by activating downstream signaling cascades, including PI3K/Akt and ERK pathways, thereby enhancing prostate cancer cell adhesion to vascular endothelium and promoting their invasive behavior [ 75 ] . The CXCL12/CXCR4 axis is also involved in the recruitment of immune cells. CXCL12 can attract monocytes, which subsequently differentiate into tumor-associated macrophages or myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), thereby promoting the formation of an immunosuppressive microenvironment [ 76 ] . This enables prostate cancer cells to evade immune surveillance, thereby facilitating their local growth and invasion. In the bone metastatic stage, the role of the CXCL12/CXCR4 axis becomes more complex. The axis not only serves as a signal guiding cancer cells to migrate to the bone marrow but also acts as an amplifier of immune suppression. Specifically, CXCL12, highly expressed by bone marrow stromal cells, attracts CXCR4-positive prostate cancer cells to the bone microenvironment in a directional manner [ 77 ] . After being recruited to the bone microenvironment, prostate cancer cells can induce the expression of integrin αvβ3, thereby augmenting their adhesive capacity [ 75 ] . Concurrently, CXCL12/CXCR4 signaling indirectly upregulates RANKL expression in prostate cancer cells, promoting osteoclast differentiation and consequently driving bone destruction and tumor progression [ 78 ] . The CXCL12/CXCR4 pathway also facilitates the activation of the β-catenin/TCF/LEF transcriptional complex, leading to the upregulation of Wnt target genes, including CXCL12 itself. This establishes a positive feedback loop that further amplifies the signaling [ 19 ] . Meanwhile, immune cells recruited by CXCL12 may interact with osteoclasts and cancer cells, contributing to the formation of a more complex immunosuppressive network [ 79 ] . Sun et al [ 80 , 81 ] shown that inhibition of CXCR4 – through the administration of neutralizing antibodies or the antagonist AMD3100 – leads to a marked reduction in both bone metastases and prostate cancer tumor burden in murine models. Similarly, Cordier et al [ 82 ] demonstrated that combined inhibition of CXCR4 and TRPV6, a calcium channel involved in prostate cancer progression, significantly can suppress bone metastasis. TGF-β and IGF-1 TGF-β and IGF-1 facilitate osteogenesis and also drive prostate cancer progression through separate molecular mechanisms. High circulating IGF-1levelshave have been associated with an increased risk of prostate cancer, particularly in cases of low-grade prostate cancer [ 83 ] . IGF-1 receptor is consistently overexpressed in metastatic prostate cancer and is associated with poor prognosis [ 22 ] . IGF-1 promotes prostate cancer progression by activating the IGF-1 receptor, which then activates multiple signaling pathways, including the PI3K and MAPK pathways [ 22 , 84 ] . TGF-β appeared to play a dual role in prostate cancer proliferation. In the early stages of prostate cancer, TGF-β acts as a tumor suppressor. It maintains tissue homeostasis by inhibiting cell proliferation and inducing cell-cycle arrest, such as G1 phase arrest, in normal prostate epithelial cells and early-stage prostate cancer cells [ 85 ] . As prostate cancer progresses, cancer cells develop resistance to the growth-inhibitory effects of TGF-β, enabling TGF-β to switch toward promoting disease progression. TGF-β enhances the invasive properties and migratory of prostate cancer cells by inducing EMT [ 86 ] . Meanwhile, it inhibits the functions of immune effector cells, including T cells and NK cells, thereby establishing an immunosuppressive microenvironment that facilitates the evasion of immune surveillance by prostate cancer cells [ 87 ] . In addition, TGF-β acts synergistically with multiple pathways, including the CXCL12/CXCR4, RANKL/RANK/OPG, and Wnt signaling pathways. Research indicates that TGF-β increases CXCR4 expression, boosting prostate cancer cells’ response to CXCL12 and aiding their migration and bone metastasis [ 88 ] . In the tumor-bone microenvironment, TGF-β indirectly prompts prostate cancer cells to express RANKL, fueling the vicious cycle of prostate cancer bone metastasis [ 78 ] . TGF-β is also capable of activating non-Smad signaling pathways, including the PI3K/AKT and Rho GTPases pathways, which interact synergistically with the Wnt signaling pathway to facilitate EMT [ 87 ] . A study has shown that targeting TGF-β signaling, such as through the use of TGF-β inhibitors, may slow the progression of prostate cancer, particularly in metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC) [ 89 ] . Current treatment strategies and dual-action drugs development Basic supportive care Bone health management remains a challenge in men with prostate cancer. The new guidelines from the American Urological Association (AUA/SUO) and the National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) recommend routine BMD assessment in patients, particularly those receiving long-term ADT [ 90 , 91 ] . Specific methods include baseline measurement performed using dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry and assessment of the 10-year fracture risk using the FRAX tool [ 92 ] . Lifestyle interventions have been established as an important strategy for managing osteoporosis in patients with prostate cancer. The NCCN guidelines (v2.2025) and European Association of Urology guidelines (EAU, 2025) strongly recommend that all prostate cancer patients are strongly advised to supplement with calcium and vitamin D (1000–1200 mg of calcium daily and maintain serum vitamin D levels at 30–50 ng/ml), while also implementing lifestyle interventions including weight-bearing exercise, smoking cessation, and limiting alcohol consumption [ 46 , 91 ] . Although current clinical evidence has not consistently demonstrated that exercise training significantly increases BMD in patients with prostate cancer, numerous studies have highlighted its important role in promoting bone-related health. Cormie et al [ 93 ] demonstrated that a 12-week resistance training program could be safely implemented even in prostate cancer patients with bone metastases. The intervention led to approximately an 11% increase in muscle strength, a 5% improvement in endurance, a 12% enhancement in walking ability, and a 24% increase in physical activity levels. Similarly, the study by Winters-Stone et al [ 94 ] showed that regular resistance and impact training significantly improved muscle strength, self-reported physical function, and reduced functional disability in prostate cancer survivors. In addition, differences in patient age and ethnicity should not be overlooked. Elderly patients experience age-related bone loss at an average rate of 1–2% per year, and this rate may double following ADT compared to younger individuals [ 46 ] . BMD tends to be higher and osteoporosis less prevalent in Blacks than in Whites [ 95 ] . Among patients with prostate cancer receiving ADT, Black individuals also show higher BMD and lower fracture risk compared to Whites [ 96 ] . For patients at high risk of bone-related events, a combination of drug therapy and lifestyle interventions is recommended. Bisphosphonates Bisphosphonates preserve bone structural integrity by suppressing osteoclast activity and decelerating bone resorption. Alendronate and risedronate have been clinically validated for maintaining BMD in patients undergoing ADT. In a randomized trial involving 104 non-metastatic prostate cancer patients, those treated with risedronate maintained lumbar spine BMD over a 24-month period, whereas the placebo group exhibited a substantial reduction (13.6% decrease at 24 months) [ 97 ] . Oral formulations, including risedronate (35 mg weekly) and alendronate, have been approved by the European Medicines Agency for the management of male osteoporosis [ 98 ] . Intravenous zoledronic acid is also frequently administered for the treatment of osteoporosis, typically at a dose of 5 mg annually via intravenous infusion. In contrast, for patients with bone metastases, a dosage of 4 mg every 3–4 weeks is recommended. Several studies have demonstrated that zoledronic acid can reduce the incidence of SREs by 30–50%. However, no significant improvement in overall survival has been observed among prostate cancer patients [ 99 , 100 ] . Denosumab In patients with prostate cancer, denosumab is employed for the management of osteoporosis and bone metastases to delay and reduce SREs, enhance quality of life, and extend survival. The mechanism of action of denosumab involves high-affinity binding to RANKL, thereby inhibiting the RANKL-RANK signaling pathway [ 101 ] . Significant increases in BMD and reductions in fracture risk have been demonstrated in pivotal clinical trials. In a randomized, double-blind study involving 734 prostate cancer patients receiving ADT, those treated with denosumab (60 mg subcutaneously every 6 months) exhibited lumbar spine BMD increases of 6.7% at 1 year and 8.9% at 2 years, significantly exceeding those observed in the placebo group [ 102 ] . In the context of prostate cancer bone metastases, denosumab not only suppresses osteoclast-mediated bone destruction but also indirectly diminishes the survival and invasiveness of prostate cancer cells within the bone microenvironment, thereby contributing to delayed onset of SREs, particularly pathological fractures [ 103 , 104 ] . In terms of SRE prevention, denosumab has demonstrated greater efficacy than zoledronic acid and does not require dose modification based on renal function [ 105 – 107 ] . Among patients with metastatic CRPC, administration of denosumab (120 mg every 4 weeks) significantly prolonged the time to first SRE compared to zoledronic acid (20.7 months vs 17.1 months) [ 106 ] . Additionally, denosumab has been shown to extend bone metastasis-free survival. A study by Smith et al [ 103 ] reported that denosumab delayed the time to first bone metastasis by 4.2 months in patients with CRPC. However, its use has been associated with relatively high incidences of osteonecrosis of the jaw (1.8–3.7%) and hypocalcemia (up to 13%), necessitating a careful risk-benefit evaluation. Currently, there is no supporting evidence that combination therapy with bisphosphonates enhances therapeutic efficacy. According to the 2024 APCCC, 92% of experts endorsed either discontinuation or reduced frequency of bone-targeted agents (e.g., denosumab) after 2 years of administration [ 29 ] . Nonetheless, rebound bone resorption following denosumab discontinuation may elevate the risk of vertebral fractures, thereby supporting a transition to bisphosphonates (e.g., alendronate or zoledronic acid) to stabilize bone metabolism. Radium-223 To date, radium-223 is the only bone-seeking radiopharmaceutical shown to prolong overall survival and prevent symptomatic skeletal events in men with metastatic CRPC and bone metastases [ 91 ] . It is a targeted alpha therapy that induces localized cytotoxicity in bone metastases [ 108 ] . In the ALSYMPCA trial, radium-223 significantly improved median overall survival (14.9 vs 11.3 months); and delayed the time to first symptomatic skeletal event (15.6 vs 9.8 months) compared with placebo [ 109 ] . To reduce the risk of fractures, bone protectors should be used in combination with radium-223, and combination with abiraterone is prohibited [ 46 ] . Guidelines consistently recommend radium-223 for patients with symptomatic bone metastases and no visceral disease [ 46 , 91 , 110 ] . Dickkopf-1: a shared target for prostate cancer and osteoporosis treatment The majority of patients with advanced prostate cancer eventually progress to CRPC following prolonged endocrine therapy [ 111 ] . For individuals with CRPC, few effective treatment options currently exist beyond chemotherapy, and CRPC has emerged as the predominant cause of mortality in this population. Genetic mutations have been identified in approximately 10–20% of patients with advanced prostate cancer, resulting in aberrant activation of the Wnt signaling pathway [ 112 , 113 ] . Consequently, the combination of Wnt pathway-targeted agents with endocrine therapy or other treatment strategies has become increasingly necessary and may enhance therapeutic efficacy across diverse prostate cancer subtypes. Within the Wnt signaling pathway, Dickkopf-1 (DKK1) has been identified as a highly promising target for the concurrent treatment of prostate cancer and osteoporosis. Among the DKK family members, DKK1 is the most extensively studied and has been recognized as one of the most potent extracellular inhibitors of Wnt signaling [ 114 ] . It functions by binding to the Wnt co-receptors LRP5/6, thereby preventing the formation of the FZD-LRP complex and subsequently inhibiting β-catenin accumulation. However, emerging evidence suggests that elevated DKK1 expression, particularly in serum, paradoxically facilitates prostate cancer progression and is associated with poor prognosis, bone metastases, and therapeutic resistance [ 53 , 115 ] . This paradoxical effect is thought to involve the activation of non-canonical Wnt pathways (e.g., Wnt/PCP or Wnt/JNK), which may be triggered by DKK1-mediated inhibition of the canonical Wnt pathway, ultimately contributing to bone metastasis [ 116 ] . Additionally, DKK1 may interact with the CKAP4 receptor, subsequently activating the PI3K/AKT signaling cascade. Its specific manifestations are a reduction in NK cells and CD8 + T cells, accompanied by an increase in M2 macrophages and resting NK cells, resulting in impaired immune surveillance that may indirectly promote androgen resistance [ 117 , 118 ] . DKK1 secreted by prostate cancer cells has also been shown to inhibit osteoblast differentiation and activity, thereby enhancing bone resorption. Simultaneously, it upregulates RANKL, a key osteoclast-activating factor, contributing to the formation of osteolytic lesions in bone metastases [ 119 ] . Current evidence suggests that inhibition of DKK1 effectively suppresses prostate cancer progression. In the study conducted by Hall et al [ 120 ] , the knockout of DKK1 in osteolytic prostate cancer cells was shown to delay the development of both soft tissue and bone tumors. In a study by Rinella et al [ 121 ] , silencing or knockout of DKK1 significantly inhibited the growth, migration, and expression of metastasis-related genes in metastatic CRPC cells. Subsequent in vivo experiments in mice further demonstrated that DKK1 deficiency slowed prostate cancer growth and inhibited lung metastasis. Research by Wise et al [ 122 ] demonstrated that monoclonal antibodies targeting DKK1 (e.g., DKN-01) reduced tumor burden in prostate cancer mouse models, with therapeutic effects dependent on NK cell activity. Subsequently, Wise et al [ 123 ] initiated a phase 1/2 clinical trial to evaluate the efficacy of DKN-01 as monotherapy or in combination with docetaxel in patients with metastatic CRPC. However, patient recruitment for this trial was slow, as it required the enrollment of individuals with high DKK1 expression or specific Wnt signaling pathway mutations. Despite the eventual inclusion of patients with treatment-emergent neuroendocrine prostate cancer and aggressive variant prostate cancer, enrollment remained limited, with only 18 participants – 10 assigned to monotherapy and 8 to combination therapy. The results showed that the combination of DKN-01 and docetaxel demonstrated a partial response rate of 71%. Only three patients experienced a durable response, while the others failed to maintain a significant therapeutic effect during follow-up. The median progression-free survival in the combination therapy group was 5.7 months, which is slightly higher than the expected outcomes of docetaxel monotherapy reported in the literature. This modest improvement still falls short of the durable efficacy required to meet the therapeutic needs of patients with metastatic CRPC. Ultimately, the trial was terminated early. The suboptimal outcomes of the trial may be partly attributed to the selection of patients with a predisposition toward Wnt signaling pathway mutations. This is because the antitumor effects of DKK1 inhibition may, in part, be mediated through suppression of the DKK1/CKAP4 axis. DKK1/CKAP4 axis markedly related to tumor-immune microenvironment and immune infiltration [ 117 , 124 , 125 ] . DKK1 may drive the accumulation of suppressive myeloid populations, ultimately reducing T cell and NK cell responses in prostate cancer [ 22 , 122 ] . Research indicates that prostate cancer exhibiting elevated DKK1 expression demonstrates diminished infiltration of CD8 + T cells and reduced levels of activated NK cells. Furthermore, inhibition of DKK1 restores the antitumor activity of NK cells in prostate cancer models [ 122 ] . Comparable mechanisms have undergone more extensive validation in the contexts of breast cancer, melanoma, and colorectal cancer [ 126 – 128 ] . DKK1 has been extensively investigated in the context of osteoporosis. Elevated serum DKK1 levels have been reported in patients experiencing long-term immobilization or disuse-induced bone loss [ 129 ] . In a study of Mediterranean anemia-related osteoporosis, serum DKK1 concentrations were found to be elevated prior to treatment and significantly reduced following zoledronic acid administration, accompanied by concurrent improvements in BMD [ 130 ] . In both ovariectomized mice and rhesus macaques, subcutaneous administration of the anti-DKK1 antibody RH2-18 resulted in a significant enhancement of bone mineral density, with particularly notable improvements in the trabecular compartment [ 131 ] . A preclinical investigation targeting multiple myeloma employed the anti-DKK1 antibody BHQ-880 in mouse models, with results indicating markedly increased osteoblast numbers, elevated serum human osteocalcin levels, and enhanced trabecular bone formation [ 132 ] . The proposed mechanism involves activation of the Wnt signaling pathway following DKK1 inhibition, thereby enhancing osteoblast function [ 55 ] . CXCR4 antagonist: dual targeting of osteoporosis and prostate cancer Multiple studies have confirmed that CXCR4 antagonists are capable of effectively inhibiting prostate cancer progression. For example, Sun et al [ 81 ] demonstrated that CXCR4 antagonists (e.g., anti-CXCR4 antibodies or the peptide antagonist TN14003) significantly suppressed prostate cancer bone tumor growth and reduced levels of the bone resorption marker pyridinoline. Benslimane-Ahmim et al [ 67 ] demonstrated that OPG activates the CXCL12/CXCR4 signaling axis in prostate cancer. In vitro, conditioned medium from OPG-pretreated endothelial colony-forming cells significantly enhanced the chemotactic response of prostate cancer cells, and this effect was partially blocked by the CXCR4 antagonist AMD3100. Additionally, Domanska et al [ 133 ] found that the CXCR4 antagonist AMD3100 enhanced the sensitivity of prostate cancer cells to docetaxel chemotherapy, an effect validated through both in vitro and in vivo (mouse model) experiments. The underlying mechanism is believed to involve AMD3100-mediated blockade of the CXCL12/CXCR4 axis. This disruption interferes with the interaction between prostate cancer cells and the bone marrow microenvironment, thereby reducing resistance to chemotherapeutic agents. In the context of bone metabolism, CXCR4 antagonists have been shown to suppress osteoclast formation via a distinct mechanism while preserving osteogenic function. For instance, AMD3100 directly inhibits CXCL12/CXCR4 interactions within the bone marrow, thereby facilitating the mobilization of hematopoietic stem cells into the circulation and indirectly reducing osteoclast numbers at bone surfaces [ 134 ] . In ovariectomized animal models simulating osteoporosis, blockade of the CXCR4 signaling pathway (e.g., through AMD3100 administration) has been associated with reductions in bone resorption markers and increases in BMD [ 135 ] . Although AMD3100 mobilizes hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells in healthy mice, it does not exert a significant effect on BMD, indicating that its action may depend on the pathological hyperactivity of osteoclasts observed in osteoporosis. In a separate study on diabetic osteoporosis, the CXCR4 antagonist AMD3465 was reported to reverse osteoblast apoptosis and downregulate matrix metalloproteinase expression, thereby attenuating the degradation of the bone matrix [ 136 ] . Overall, CXCR4 antagonists exhibit potential as dual-function agents for the treatment of both osteoporosis and prostate cancer. In patients with early-stage prostate cancer, these compounds may reduce the risk of bone metastasis and delay the decline in BMD. In advanced-stage prostate cancer patients receiving chemotherapy, CXCR4 antagonists may also enhance tumor responsiveness to docetaxel. Challenges in the clinical application of CXCR4 antagonists and anti-DKK1 antibodies Although CXCR4 antagonists and anti-DKK1 antibodies hold therapeutic potential, they still face significant translational and clinical challenges before being widely applied in the management of prostate cancer and osteoporosis. Anti-DKK1 therapy may paradoxically activate the canonical Wnt pathway, which is closely associated with the proliferation and metastasis of prostate cancer cells. Signaling redundancy among the Wnt, CXCR4, and TGF-β axes may enable cancer cells to bypass the blockade of a single pathway, thereby limiting therapeutic efficacy. Moreover, the role of DKK1 in immune regulation may be modulated by the functional status of the host immune system [ 137 ] . In hosts lacking functional NK cells, the therapeutic efficacy of anti-DKK1 strategies may be limited. Similarly, CXCR4 antagonists such as AMD3100 and AMD3465 may alter the migration of immune cells [ 138 ] . They may alter the infiltration pattern of immune cells within the prostate cancer microenvironment, thereby potentially affecting antitumor immune responses. More importantly, the CXCL12/CXCR4 pathway intersects with the PI3K-Akt and TGF-β pathways, both of which play critical roles in the immunosuppressive microenvironment of bone metastases. This suggests that inhibiting CXCR4 may trigger unpredictable immunological effects. Therapeutic efficacy may vary dramatically depending on different subtypes of prostate cancer. For instance, neuroendocrine CRPC is independent of androgen receptor signaling and driven by complex mutations. In this subtype, anti-DKK1 therapy may offer limited benefit or even pose a risk, since activation of the Wnt pathway could synergize with non–androgen receptor pathways to promote tumor progression. In contrast, patients with bone metastasis-dominant CRPC may benefit the most from anti-DKK1 treatment. By inhibiting DKK1, it may be possible to restore osteoblast function, reduce skeletal-related events, and potentially impair tumor–bone microenvironment interactions that support metastatic growth. Similarly, the effectiveness of CXCR4 antagonists may differ across subtypes due to variations in prostate cancer homing dependence and immune microenvironment composition. From a clinical perspective, the monoclonal antibody DKN-01, which targets DKK1, has shown some promise in early-phase studies with prostate cancer. However, its phase I/II trials were prematurely terminated due to limited durable responses and unclear biomarker-based patient selection. This underscores the need for more effective stratification strategies, particularly for patients with CKAP4 positivity or high DKK1 expression. Although DKK1 blockade has demonstrated therapeutic potential, its selectivity remains uncertain due to the expression of DKK1 in normal tissues; systemic inhibition may disrupt bone homeostasis or immune tolerance. Similarly, CXCR4 is broadly expressed in hematopoietic and endothelial cells, raising concerns about off-target effects and hematologic toxicity. High doses of CXCR4 antagonists have been associated with leukocytosis, gastrointestinal symptoms, and immunosuppression. Despite encouraging results from preclinical studies, clinical efficacy has remained limited and inconsistent. For example, CXCR4 antagonists have demonstrated anti-metastatic activity in preclinical studies using prostate cancer models; however, clinical data in humans remain scarce, and these agents have not yet been approved for the treatment of this indication. Dual-target therapies often face pharmacokinetic challenges, including mismatched half-lives, inconsistent bioavailability, overlapping toxicities, and complex dosing schedules that hinder clinical translation. These issues are well illustrated by inhibitors targeting the Wnt and CXCL12/CXCR4 pathways. Wnt signaling typically requires sustained suppression to affect transcriptional programs and the tumor–bone microenvironment, whereas CXCL12 signaling is rapid and dynamic, responding to chemokine gradients that regulate immune cell trafficking and prostate cancer cells homing. As a result, Wnt inhibitors such as anti-DKK1 antibodies usually exhibit longer half-lives and delayed effects, while CXCR4 antagonists like AMD3100 are rapidly cleared and demand frequent dosing or continuous infusion. This pharmacokinetic mismatch complicates co-administration, increasing the risk of subtherapeutic exposure or cumulative toxicity. Moreover, because both pathways intersect in the bone marrow niche, simultaneous inhibition may disrupt bone remodeling, hematopoiesis, or immune surveillance. These challenges underscore the importance of innovative strategies – such as advanced drug delivery systems or sequential dosing regimens – to optimize therapeutic synergy while minimizing adverse effects. Therefore, the clinical translation of CXCR4 antagonists and anti-DKK1 antibodies for the treatment of prostate cancer and osteoporosis reveals several key gaps. A primary challenge is the complex interplay of their mechanisms, as anti-DKK1 therapy may inadvertently activate the canonical Wnt pathway associated with cancer cell proliferation, while CXCR4 signaling intersects with immunosuppressive pathways, potentially leading to unpredictable immune responses. Furthermore, clinical trial designs have proven inadequate, with premature terminations due to unclear biomarker selection and limited durable responses. Safety concerns arise from the non-selective inhibition of DKK1 and CXCR4, risking disruption of bone homeostasis and immune tolerance. Additionally, pharmacokinetic mismatches complicate co-administration, increasing the risk of suboptimal dosing and cumulative toxicity. These challenges emphasize the need for innovative strategies to optimize therapeutic efficacy while mitigating adverse effects. Future directions and translational outlook Alghamri et al [ 139 ] developed synthetic protein nanoparticles by polymerizing human serum albumin and modifying them with the tumor-penetrating peptide internalizing RGD, enabling targeted encapsulation and systemic delivery against the CXCL12/CXCR4 signaling axis. This delivery system improved the pharmacokinetic properties of the therapeutic agents (e.g., half-life and bioavailability) and significantly reduced the intratumoral infiltration of CXCR4 + monocytic MDSCs in glioblastoma model, thereby promoting immune microenvironment remodeling and inducing immunogenic cell death. This system shows promise for future use in prostate cancer and osteoporosis. Such a platform holds promise for the co-delivery of anti-DKK1 agents and CXCR4 antagonists, enabling simultaneous targeting of bone metabolic dysregulation and disease progression, while overcoming challenges related to pharmacokinetic mismatch and off-target risks. Moreover, after the pharmacokinetic challenges are resolved, the combination of anti-DKK1 and CXCR4 inhibitors with ADT will represent a promising yet underexplored area of research. Bispecific therapies (e.g., sclerostin–DKK1 bispecific antibodies) represent a promising yet underexplored avenue. Sclerostin is a secreted factor produced by osteocytes [ 75 ] . Similar to DKK1, it blocks Wnt signaling by binding to LRP5 and LRP6 [ 140 ] . The combination of anti-DKK1 antibodies and anti-sclerostin antibodies can completely relieve the inhibition of the Wnt pathway, maximizing bone formation and overcoming the limitations of monotherapy, such as the compensatory upregulation of DKK1 caused by anti-sclerostin monotherapy [ 141 ] . Animal model data show that combination therapy is superior to monotherapy in increasing bone mass and improving bone biomechanical strength, making it particularly suitable for patients with severe osteoporosis and those who do not respond well to single-agent treatments [ 142 ] . Meng et al [ 143 ] developed a targeted antibody payload platform that enables simultaneous neutralization of SOST and DKK1 via a single engineered dual-specific antibody. This bispecific molecule exhibited markedly enhanced bone anabolic activity compared to monotherapy in preclinical models. A phase II clinical trial evaluating AGA-2118, a bispecific antibody targeting both DKK1 and SOST, is currently in the patient recruitment stage for the treatment of osteoporosis. However, the role of anti-sclerostin antibodies in prostate cancer remains unclear, and future research should first clarify their specific effect on prostate cancer. The RANKL vaccine shows potential in preventing bone metastasis and treating bone-related diseases. This vaccine works by inducing a specific immune response to block the binding of RANKL to RANK, thereby inhibiting osteoclast activity, reducing bone resorption, and preventing the occurrence of bone metastasis [ 144 ] . By enhancing the immune system’s recognition and neutralization of RANKL, the RANKL vaccine holds promise in slowing the progression of bone lesions. However, its research is still in the early stages, and more clinical studies are needed to further validate its safety, efficacy, and long-term effects. Patient stratification will play a crucial role in future treatments and clinical trials. By stratifying patients based on specific biomarkers, such as the expression levels of CXCR4 or DKK1, treatments can be made more personalized and precise. This approach can significantly improve treatment outcomes and reduce unnecessary side effects. Currently, there is a gap in the research on biomarkers for osteoporosis in prostate cancer patients. Future studies should focus on identifying predictive biomarkers to provide more accurate treatment strategies (e.g., gene therapies) for clinical practice. A deeper understanding of the tumor–immune–bone axis is also crucial. For example, DKK1 blockade may elicit distinct immunological effects depending on the composition of immune cells within the bone marrow microenvironment. Similarly, CXCR4 inhibition also alter the recruitment of MDSCs and the mechanisms underlying their interactions with the stroma and prostate cancer cells. These immunological consequences should be systematically evaluated in future trials. Conclusions The association between prostate cancer and osteoporosis cannot be attributed solely to treatment-related causal factors. Signaling pathways – such as the RANKL/RANK/OPG, Wnt, CXCL12/CXCR4, TGF-β, and IGF-1 – form a complex regulatory network between prostate cancer and bone metabolism. Given the involvement of these signaling pathways in both the progression of prostate cancer and the regulation of bone metabolism, the development of therapeutic agents targeting these pathways may represent a promising strategy for the simultaneous treatment of prostate cancer and osteoporosis (Fig. 5 ). For instance, anti-DKK1 antibodies (e.g., DKN-01 and BHQ880) have demonstrated anti-prostate cancer activity in preclinical models and early-phase clinical trials. In osteoporotic animal models, anti-DKK1 antibodies have been shown to increase bone mineral density. Dual-target therapies, including those directed at DKK1 and CXCR4, demonstrate substantial therapeutic potential by potentially enhancing efficacy through synergistic effects and mitigating the risk of drug resistance. At present, our comprehension of the intricate mechanisms underlying the actions of these drugs is inadequate. Achieving precise drug delivery to both prostate cancer cells and the bone microenvironment continue to pose a significant challenge.
