Abstract Background Patients undergoing peritoneal dialysis are at high risk of infection, which significantly impacts morbidity and mortality. This retrospective study aimed to evaluate the association of thymopentin use with infection risk, immune function, and inflammatory markers in peritoneal dialysis patients. Methods Clinical data from 100 patients undergoing peritoneal dialysis were collected and analyzed. According to the treatment regimens received, patients were divided into a control group (standard therapy) and a thymopentin group (standard therapy combined with thymopentin). Thymopentin was administered subcutaneously at a dose of 10 mg daily for the first 5 days, followed by 10 mg three times per week (Monday, Wednesday, and Friday) for 23 consecutive weeks. Patients were followed for a total of 48 weeks. Infection rates, immune function, and levels of inflammatory markers were compared between the two groups. Results The thymopentin group demonstrated a lower infection incidence than the control group (0.73 vs. 1.00 per person-year). Thymopentin use was associated with significantly reduced overall infection rates ( P &lt; 0.001) and peritonitis ( P = 0.031). Multivariate analysis confirmed a lower infection risk (HR = 0.54, 95% CI: 0.30–0.95; P = 0.034). Inflammatory markers showed favorable changes: interleukin-2 receptor (IL-2R) levels increased at 12 and 24 weeks ( P = 0.002 and 0.020), interleukin-6 (IL-6) decreased at 12 weeks ( P = 0.036). Serum albumin rose at both time points ( P = 0.023 and 0.040). No significant changes were observed in CD3⁺/CD4⁺ or CD8⁺ T cells or the CD4/CD8 ratio. Conclusion Thymopentin use was associated with reduced infection risk and favorable changes in inflammatory and nutritional markers among peritoneal dialysis patients. These findings suggest potential benefits of Thymopentin in this population. Clinical trial number Not applicable. Introduction End-stage renal disease (ESRD) represents a significant public health burden. Compared to patients with other severe conditions, such as cancer, diabetes, and cardiovascular diseases, patients with ESRD experience higher mortality rates and reduced life expectancy [ 1 ]. Renal replacement therapy, which includes hemodialysis (HD) and peritoneal dialysis (PD), is a life-sustaining treatment for patients with ESRD. In high-income regions, such as Europe and the United States, PD accounts for 5–10% of all dialysis patients, whereas in low- and middle-income countries, this proportion can be as high as 75% [ 2 ]. Infection, a common complication, is a major risk factor for hospitalization and the second leading cause of death following cardiovascular disease in dialysis patients [ 3 ]. The overall survival and hospitalization rates are comparable between HD and PD patients [ 4 ], but PD patients exhibit a higher incidence of infection and infection-associated mortality [ 5 – 7 ]. Reducing the infection rate among PD patients can effectively enhance their prognosis and quality of life. Thymosin, particularly thymosin α1 and thymopentin(TP), is commonly used as an immunomodulator in clinical practice. It can effectively reduce the incidence of infection in patients with severe pancreatitis and those undergoing chemoradiotherapy for tumor treatment [ 8 , 9 ]. Furthermore, thymosin not only alleviates lung injury and increases the survival rate of sepsis patients but also improves the prognosis of kidney transplant recipients experiencing acute respiratory distress caused by cytomegalovirus or fungal infections [ 10 – 13 ]. In summary, thymosin can enhance immune resistance by modulating immune function. However, its potential to prevent infection in PD patients remains unclear. Therefore, we retrospectively analyzed clinical data of PD patients to investigate the effectiveness and safety of thymopentin in infection prophylaxis and its impact on immune function and inflammatory status. Methods Study population This was a single-center, retrospective cohort study conducted at Ruijin Hospital between October 2019 and November 2022. Patients undergoing maintenance PD were identified through the hospital electronic medical record system. Eligible patients were aged 18–75 years, diagnosed with ESRD, and had been receiving regular PD for at least 3 months. Patients were required not to be receiving steroids or other immunosuppressive agents at the time of the study. Patients were excluded based on medical records if they had documented use of steroids or immunosuppressive agents during the study period, severe heart disease (NYHA class III–V), known or suspected active pulmonary tuberculosis, severe liver disease (alanine aminotransferase or aspartate aminotransferase levels persistently exceeding twice the normal value), newly diagnosed malignancy within the past 5 years or ongoing chemoradiotherapy, severe immunosuppressive conditions such as Human Immunodeficiency Virus infection or splenectomy, documented neutropenia (neutrophil count &lt; 1500/mm³), or pregnancy. Patients were categorized into two groups according to whether they received TP during routine clinical care. Those treated with TP were included in the TP group, while patients who continued standard PD without TP served as controls. In the TP group, patients received subcutaneous injections of thymopentin (10 mg; prefilled injection, Hainan Zhonghe Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd.) once daily for the first 5 days, followed by 10 mg three times per week (on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday) for 23 consecutive weeks, in addition to standard PD therapy. The control group received standard PD therapy alone. Our study was approved by the Institutional Review Board of Ruijin Hospital, Shanghai Jiao Tong University School of Medicine and conducted in accordance with the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki. Endpoint The primary endpoint was the incidence of infection within 48 weeks after the initiation of TP therapy, as documented in medical records. Secondary endpoints included changes in immune and inflammatory biomarkers when available, assessed at approximately 12-week intervals. These markers comprised CD3 + , CD4 + , and CD8 + T-cell counts, the CD4/CD8 ratio, and serum levels of interleukin-2 receptor (IL-2R), interleukin-6 (IL-6), and tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α). Additional laboratory parameters, including albumin (Alb) and hemoglobin (Hb), were also analyzed. Severe infection was defined as requiring intravenous antibiotics or resulting in hospitalization or death [ 14 ]. Safety assessment Adverse events (AEs) and serious adverse events (SAEs) were identified through review of hospitalization records, laboratory results, and physician notes. SAEs were defined as life-threatening events, events resulting in death, hospitalization or prolonged hospital stay, permanent organ dysfunction, or significant functional impairment. Statistical analysis Data were analyzed using IBM SPSS Statistics 25 (IBM Corp., USA). Categorical variables were summarized as frequencies, percentages, or composition ratios. Continuous variables were expressed as the mean ± standard deviation (X ± SD) for normally distributed data and as the median (interquartile range, IQR) for nonnormally distributed data. A Cox proportional hazards model was employed to identify factors associated with the first infection during the observation period, using age, sex, dialysis duration, thymopentin treatment, and serum albumin levels as covariates. Between-group comparisons of changes from baseline at each follow-up point were conducted using independent t-tests or Mann–Whitney U tests. Statistical significance was set at P &lt; 0.05, with P &lt; 0.01 considered highly significant. Results Patient characteristics A total of 100 patients were included in the analysis, with 50 in the TP group and 50 in the control group. The mean age of patients in the TP group was 57.84 ± 12.34 years, with a median dialysis duration of 39 (12–145)months. In the control group, the mean age was 53.33 ± 11.72 years, and the median dialysis duration was 39(13–142) months. Chronic glomerulonephritis has emerged as the most prevalent primary disease, whereas hypertensive nephropathy and diabetic nephropathy are the most common secondary causes. There were no statistically significant differences between the two groups of patients in terms of sex, age, duration of dialysis, body mass index (BMI), white blood cell (WBC), C-reactive protein (CRP), Alb, high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL-C) and low-density lipoprotein Cholesterol(LDL-C). During follow-up, one patient from each group was lost at 12 weeks, and one additional patient from the TP group was lost at 48 weeks (Table 1 ). Evaluation of safety A total of 21 patients (21.0%) experienced at least one adverse event during the study period, the most common being cardiovascular disease. The TP group reported 12 adverse events, whereas the control group reported 24 events ( P = 0.012). There were 16 serious adverse events in 14 patients (resulting in hospitalization or death); 1 patient in each group experienced 2 serious adverse events. One patient in both the TP group and the control group died. The causes of death were sudden cardiac arrest and myocardial infarction (Table 4 ). Except for the two patients who died, all others achieved clinical improvement and were subsequently discharged. None of the adverse events were related to the drug intervention. Thymosin is primarily employed in the management of acute and chronic infections, malignancies, and immunodeficiency disorders. Limited research has explored the efficacy of thymosin in infection prevention [ 8 , 15 ]. This study is the first to investigate the prophylactic use of thymopentin in peritoneal dialysis patients and demonstrates that it significantly reduces the overall incidence of infections, particularly peritonitis, without an associated increase in adverse events, suggesting its potential as a safe prophylactic immunomodulator in this population. Thymosin has been shown to promote the differentiation of progenitor cells into CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T lymphocytes, thereby contributing to the maintenance of a balanced CD3⁺/CD4⁺/CD8⁺ T cell ratio [ 8 , 10 , 16 ]. Patients with ESRD frequently exhibit T cell–mediated immune dysfunction, characterized by reduced numbers of CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T lymphocytes and a decreased CD4⁺/CD8⁺ ratio [ 17 – 19 ]. However, our study did not observe statistically significant differences in changes in T cell between the two groups. Notably, our data revealed a significant increase in IL-2R levels in the TP group. IL-2, a versatile cytokine primarily secreted by T cells, plays a crucial regulatory role in the body’s immune system [ 20 – 22 ]. Thymosin treatment has been shown to enhance the immune response by stimulating IL-2 secretion and upregulating IL-2R expression [ 23 – 26 ]. The biological impact of IL-2 is mediated primarily by the high-affinity IL-2R, which is typically not expressed in quiescent T lymphocytes. The observed increase in IL-2R levels after TP therapy may indicate a potential association with T-cell activation and immune recovery in PD patients. Further prospective studies are warranted to clarify the specific effects of thymopentin on T-cell function. Another important observation was that IL-6 elevation was significantly suppressed in the TP group. In comparison to healthy individuals, patients with ESRD often present with increased circulating levels of inflammatory mediators, such as IL-6 [ 27 ]. In dialysis patients, elevated IL-6 is not only an independent risk factor for renal osteodystrophy and cardiovascular disease, but also contributes to peritoneal membrane dysfunction by promoting epithelial–mesenchymal transition [ 28 – 30 ]. Thymosin has shown potential in reducing pro-inflammatory cytokines such as TNF-α and IL-6 in patients with severe infections [ 11 , 23 , 31 ]. Subgroup analysis revealed that patients with shorter dialysis duration in the TP group exhibited lower TNF-α levels, suggesting that early administration of thymopentin may potentially be beneficial in improving the inflammatory status of peritoneal dialysis patients. Metabolic and nutritional disorders, referred to as protein‒energy wasting of chronic kidney disease, are common in dialysis patients and are associated with adverse clinical outcomes, leading to increased rates of hospitalization and mortality [ 32 ]. Alb is a crucial parameter for assessing the nutritional status of dialysis patients. Hypoproteinaemia is a recognized risk factor for infection, particularly in those undergoing peritoneal dialysis. A reduced level of Alb prior to peritoneal dialysis is considered a risk factor for early peritonitis, and a low Alb level has been associated with increased rates of mortality and cardiovascular events in peritoneal dialysis patients [ 33 , 34 ]. In this study, thymopentin administration was associated with the prevention of serum albumin decline in patients undergoing PD. While this finding suggests a potential beneficial effect on nutritional status, the underlying mechanisms remain unclear, and evidence on the relationship between thymopentin and albumin regulation is still limited. Limitations of this study This study has several limitations. First, the relatively small sample size may limit the statistical power and the robustness of the findings. Second, as both IL-6 and Alb levels can be affected by infection status, caution is warranted when interpreting their association and inferring causality. Third, the study was conducted at a single center and involved only a Chinese patient cohort, which may restrict the generalizability of the results to broader peritoneal dialysis disease populations. In addition, data on dialysis adequacy and peritoneal transport status were not collected, which may have influenced certain clinical outcomes. These important parameters should be incorporated into future research designs. Therefore, future large-scale, multicenter studies across diverse populations are needed to validate these findings and explore their long-term implications. Lastly, the study was partially funded by the manufacturer of thymopentin. Although all data analyses and interpretations were conducted independently by the authors, this potential conflict of interest should be acknowledged when considering the results. Conclusions Thymopentin was associated with reduced infections—particularly peritonitis, and improvements in immune and nutritional markers, including increased IL-2R expression, decreased IL-6 levels, and elevated serum albumin, in patients undergoing peritoneal dialysis. No significant changes were observed in CD3⁺, CD4⁺, or CD8⁺ T-cell counts, nor in the CD4/CD8 ratio. Importantly, no increase in adverse events was reported in the thymopentin group, supporting its favorable safety profile and potential clinical value. However, given the retrospective, single-center design of this study, these findings should be interpreted with caution. Larger, multicenter, prospective trials are warranted to validate these preliminary results and further investigate the clinical utility of thymopentin in this patient population.
