# 11-kpv

"The Anti-Inflammatory Masterpeptide"

Three amino acids — Lysine, Proline, Valine — cleaved from alpha-MSH. A tiny peptide with massive anti-inflammatory power that crosses the blood-brain barrier and fights neuroinflammation at its source.

Directly targets **Root Cause #1 — Systemic Inflammation** by inhibiting NF-kB, the master inflammatory switch, and crossing the blood-brain barrier to calm neuroinflammation.

KPV is a tripeptide fragment cleaved from the C-terminal end of alpha-melanocyte stimulating hormone (alpha-MSH). Despite being only three amino acids long, it retains the full anti-inflammatory potency of the parent hormone while being small enough to cross the blood-brain barrier.

Nuclear Factor kappa-B controls the expression of over 400 inflammatory genes. When NF-kB translocates from the cytoplasm to the nucleus, it triggers a cascade of inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-alpha, IL-1beta). KPV prevents this translocation — keeping the master switch in the "off" position.

ClinicalMost anti-inflammatory compounds cannot reach the brain. KPV's lipophilic structure allows it to cross the blood-brain barrier and directly modulate microglial cells — the brain's immune system. This makes it uniquely effective against neuroinflammation driving Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and MS.

The triple GLP-1/GIP/Glucagon agonist that resets metabolic function from the ground up.

Alpha-melanocyte stimulating hormone (alpha-MSH) is a 13-amino-acid neuropeptide produced by the pituitary gland, skin cells (keratinocytes), and immune cells. It is one of the body's most potent endogenous anti-inflammatory molecules. KPV represents the C-terminal tripeptide fragment — amino acids 11, 12, and 13 — which research has demonstrated retains the full anti-inflammatory activity of the parent hormone.

The anti-inflammatory action of alpha-MSH does not depend on the full 13-amino-acid sequence. The C-terminal tripeptide (KPV) contains the core pharmacophore — the minimum structural unit required for biological activity. This has been confirmed through systematic truncation studies:

Unlike most peptides, oral KPV is **not destroyed by digestion**. Its tiny size (three amino acids) allows partial survival through the GI tract, and for gut inflammation specifically, local delivery to the intestinal mucosa is actually the *preferred* mechanism. Oral KPV reaches the inflamed gut lining directly. For systemic inflammation, SubQ remains the gold standard.

One of KPV's most compelling clinical applications is in inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) — a spectrum that includes Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis. The mechanism is uniquely suited to GI inflammation because KPV targets the exact pathways that drive mucosal immune dysfunction.
