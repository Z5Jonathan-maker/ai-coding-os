# Synergies from a distance: Inspirations from the struggles of Dr James M Krueger

Abstract In this article for Dr Krueger's Festschrift, I trace how his early career influenced many aspects in the fields of sleep, neuroimmunology, and the microbiome. Mostly, however, I trace how his career and interests intertwined with those of Abba J. Kastin and mine and how he exerted a profound influence on the direction of our studies. Dr. Krueger, while developing his career as a sleep researcher, encountered resistance to his work that required two major paradigm shifts: 1) that bacterial products could affect sleep and 2) that small peptides can cross the blood-brain barrier (BBB) in sufficient amounts to affect brain functioning. Dr Kastin had also shown that small peptides administered peripherally could affect brain function and postulated that this was because they could cross the BBB. Our efforts to determine whether peptides could or could not cross the BBB were bolstered by Dr Krueger's exemplary struggles. Introduction Dr James M. Krueger now stands as an eminent, renowned scientist who is internationally respected for his many accomplishments in the field of neuroscience. He is especially well known for his pioneering work on sleep, sleep factors, cytokines, and the effects of infections on sleep. Much of the influence that Dr Krueger has had on science and his peers has been exerted through his publications, mentoring, and talks at scientific venues. However, Jim exerted tremendous influence on at least two other scientists without his direct knowledge or actions. Furthermore, he exerted those influences most strongly on Abba J Kastin and me when Jim was in his junior years and himself struggling against a conservative field to have his pioneering ideas accepted. This brief dedication to Jim's scientific accomplishments highlights how Abba and I swam upstream against many of the same scientific currents as Jim, how we were inspired by him, and how sometimes our parallel universes aligned. 2. Bugs, brains, barriers, and sleep Dr Krueger seems to have started his scientific career safely enough with his first publication with Dr RH Cagan on the biochemistry of taste in catfish whiskers ( Krueger and Cagan, 1976 ). Jim was Dr Cagan's first graduate student and Dr Cagan had an appointment at the VA in Philadelphia. Dr Cagan eventually left academia for a career as Director of Research at Proctor and Gamble. By 1974, Jim had moved to Harvard to work with Manfred Karnovsky, mentor to Dr Cagan, and Dr John R Pappenheimer. Dr JR Pappenheimer did seminal work in many aspects of physiology including blood-brain-cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) transport and sleep. Dr Pappenheimer's sister, Anne, was one of the first women to become a professor at Harvard, his brother Alwin was a National Academy member who did work on bacterial toxins, and his father was a distinguished pathologist at Columbia University. Soon Jim was studying sleep factors in brain and urine. Sleep-promoting factor (Factor S) was known to be a low molecular weight peptide and Jim set out to identify that peptide ( Krueger et al., 1978 ). However, the methodologies for isolating, purifying, and sequencing low abundance peptides from brain tissue were extremely challenging. At about the same time that Jim joined John Pappenheimer's lab, a young Abba J Kastin made his journey from Harvard via Vanderbilt to the New Orleans VA and Tulane to join the lab of Dr Andrew V Schally. Dr Kastin was a young, clinically trained endocrinologist that joined Dr Schally's team in the mid 1960's. Dr Schally believed that the hypothalamus controlled the pituitary gland, and thus much of the endocrine network, by secreting vanishingly small amounts of substances into the plexus that connected the hypothalamus with the pituitary ( Saffran et al., 1955 ). Schally's isolation equipment included a column of Sephadex® about 10 feet tall to separate candidate peptides from approximately 6,000,000 sheep hypothalami. The column was located on the ground level of a small, two story building with rather low ceilings. The height of the column was greater than the height of its room and so a hole was drilled through the first floor ceiling (AKA the 2nd story's floor) through which the column projected, and the column loaded from the second floor. Schally eventually identified the tripeptide thyrotropin-releasing hormone and the decapeptide luteinizing hormone releasing hormone for which he won the Nobel Prize in 1977 ( Anderson et al., 1971 ; Matsuo et al., 1971 ). While Dr Schally's primary goal was to demonstrate that these peptides from the brain controlled a peripheral tissue (the pituitary) ( Nair et al., 1971 ), Dr Kastin very soon showed that they also affected brain function when administered peripherally ( Kastin et al., 1975 ). In the meantime, Jim Krueger had isolated from 15,000 brains of sleeping rabbits and 5000 L of human urine enough Factor S to identify the amino acid composition ( Krueger et al., 1982 ). One of the amino acids was diaminopimelic acid. This indicated that the peptide was of bacterial origin. In the current scientific environment where it is accepted that the microbiome exerts great influence on the brain, Dr Krueger's publication that a bacterial product can influence something as fundamental as sleep and do so in a physiologic way would likely be met with incredible enthusiasm. But in the early 1980's, his findings were, to be polite, widely doubted; indeed, he was ridiculed and even booed at some of his talks. It lies to some future graduate student in science with a bent towards history or some graduate student in history with a bent towards science to discover to what extent the hardscrabble that Jim endured with his prescient discovery inoculated the wider scientific community for its currently epidemic enthusiasm for all things microbiome. Jim's finding of a microbial product influencing the brain implied another highly controversial aspect of physiology that once again involved the New Orleans group and would also take years of hardscrabble to have accepted ( Banks, 2023 ). Dr Kastin in the early 1970's had discovered that peptides administered peripherally to animals altered their behavior ( Kastin et al., 1972 ), just as Dr Krueger would demonstrate for his bacterial peptides. As a possible mechanism as to how a peptide administered peripherally could influence the brain, Abba proposed that peptides might be able to cross the blood-brain barrier (BBB) ( Pelletier et al., 1975 ). This, again to be polite, was also widely doubted and Abba received significant criticism from many corners of the scientific community. Indeed, it took Abba and me about 10 years to persuade the majority of the BBB community that many peptides could cross the BBB in physiologically meaningful amounts, as documented in an article in Abba's Festschrift ( Banks, 2015 ). We knew of Jim's struggles and his example inspired us to endure the early years of criticism. Indeed, perhaps Jim's biggest influence on me has been how he from my perspective answered unfair criticism with grace towards his critics and with tenacity towards his work. BBB passage of peptides and regulatory proteins is now widely accepted in fields such as neuroimmunology and gastrointestinal endocrinology, but is still largely neglected in CNS drug development. In order to determine whether a peptide could cross the BBB as an intact molecule, we chose another sleep-related molecule, delta-sleep inducing peptide (DSIP). This nonapeptide had been isolated from rabbits, sequenced, and synthesized by Monnier and Schoenenberger and shown not to be Pappenheimer's Factor S ( Monnier et al., 1977 ). They had shown that it acted within brain and required the entire 9 amino acids to affect sleep. Dr Kastin applied to the study of DSIP what at that time was a relatively new technology: he developed a radioimmunoassay for DSIP ( Kastin et al., 1981 ). Solomon Berson and Rosalyn Yalow had developed the radioimmunoassay in the 1950's, combining the sensitivity allowed by radioactivity and the specificity provided by immunoglobulins to measure the level of hormones in blood and other tissues. During the 1960's, Schally and many others were transitioning from the use of bioassays to radioimmunoassays. For this work, Dr Yalow shared the 1977 Nobel Prize with Dr Schally and Dr Roger Guillemin. Dr Kastin's radioimmunoassay required 8 of the 9 amino acids of DSIP for interaction. With Sephadex® chromatography, we were able to isolate the octapeptide from the nonapeptide. Using these approaches as well as others, we were able to show that DSIP and DSIP analogs crossed from blood into brain tissue and the cerebrospinal fluid in intact form in rats and dogs ( Banks and Kastin, 1985a ). 3. The wilderness years Jim continued to do work on muramyl peptides in the 1980's, extending and strengthening his original observations. The 1980's for Abba and me were also years that we continued and expanded our work relating to peptide permeation of the BBB. After demonstrating that DSIP and its analogs entered the CSF ( Banks and Kastin, 1983 ) by virtue of their lipid solubility, we turned our attention to other peptides. We showed that other peptides could cross the BBB as well and that lipid solubility could account for the degree to which some of these could cross ( Banks and Kastin, 1985b ). Years earlier, Oldendorf had shown that many substances crossed the BBB ( Oldendorf et al., 1972 ), but noted hexoses and some amino acids crossed much faster than predicted ( Oldendorf, 1971 ). He eventually demonstrated that this was because these substances were being transported in the blood-to-brain direction by a saturable system. Similarly, we found that enkephalins and other peptides with an N-terminal tyrosine crossed the BBB much less than predicted by their lipid solubility. We found that these were also being transported across the BBB, but in the brain-to-blood direction ( Banks et al., 1990 ). We developed or adapted methods that allowed us to quantify the rate by which substances crossed the BBB and that in turn allowed us to study the effects of physiological changes and insults on the peptide permeation across the BBB. By the late 1980's and early 1990's, the idea that peptides can cross the BBB and exert effects on brain was increasingly accepted and so we began to look for other areas to study. Once again, Jim Krueger was our bellwether. 4. Cytokines, the navy, and sleep After a decade or so of establishing the importance of peptide sleep factors, Jim in the early 1980's began to work on cytokines. Cytokines are regulatory proteins and so can be considerably larger than peptides. Jim worked with many of the other pioneers in the cytokine field, demonstrating sleep effects literally as the cytokines were being identified ( Opp and Krueger, 1991 ; Opp et al., 1991 ; Kapas et al., 1992 ; Taishi et al., 1998 ; Krueger et al., 2001 , 2011 ). Jim's pioneering work formed the basis of much of what developed into the field of neuroimmunology. One aspect of Jim's work demonstrated that peripherally administered cytokines had profound effects on behavior. After a decade or so of establishing that peptides can cross the BBB, Kastin and I in the late 1980's began to work on cytokines as well. Attracted by work that showed that peripherally administered cytokines had profound effects on behavior, we asked if cytokines could cross the BBB. By this time, the BBB field had developed the techniques to readily address this question in a very straightforward, quantitative way and we easily showed that the interleukin-1's, tumor necrosis factor-alpha, and interleukin-6 each crossed the BBB using their own unique transport systems ( Banks et al., 1995 ). Work in this area shows that the central nervous and immune systems are linked by many mechanisms, including afferent nerve conduction and actions at the circumventricular organs ( Watkins et al., 1995 ). But it is the BBB that uniquely first separates the blood-borne components of the immune system from the CNS and then reconnects them through regulated mechanisms ( Erickson and Banks, 2018 ). The Office of Naval Research (ONR) played an important part in the life of both labs during the formative cytokine years. Early in Jim's career, the ONR had supported his work. Later, Jeannine Majde, an ONR program officer trained as a virologist, advised Jim on experiments related to viruses and sleep. Dr Majde was also interested in our work on cytokines and the BBB and provided some funding at a crucial point in that research. Dr Majde was a true phenomenon. Unlike many with an encyclopedic knowledge, her brain had collated, indexed, cross-referenced, and integrated it all so that to me she seemed equally at ease in reviewing the published literature, identifying emerging trends, or speculating on what this all meant. After these early years, interest in Jim's work blossomed and he had many wonderful post-docs, whom I've had the privilege to count as friends and colleagues, who have gone on to spectacular careers in their own right. They all count their formative years with Jim as essential to their careers. But I count his formative years as crucial to mine. Funding Supported by VA ACOS Support Fund 2024.
