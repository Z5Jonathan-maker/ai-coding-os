Background Myostatin (MSTN) is a well-known negative regulator of skeletal muscle growth, and its pharmacological blockade, such as with follistatin (FST), an endogenous MSTN inhibitor, is under active investigation as a treatment for muscle-wasting conditions. However, the dynamics of MSTN signaling during in vitro myogenesis and its modulation by culture conditions remain incompletely understood. Methods Primary myoblasts were isolated from wild-type (WT), Mstn −/− , and FST transgenic ( F66 ) mice to evaluate the effects of MSTN inhibition on myotube formation. Myoblasts were differentiated on Matrigel-coated surfaces in the presence of horse serum. Myotube maturation was evaluated by confocal microscopy and reverse transcription-polymerase chain reaction; protein synthesis was assessed using a puromycin incorporation assay; and MSTN concentrations in serum were quantified by enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay. Results Mstn expression peaked at 24 hr of differentiation, coinciding with early myotube formation, and progressively declined as myotubes matured. Both Mstn −/− and F66 myotubes showed increased thickness and protein synthesis compared to WT controls, with more pronounced effects observed in F66 myotubes. Consistent with this, F66 myotubes also displayed higher expression of myogenic maturation markers. Notably, horse serum used in the culture medium contained detectable levels of MSTN, which may have partially restored MSTN signaling in Mstn −/− cultures and masked the full knockout phenotype. Conclusions FST overexpression induces greater myotube hypertrophy and protein synthesis than Mstn deletion, likely due to its broader inhibition of both endogenous and serum-derived MSTN. These findings highlight the importance of serum composition in interpreting phenotypes from in vitro knockout models targeting secreted factors such as MSTN. Naturally occurring loss-of-function mutations in the MSTN gene result in a hypermuscular phenotype across multiple species, including humans, cattle, and sheep.[ 1 – 4 ] In mice, genetic deletion of Mstn leads to a two-fold increase in muscle mass throughout the body due to both hypertrophy (increased fiber size) and hyperplasia (increased fiber number).[ 5 ] This dramatic phenotype has motivated the development of pharmacological strategies aimed at inhibiting MSTN signaling as a therapeutic approach for muscle-wasting conditions such as sarcopenia, cachexia, and muscular dystrophy.[ 1 ] One of the promising MSTN-targeting strategies under clinical investigation is the use of follistatin (FST), an endogenous glycoprotein that binds and neutralizes MSTN and other TGF-β family members, including activin A and growth differentiation factor 11 (GDF11).[ 1 ] While in vivo studies have consistently confirmed MSTN’s suppressive role in muscle growth, the regulation and functional consequences of MSTN signaling during in vitro myogenesis remain less clearly defined. Many prior studies have utilized exogenous recombinant MSTN treatment in C2C12 myoblasts,[ 6 – 9 ] whereas relatively few have investigated primary myoblasts derived from Mstn -deficient mice,[ 10 ] which may provide more physiologically relevant findings. Importantly, in vitro knockout models require careful attention to the composition of the culture environment, particularly the serum used for cell growth and differentiation. Recent work demonstrated that culturing cells in serum from aged versus young mice leads to significant alterations in cellular behavior, including changes in proliferation and markers of senescence.[ 11 ] Similarly, myotubes cultured in serum from older adults display reduced diameter and protein synthesis activity compared with those cultured in young adult serum.[ 12 ] These findings underscore that serum-derived factors can significantly influence in vitro phenotypes and may obscure the effects of genetic manipulations, particularly when studying secreted signaling molecules. In this study, we compared the effects of MSTN inhibition via genetic deletion of Mstn and transgenic overexpression of FST in primary myoblasts undergoing in vitro myogenesis. We show that FST overexpression induces more pronounced myotube hypertrophy and protein synthesis activity than Mstn deletion, likely due to its broader inhibition of MSTN activity, including MSTN derived from the culture serum. Furthermore, we demonstrate that horse serum, a commonly used supplement for myoblast culture and differentiation, contains measurable levels of MSTN, which may partially compensate for the absence of endogenous MSTN and obscure the full phenotype in Mstn -deficient cells. Our findings highlight the importance of considering serum composition when interpreting results from in vitro knockout models targeting secreted factors such as MSTN. METHODS 1. Mice All mice were maintained on a C57BL/6 background. Mstn −/− and F66 mice were kindly provided by Se-Jin Lee (formerly at Johns Hopkins University, currently at the University of Connecticut School of Medicine) and have been previously described.[ 13 ] Mice were housed in a specific pathogen-free facility with a 12-hr light/dark cycle and ad libitum access to food and water. All animal procedures were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) of Seoul National University and conducted in accordance with institutional guidelines. 2. Myoblast isolation and differentiation Primary myoblasts were isolated from skeletal muscles of mice using a standard pre-plating method. Briefly, dissected muscles were finely minced and enzymatically digested in a solution containing collagenase type IV (Gibco, New York, NY, USA) and dispase II (Gibco) for approximately 1 hr. The resulting cell suspension was plated onto non-coated culture dishes to allow fibroblasts to adhere. After 1 hr, the supernatant enriched in myoblasts was transferred to a fresh non-coated dish. This differential adhesion step was repeated multiple times to minimize fibroblast contamination. The final cell suspension was then seeded onto 0.1% gelatin-coated dishes and expanded in F-10 nutrient medium (Gibco) supplemented with 20% horse serum, 1% penicillin-streptomycin, and 2.5 ng/mL basic fibroblast growth factor (Promega, San Luis Obispo, CA, USA). Myogenic differentiation was initiated by switching to Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM) supplemented with 5% horse serum and 1% penicillin–streptomycin. For myotube formation, myoblasts were plated on dishes coated with Matrigel (1 or 3 mg/mL). Myotube thickness was quantified by averaging measurements taken at five evenly spaced points along individual myotubes. 3. Confocal imaging Differentiated myotubes were fixed and stained with phalloidin (Biotium, Fremont, CA USA) to visualize filamentous actin and with DAPI to label nuclei. Confocal imaging was performed using a Zeiss LSM 800 microscope (Zeiss, Oberkochen, Germany), equipped with a Plan-Apochromat 63×/1.4 NA oil immersion objective, with or without Airyscan mode. Image processing and three-dimensional (3D) reconstructions were performed using Zen Blue edition (Zeiss) and Imaris software (Bitplane, Zurich, Switzerland). 4. Puromycin assay Puromycin assay was performed as previously described. [ 14 ] Differentiated myotubes were incubated with 1 μM puromycin (InvivoGen, San Diego, CA, USA) for 30 min to label nascent polypeptides. Cells were then lysed using Pro-Prep lysis buffer (iNtRon Biotechnology, Seongnam, Korea), and protein samples were denatured at 100°C for 5 min in sample buffer (Cure Bio, Seoul, Korea). Equal amounts of protein were separated by sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis and transferred to polyvinylidene difluoride membranes. Membranes were blocked with 5% skim milk for 1 hr at room temperature and incubated overnight at 4°C with anti-puromycin antibody (PMY-2A4, DSHB; 1:200). After washing, membranes were incubated with horseradish peroxidase-conjugated anti-mouse IgG (1:2000; Thermo Fisher Scientific, Inc., Waltham, MA, USA). Bands were detected using the SuperSignal chemiluminescence reagents (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Inc.). 5. Reverse transcription-polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) Total RNA was extracted using the QIAzol Lysis Reagent (Qiagen, Valencia, CA, USA) and the AccuPrep Universal RNA Extraction Kit (Bioneer, Daejeon, Korea), following the manufacturers’ protocols. cDNA was synthesized using the PrimeScript RT Reagent Kit (Takara Bio, Inc., Kyoto, Japan). Quantitative RT-PCR was performed using TB Green Premix Ex Taq II (Takara Bio, Inc.) on a StepOnePlus Real-Time PCR system or QuantStudio 3 Real-Time PCR system (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA). Relative gene expression was calculated using the standard 2 (−ΔΔCt) method with 18S rRNA as the internal control. Primers for Mstn , Gdf11 , and Inhba were previously reported.[ 13 ] The following primer sequences were used for other targets: Fst forward: TCATGGACCGAGGAGGATGT; Fst reverse: TCCACGTTCTCACACGTTTCTTT; Mrf4 forward: ATCAGCTACATTGAGCGTCTACA; Mrf4 reverse: CCTGGAATGATCCGAAACACTTG; Myh3 forward: TCTCTGTCACAGTCAGAGGTGT; Myh3 reverse: GGCTGGTTCTGAGCTTCGAT; Myh4 forward: GGTGAAGAGCCGAGAGGTTC; Myh4 reverse: CTCCTGTCACCTCTCAACAGAAAG; Myh1 forward: GAAGTTGCATCCCTAAAGGCAG; Myh1 reverse: GTTCTGAGCCTCGATTCGCT; Myh2 forward: ACAAGCTGAGGAGGCTGAGG; Myh2 reverse: CAGCATCGGGACAGCCTTAC; Myh7 forward: CAACCTGTCCAAGTTCCGCA; Myh7 reverse: TACTCCTCATTCAGGCCCTTG; 18S forward: CTGCCCTATCAACTTTCGATGGTAG; 18S reverse: CCGTTTCTCAGGCTCCCTCTC. 6. Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) Serum MSTN levels were measured using a commercially available ELISA kit (Catalog No. DGDF80; R&amp;D Systems, Minneapolis, MN, USA) following the manufacturer’s instructions and as previously described.[ 15 ] Briefly, serum samples were acid-activated with 1N HCl, neutralized, and subsequently diluted using the provided dilution buffer. Samples were assayed in duplicate. Dilution factors were as follows: WT and Mstn +/− mouse serum, 40- and 80-fold; goat serum, 4-, 8-, and 16-fold; horse serum, 4-, 8-, 16-, and 32-fold. For each condition, the average value of duplicates was plotted as individual data points on a bar graph. Optical density was measured at 450 nm with reference correction at 540 nm, and a standard curve was generated using a four-parameter logistic regression model. 7. Statistical analysis All data are presented as mean±standard error of mean. Statistical analysis was performed using one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s post hoc test for Figure 1 to 3 , and t- test for Figure 4 . A P value of less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant. All statistical analyses were conducted using SPSS statistics version 26 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA). RESULTS 1. Dynamic expression patterns of Mstn , Gdf11 , activin A ( Inhba ), and Fst during in vitro myogenesis To investigate the expression patterns of Mstn, Gdf11, Inhba , and Fst during in vitro myogenesis, we examined primary myoblasts isolated from wild-type (WT) mice as they underwent differentiation into myotubes on Matrigel-coated surfaces. Upon differentiation induction, myoblasts began to fuse within the first 24 hr, forming immature multinucleated myotubes, which progressively matured and hypertrophied over the next 24 to 36 hr ( Fig. 1A ). Gene expression analysis revealed a significant increase in both Mstn and Fst expression at 24 hr post-differentiation, at which immature myotubes form ( Fig. 1B ). Interestingly, Mstn expression exhibited a sharp decline by 48 and 60 hr, suggesting that MSTN may primarily function during the early stages of myotube formation, whereas its expression becomes downregulated as myotubes mature and undergo hypertrophy. In contrast, Gdf11 and Inhba expression remained relatively stable until 48 hr post-differentiation, after which it significantly increased. Overall, Inhba levels were consistently lower than those of Mstn, Gdf11 , and Fst throughout the course of myogenesis. These results suggest that MSTN plays a key role in regulating early myotube formation, while the concurrent increase in FST may act to counterbalance MSTN activity. The subsequent decline in MSTN levels may facilitate myotube maturation. 2. FST-overexpressing ( F66 ) myotubes exhibit enhanced growth compared to WT and Mstn −/− myotubes To evaluate the effects of MSTN inhibition on myotube hypertrophy and maturation, we cultured myoblasts from WT, Mstn −/− , and F66 mice. F66 is a transgenic line that overexpresses FST, an endogenous inhibitor of MSTN. Confocal and 3D-reconstructed imaging revealed visibly thicker myotubes in both Mstn −/− and F66 cultures, with F66 myotubes appearing the most hypertrophic at 48 hr post-differentiation ( Fig. 2A ). Quantitative analysis confirmed a significant increase in myotube thickness in F66 compared to WT and Mstn −/− myotubes ( Fig. 2B ). Although Mstn −/− myotubes showed a modest increase in thickness compared to WT, the magnitude of hypertrophy was substantially less than that observed in F66 myotubes. In addition, expression of myotube maturation markers myogenic regulatory factor 4 ( Mrf4 ) was most strongly upregulated in F66 myotubes ( Fig. 2C ). The expression of embryonic myosin heavy chain 3 ( Myh3 ), which encodes a developmental myosin isoform expressed in early-formed myotubes, was comparable across all groups, confirming that myotube formation was successfully achieved in each group. To assess fiber type specification, we measured expression of myosin heavy chain isoforms associated with different fiber types. Notably, Myh4 , which encodes the type 2B fast glycolytic isoform, was significantly upregulated in F66 myotubes compared to WT ( Fig. 2D ). Although not statistically significant, Myh4 expression was also elevated in Mstn −/− myotubes, consistent with previous in vivo findings that MSTN deficiency promotes type 2B fiber formation.[ 16 ] In contrast, expression levels of other isoforms, Myh1 (type 2X), Myh2 (type 2A), and Myh7 (type I), were similar across all groups. Taken together, the data demonstrate that while both Mstn deletion and FST overexpression contribute to increased myotube thickness and the formation of type 2B isoforms, FST overexpression has a more pronounced effect on both the growth and maturation of myotubes, as evidenced by greater hypertrophy and stronger upregulation of maturation marker expression. 3. Protein synthesis is elevated in Mstn −/− and F66 myotubes To assess whether the increased size of Mstn −/− and F66 myotubes was accompanied by elevated protein synthesis, we performed a puromycin incorporation assay to quantify nascent polypeptide synthesis. Myotubes differentiated for 48 hr were incubated with puromycin for 30 min, and puromycin-labeled proteins were subsequently detected by immunoblotting ( Fig. 3A ). Both Mstn −/− and F66 myotubes showed increased levels of puromycin incorporation compared to WT controls, indicating enhanced global protein synthesis. Notably, F66 myotubes exhibited the strongest signal, with an approximate 3-fold increase over WT, while Mstn −/− myotubes displayed an approximate 2-fold increase ( Fig. 3B ). These findings suggest that MSTN inhibition enhances protein synthesis in differentiating myotubes, and that FST overexpression, which blocks signaling of multiple TGF-β family members including MSTN, exerts a more pronounced effect than Mstn deletion. 4. Horse serum used for myoblast and myotube culture contains measurable levels of MSTN Given the use of horse serum in myoblast culture, we quantified MSTN concentrations in serum samples from multiple species, including mouse, goat, and horse. ELISA revealed MSTN levels in WT and Mstn +/− mouse serum that were consistent with our previous report,[ 15 ] whereas MSTN was undetectable in Mstn −/− serum, serving as a negative control ( Fig. 4 ). Notably, horse serum contained a measurable concentration of MSTN, raising the possibility that residual MSTN in horse serum may partially compensate for the genetic deletion of Mstn in vitro , potentially masking the full phenotype of Mstn −/− myotubes. In contrast, the effects observed in F66 myotubes overexpressing FST may reflect more complete inhibition of MSTN signaling, including that derived from the serum. DISCUSSION In this study, we investigated the effects of MSTN inhibition on in vitro myogenesis by comparing genetic deletion of Mstn and transgenic overexpression of FST, an endogenous MSTN inhibitor. Our findings demonstrate that FST overexpression induces a more pronounced enhancement of myotube maturation and protein synthesis than Mstn deletion. Furthermore, we highlight an important technical consideration: the presence of MSTN in serum-supplemented culture media, which may confound the interpretation of in vitro knockout models. We first observed that both Mstn and Fst were rapidly upregulated within the first 24 hr of differentiation, a critical period for myoblast fusion and early myotube formation. This expression pattern suggests that MSTN acts to restrict excessive fusion and growth, while the concurrent increase in FST may act as a compensatory feedback mechanism to counteract MSTN activity and allow differentiation to proceed. As differentiation progressed, Mstn expression declined sharply by 48 to 60 hr, coinciding with increased myotube maturation and hypertrophy. This pattern implies that downregulation of MSTN alleviates its inhibitory effect, thereby facilitating further growth and maturation. To functionally assess the impact of MSTN inhibition, we compared the phenotypes of myotubes derived from Mstn −/− and F66 mice. Both showed increased myotube thickness relative to WT controls, but the effects were significantly more pronounced in F66 myotubes, which also exhibited the highest expression of maturation markers. Similarly, global protein synthesis was elevated in both Mstn −/− and F66 myotubes, with F66 again showing a stronger effect. One likely explanation is that FST neutralizes not only MSTN but also other TGF-β superfamily ligands such as activin A, which has been reported to play a critical role in regulating muscle mass.[ 17 ] In our system, however, Inhba expression remained relatively low and did not significantly increase until later stages of differentiation. This suggests that, at least during early in vitro myogenesis, MSTN is the dominant inhibitory signal and FST’s effects are likely mediated primarily through MSTN antagonism. A notable technical consideration from our study is the influence of culture serum on phenotypic outcomes in in vitro knockout models. We found that horse serum contains a detectable level of MSTN, which may partially restore MSTN signaling in Mstn −/− myoblast cultures, thereby attenuating the full phenotypic consequences of Mstn deletion. In contrast, the enhanced effects observed in F66 myotubes likely reflect more complete inhibition of MSTN, including serum-derived MSTN, by FST overexpression. Although not directly tested here, similar considerations should be applied to myoblast proliferation assays, where serum concentrations are higher and MSTN-mediated effects may be more evident. These findings underscore the importance of validating the composition of serum supplements when interpreting data from in vitro knockout experiments, especially those involving secreted signaling proteins such as MSTN. Beyond technical considerations, our results provide important implications for MSTN-targeted therapeutic development. The enhanced anabolic response observed with FST overexpression suggests that FST-based strategies may achieve greater efficacy than MSTN blockade alone. However, because FST also inhibits other TGF-β family members, its broader activity raises safety concerns, as we have previously reported increased bone fractures in F66 mice.[ 13 ] Additionally, the presence of residual MSTN in culture serum may partially restore signaling in vitro , potentially leading to an underestimation of therapeutic potency. These results highlight the importance of carefully validating serum composition when using in vitro platforms for preclinical screening of MSTN-targeted agents. In conclusion, our study reveals a dynamic and stage-specific regulation of Mstn during myogenesis, characterized by early activation followed by suppression as differentiation progresses. We demonstrate that FST overexpression induces a stronger hypertrophic and anabolic response than Mstn deletion, likely due to broader inhibition of MSTN signaling from both endogenously produced and serum-derived sources. Notably, the presence of MSTN in serum-supplemented culture media may influence the phenotypic outcomes observed in in vitro knockout models. This factor should be carefully considered when designing experiments and interpreting results in MSTN-targeted studies.
