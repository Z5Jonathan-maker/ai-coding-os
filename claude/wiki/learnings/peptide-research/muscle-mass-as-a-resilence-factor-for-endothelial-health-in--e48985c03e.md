It is estimated that over 135 Million Americans exceed the upper limits of ideal body mass with over 30 million frankly obese with numbers projected to increase 1 . Obesity drives a number of subsequent morbidities including certain cancers, notably pancreatic cancer 2 , dementia 3 , bone and joint disorders 4 and sleep apnea 5 . It’s most prominent threat, however, is the induction of what can be broadly defined as metabolic disease. A cardinal feature of this disease is insulin resistance, the precursor state to Type 2 diabetes, which itself is the gateway to renal failure and cardiovascular morbidty 6 . The mechanistic links connecting obesity, metabolic disease and downstream complications are likely multifarious and remain incompletely understood. The desire for sustenance is among the most powerful of biological urges and the evolutionary funnel that has prioritized the drive to eat has been starvation, one of the greatest historical threats to life. Organisms have additionally evolved mechanisms to become more metabolically “thrifty” when food is scarce which counters efforts to lose weight through caloric restriction by becoming more energetically efficient 7 , 8 . These factors are considerable obstacles to resolving excess weight and despite broad and varied strategies for weight loss, relapse to historical dietary habits is frequent. Many regain more weight than they lost, a process known as “yo-yo dieting” which may ultimately do more harm than good 9 . The counterbalance of risk factors are resilience factors. A potential offset to increased fat mass is skeletal muscle. As the energetic engine for motion, muscle has a high basal metabolic rate and takes up glucose with no mechanism for releasing it back into the bloodstream. Moreover, muscle is heavily engaged in cross-talk with other organs through the exchange of metabolic factors and proteins known as myokines. Increased muscle mass protects against disease even in the absence of weight loss, a concept referred to in the vernacular as “Fit but Fat” 10 , 11 . Exploitation of the salutatory effects of skeletal muscle to curb excess adiposity or its negative consequences provides an approach to countering the impact of the obesity epidemic if it can be developed therapeutically. This is an important topic for the widely used weight loss drugs like Ozempic (semaglutide) which are very effective in reducing adiposity but at the cost of muscle mass, possibly limiting their full potential 12 , 13 . Herein, we will summarize the important role skeletal muscle mass plays in protecting both metabolic and vascular health in the context of obesity and advance the concept that body composition, as opposed to absolute weight, should be the target for optimal health. Obesity’s effects on vascular health in overview Obesity can start at any phase of life, but generally falls into two patterns: Lifetime obesity in which individuals are overweight as children and remain so for the duration of their lives or later life obesity in which life events reduce activity and lead to weight gain 14 . The two trends in obesity vary in the onset of metabolic and vascular symptoms and genetic susceptibility, but eventually converge on a common set of metabolic and cardiovascular variables. Obesity is associated with disruptions of metabolism and endocrine processes. These can include changes in thyroid, adrenal and sex hormone function 15 – 17 , but the most prevailing effect on metabolism is the diminution in insulin signaling. Resistance to insulin occurs in a number of tissue compartments such as the fat, liver, muscle and brain by mechanisms that vary by age, site and duration of obesity. Loss of insulin signaling in the vasculature has been reported in models of obesity and although genetic knockout of insulin receptors in endothelial cells can impact insulin resistance and obesity, the loss of endothelial insulin signaling does not appear to drive vascular dysfunction on its own. The most common readouts of insulin resistance are elevated glucose and dyslipidemia, primarily in the form of increased triglycerides. Exactly how obesity elicits these disturbances in metabolism is incompletely understood. Currently over 33,000 reviews on aspects of insulin resistance are cataloged in PubMed. In this review, we will focus on the intersection of muscle mass and vascular function in the context of obesity. The primary function of the cardiovascular system is to service metabolism, delivering nutrients and removing waste. As metabolism becomes deranged, the function of the cardiovascular system predictably also deteriorates and the most common cause of death and other morbidities in obesity is vascular dysfunction—infarctions of the heart and brain, kidney disease and cardiac failure. While alterations in innervation and smooth muscle function are contributory, wide agreement exists that the cardinal initiating event in the progression of obesity-related vascular disease is endothelial dysfunction. Endothelial dysfunction in obesity arises from several, likely interrelated mechanisms. These include persistent inflammation and adherence of immune cells, imbalances in vasodilator (reduced) and vasoconstrictor (increased) factors released by the endothelium and changes in the control of thrombosis 18 , 19 . Of these, one of the best studied is loss of perfusion through impaired endothelium-dependent vasodilation 20 . The endothelium controls perfusion by sensing factors such as flow and circulating ligands and integrates those signals into appropriate changes in vascular tone by communicating with the underlying vascular smooth muscle. The most prominent mechanism of communication is the production of nitric oxide, a vasoactive gas, in the endothelium that relaxes smooth muscle and promotes perfusion 21 . In this review, will focus on nitric-oxide mediated dilation as a key readout of obesity’s effects on vascular function. Since the declaration of the “obesity epidemic” in the late 1990’s 22 , 23 , hundreds of studies have explored the impact of obesity on endothelium-mediated vasodilation. The primary mechanism identified in the majority of these studies is the loss of nitric-oxide (NO) bioavailability 24 – 26 . In most studies, this does not reflect the loss of NO-producing capacity as eNOS and its ancillary signaling processes remain largely intact and in some studies, even augmented 27 – 29 . In contrast, the quenching of NO by oxidant radicals is widely supported in a number of studies, especially by the overexpression or over-activity of NADPH Oxidases (NOX). Deletion of NOX1 has been demonstrated to protect endothelial function despite persistent metabolic dysfunction 30 . Indeed, overexpression of NOX may explain the semi-paradoxical increase in eNOS seen in obesity as NOX-mediated loss of NO bioavailability has been shown to prevent feedback inhibition of NO on eNOS expression and activity, resulting in marked upregulation 31 . Links between metabolic function and vascular disease In a perfect world, obesity would be remedied through selective loss of unhealthy body fat without collateral loss of muscle or healthy fat. In the real world, weight loss is complicated by age, reduced activity levels and genetic predispositions and reductions in body mass are accompanied by less than optimal outcomes such as frailty. Moreover, the existence of populations of metabolically healthy obese (MHO) individuals suggests that the relationships between body fat, metabolic dysfunction and vascular disease are more complicated than simple binary relationships linking excess adipose with declining vascular health. Given the failure of many individuals to reestablish a lean body composition, breaking the links between metabolic dysfunction and vascular disease becomes paramount. Insulin resistance is the most common cause of metabolic dysfunction, and it shouldn’t be surprising that improvement in insulin signaling was an early attractive target to break the link between endothelial dysfunction and metabolic disease. Many early pharmacologic studies had positive outcomes demonstrating in both human and animal studies that first-line diabetic therapy with metformin improved vascular outcomes 32 , 33 . Later studies with PPAR agonists 34 and SGLT inhibitors 35 also produced positive results. However, the mechanisms driving these effects became difficult to interpret when it became apparent that many of these agents have direct effects as vasodilators or improve outcomes in individuals with normal metabolism, thus blurring the lines between effects on metabolism versus endothelial health 36 – 38 . Using knockout approaches, targeting pathways that restrain insulin signaling has also been fruitful. One of the most prominent pathways studies has been Protein Tyrosine Phosphatase 1B (PTP1B). PTP1B provides a potentially very active target as it restrains both insulin and leptin-mediated signal transduction, combining the potential for weight loss as well as improved insulin sensitivity via inhibition of a single pathway 39 , 40 . PTP1B expression or activity is elevated in obese humans 41 , 42 and its deletion in knockout mice prevented diet-induced obesity 43 . Ali et al crossed PTP1B null mice with leptin receptor mutant db/db mice to isolate the effects of insulin resistance and circumvent changes in body weight (loss of obesity) in diet-fed models. Obese mice lacking PTPT1B demonstrated improved insulin signaling, specifically in muscle and fat, and normalization of endothelium-dependent vasodilation. Loss of PTP1B in lean mice revealed that there were no off-target effects of PTP1B on the vasculature as determined by the lack of changes in endothelial function and in the synthesis of nitric oxide. Obese mice displayed high levels of NOX1 expression and oxidant stress that were absent in obese mice lacking PTP1B 44 . At the whole animal level, blood pressure was normalized to control levels with PTP1B deletion 45 . These findings also indicated that insulin resistance, specifically in muscle and fat, could be an upstream driver of vascular dysfunction. Normalization of insulin resistance manifests through two major changes in plasma variables: Resolution of dyslipidemia and improvement of glucose tolerance. In vitro studies administering free fatty acids resulted in reduced production of NO 46 , but in vivo mechanistic studies have proven more elusive in assigning a contributory role of plasma triglycerides to endothelial dysfunction. Fish oils are commonly used to manipulate fatty acid profile and improve endothelial dilation with no change in overall lipids 47 . Fenofibrates lower lipids generally making it difficult to ascribe improved endothelial function to specific lipids 48 . While many insulin-sensitizing interventions lower both glucose and lipids, pharmacologic or genetic tools that specifically resolve triglyceride dyslipidemia alone are lacking and most in vivo studies involving triglycerides are essentially correlative. Early endothelial dysfunction was observed in an AAV model of obesity absent a significant rise in circulating triglycerides 49 . In contrast, considerable evidence suggests that glucose can be a major cause of endothelial dysfunction. Application of glucose directly to endothelial cells causes an array of endothelial injuries but sustained hyperglycemia at levels that could injure endothelial cells directly is rare in human patients 50 , 51 . A more likely mechanism is the accumulation, over time, of glucose-modified substrates, referred to as Advanced Glycation End products or AGEs. In 1992, Neeper and co-workers described a receptor for AGEs (RAGE) that was later linked to impaired vasodilation, endothelial inflammation and accelerated atherosclerosis in metabolically compromised mice 52 . More recently, alternative AGE receptors have been identified including Galectin-3 (LGALS3), a member of β-galactoside-binding lectin family 53 . Padgett et al measured AGE receptors in the microvasculature of obese mice and found that while RAGE was increased measurably, LGALS3 showed marked and more significant increases in expression. Deletion of LGALS3 both globally and specifically in endothelial cells resolved endothelial dysfunction in the face of persistent hyperglycemia 54 . Pharmaceutical targeting of these pathways remains an important but unachieved objective in resolving obesity-related vascular dysfunction. Effects of muscle mass as a resilience factor in obesity The central role of glucose as an endothelial injurant predicts that targeting skeletal muscle, a primary site of glucose disposal, would mitigate the effects of metabolic dysfunction on cardiometabolic health. Loss of muscle mass is a common aspect of aging, with individuals losing on average ~1% of muscle per year after age 50 55 , 56 . Obese individuals, while often starting with higher absolute muscle mass when young, have an accelerated decline as they age and become less active resulting in a condition called sarcopenic obesity 57 . While a precise definition of sarcopenic obesity is lacking, it is widely agreed that the condition is increasing as the world population becomes older and more obese and was 20% or higher as early as 2015 58 . The impacts of low muscle mass on human health are considerable. The most prevalent of these is frailty, resulting in higher rates of falls, fractures and immobility leading to institutionalization. An emerging appreciation is evidence that sarcopenia accelerates metabolic disease, especially in obese individuals 59 . Obese individuals with low muscle mass are often in the highest quartiles of indices of glucose intolerance in population studies and present with the highest incidence of diabetic complications 60 . While commonly thought of as a disease of advanced age, a recent study from Kim and colleagues found protective effects of muscle mass in a population averaging 55 years of age, suggesting that benefits are afforded or lost even before the onset of frailty 61 . How muscle mass confers these benefits is a subject of considerable debate. One potential avenue is via cross-talk between muscle and other organs through signaling molecules known as myokines. The term myokine was defined by the first myokine to be discovered which was myostatin in 1997. Se-Jin Lee named this novel protein based on the Latin roots myo- (or muscle) and statin (or stop) based on the ability of myostatin to limit muscle growth. In support of this, deletion of myostatin increased muscle mass by 50–100% in mice 62 and was later to found to be a naturally occurring mutation in humans 63 , whippets 64 and Belgian blue cattle 65 . Deletion of myostatin was found to limit weight gain on a high fat diet 66 and skeletal muscle cross-talk was verified as a mechanism when deletion of myostatin in fat alone failed to produce a phenotype 67 . McPherson et al crossed the myostatin KO with genetically obese agouti mice and found that, even without weight loss, increased muscle mass was sufficient to reduce the extent of hyperglycemia 68 . The identification of myostatin opened the door to the discovery of additional myokines. One of the most prominent is interleukin-6 (IL-6), a pro-inflammatory cytokine produced in a number of tissues. IL-6 is thought to link muscle activity to changes in a number of target tissues, including the liver, adipocytes and bone 69 – 71 . While muscle is not the most prominent source of IL-6, as a tissue it displays the highest expression of the IL-6 receptor, making it somewhat difficult to discern how much of the benefits of muscle-derived IL-6 are direct autocrine effects of IL-6 versus indirect effects of Il-6 on muscle which in turn releases other factors which are causative such as changes in muscle insulin signaling or suppression/release of other myokines. Moreover, while IL-6 at one level may promote muscle hypertrophy, at high levels it promotes inflammation and atrophy reflecting a more complicated relationship 72 . Irisin was the second to be identified largely muscle restricted gene (excepting some expression in the cerebellum). Derived from the fibronectin gene, the unique signature of irisin is its ability to stimulate thermogenesis by “beiging” white adipose to a more brown adipocyte phenotype 73 . Infusion of irisin stimulates thermogenesis and weight loss 74 , 75 , while loss of irisin in knockout mice exacerbates insulin resistance on a high fat diet 76 , 77 . Myonectin, also known as erythroferrone, is a myokine thought to link muscle metabolism with lipid homeostasis, especially in the steatotic liver 78 . Deletion of myonectin impairs the ability to clear lipids from the blood stream but protects against liver steatosis 79 . An array of other myokines – decorin, BDNF, SPARC – have been identified in the last decade, but the primacy of muscle as the source of expression and relative effects on muscle and metabolism remain less than clear 80 . Cardioprotective effects of muscle mass in obesity While loss of muscle mass is known to predict metabolic health, it has only recently been examined as a predictor of cardiovascular health. Data from the NHANES data revealed that the combination of lower body fat and higher muscle mass was associated with more protection than lower body fat alone 61 . Moreover, the ATTICA study demonstrated that even in middle-aged populations, prior to the onset of evident cardiovascular disease, muscle mass predicted the later development of disease as an independent resilience factor 81 . While physical activity is most commonly associated with the preservation of muscle mass and beneficial effects on metabolism, similar relationships between muscle mass and metabolism can also be observed in sedentary individuals. Correlative relationships can be drawn between the relative degree of skeletal muscle, even in young persons, and stress responses such as elevated systolic blood pressure and pressure natriuresis that predict long term risk of hypertension 82 . Resistance training is the best-known and most effective way to increase muscle mass but many populations – the aged, the very obese or those with prior injuries – may not be able to exercise at a level that confers full benefits. Identification and exploitation of pathways activated by resistance training to confer health benefits in these populations would be of particular value but such remain incompletely understood. While the myokines identified to date have a myriad array of metabolic effects, the essential question in the context of obesity-related vascular disease is which among them reduces cardiovascular disease burden and by what mechanism. To date studies have examined the impact of myokines on cardiovascular function by loss and gain of function studies respectively via gene knockout and infusions of recombinant proteins. Interference with deleterious myokines have mainly focused on myostatin, either by deletion of the gene itself or by blocking myostatin signaling with the endogenous inhibitory protein, follistatin. The first studies assessing the impact of myostatin on the cardiovascular system used transgenic mice over-expressing follistatin and determined whether interference with myostatin signaling improved muscle regeneration and angiogenesis. Healing from a laceration injury was associated with higher capillary density with greater muscle regeneration which was presumed to accelerate recovery 83 . Studies in normal myostatin knockout mice generally suggest modest effects of hypermuscularity on vascular function such as mild increases in endothelium-dependent vasodilation or somewhat prolonged reactive hyperemia 84 , 85 , likely explained by the physical effects of larger muscle. Myostatin does not appear to have direct effects on blood vessels, at least when applied acutely 86 . In contrast, deletion of myostatin has potent effects in the context of disease states. Early studies in which myostatin knockout mice were crossed with LDL receptor knockout mice noted marked improvements in the extent of atherosclerotic lesions on a high fat diet. This reduction was associated with improved lipoprotein profiles, increased insulin sensitivity and reduced hepatic steatosis, but also lower body weight and fat mass, obscuring the mechanisms which could result from either changes in muscle mass or from weight loss 87 . While myostatin is not expressed in normal blood vessels at baseline, Verzola and co-workers observed the induction of myostatin in several cell types in atheromatous lesions and in A7r5 cells associated with SMC de-differentiation 88 . To date, the consequences of vascular-specific deletion of myostatin have not been explored and the extent to which these effects are impactful remains unknown. Assessing whether myostatin or any other myokine have direct effects on cells of the vascular wall remains an important consideration and needs to be included in consideration of experimentation models in which they are manipulated. More recently, the impact of myostatin deletion in genetic obesity have been examined, obviating the confounding effects of weight loss reported previously 66 . Using the db/db mouse model, Qiu and co-workers discovered that the development of sarcopenic obesity in this model was prevented by deletion of myostatin which occurred without loss of body weight or body fat. Preservation of muscle mass sustained insulin sensitivity, lowered HbA1c to non-diabetic levels and reduced the impact of obesity in endothelium dependent vasodilation in conjunction with reduced NOX1- mediated oxidant stress 86 . In vivo, deletion of myostatin prevented hypertension and protected the kidneys from glycemic injury 89 and limited hepatic steatosis 90 . Finally, deletion of myostatin prevented the induction of NOX and Galectin-3 in the endothelium of obese mice, suggested that preservation of muscle glucose metabolism is the mechanism of the effects of myostatin deletion 54 (Fig. 1 ). Other TGF-β superfamily members include the activins (activin A) and GDF11 which are not strictly myokines per se but have also been shown to promote muscle loss. They bind to the ACVR2A which is also the common receptor for myostatin 91 . Whether loss of activin A or GDF11 promote similar benefits to skeletal muscle and cardiovascular function in the context of obesity remains to be determined. Future directions Since its discovery, myostatin has become the most studied and best known of the myokines and its ability to restrain hypertrophy is unambiguous across a broad range of species. Despite this, myostatin has proven remarkably resistant to pharmacotherapy, producing modest results in adults 92 . It would therefore be important to understand how myostatin deletion achieves its physiologic benefits, so that more distal processes might be targeted. How the effects of myostatin deletion translate into cardiometabolic protection, however, remain unclear. Some studies document that myostatin may directly modify insulin signaling pathways 93 , 94 . Myostatin may alter the composition of muscle by changing its fiber type, notably increasing Type II fiber types 95 . Myostatin deletion may disinhibit the release of other myokines, such as irisin, which can secondarily improve metabolic function 96 . Finally, myostatin may prevent the deleterious effects of metabolic changes on muscle itself, such as limiting the degree of myosteatosis, an enrichment of muscle fibers with lipid thought to induce insulin resistance and hyperglycemia 97 . The inability of pharmacological strategies to target myostatin in adults may be due to the greater effects of myostatin during development where it restrains myogenesis. In adults, improved strategies that block the effects of multiple factors that negatively regulate skeletal muscle such as the soluble ACVR2A receptors and neutralizing antibodies have emerged. Much work is needed to fully unravel how these approaches, as well as the manipulation of other myokines, benefits cardiometabolic health, especially in the context of obesity, in populations at risk of sarcopenia and to assess the risk of GLP-1 agonists in otherwise healthy patients seeking simple weight loss.
