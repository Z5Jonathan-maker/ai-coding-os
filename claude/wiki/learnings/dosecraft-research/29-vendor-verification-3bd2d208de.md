# 29-vendor-verification

The 7 red flags that expose bunk peptide vendors. The green flags that identify legitimate sources. And how to read a Certificate of Analysis like a professional.

If BPC-157 does not work for you, you bought bunk. 100%. Quality sourcing is not optional — it is the foundation of every successful protocol.

Any one of these flags should trigger extreme caution. Two or more? Walk away. Your health and your money are at stake.

If a vendor cannot provide a COA from an independent lab for every batch, they are hiding something. An in-house "test result" is worthless — they are grading their own homework. Demand third-party verification.

Quality peptide synthesis, purification (HPLC), lyophilization, and testing costs money. If a vendor is selling BPC-157 at half the market rate, they are cutting corners — on purity, on testing, or on the peptide itself. You get what you pay for.

High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is the gold standard for verifying peptide identity and purity. Without it, you have no proof the vial contains what the label claims. Minimum acceptable purity: 98%.

Pre-mixed "BPC + TB-500 blend" vials prevent proper dosing control. You cannot adjust individual compound doses. Blends hide under-dosing and allow vendors to cut expensive compounds with cheap fillers. Individual peptides only.

A single COA from 2022 does not verify today's batch. Every production batch must be independently tested. Ask for the batch number on your vial and match it to a COA. If they cannot provide batch-specific results, the COA is theater.

Any vendor selling "oral BPC-157 capsules" or "sublingual TB-500" is either ignorant or dishonest. As Chapter 26 established: oral peptides do not achieve systemic bioavailability. A vendor making these claims does not understand their own products.

Endotoxins are bacterial cell wall fragments that cause fever, inflammation, and potentially sepsis when injected. The LAL (Limulus Amebocyte Lysate) test detects endotoxin contamination. Injectables MUST pass endotoxin testing. If a vendor does not test for endotoxins, you are playing Russian roulette.

A legitimate COA contains specific data points. Here is what to look for and what each means:

| COA Field | What It Means | What to Verify |
|---|---|---|
| Peptide Identity | Confirmed via mass spectrometry (MS) | Molecular weight matches known peptide |
| Purity (HPLC) | Percentage of target peptide vs impurities | Must be ≥ 98% for research grade |
| Appearance | Physical description of lyophilized product | White to off-white powder (most peptides) |
| Endotoxin (LAL) | Bacterial contamination level | < 0.5 EU/mg — must PASS |
| Sterility | Bacterial/fungal growth test | No growth detected |
| Batch / Lot Number | Unique identifier for this production run | Must match the number on YOUR vial |
| Testing Lab Name | Independent laboratory that performed analysis | Named, searchable, not "in-house" |

Expert If any of these fields are missing, the COA is incomplete. If the testing lab is not named or is the vendor themselves, the COA is unreliable.

HPLC separates the components of a sample by passing it through a column under high pressure. Each component moves through the column at a different speed based on its chemical properties, producing a distinct "peak" on a chromatogram. The target peptide's peak is measured against known standards to determine **purity percentage**.

**What it tells you:** What percentage of the vial contents is actually the peptide claimed on the label. Acceptable purity: ≥98% for research grade. Below 95% is substandard. Below 90% is essentially a different product contaminated with synthesis byproducts, truncated peptide fragments, and unreacted amino acids.

Mass spectrometry measures the **molecular weight** of the compound. Every peptide has a unique molecular weight determined by its amino acid sequence. MS confirms identity — proving the compound IS the peptide claimed, not a different peptide or chemical. Often combined with HPLC as "LC-MS" (liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry) for simultaneous purity + identity verification. If a COA shows HPLC but no MS, the purity number could technically belong to the wrong compound.
