# Merging T1D and T2D Management: Shared Strategies for Common Goals—Building on Early Lessons From GLP-1 Trials in T1D

The care of people with type 1 diabetes (T1D) has seen a seismic shift in recent years. Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) and automated insulin delivery (AID) are now the recommended standards of care in T1D ( 1 ), with clinical trial data showing their significant impact on reducing the burden of hypoglycemia and improving time in target glucose range (TIR). However, in clinical practice, reaching and maintaining HbA 1c &lt;7% or TIR &gt;70% remains difficult for many with T1D ( 2 ), putting these individuals at increased risk for cardiovascular disease (CVD), microvascular complications, and excess mortality ( 3 ). A consensus on how to implement known and novel management strategies to mitigate these risks in individuals with T1D is lacking ( 4 ). The world of type 2 diabetes (T2D) management has also undergone a transformation due to the introduction of glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1) and GLP-1/glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP) receptor agonists as well as sodium–glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors (SGLT2is) ( 5 ). These novel medications with pleiotropic effects have expanded T2D glycemic management algorithms ( 6 ) to include a major focus on CVD and chronic kidney disease risk reduction. To date, the cardiorenal benefits of the GLP-1 and SGLT2i therapies have not been applied broadly to people with T1D who are likely to benefit. This is largely because additional evidence is needed to safely extend the benefits of these two medication classes to people with T1D. Moreover, obesity and overweight, also addressed in recent T2D management algorithms, affect 62% of people living with T1D ( 7 ); however, until recently ( 8 ), there was no mention of weight management in T1D treatment guidelines. GLP-1 and SGLT2i therapies have the potential to reduce CVD and chronic kidney disease risk in individuals with overweight or obesity with either T1D or T2D by reducing hyperglycemia, inflammation, and the metabolic disorders associated with obesity ( 9 ), assuming additional studies confirm that new diabetic ketoacidosis risk mitigation strategies, particularly for SGLT2i users with T1D, can be safely implemented. New clinical trials also show that CGM and AID systems ( 10 ) may be the most effective tools to improve hyperglycemia (as measured by HbA 1c and TIR) in individuals who require insulin therapy regardless of the originating pathophysiology (e.g., insulin deficiency followed by insulin resistance, as in many with T1D, or insulin resistance followed by β-cell dysfunction, as in individuals with T2D). To this end, we are witnessing a merging of T1D and T2D management strategies, guided by the common diabetes care goals of improved glycemic management, cardiovascular, kidney, and metabolic risk reduction, and attaining a healthy body weight while also striving to optimize quality of life for all people with diabetes ( Fig. 1 ). Figure 1 The merging of T1D and T2D management strategies can facilitate the achievement of common diabetes care goals. The T1D path, informed by the Diabetes Control and Complications Trial (DCCT), is moving from HbA 1c and home glucose monitoring to utilizing CGM and AID systems for management, which is guided by analysis of the AGP report and 4T Study principles (teamwork, targets, technology, and tight control). The T2D path, informed by the UKPDS, is also using more CGM, but focuses mainly on the new medication classes of glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1), glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP)/GLP-1, receptor agonists, and SGLT2is. These medications were initially used for glycemic control, but after multiple CVOTs, the use expanded to include risk reduction for cardiovascular and kidney disease and weight loss. Both paths utilize medical nutrition therapy and behavioural interventions as part of lifestyle and decision support, along with cardiovascular risk factor management. Shared management strategies include the use GLP-1, GIP/GLP-1, SGLT2i medications, AID systems and digital tracking and management apps, along with ever increasing input from artificial intelligence. Common goals of care for people with T1D or T2D include optimizing glycemic management, defined by TIR or time in TITR along with TBR, preventing cardiovascular and kidney disease, obtaining a healthy body weight, and improved quality of life. The Adjunct Semaglutide Treatment in Type 1 Diabetes (ADJUST-T1D) randomized controlled study ( 11 ) showed that GLP-1, originally considered only a T2D or weight loss medication, significantly improved both glucose and weight management in individuals with T1D and obesity. In a post hoc analysis of this study, Karakus et al. ( 12 ) describe the impact of semaglutide therapy on the relationship between weight loss and insulin dose reduction in adults with T1D using AID. They report a 20–30% reduction in total daily dose of insulin and that this reduction was rapid and sustained, largely driven by reductions in carbohydrate-based insulin boluses (by 4 weeks, mean carbohydrate intake was reduced 28%, 137 g/day vs. 99 g/day, P &lt; 0.001) and a between-group weight loss difference of 8.8 kg. The ADJUST-T1D study and the analysis on insulin reduction are both limited by the small number of participants (36 per treatment arm) and by only including those using AID therapy. In addition, the semaglutide dose was limited to 1.0 mg/week, leaving unanswered the question of higher semaglutide doses such as those used for T2D diabetes management and weight loss that may result in an even more significant insulin reduction, potentially raising the risk for elevated ketone levels. A similar small (12 individuals per study arm) randomized controlled trial by Snaith et al. (Tirzepatide in Type 1 Diabetes: Cardiometabolic Effects [TIRTLE1] study) ( 13 ), using only 5.0 mg tirzepatide (dual GLP-1/GIP agonist) as adjunctive therapy in adults with T1D and BMI &gt;30 kg/m 2 , also demonstrated a rapid (2-week) reduction in total daily insulin with a 49% reduction in bolus insulin. This 12-week study found similar outcome improvements for those on AID versus multiple daily injections. Remarkably, 100% of participants in the tirzepatide arm lost 5% body weight and 45% lost &gt;10% body weight compared with 9% and 0%, respectively, in the placebo arm; this was an 8.7-kg greater weight loss for tirzepatide compared with placebo by study end. The rapid insulin dose reduction required in each of these studies highlights the need for a team-based care model and clinical workflow that allows for safe and timely patient monitoring. In addition, the insulin reductions were greater than would be expected based on the reported decreases in carbohydrate intake. This discrepancy could be explained by factors such as GLP-1– or GLP-1/GIP–induced changes in gastric emptying and/or insulin sensitivity, diet quality or type of carbohydrates consumed, or differences in carbohydrate counting accuracy that could change because of these medications. This highlights the need for future research (in both T1D and T2D) to enhance diet and lifestyle assessments, including more nuanced reporting of dietary intake and food-related behaviors ( 14 , 15 ) as well as ways to incorporate promotion of a healthy diet and lifestyle as the foundation of diabetes care, especially during GLP-1 or GLP-1/GIP use ( 16 ). While ADJUST-T1D and TIRTLE1 are small trials, they have important clinical implications. These trials reinforce the value of advancing a diabetes management road map that merges the key T1D and T2D management strategies that have evolved over the last 50 years and have seen the most transformative progress in the last decade ( Fig. 1 ). Now is the time to embrace a mentality of the best of both worlds, or, in this case, the best of each treatment pathway. The findings in the 1990s of metabolic memory in T1D and the legacy effect in T2D emphasize the need for much earlier management interventions in both T1D and T2D. Early interventions may entail using strategies from the 4T Study ( 17 ) that focus on team care, glycemic targets, advanced technology, and tight control at the onset of T1D or intensive lifestyle with targeted medications like GLP-1 or GLP-1/GIP, aimed at T2D remission or prevention. Another nod to merging therapeutic strategies is the growing evidence that CGM and GLP-1 therapies may be better together than when used alone ( 18 , 19 ). In addition to early interventions and combined therapies, continued research is needed to build a precision medicine management pathway (depicted as under construction in the figure), such as the efforts around immune modulation with teplizumab in T1D ( 20 ), preventing the progression of prediabetes ( 21 , 22 ), or metabolite-based T2D risk prediction ( 23 ). In the past, T1D management focused primarily on advancing technology to optimize and sustain glycemic control, while T2D management focused on new medications to address hyperglycemia, cardiovascular, kidney, and metabolic care, and weight loss; however, both pathways have always recognized the value of education with lifestyle and decision support to enhance and sustain the effectiveness of the core strategies. Looking ahead, work must continue toward a diabetes cure, and in the meantime, further merging of T1D and T2D treatment strategies could finally break down therapeutic inertia and help reach the common goals of care for all people with diabetes. This will require continued work on new care models, greater efforts toward equity of care, and personalizing the merged strategies to meet the needs of each person with diabetes ( 24 ).
