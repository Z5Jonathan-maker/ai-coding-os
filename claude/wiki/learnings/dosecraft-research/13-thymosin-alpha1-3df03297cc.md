The thymus gland — the body's immune training academy — begins shrinking after puberty and continues degrading throughout life. By age 50, the thymus has lost approximately 80% of its functional tissue, replaced by fatty deposits. This process, called thymic involution, is one of the primary reasons the immune system becomes both weaker and more error-prone with age.

| Age | Thymic Function | T-Cell Output | Immune Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Birth-Puberty | Peak function — full thymic mass | Maximum naive T-cell production | Robust immune education, rapid pathogen learning |
| 20-40 | Gradual decline — 1-3% mass loss/year | Declining but adequate | Beginning of immune memory reliance over new T-cell training |
| 40-60 | Significant atrophy — fatty replacement | Markedly reduced | Increased autoimmune errors, slower response to novel pathogens |
| 60+ | Severe involution — minimal functional tissue | Critically low | Immunosenescence: poor vaccine response, cancer susceptibility, chronic inflammation |

TA-1 does not reverse thymic involution structurally. Instead, it compensates for the thymus's declining output by providing the molecular signals that the shrinking thymus can no longer produce in adequate quantities — **functioning as a synthetic thymic output replacement**.
