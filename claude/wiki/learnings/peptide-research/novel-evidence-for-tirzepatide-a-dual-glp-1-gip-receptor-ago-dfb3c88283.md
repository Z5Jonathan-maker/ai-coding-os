# Novel evidence for tirzepatide, a dual GLP-1/GIP receptor agonist, to reduce the motivation for cocaine in rodents

The motivation to consume food is remarkably strong as it is necessary for survival, and the ability of GLP-1 receptor agonists to reduce such motivation in the absence of significant behavioural impairments is viewed as nothing short of incredible. 1 One “side-effect” reported by those on GLP-1 receptor agonists to treat obesity or insulin resistance is a reduction in motivation to seek addictive drugs, including alcohol. 2 , 3 Feeding behaviour is regulated by several peripheral and central signals involving multiple brain regions. The motivation to seek addictive drugs is regulated by similarly complex neurocircuitry utilising several neurotransmitter systems. The ability of a single peptide receptor—the GLP-1 receptor—to reduce the motivation to seek food and addictive drugs may be a “silver bullet” in the treatment of the complex disorders of obesity and substance use disorder (SUD). Neurons of the nucleus of the tractus solitarius release GLP throughout the brain, and GLP-1 receptors are found in several areas that regulate the motivation to seek and consume food and drugs, including the hippocampus, amygdala, bed nucleus of the stria terminalis, lateral septum, ventral tegmental area (VTA), and nucleus accumbens; however, GLP-1 agonists do not cross the blood brain barrier and instead are thought to act on the brain via circumventricular organs and by direct action in several select sites adjacent to the ventricles. 4 , 5 Tirzepatide (Zepbound/Mounjaro) is a dual agonist at GLP-1 and glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP) receptors. The recent paper by Edvardsson and colleagues 6 published in eBioMedicine describes a set of studies that comprehensively examine the effects of tirzepatide on cocaine-related behaviours in rodents, finding that tirzepatide reduces cocaine intravenous self-administration, the motivation to seek cocaine when the effort required for an infusion is high, the reinstatement of cocaine-seeking after a period of cocaine abstinence, and cocaine reward. Additionally, the expression of cocaine-induced locomotor sensitisation was blocked by tirzepatide, which can be interpreted as a reduction in the progressive increases in cocaine's effects on hyperactivity. A strength of the study is that multiple aspects of cocaine seeking were assessed in rodents that translate to the human experience—the motivation to seek drug when the effort required to get it is increased, relapse after a period of abstinence, and the seeking of environments that were paired with drug effects. These behavioural effects were accompanied by decreased cocaine-evoked dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens. These data support and extend preclinical findings on this topic that tested other GLP-1 receptor agonists, including exendin-4 and semaglutide, for their ability to reduce cocaine seeking, 7 , 8 and indicate that targeting both GLP-1 and GIP receptors may provide a benefit over targeting GLP-1 alone. Thus, to date, a cohesive body of evidence exists that GLP-1 receptor agonists can reduce cocaine reward and seeking in rodents. Currently, anecdotal evidence exists for the ability of GLP-1 receptor agonists to reduce the motivation to consume drugs in the clinical population prescribed GLP-1 agonists for obesity and diabetes, and some off-label prescriptions for this purpose are likely occurring. Based on this promising work in rodents, a clinical trial for the GLP-1 receptor agonist semaglutide to treat cocaine use disorder is in progress ( NCT07227948 ). Tirzepatide represents a related, but distinct treatment as it also targets GIP, and should be considered for clinical trial for cocaine use disorder in humans. The key takeaway is that GLP-1 and GIP receptor agonism reduces the motivation to seek cocaine in male rodents and these effects are likely related to a reduction in cocaine-evoked dopamine release in a key brain region for reward and reinforcement—the nucleus accumbens. Unanswered questions include whether the same results would be observed in female rodents, which have a distinct but overlapping neurobiology governing cocaine-seeking with male rodents. The present manuscript established that tirzepatide does not cause general sedation or nausea in rats, indicating that its effects are specific to motivated behaviour, a significant strength. However, one significant concern is that the doses of tirzepatide that reduced cocaine-seeking also reduced food-seeking. As unwanted weight loss in persons with SUD could further worsen health outcomes, additional work to identify SUD-specific neurobiological mechanisms of GLP-1/GIP agonist actions that spare appetite and weight loss should be a priority.
