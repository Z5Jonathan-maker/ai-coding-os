Stroke remains a leading cause of global disability, perpetuated by maladaptive neuroinflammation that drives secondary injury and impairs recovery. An early reparative (M2) state rapidly transitions into a dominant destructive (M1) phenotype within days, worsening tissue damage through the release of cytokines [tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNFα), interleukin-6 (IL-6)], blood–brain barrier disruption, and amplified peripheral immune cell infiltration. Emerging pharmacological interventions, such as the free radical scavenger edaravone, the neurotrophic factor cerebrolysin, and the excitotoxicity modulator citicoline, demonstrate promising neuroprotective potential when strategically timed. Additionally, novel non-pharmacological approaches, including repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation, stem cell therapy, and nanoparticle-based drug delivery, offer innovative pathways for targeting neuroinflammation. However, translational challenges persist, including narrow therapeutic windows, biomarker heterogeneity, and preclinical-to-clinical gaps. Future progress necessitates precision medicine paradigms integrating spatiotemporal drug delivery, biomarker-guided intervention timing, and synergistic combinatorial regimens targeting acute injury and chronic repair phases. By bridging mechanistic insights with clinical applications, this review delineates neuroinflammatory modulation as a pivotal frontier for redefining stroke recovery while outlining essential research trajectories to overcome existing barriers. Systematic search of electronic databases including PubMed, Web of Science, Embase, and Cochrane (1996–2025) was performed, with eligible studies assessed using PRISMA guidelines. Findings on neuroinflammation, mechanism, or interventions in ischemic stroke were narratively synthesized through thematic analysis. This review summarizes current insights into post-stroke neuroinflammatory mechanisms, with a focus on the dual role of microglial polarization. Introduction Stroke is a leading global cause of death and disability, with 11.9 million new cases and 93.8 million prevalent cases in 2021 [ 1 ]. Ischemic stroke, representing 65.3% of cases, is driven primarily by modifiable risk factors including hypertension, obesity, smoking, and atrial fibrillation [ 1 – 4 ]. Current therapeutic paradigms, which are restricted to time-sensitive reperfusion through intravenous tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) and mechanical thrombectomy, fail to address the secondary neuroinflammatory cascade that perpetuates neuronal injury. With the global economic toll of stroke projected to double from the current US$890 billion by 2050 (0.66% of global GDP), the imperative to shift from reactive reperfusion to proactive neuroprotection has never been more pressing [ 2 ]. Post-stroke neuroinflammation involves dynamic cross talk between ischemia and immune activation. Hypoxia activates microglia, the central nervous system (CNS)’s primary immune sentinels, and within days, the activated microglia release predominantly pro-inflammatory cytokines, such as interleukin (IL)-1β and tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNFα), as well as other mediators, including matrix metalloproteinases and reactive oxygen species. This disrupts the blood–brain barrier (BBB) integrity, facilitating the infiltration of peripheral immune cells, including neutrophils, monocytes, and T lymphocytes, which in turn amplifies cerebral inflammation [ 3 ]. While acute inflammation aids repair, chronic neuroinflammation drives injury through sustained microglial activation and oxidative stress. The translational promise of targeting neuroinflammation in stroke lies in its dual capacity to exacerbate injury and facilitate repair. Success requires selective inhibition of detrimental pathways without disrupting reparative functions. This review examines key mechanisms, cellular contributors, and temporal dynamics of stroke neuroinflammation. It also evaluates emerging anti-inflammatory strategies, from pharmacological management to targeted delivery systems. By bridging preclinical insights and clinical applications, this review aims to identify challenges and future directions, emphasizing the need for innovative approaches to improve stroke outcomes. Methods Systematic searches of electronic databases (PubMed/MEDLINE, Web of Science, Embase, Cochrane) for studies published between January 1996 and July 2025 were conducted using keywords including neuroinflammation, ischemic stroke, microglia, neuromodulation, oxidative stress, and blood–brain barrier permeability. Included studies were original research, reviews, meta-analyses, and clinical trials focusing on neuroinflammatory mechanisms or interventions in ischemic stroke, while excluding publications on hemorrhagic stroke or those without primary data. Studies were assessed on the basis of the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis (PRISMA) guidelines. Data on study characteristics, interventions, and outcomes were extracted and synthesized narratively using a thematic analysis approach to identify key patterns and insights. This article is based on previously conducted studies and does not contain any new studies with human participants or animals performed by any of the authors. Mechanisms of Neuroinflammation in Stroke Temporal Dynamics in Microglial Activation Microglia are the central mediators of post-stroke inflammation. Microglial polarization into the M1, pro-inflammatory phenotype and the M2, reparative phenotype critically shapes the inflammatory response following ischemic stroke [ 5 ]. While initially adopting a protective, tissue-reparative M2 phenotype, microglia within peri-infarct regions shift rapidly towards a dominant pro-inflammatory M1 state within days. This M1 polarization persists predominantly through the subacute and chronic phases, driving secondary brain injury. Although the transient M2 response represents an endogenous effort to limit damage and clear debris, it typically subsides within 7 days and remains insufficient thereafter [ 6 ]. While the inflammatory responses from M1 may help contain the injury, prolonged neuroinflammation can worsen outcomes by exacerbating tissue injury and impeding recovery [ 7 ]. Polarization of the M1 and M2 Microglia The polarization of microglia into pro-inflammatory (M1) or anti-inflammatory (M2) phenotypes is governed by distinct molecular mechanisms that influence neuroinflammatory outcomes following stroke. During the acute phase, mediators such as IL-4, released from damaged neural tissue, promote the neuroprotective M2 phenotype [ 8 ]. Primarily, this response mitigates secondary injury through the phagocytic clearance of necrotic neurons and myelin [ 9 ] and the secretion of anti-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-10 to attenuate the inflammatory response [ 10 ]. Furthermore, M2 releases growth factors, including the brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), to enhance synaptic plasticity, by enhancement of glutamatergic transmission and regulation of proteins like synaptophysin [ 11 , 12 ]. Collectively, these actions help preserve the peri-infarct region and limit ischemic injury progression. However, this beneficial M2 polarization is short-lived, and a shift towards the M1 phenotype predominates during the subacute and chronic phases. This transition is triggered by damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), such as the high-mobility group box 1 (HMGB1), which bind to microglial pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) such as Toll-like receptors 2 and 4 (TLR2/4), activating the MAL/NF-κB pathway driving pro-inflammatory cytokine release [ 13 ]. DAMP–PRR interaction activates the MAL-dependent (MyD88 adaptor-like protein) nuclear factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells (NF-κB) pathway, leading to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNFα, IL-6) [ 14 , 15 ]. Consequently, these cytokines prime circulating neutrophils, triggering the release of neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs) that occlude microvessels, causing secondary thrombosis and extended ischemic penumbra. Additionally, primed neutrophils release reactive oxygen species (ROS) and proteases, which exacerbate parenchymal damage. Simultaneously, NF-κB activation produces matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) [ 16 ], degrading the BBB to further enhance immune cell infiltration. Notably, M1 activation also directly generates high levels of ROS, inducing mitochondrial fragmentation and dysfunction, amplifying neuronal damage [ 17 ]. Finally, activated M1 microglia upregulate major histocompatibility complex (MHC2) molecules and co-stimulatory molecules (CD80/86), activating adaptive immunity through T cell recruitment, thus perpetuating inflammation [ 18 ]. Thus, the M1/M2 equilibrium represents a pivotal therapeutic target for mitigating post-stroke injury, with microglia adopting heterogeneous states with overlapping pro- and anti-inflammatory features (Fig. 1 ). They accumulate in the perivascular regions near the infarct, thus playing a crucial role in the process of hemorrhagic transformation [ 19 , 20 ]. Post-stroke BBB disruption permits extravasation of vitronectin and other circulating extracellular matrix proteins into the brain parenchyma, further activating microglia. Once inside, it acts as a potent stimulus for microglial activation, leading to the production of pro-MMP9 through activation of the NF-κB pathway. Both MMP2 and MMP9 specifically target the tight junction proteins integral to BBB integrity, notably occludin, cleaving them into smaller fragments. This proteolytic effect exhibits dual pathological effects. The direct loss of occludin compromises BBB structural integrity, while the resultant fragments (particularly the 55-kDa fragment of occludin) exhibit cytotoxic properties that trigger endothelial cell apoptosis, further destabilizing the endothelial monolayer and enhancing its permeability [ 21 ]. Additionally, microglia-derived MMP9 acts synergistically with neutrophil-produced MMP9, establishing a feed-forward loop that perpetuates BBB disruption through type IV collagen degradation, thus leading to microvascular compromise [ 22 ]. This cascade of BBB breakdown ultimately exacerbates stroke pathology by facilitating the infiltration of peripheral immune cells, leading to increased cerebral edema and secondary tissue damage [ 23 ] (Fig. 2 ). Microglial-derived cytokines prime circulating neutrophils, upregulating adhesion molecules such as ICAM-1 on endothelial cells of the BBB and creating a chemotactic gradient that directs neutrophils to the site of injury [ 24 ]. Upon activation, neutrophils execute several critical effector functions. They produce ROS through the NADPH oxidase 2 (NOX2) complex, exacerbating brain injury in the acute phase while controlled ROS production may facilitate repair mechanisms in the later phases [ 25 ]. This production of ROS is not exclusive to neutrophils; microglial cells can also generate ROS, contributing to the overall oxidative stress in the ischemic environment. Additionally, neutrophils release MMP9, which compromises the integrity of the BBB [ 26 ]. Similar to ROS production, MMP9 can also be produced by microglia, further impacting BBB integrity. A unique form of cell death known as NETosis occurs in neutrophils, resulting in the formation of neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs) composed of extracellular DNA for inflammation. These NETs contribute to microvascular occlusion and secondary thrombosis through platelet–neutrophil interaction [ 27 ] (Fig. 2 ). Neutrophils also further secrete pro-inflammatory mediators IL-1β and TNFα activate microglia, amplifying a positive feedback loop and exacerbating tissue damage in the ischemic brain [ 28 ]. Overall, neutrophils emerge as critical mediators in the complex inflammatory response following stroke, sharing several functional roles with microglia. T Cells In ischemic stroke, M1 microglia play a crucial role in initiating and perpetuating neuroinflammation by activating CD4 + T cells through antigen presentation. This process involves two distinct signals. The first signal involves TLR stimulation, which upregulates the expression of MHC class II, which enables microglia to present myelin antigens to T cell receptors (TCRs) on CD4 + T cells [ 29 ]. The second signal is provided by the binding of CD28 and B7 on T cells, which delivers essential co-stimulation [ 30 ]. Without this second signal, T cells become anergic rather than activated. When both signals are present, CD4 + T cells become fully activated and readily infiltrate the CNS. This is facilitated by the disruption of the BBB, a common consequence of ischemic stroke. Among activated CD4 + T cells, Th1 cells dominate early responses by secreting interferon-γ (IFNγ), a key cytokine that primes macrophages toward the pro-inflammatory (M1) phenotype [ 31 ]. Through STAT1 phosphorylation mediated by JAK1/2, IFNγ activates the NLRP3 inflammasome in microglia, driving M1 polarization and the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-1β and IL-18 [ 32 ]. In contrast, Th17 cells aggravate neuroinflammation via interleukin-17 (IL-17), which promotes neutrophil recruitment, BBB disruption, and heightened vascular permeability, thereby amplifying the inflammatory cascade [ 33 ] (Fig. 2 ). Production of Reactive Oxygen Species During cerebral ischemia, excessive ROS are generated from several sources, causing neuronal injury through oxidative stress. Activated microglia are the main contributors, producing superoxide via upregulated NADPH oxidase enzymes such as NOX2 [ 34 ]. Infiltrating neutrophils add to this burden through their own NADPH oxidase activity, while stressed neurons release ROS from dysfunctional mitochondrial electron transport chains. Accumulated ROS triggers multiple harmful effects. It damages proteins, lipids, and DNA within mitochondria, leading to impaired energy production and loss of membrane potential. This disruption opens the mitochondrial permeability transition pore (MPTP), which further amplifies ROS generation. Mitochondrial DNA released into the cytoplasm then activates the NLRP3 inflammasome, driving inflammation and pyroptotic cell death. ROS also weakens the BBB, enabling leukocyte infiltration during the early stages of ischemia [ 25 ]. In addition, lipid peroxidation, protein oxidation, enzyme inactivation, and DNA breaks directly injure cells [ 35 ]. Together, these oxidative processes intensify the initial ischemic insult (Fig. 2 ). Stroke Neuroinflammatory Modulation Therapeutics Pharmacological Strategies Edaravone Edaravone is a low-molecular-weight, lipophilic compound that effectively mitigates neuroinflammation and oxidative stress in ischemic stroke. Its unique chemical structure allows it to exist in both neutral and anionic forms, with this dual reactivity enabling efficient BBB penetration to facilitate the rapid scavenging of hydroxyl radicals, peroxynitrite, and other reactive nitrogen species [ 36 , 37 ]. By reducing cytotoxic free radicals, edaravone suppresses NF-κB activation and inflammasome formation, thus leading to further preservation of the BBB. Preclinical studies substantiate these mechanisms: rodent models demonstrate significant reductions in infarct volume and improved neurological outcomes following edaravone administration [ 38 ]. Clinically, the TASTE trial (Treatment of Acute Ischemic Stroke with Edaravone) revealed that intravenous infusion of 15 ml edaravone-dexborneol concentrate twice daily conferred synergistic benefits, with patients exhibiting significantly improved functional outcomes as measured by the modified Rankin Scale (mRS) and National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale (NIHSS) scores. Sublingual edaravone formulations may accelerate systemic absorption with detectable clinical outcomes by day 14 post stroke [ 39 ]. Consequently, edaravone is now integrated into ischemic stroke guidelines in Japan, recommending intravenous administration (30 mg twice daily) within 24–48 h of symptom onset for 14 days [ 39 ]. Similarly, the Chinese Stroke Association endorses this regimen as a neuroprotective intervention for acute ischemic stroke [ 40 ]. Ongoing clinical trials are evaluating edaravone’s efficacy alongside reperfusion therapies, including endovascular hypothermia, mechanical thrombectomy, highlighting its potential for broader integration into stroke treatment protocols [ 41 ] (Table 1 ). Key agents such as edaravone, cerebrolysin, citicoline, and nerinetide show varied benefits in acute stroke and neuroprotection, while non-pharmacological approaches—including repetitive TMS, stem-cell therapy, and nanoparticles—demonstrate promise for functional recovery and neuroinflammatory modulation across preclinical and clinical stages Cerebrolysin Cerebrolysin, a peptide-based neurotrophic preparation, mitigates post-stroke neuroinflammation by mimicking endogenous neurotrophic factors including BDNF, nerve growth factor (NGF), and ciliary neurotrophic factor (CNTF) [ 42 ]. These factors enhance neuronal survival, cerebral plasticity, and synaptogenesis while critically shifting microglial from pro-inflammatory M1 to protective, tissue repair M2 phenotypes. Under cerebrolysin stimulation, M2 microglia and astrocytes further amplify BDNF production creating a self-amplifying restorative cycle [ 43 ]. Furthermore, cerebrolysin phosphorylates the cAMP response element binding protein (CREB), driving restorative pathways such as the sonic hedgehog (Shh) pathway and PI3K/Akt signaling, further increasing M2 signaling [ 44 , 45 ]. Clinical evidence supports the efficacy of cerebrolysin in stroke therapy. The CARS trial demonstrated improvement in upper extremity motor function when cerebrolysin was combined with standard rehabilitation in patients with stroke [ 46 ]. When cerebrolysin was combined with mechanical thrombectomy within 8 h of anterior circulation stroke, cerebrolysin improved NIHSS score and mortality rates, with the greatest effects observed in the first week post stroke [ 47 ]. Similarly, another trial confirmed that combination therapy of cerebrolysin with another reperfusion therapy, recombinant tPA, was clinically effective to stabilize BBB permeability and to improve functional outcomes [ 48 ]. A recent ESCAS study reported improved post-stroke verbal outcomes when cerebrolysin was paired with speech therapy [ 49 ]. Given the robust clinical evidence, the European Academy of Neurological (EAN) guidelines strongly recommend cerebrolysin as an add-on therapy to standard rehabilitation within the first 7 days after stroke, at a dose of 30 ml/ day intravenously for a minimum of 10 days [ 50 ] (Table 1 ). Citicoline Citicoline, an endogenous nucleotide, modulates post-stroke neuroinflammation through two key mechanisms. First, it serves as a phosphatidylcholine precursor, enhancing neuronal membrane repair and stability, which reduces secondary injury caused by fatty acid release [ 51 ]. Secondly, it directly suppresses neuroinflammatory cascades by inhibiting phospholipase A 2 activity. This induces deacetylation of TP53, minimizing downstream neuroinflammatory and p53-dependent apoptosis [ 52 ]. NF-κB activation is also reduced, downregulating DAMP molecular production, thus reducing MMP9 production, helping to preserve the integrity of the brain parenchyma’s extracellular matrix [ 53 ]. In the ICTUS (International Citicoline Trial on Acute Stroke), a 3-day treatment regimen (1 g every 12 h) followed by a 6-week period of 500-mg tablets reveals no clear therapeutic benefits, but subgroup analysis showed improved mortality and functional outcome in certain patient populations [ 54 ]. Particularly, older patients above 70 years and those with milder strokes (NIHSS &lt; 14) showed trends towards improvement [ 55 ]. A potential explanation for this observation is due to fundamental differences in brain response to ischemic injury with ageing. Normal ageing processes modify the tight junction proteins, increasing BBB permeability and also decreasing cerebral blood flow and oxygen consumption [ 56 ]. Such vulnerability may then be more specifically targeted by citicoline, explaining the better overall clinical results in older patients. Overall, results of the ICTUS trial were likely confounded by heterogeneity in stroke severity in the treatment group, together with the lack of penumbra imaging for patient selection. A combined therapy shows promise, with greater motor and cognitive parameter improvement, but future studies will be needed to expand on the results of the ICTUS trial to explore optimal patient population and dosage regimen that maximizes the benefit of citicoline. Better patient stratification strategies through stroke severity assessment and imaging features may help reveal the therapeutic effects of citoline (Table 1 ). Nerinetide Nerinetide is another potentially neuroprotective agent with a high therapeutic potential. On the basis of preclinical studies, nerinetide can reduce infarct volume and improve neurobehavioral outcome within animal middle cerebral artery occlusion models [ 56 ]. Through uncoupling postsynaptic density protein 95 (PSD-95), a key scaffolding protein for NMDA receptors, this inhibits the production of neuronal nitric oxide synthase (nNOS), which limits the conversion of nitric oxide and superoxide to peroxynitrite (ONOO − ) and reduces NF-κB inflammatory pathway activation [ 56 , 57 ]. In clinical trials, nerinetide has shown mixed results, with the greatest benefit shown when initiated within 3 h of symptom onset [ 58 ]. However, the ESCAPE-NA1 trial has revealed an important drug interaction between nerinetide and thrombolytics, which reduces the efficacy of neuroprotective treatment [ 58 ]. Activation of plasminogen to plasmin mediated by thrombolytic agents also cleaves nerinetide’s C-terminal lysine residue, reducing the effective plasma concentration and therapeutic effect [ 59 ]. The drug’s rapid pharmacokinetics ( T max = 10 min) further underscores the importance of early administration. This explains why subgroup analysis only found benefit in patients who had avoided thrombolytic therapy in the ESCAPE-NA1 trial (7.5% mortality reduction) [ 59 ]. Conflicting results between clinical and preclinical studies have limited the recommendation of nerinetide in clinical guidelines. Ongoing research aims to identify potential population subgroups and compatible reperfusion protocols, such as a potential plasmin-resistant analogue or non-thrombolytic regimens (Table 1 ). Non-pharmacological Strategies Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation In addition to the use of pharmacological agents in modulating neuroinflammation, repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) has emerged as a promising neuromodulatory therapy for ischemic stroke. Large-scale meta-analyses have demonstrated that rTMS improves cortical function such as upper limb motor control, language, cognition and visuospatial abilities [ 60 ]. Many different forms of rTMS exist, including high frequency, low frequency, and intermittent theta burst stimulation. While the specifics of these methods vary, the precise distinction in therapeutic effectiveness remains unclear. In animal models, rTMS alters neuronal membrane potential to reduce TLR/NF-κB/NLRP3 signaling pathway activation. This results in a reduction in TNFα, IL-1β, and IL-6 levels while promoting the production of anti-inflammatory cytokines [ 61 ]. These cytokine changes suppress microglial activation factors such as Iba1 and astrocyte marker glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP), inhibiting M1 polarization while promoting M2 microglia formation [ 62 ]. Notably, low-frequency rTMS applied to the intact primary motor cortex has achieved level A evidence for the improvement of upper limb paralysis within the subacute phase of stroke, while direct high-frequency stimulation of the affected motor cortex is also showing increasingly promising results [ 63 ]. Other than improvement in motor function, impairments in dysphagia, aphasia, and cognitive dysfunction may also be improved by TMS therapy [ 64 ] (Table 1 ). Stem-Cell Therapy Stem cell-based therapy has appeared to be a promising treatment approach for the modulation of stroke outcomes. Two main branches of stem cell therapy exist, autologous or allogenic stem cell therapy, with the two main cell lineages investigated being mesenchymal stem cells (MSC) and bone marrow stem cells (BMSC). Through paracrine signaling, secretion of a wide range of neurotrophic factors, including BDNF, NGF, and TGFβ, contributes to shifting the polarization of microglia from an M1 phenotype to an M2 phenotype [ 65 ]. In the TREASURE clinical trial, MultiStem, an allogenic bone marrow-derived multipotent adult progenitor cell therapy, was administered within 18–36 h post ischemic stroke; clinical outcome improvement was observed in population groups with larger ischemic core volume (≥ 50 ml) and younger patients (≤ 64 years old) [ 66 ]. Another small-scale clinical trial explored the use of SB623, another form of allogenic stem cell implanted by stereotactic technique [ 67 ]. Although the sample size was small, highly promising clinical results were seen with global improvement across stroke outcomes measured on the basis of the European Stroke Scale, NIHSS score, and motor function scores, with these improvements sustained up to 12 months post implantation [ 67 ]. Such studies point to a promising future with stem cell therapies potentially offering more sustained and long-term benefits. Currently, there are many ongoing longer-term trials, with animal studies looking at modified forms of stem cell therapy to improve therapeutic potential for stem cell therapies [ 68 ] (Table 1 ). Nanoparticles One of the key therapeutic challenges in the treatment of ischemic stroke is drug availability within the brain parenchyma as a result of the BBB. Nanoparticles have been investigated in multiple preclinical studies as a promising mode of drug delivery to improve therapeutic effect, while also minimizing systemic side effects. Potential neuroprotective effects of nanoparticles have been explored through mechanisms such as oxygen carriers, reduction of excitotoxicity, or direct delivery of antioxidant agents [ 69 ]. To improve oxygenation of oxygen-deprived penumbra, hemoglobin-encapsulated nanoparticles have been engineered to improve tissue oxygenation. Nanoparticle encapsulation reduces the natural toxicity of hemoglobin and extends its half-life [ 70 ]. NMDA receptor-inhibiting nanoparticles have also been created with modified peptides that recognize brain parenchyma tissue and are combined with glutamate receptor antagonist or calcium channel blocker to enhance therapeutic effect [ 71 ]. Although preclinical trials have shown potentially promising results, many early-phase clinical trials have failed to show effective translation of these benefits. Despite preclinical promise, early phase clinical trials have yet to replicate these benefits. Optimizing nanoparticle delivery methods and refining patient selection criteria may be necessary to unlock their full therapeutic potential (Table 1 ). Discussion Post-stroke neuroinflammation rapidly shifts from a transient reparative (M2) microglial state towards a destructive (M1)-dominant phenotype within 3–7 days, effectively eclipsing early protective mechanisms [ 6 , 72 ]. Consequently, a central therapeutic strategy involves sustaining M2-mediated repair while suppressing M1 polarization, leveraging this critical temporal window for intervention [ 72 ]. Yet, a fundamental challenge arises from the dual roles of key inflammatory pathways involved, where inhibiting the same signal can produce opposing effects. For example, HMGB1 drives early M1 polarization but later supports tissue repair, making timing-specific therapeutic targeting currently unachievable in clinical practice [ 73 ]. In the current review, the therapeutics discussed primarily target downstream mediators of M1-driven neuroinflammation or enhance M2-mediated repair. Edaravone preserves BBB integrity, but its neuroprotective window is limited to the first 48 h post stroke, preceding M1 microglial dominance [ 74 ]. Cerebrolysin prolongs the repairing phase by sustaining M2 activity, though its evidence in severe stroke cases remains limited [ 75 ]. Citicoline stabilized neuronal membranes and extracellular matrix integrity, yet its benefits appear confined to older patients with milder strokes, relegating it to an adjunctive role [ 54 ]. Finally, nerinetide attenuates excitotoxicity through PSD-95 uncoupling, but its therapeutic potential is curtailed by pharmacokinetic interactions with tPA, restricting use to thrombolysis-ineligible patients [ 58 ]. Non-pharmacological approaches face distinctive challenges. rTMS modulates microglial polarization noninvasively, but optimal stimulation parameters remain undefined. Stem cell therapy and nanoparticle-based delivery show preclinical promise, but optimal stimulation parameters remain undefined. Stem cell therapy and nanoparticle-based delivery show preclinical promise, yet translational hurdles concerning delivery, precision, and scalability impede clinical adoption [ 76 , 77 ]. Despite these limitations, neuroinflammatory modulation remains a promising avenue for improving post-stroke outcomes. Advancements in this area may rely on several key aspects. First, novel delivery routes, such as intranasal administration or advanced nanoparticles, could enhance CNS bioavailability while minimizing systemic dissemination [ 77 ]. Secondly, further research should focus more specifically on the phase-specific characteristics of biomarkers such as HMGB1 to optimize the therapeutic window of each patient. Furthermore, combination regimens targeting multiple facets of the neuroinflammatory cascade may yield enhanced clinical efficacy. In this regard, the gut–brain axis has emerged as a critical modulator of CNS inflammation following stroke. Gut microbiome dysbiosis and associated inflammatory signaling have been shown to compromise microglia activation and exacerbate tissue damage [ 78 ]. Thus, leveraging interventions to restore gut homeostasis may therefore provide a complementary strategy to optimize post-stroke neuroinflammatory management and improve overall recovery. Conclusion The dynamic polarization of microglial phenotypes, shifting from a reparative M2 state to a destructive M1 state, represents a critical therapeutic window to mitigate neuronal injury and improve long-term recovery post stroke [ 6 , 72 ]. This review has synthesized the key mechanisms driving this deleterious transition, including DAMP release, dysregulated IFNγ signaling, ROS cascades, and MMP-mediated disruption [ 79 , 80 ]. Encouragingly, emerging pharmacological and non-pharmacological strategies offer promising avenues for modulating post-stroke neuroinflammation. Acute administration of ROS scavengers like edaravone, as well as interventions promoting a sustained M2 microglial phenotype (such as cerebrolysin), have demonstrated therapeutic potential when optimally timed. Additionally, novel approaches such as rTMS for microglial reprogramming, stem cell therapies for paracrine immunomodulation, and nanoparticle-based drug delivery systems represent innovative pathways for targeting neuroinflammation. However, significant barriers remain, including temporal constraints during therapeutic administration, patient heterogeneity limiting broader applicability, and translational gaps in transitioning these strategies from preclinical models to clinical practice. Continued research evaluating novel delivery routes, biomarker-guided personalization, and synergistic combination regimens holds the promise of further advancing the standard of stroke care. Ongoing clinical studies in strategies to activate the brain–body interface—using modulatory therapies targeting the autonomic nervous system and optogenetic therapy combined with brain–computer interface technology—may promote recovery for patients with stroke and help reduce the global burden of stroke-related disability [ 77 ].
