# Mitochondria as a therapeutic target in neurodegeneration caused by hypoxia and ischemia during the perinatal period

Abstract Perinatal hypoxia, also known as neonatal hypoxia-ischemia (HI), can pose a significant threat to the life and health of newborns, leading to hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy (HIE) and numerous organ dysfunctions, including the nervous system. Mitochondria, which are key organelles in maintaining cellular homeostasis, adenosine triphosphate (ATP) production, regulation of apoptosis, and the cell’s response to oxidative stress, appear to be particularly vulnerable to hypoxic damage. In the course of HIE, a number of mitochondrial functions may be impaired, including inhibition of the respiratory chain, increased production of reactive oxygen species (ROS), loss of mitochondrial membrane potential, or activation of apoptotic pathways. As a result of HI, mitochondrial dynamics related to the processes of mitochondrial fusion, division, and autophagy are also changed, which contributes to exacerbating neuralcell damage. Because of the significant role of mitochondria in the pathophysiology of HIE, they represent a promising therapeutic target. This article presents the current state of knowledge on mitochondrial damage mechanisms in HI and discusses potential therapeutic strategies, such as modulators of mitochondrial dynamics, antioxidant compounds, or inhibitors of specific mitochondrial pathways. A better understanding of these mechanisms may contribute to the development of more effective treatments for neurodegeneration associated with perinatal hypoxia. Introduction The state of perinatal asphyxia, otherwise known as neonatal hypoxia-ischemia (HI), occurs due to the interruption of blood flow and thus gas exchange immediately before, during, or just after birth [ 1 – 3 ]. The occurrence of such a condition can contribute to the development of neonatal hypoxia-ischemic encephalopathy (HIE), leading to systemic disorders, including neurological, respiratory, cardiac, hepatic, or renal dysfunction [ 2 , 3 ]. As a result of limited access to oxygen, glycolysis processes are impaired, which can ultimately lead to impaired development of organs, including, but not limited to, the brain [ 2 ]. In highly developed countries, the incidence of perinatal HI events is 2 per 1,000 births, while in countries with limited access to medical care, the ratio can be up to ten times higher [ 2 ]. In addition, for newborns born prematurely, with extremely low birth weight, the risk of perinatal asphyxia can be as high as 60% [ 4 ]. In the context of hypoxic-ischemic neurodegeneration, mitochondrial function appears to be particularly important, as hypoxia and ischemia lead to animpairment of the function of these organelles. Following a HI event, several changes occur in the mitochondria that have a significant impact on nerve cell function. Oxygen deficiency leads to inhibition of the respiratory chain, resulting in decreased adenosine triphosphate (ATP) production and disruption of ionic homeostasis [ 5 ]. Increased production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) causes oxidative stress, damaging proteins, lipids, and DNA [ 6 ]. Opening of the mitochondrial permeability transition pore (mPTP) leads to loss of mitochondrial membrane potential and interruption of ATP production, which is a key step in the mechanisms leading to apoptosis and cell necrosis [ 7 ]. Release of cytochrome c from mitochondria initiates a cascade of events leading to caspase activation and cell death [ 8 ]. HI also affect mitochondrial dynamics, disrupting the processes of biogenesis, fusion, fission, and mitophagy, leading to dysfunction of these organelles [ 9 ]. In addition, cells switch to anaerobic metabolism, leading to acidification of the cellular environment and further damage to mitochondria [ 10 ]. All of these changes can contribute to neuronal death and impaired brain function due to HI. From a clinical point of view, it is not only the HI event itself that is dangerous to the body. Sudden restoration of perfusion after an episode of ischemia triggers a cascade of pathological changes involving ROS production, calcium homeostasis disorders, endothelial dysfunction, and microcirculation. These are key components of ischemic-reperfusion (I/R) injury in a clinical context [ 11 ]. At the mitochondrial level, the main mechanism causing this condition is the so-called mitochondrial “ROS burst” in the initial phase of reperfusion. This mechanism results primarily from reverse electron transport (RET) through complex I of the respiratory chain, leading to massive production of peroxide, with a devastating effect on mitochondrial function [ 12 ]. Another key element of reperfusion pathophysiology is the pathological opening of the mPTP triggered by a sudden influx of Ca²⁺ and oxidative stress. Its opening leads to loss of membrane potential, oxidative phosphorylation dysfunction, cytochrome c release, and activation of cell death. Given the above evidence, mitochondria appear to be potential therapeutic targets in pathophysiological conditions leading to neurodegenerative disorders. Strategies aimed at stabilizing mitochondrial dynamics, reducing oxidative stress, or improving metabolic function may not only limit damage to central nervous system cells, but also promote their regeneration. Mitochondrial dynamics modulators, mPTP pore formation inhibitors, or antioxidants are just a few examples of methods used in the treatment of hypoxic-ischemic neurodegeneration [13– [ 16 ]. Due to their unclear mechanism of action, they still require further research and optimization. With a better understanding of their mechanisms, it will be possible to develop new, effective therapies against neurodegeneration. The purpose of this article is to present the current state of knowledge on the mechanisms of neurodegeneration caused by perinatal HI and to discuss research directions related to potential therapeutic strategies targeting mitochondria. The paper aims to provide not only a synthetic presentation of the solutions described to date, but also to identify the limitations encountered in current research and to determine which elements of these strategies require further development, refinement, or verification in future experimental and clinical studies. In addition, attention is drawn to other, hitherto unexplored therapeutic pathways whose mechanisms of action have been described in the context of other physiological systems, but whose potential application in neuroprotection and the treatment of HI-related neurodegeneration has not yet been sufficiently documented. Mitochondria – structure and function Mitochondria, as key organelles, play an important role in maintaining cellular homeostasis by providing energy in the form of ATP and regulating processes such as apoptosis and the response to oxidative stress [ 17 ]. They are made up of a double membrane with corresponding areas in between, known as the intermembrane space (IMS) and the mitochondrial matrix [ 18 ]. Although the IMS accounts for only 5% of the mitochondrial proteome, it plays a crucial role in various cellular processes, such as molecular exchange between the mitochondrion and cytosol, the biogenesis of respiratory chain components, and the initiation of apoptotic cascades [ 19 ]. In addition, IMS regulates the structural integrity and morphogenesis of mitochondria [ 20 ]. In contrast, the mitochondrial matrix, which contains numerous enzymes responsible for the oxidation of fats and carbohydrates, is primarily associated with tricarboxylic acid cycle metabolism (TCA) cycle [ 20 ]. The region comprising the inner mitochondrial membrane and the mitochondrial matrix serves as a functional compartment for processes such as protein synthesis, the urea cycle, and amino acid metabolism [ 21 ]. These organelles also have their own genome that allows independent synthesis of mitochondrial proteins involved in their active function. Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) encodes 13 proteins that play a key role in the function of the oxidative phosphorylation system, which includes four respiratory chain complexes and ATP synthase. However, in order to function and replicate mtDNA, mitochondria require factors encoded by nuclear DNA. These factors are essential for the mechanisms of replication, transcription, or signaling pathways required for proper mitochondrial function in response to changing cellular demands [ 22 ]. Another unique feature of mitochondria is their ability to self-replicate or degrade through controlled mitochondrial membrane dynamics called fusion andfission [ 23 ]. Image created with BioRender.com Mitochondrial dynamics and their regulation in hypoxic neurodegeneration Mitochondrial dynamics is a process involving two key mechanisms: fusion and fission [ 24 ]. Both of these processes are essential for the proper functioning of mitochondria, as they enable them to respond to changes in the cellular environment, exchange genetic material, proteins, and lipids, and adapt to changing energy needs [ 25 ]. Under physiological conditions, the mechanisms of fusion and fission are tightly regulated, ensuring that mitochondria are able to respond appropriately to changing metabolic conditions, but also protecting cells from oxidative stress and damage [ 26 ]. Mitochondrial fusion and fission processes Mitochondrial fusion involves the fusion of neighboring organelles to compensate for damage and increase the mitochondria’s resistance to stress. As a result of fusion, mitochondria form a more interconnected, integrated network, which improves their efficiency in ATP production and stabilizes the cell’s metabolic functions. This process is regulated by proteins such as mitofusin 1 (Mfn1) and mitofusin 2 (Mfn2), which enable the outer mitochondrial membranes to fuse, and the mitochondrdynamin-like GTPase (OPA1) protein, responsible for fusing the inner membranes (Fig. 2 ). Normal activity of that protein is crucial for cell health, and their deficiency or dysfunction can lead to degeneration of cells, including neurons [ 27 , 28 ]. Fig. 2 Mitochondrial dynamics. The fission process is controlled by the dynamin-related protein 1 (DRP1), which is recruited to the outer membrane of mitochondria, where it forms rings that initiate separation [ 27 , 28 ]. Image created with BioRender.com Mitochondrial fragmentation (fission), on the other hand, involves the separation of mitochondria into smaller units, allowing the rapid isolation of damaged mitochondrial fragments, which can then undergo autophagy, i.e., a process of degradation and removal by the cell. Fragmentation is mainly controlled by the dynamin-related protein 1 (DRP1), which is recruited to the outer membrane of mitochondria, where it forms rings that allow its separation (Fig. 2 ). Under physiological conditions, the balance between fusion and fragmentation is tightly controlled, allowing mitochondria to adapt to the changing demands of the cell [ 27 , 28 ]. The role of mitochondrial dynamics in hypoxia Under hypoxia, the balance between mitochondrial fusion and fission is disrupted, leading to excessive fragmentation. This phenomenon is often the result of increased activity of DRP1, which becomes phosphorylated and translocates to the mitochondrial membrane, which stimulating mitochondrial separation [ 29 ]. Excessive fragmentation leads to the breakdown of the mitochondrial network, which reduces the efficiency of ATP production and promotes increased release of ROS. Elevated ROS amounts damage mitochondrial components, including proteins, lipids, and mtDNA, leading to increasing oxidative stress, further exacerbating fragmentation [ 30 ]. An growing evidence shows that excessive mitochondrial fragmentation under hypoxic conditions increases the vulnerability of neurons to damage and cell death. In neurons, which are particularly sensitive to ATP deficiency and oxidative stress, excessive mitochondrial fragmentation can lead to irreversible damage. Dysfunctional mitochondria in neurons trigger a cascade of processes, such as the release of proapoptotic proteins, including cytochrome c, that initiate programmed cell death (apoptosis) [ 31 ]. Therapies targeting regulation of mitochondrial dynamics Understanding the role of mitochondrial dynamics in hypoxia has opened the way for research into substances that modulate this process as potential neuroprotective therapies. Among others, DRP1 inhibitors, such as Mitochondrial Division Inhibitor-1 (Mdivi-1), are promising therapeutic candidates because they inhibit excessive mitochondrial fragmentation [ 32 ]. By limiting DRP1 activation and its recruitment to the outer mitochondrial membrane, Mdivi-1 reduces mitochondrial fission and supports the maintenance of mitochondrial network integrity. In animal model studies, Mdivi-1 has been shown to protect neurons from hypoxia-induced damage by stabilizing mitochondrial function and reducing ROS production [ 13 ]. Recent studies published after 2020 confirm that Mdivi-1 exerts a protective effect under hypoxic conditions through multilevel modulation of mitochondrial dynamics and function. In studies on vessels and vascular smooth muscle cells (VSMCs) after hypoxic injury, Mdivi-1 was shown to inhibit DRP1 phosphorylation at Ser-616 and limit its translocation to mitochondria, leading to a reduction in excessive mitochondrial fission and partial stimulation of mitochondrial fusion [ 33 ]. At the same time, Mdivi-1 increases the expression of antioxidant enzymes such as SOD1 and catalase and activates the Nrf2 pathway, leading to a reduction in hypoxia-induced oxidative stress [ 33 ]. Furthermore, mechanistic analysis indicates that Mdivi-1 may act independently of DRP1 - it inhibits complex I of the respiratory chain, which affects the assembly of respiratory supercomplexes, disrupts calcium homeostasis, and reduces the efficiency of ATP production while decreasing ROS generation [ 34 ]. Further studies point out that the effects of Mdivi-1 are not entirely consistent with simple DRP1 inhibition: it also affects antioxidant signaling - in models of ischemic-reperfusion injury, it activates Nrf2 and pathways such as HO-1/GPX4, which translates into a reduction in ferroptosis [ 35 ]. Consequently, although Mdivi-1 is a valuable tool for modulating DRP1-dependent mitochondrial dynamics, it does not act solely by inhibiting fission, its full pharmacological profile also includes effects on bioenergetics, calcium homeostasis, and cellular antioxidant mechanisms. This complexity of action should be taken into account when interpreting results and designing future studies. Another therapeutic approach may be to modulate the activity of mitochondrial fusion proteins such as Mfn1, Mfn2, and OPA1. Regulation of these proteins can help restore the balance between fusion and fragmentation. Increasing the activity of fusion proteins promotes the formation of an integrated mitochondrial network, which improves the metabolic efficiency of neurons and increases their resistance to oxidative stress [ 36 , 37 ]. Moreover, experimental studies have shown that stimulation of Mfn2 expression contributes to better mitochondrial organization and improved synaptic function in conditions of neuronal damage [ 14 , 38 ]. Xi et al. [ 14 ] demonstrated that MitoQ activates peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor-γ coactivator 1α (PGC-1α), leading to an upregulation of Mfn2 at both mRNA and protein levels in a 6-hydroxydopamine (6-OHDA) model of Parkinson’s disease. This Mfn2-dependent mitochondrial fusion was associated with preservation of mitochondrial morphology, reduced reactive oxygen species (ROS) accumulation and decreased apoptosis of dopaminergic neurons in vitro and in vivo [ 14 ]. The molecular mechanism of action of MitoQ under hypoxia (and other stress conditions) involves several key processes: through its coupling with the lipophilic cation triphenylphosphonium (TPP⁺). MitoQ accumulates in mitochondria, where it is reduced to hydroquinone and directly neutralizes ROS, limiting the oxidative activation of fission signaling. In cell models, MitoQ inhibits the translocation of Drp1 to the mitochondrial membrane, thereby inhibiting excessive mitochondrial fission [ 39 ]. At the same time, MitoQ stimulates PGC-1α, which acts as a transcriptional coactivator supporting Mfn2 expression; this process is essential for modulating mitochondrial fusion, as knocking down PGC-1α eliminates MitoQ’s ability to increase Mfn2 levels [ 40 ]. Furthermore, independent of its antioxidant activity, MitoQ has been shown to increase mRNA and protein levels of Mfn1, Mfn2, and OPA1 in tissues subjected to oxidative stress, contributing to the restoration of the mitochondrial network [ 41 ]. Consequently, MitoQ can act as a regulator of mitochondrial dynamics under hypoxic conditions: it works by inhibiting Drp1-dependent fragmentation, promoting fusion by inducing of Mfn2 (and other fusion proteins) via PGC-1α, and modulates redox status and mitochondrial bioenergetics. Thanks to this multifaceted activity, MitoQ has therapeutic potential as a neuroprotective and therapeutic agent in diseases associated with mitochondrial damage. Research perspectives on mitochondrial dynamics in hypoxic neurodegeneration Understanding mitochondrial dynamics and their regulation represents an important advance in research on neurodegeneration caused by hypoxia. Future research should focus on identifying new substances that could selectively modulate fusion and fission processes in mitochondria and promote the elimination of damaged mitochondria. In addition, the development of nanotechnology could make it possible to precisely deliver drugs to mitochondria, increasing their effectiveness and minimizing side effects. Therapies targeting mitochondrial dynamics have the potential to become effective interventions for treating hypoxia-related neurodegenerative diseases, helping to slow or halt neuronal degeneration. Oxidative stress and the role of ROS in hypoxic neurodegeneration Oxidative stress plays a key role in hypoxia-induced neurodegenerative processes. Under hypoxic conditions, mitochondrial dysfunction leads to increased production of ROS, which trigger cascades of cellular damage, damaging DNA, proteins, and lipids. In cells of the central nervous system, which are particularly susceptible to oxidative stress due to their high energy demands and relatively low levels of antioxidant enzymes, ROS accelerate degeneration, affecting functional capacity and cell survival. Understanding the mechanisms of ROS formation and their role in hypoxia-induced neurodegeneration may provide valuable information for developing therapies aimed at neutralizing or eliminating them, as well as protecting mitochondria [ 42 ]. ROS formation in mitochondria Under physiological conditions, mitochondria generate ROS as a by-product of electron transport in the respiratory chain. They play an important role in the regulation of signaling pathways such as MAPK/ERK, PI3K/Akt, and Nuclear factor kappa B (NF-κB), as well as the activation of transcription factors (e.g., myogenic differentiation 1 - MyoD, runt-related transcription factor 2 - Runx2, nuclear factor erythroid 2-related factor 2 - Nrf2), that influence the expression of genes responsible for cell differentiation [ 43 , 44 ]. At moderate concentrations, ROS participate in epigenetic changes and support the activation and differentiation of stem cells (including osteogenesis, neurogenesis, and myelination) [ 45 , 46 ]. Maintaining redox balance is therefore essential because low or moderate concentrations of ROS promote developmental processes, while excess ROS can lead to oxidative stress, cell damage, and apoptosis. Mitochondrial antioxidant defense systems, such as superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase, and glutathione peroxidase remain as the first defense. On the other hand, redox systems based on thioredoxin (Trx) and glutathione (GSH) play a central role in modulating hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) emission. Thioredoxin reductase-2 (TrxR2) together with Trx2 and peroxiredoxin-3 (Prx3) maintain minimal H₂O₂ levels, and their inhibition significantly increases its emission [ 47 , 48 ]. At the same time, the bioenergetic state of mitochondria, i.e., their ability to produce ATP, plays a key role in redox balance. In this context, the activity of mitochondrial creatine kinase (Mt-CK), responsible for the rate of ATP/ADP buffering, proves to be critical. Its deficiency promotes excessive ROS emission (which has been demonstrated at least in cardiac mitochondria) [ 49 ]. Under hypoxic conditions, redox imbalance positively correlates with oxidative phosphorylation disorders, leading to electron accumulation, increased ROS emission, damage to mitochondrial membranes, proteins, and mtDNA, and escalation of oxidative stress, which ultimately initiates neuronal degenerative processes leading to gradual neurodegeneration. Mechanisms of ROS action in neurodegeneration ROS affect nerve cell function in multiple ways. First, they cause direct damage to cellular structures, including membranes, which are particularly susceptible to lipid peroxidation. This process leads to a reduction in the integrity of mitochondrial membranes, which disrupts ion balance, membrane permeability, and causes a loss of membrane potential (ΔΨm) [ 50 , 51 ]. Without maintenance of the membrane potential, mitochondria are unable to efficiently produce ATP (Fig. 3 A, B), which ultimately leads to cell death. This activates signaling pathways that induce apoptosis. High levels of ROS induce DNA damage and activate proapoptotic proteins such as p53, which leads to to caspase activation and degradation of cellular structures [ 52 ]. ROS also affect mitochondrial membrane permeability, allowing the release of proapoptotic proteins such as cytochrome c, which also activate caspases and lead to cell death [ 51 ]. The images were captured using LSM 780/ ELYRA PS.1 superresolution confocal system (Carl Zeiss, Jena, Germany) Antioxidants and their role in hypoxic neuroprotection Neurons have a number of defense mechanisms to counteract oxidative stress. Antioxidant enzymes such as SOD, catalase, and glutathione peroxidase are responsible for neutralizing ROS and protecting cells from their damaging effects. However, under hypoxic conditions, their activity is often insufficient to counterbalance ROS overproduction, leading to the accumulation of ROS and increased oxidative stress. Antioxidant-based therapies are one of the most studied approaches in protecting neurons from the effects of hypoxic neurodegeneration. Some of the potentialtherapies include resveratrol, mitochondrial peptides, or dimethyl fumarate, which are compounds with proven antioxidant and neuroprotective effects [ 53 , 54 ]. Dimethyl fumarate activates the Nrf2 pathway, which regulates the expression of antioxidant enzymes, while resveratrol supports mitochondrial function and increases the activity of sirtuins, key to regulating cellular homeostasis. Resveratrol application Resveratrol, a natural polyphenol found in grapes, among other foods, has attracted the attention of researchers for years for its antioxidant properties and potential neuroprotective effects [ 55 ]. In the context of neurological damage caused by hypoxia and ischemia, its effect on mitochondria is of particular interest [ 55 , 56 ]. The compound exhibits multidirectional protective effects under conditions of oxidative stress [ 57 – 60 ]. Resveratrol acts as a direct antioxidant, neutralizing ROS and reducing oxidative damage. Reactive oxygen species such as H₂O₂, superoxide radical (O₂-), and hydroxyl groups (-OH) are by-products of mitochondrial metabolism [ 61 ]. Under physiological conditions, their levels are controlled by endogenous antioxidant mechanisms. However, under HI conditions, they are overproduced, leading to oxidative stress and cell damage. Resveratrol acts as an effective “scavenger” of free radicals. Its chemical structure contains 3 -OH groups, which can act as a hydrogen atom donor (H-), neutralizing reactive oxygen species [ 59 ]. Acting as a hydrogen atom donor, it converts free radicals into less reactive molecules, preventing their further reactions with lipids, proteins, or DNA. Another action is its interaction with transition metals. Resveratrol chelates transition metal ions such as iron (Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺) and copper (Cu²⁺), which catalyze Fenton reactions leading to the formation of the highly toxic hydroxyl radical [ 57 ]. This reduces the risk of oxidative degradation of mitochondrial lipids and proteins. It has been proven that resveratrol not only neutralizes reactive oxygen species but is also able to effectively eliminate reactive nitrogen species. It reacts directly with peroxynitrite (ONOO-), allowing it to reduce or prevent nitrosylation of cysteine and tyrosine residues in proteins. A study by Tsai et al. [ 62 ] also presented resveratrol as a nitric oxide (NO) neutralizer [ 60 ]. Resveratrol also exhibits antioxidant activity, among other things by, activating enzymes responsible for neutralizing reactive oxygen species produced in the body. Key enzymes activated in this context include mitochondrial SOD2 and catalase [ 63 ]. A study by Mokni et al. [ 64 ] showed that intraperitoneal administration to healthy rats increases the expression of endogenous antioxidant enzymes such as superoxide dismutase and catalase in the brain. A similar effect was observed in neuronal cells of the mouse hippocampus line HT22, subjected to oxidative stress induced by high concentrations of glutamate. Moreover, resveratrol has been proven to induce mitochondrial SOD2 expression through activation of PI3K/Akt, GSK-3β-β-catenin, or SIRT1/PGC-1α signaling pathways [ 58 ]. Sirtuin 1 (SIRT1) activation also enhances mitochondrial biogenesis and function, strengthening the cell’s defense against oxidative stress [ 65 ]. As described earlier, mitochondria are extremely dynamic organelles undergoing constant transformation (fusion and fission) and relocation depending on the current demand of the cell. Preliminary studies by Kincaid and Bossy-Wetzel [ 66 ] have shown that increased Sirt1 and Sirt3 activity intensifies metabolism by initiating fusion and inhibiting mitochondrial division. In fruit flies, compounds that activate sirtuins, such as resveratrol and NAD+, led to enhanced mitochondrial fusion, resulting in prolonged life in these organisms. However, these studies require further analysis to determine whether similar mechanisms also occur in mammals [ 66 ]. In addition to its effects on mitochondria, resveratrol is also active in the cytoplasm and cell nucleus, where it participates in the regulation of many cellular processes. Its antioxidant properties include, among others, the ability to modulate the activity of enzymes involved in the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), which contributes to the reduction of oxidative stress. An example of such an enzyme is cyclooxygenase 1 (COX1) [ 60 ]. Resveratrol has been shown to significantly reduce COX1 activity, leading to a decrease in the synthesis of prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) and the formation of 8-iso-prostaglandin F2α (8-iso-PGF2α), which are factors involved in the production of free radicals. Resveratrol also affects the activity of transcription factors such as Nrf2, which occurs in complex with the Keap1 inhibitory protein. Nrf2 regulates the expression of genes encoding antioxidant enzymes. By increasing Nrf2 activity, resveratrol promotes the synthesis of enzymes that detoxify and protect the cell from oxidative damage [ 67 ]. However, these mechanisms already take place in the area of the cytoplasm and cell nucleus, so they will not be described in this article. In summary, the available data describes the multidirectional effects of resveratrol as a potent antioxidant compound that not only reduces ROS but also protects mitochondria from their destructive effects. Under conditions of HI, its properties may play a key role in preventing glial cell damage and promoting their neuroprotective functions. However, its use faces significant limitations. First, the literature indicates that resveratrol has very low bioavailability after oral administration, which is due to both its poor water solubility and rapid phase II metabolism (glucuronidation and sulfation) in the intestine and liver. This significantly limits its therapeutic effectiveness [ 68 ]. Furthermore, despite extensive research on the use of resveratrol, few details are known about its potential interactions with other drugs. Several clinical studies have demonstrated the occurrence of side effects after using resveratrol together with other drugs. It has been shown that this compound may indirectly interact with other drugs, leading to reduced activity or overexpression of drug transporters and cytochrome P450 enzymes. This includes CYP3A4 and CYP1A1, which are important cellular pathways involved in drug metabolism [ 69 ]. In response to these limitations, authors of several scientific articles propose formulation strategies that could significantly increase the effectiveness of resveratrol. Examples include modern nanodelivery systems, such as lipid nanoparticles, nanocrystalline formulations, and polymeric carriers, which improve the solubility, stability, and absorption of resveratrol [ 70 , 71 ]. For example, in neurodegeneration models, the use of formulations containing resveratrol in nanoparticles leads to increased stability, better bioavailability, and a stronger neuroprotective effect [ 72 ]. In addition, a review of recent studies emphasizes that future work should focus on optimizing these carriers for oral or targeted delivery (e.g., to the brain), as well as determining the long-term toxicity and safety of such formulations [ 71 ]. Older clinical studies also have shown accelerated brain volume loss in Alzheimer’s patients receiving high doses of resveratrol [ 73 ]. However, recent analyses indicate that such brain volume reduction visible on MRI scans may reflect changes in neuroinflammatory activity, extracellular free water dynamics, or glial modulation rather than direct neurotoxicity. This is confirmed by advanced neuroimaging studies linking volumetric changes to inflammation, changes in the blood-brain barrier, and fluctuations in water content [ 74 – 76 ]. Furthermore, a growing body of evidence indicates that resveratrol modulates microglial and inflammatory pathways, providing a mechanistic basis for such biomarker and imaging changes without suggesting neuronal loss [ 77 ]. Importantly, clinical trials conducted in other populations including individuals with metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, mild cognitive impairment, or vascular cognitive impairment consistently show good tolerability of low to moderate doses, with adverse effects limited mainly to transient gastrointestinal symptoms, and there is no evidence of progressive neurodegeneration [ 78 – 80 ]. The lack of standardization of doses, chemical forms, and methods of administration complicates makes it difficult to compare research results. Among other things, these factors indicate the need for further research to optimize the effects of the preparations and their doses. Further work is necessary to gain a more complete understanding of its pharmacological potential, which may enable the use of resveratrol in the treatment of neurodegenerative diseases. Resveratrol remains one of the most promising molecules with antioxidant and neuroprotective properties, particularly in the context of damage caused by HI. However, without overcoming pharmacokinetic limitations - primarily through the development of advanced, stable, biocompatible delivery forms, its therapeutic potential in clinical settings will remain significantly limited. The priority for future research should therefore be to develop formulations that improve bioavailability, reduce hepatic metabolism, and ensure the safe and effective delivery of resveratrol to nerve tissue. MOTS-c - mitochondrial peptide MOTS-c (mitochondrial ORF of the 12 S rRNA type C) is an endogenous mitochondrial peptide composed of 16 amino acids, encoded by the mitochondrial genome in the 12 S rRNA region. In recent years, this peptide has become an interesting area of research in the context of a neuroprotective agent with potential applications in neurodegenerative diseases such as Parkinson’s and Alzheimer’s. It occurs physiologically in many tissues with high energy demands, including skeletal muscle, liver, and brain, and its concentration in the blood undergoes dynamic changes depending on the metabolic state of the body [ 81 ]. Its primary role is to maintain metabolic homeostasis by modulating key signaling pathways, making it an important component of mitochondrial regulators with signaling and protective functions [ 82 ]. In the context of neurodegeneration caused by HI, MOTS-c exhibits potential protective effects through multilevel mechanisms. One of these is the translocation of the peptide from the mitochondria to the cell nucleus in response to metabolic stress. After translocation, MOTS-c regulates the expression of genes containing antioxidant response elements (ARE), which increases the ability of neurons to neutralize ROS. As a result, the degree of oxidative damage to proteins, lipids, and DNA is reduced, which is one of the key factors in the pathophysiology of HI [ 82 , 83 ]. At the same time, MOTS-c activates the transcription of genes related to mitochondrial biogenesis, such as mitochondrial transcription factor A (TFAM) (Fig. 3 C and D), nuclear respiratory factor 1 (NRF1), and cytochrome c oxidase subunit 4 (COX4). The induction of these genes leads to an increase in the number and improvement in the functionality of mitochondria, which enhances the ability of neurons to produce ATP and adapt to oxygen-deficient conditions [ 84 ]. An important element of the MOTS-c mechanism of action is its close relationship with the AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) pathway. This peptide can activate AMPK both indirectly, by regulating folic acid metabolism and increasing the level of 5-aminoimidazole-4-carboxamide ribonucleotide (AICAR), and directly under conditions of energy stress [ 83 ]. AMPK activation promotes increased glucose uptake by cells, intensifies glycolytic processes, and inhibits ATP-consuming anabolic processes, allowing for more efficient use of available energy substrates. In the context of HI, where oxygen and glucose availability is limited, this mechanism can significantly improve neuron survival [ 85 ]. MOTS-c also has immunomodulatory effects. Studies indicate that the activation of peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma coactivator 1-alpha (PGC-1α) by MOTS-c is associated with a reduction in the levels of proinflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor α (TNF-α) and Interleukin-6 (IL-6) [ 86 ]. This effect may limit the secondary inflammatory cascade, which, following episodes of HI, further exacerbates neuronal damage through microglia activation and increased ROS production. Suppression of excessive inflammatory response can promote neuron protection. The literature also describes the potential involvement of MOTS-c in the regulation of apoptotic processes through the stabilization of mitochondrial membrane potential and the reduction of oxidative stress. Experimental studies have shown that MOTS-c reduces excessive production of ROS, inhibits the translocation of the proapoptotic protein Bax to mitochondria and the release of cytochrome c, and reduces caspase-9 activation. This results in a reduction in stress-induced cell death [ 87 ]. These mechanisms indicate that MOTS-c may protect mitochondrial integrity under conditions of metabolic stress, thereby reducing the risk of irreversible neuronal loss observed in the course of HI. Combining energy metabolism regulation, increased antioxidant resistance, control of mitochondrial biogenesis, and suppression of the inflammatory response, MOTS-c can be considered a promising candidate in mitochondrial-targeted therapies for neurodegeneration caused by HI (Table 1 ). Although the number of studies on MOTS-c is growing every year, many of its mechanisms of action are still not fully understood. To date, research on its effects has been conducted mainly in metabolic and cardiovascular models, but there is a lack of extensive analysis in neurobiological models and clinical trials. This requires further verification, especially in the context of long-term efficacy and therapeutic safety. M2 - anti-inflammatory microglia phenotype; NRF1 - Nuclear Respiratory Factor 1; TFAM - Mitochondrial transcription factor A; TNF- α - Tumor Necrosis Factor- α Dimethyl fumarate application Due to its multidirectional antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and mitochondrioprotective properties, dimethyl fumarate has gained particular interest in recent years as a potential therapeutic candidate for the treatment of brain damage associated with perinatal HI [ 91 ]. In animal models, dimethyl fumarate has been shown to limit damage to gray and white matter, reduce demyelination, and modulate the glial response by suppressing microglial and astroglial activation. This suggests that its action involves neurons, oligodendrocytes, astrocytes, and microglial cells [ 92 ]. Dimethyl fumarate exerts its neuroprotective effects primarily through the activation of the Nrf2/ARE transcriptional pathway and the inhibition of NF-κB signaling. Activation of Nrf2 involves its release from the Keap1 complex, enabling nuclear translocation and binding to ARE in the promoters of genes encoding key antioxidant enzymes, including heme oxygenase-1 (HO-1) and NAD(P)H: quinone oxidoreductase 1 (NQO1) [ 93 ]. Upregulation of these enzymes promotes the neutralization of ROS, thereby mitigating oxidative stress and preventing mitochondrial damage [ 94 ] Beyond its role in antioxidant defense, Nrf2 contributes to the maintenance of mitochondrial integrity and the induction of mitochondrial biogenesis, enhancing ATP production and reducing ROS generation, which together support neuronal energy homeostasis [ 95 ]. At the same time, dimethyl fumarate modulates the NF-κB pathway, a central regulator of inflammatory responses. Under ischemic or neuroinflammatory conditions, NF-κB activation stimulates the production of proinflammatory cytokines such as TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6, exacerbating neuronal damage [ 96 ]. By attenuating NF-κB signaling, dimethyl fumarate reduces cytokine secretion, limits neurotoxicity induced by microglia and astrocytes, and contributes to overall neuroprotection [ 97 ]. Dimethyl fumarate may also indirectly stabilize the blood-brain barrier (BBB). Activation of Nrf2 in brain endothelial cells increases the expression of antioxidant enzymes, which reduces oxidative stress and improves BBB integrity, reducing the penetration of inflammatory and neurotoxic factors into brain tissue [ 98 ]. In neurons and oligodendrocytes, dimethyl fumarate supports mitochondrial bioenergetics and limits ROS-induced apoptotic cascades, which may be important in protecting against neuron loss and myelination disorders observed after HI [ 93 ]. Translational studies also point to additional aspects of dimethyl fumarate bioactivity, such as its effect on the phenotype of astrocytes and microglia. It can induce the transition of microglia from the pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype to the anti-inflammatory M2 phenotype, which may be relevant in the context of the inflammatory response in the brain. Similarly, dimethyl fumarate affects astrocytes by modulating their activity and potentially altering the ratio between the neurotoxic A1 and neuroprotective A2 phenotypes, which may contribute to reducing neuronal damage and supporting repair processes [ 99 ]. These observations indicate that this compound acts in a multicellular and multimechanistic manner, involving both direct protection of mitochondria and modulation of neuroglial interactions. Despite promising therapeutic effects, the use of dimethyl fumarate (DMF) is not without significant safety concerns. Among the most common adverse events are flushing and gastrointestinal discomfort, but the most clinically serious risk is lymphopenia, which may predispose patients to opportunistic infections, including the potentially fatal progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) [ 100 , 101 ]. Importantly, the latest data emphasize that even mild lymphopenia can pose a risk. Cases of PML have been reported in patients whose absolute lymphocyte count (ALC) remained above previously accepted thresholds, prompting regulatory agencies to tighten monitoring recommendations. For example, the UK Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency now advises quarterly lymphocyte assessments and enhanced vigilance in patients with sustained moderate-grade lymphopenia [ 102 ]. Long-term pharmacovigilance data further clarify the scale and nature of this risk. A large post‑marketing series encompassing over 560,000 patient-years of exposure reported 12 confirmed PML cases, yielding an incidence of approximately 1.07 per 100,000 person‑years. Notably, all cases occurred in the context of lymphopenia, though not exclusively in those with severe or prolonged lymphocyte depletion [ 103 ]. Other studies also suggest that persistent lymphopenia (defined, e.g., as ALC &lt; 0.5–0.7 × 10⁹/L) occurs in a subset of DMF-treated patients: in one cohort, about 11% developed moderate persistent lymphopenia and around 3% developed severe, long-lasting lymphopenia [ 104 ]. Moreover, risk stratification is complex: while low ALC remains the primary marker, other immunological perturbations have been implicated. For example, a case report described a patient who developed PML despite relatively preserved total lymphocyte counts. Detailed immunophenotyping revealed profound CD8⁺ T-cell exhaustion (high PD-1 expression) and impaired anti-JCV (John Cunningham virus) T-cell responses, suggesting that qualitative changes in immune function not just absolute lymphocyte numbers may contribute to risk [ 105 ]. Another review highlighted that older age, prolonged therapy duration, and reductions in specific T-cell subsets (e.g., CD8⁺) can heighten vulnerability [ 106 ]. Predictive markers are being actively investigated. A recent prospective study identified that a pronounced drop in ALC during the first three months of DMF therapy strongly predicts subsequent lymphopenia: a ≥ 21% decline in ALC afer 3 months was associated with a substantially increased risk of moderate lymphopenia, whereas a ≥ 40% drop predicted seere lymphopenia [ 107 ]. While the therapeutic benefits of dimethyl fumarate are well established, the off-target risks most notably lymphopenia and its link to PML cannot be overlooked. The emerging evidence, including cases occurring in the absence of severe lymphocyte depletion, argues strongly for careful and ongoing hematological and immunological monitoring. In addition, there is a pressing need for further mechanistic and safety studies to better elucidate which patients are at greatest risk (e.g., based on T-cell subsets, age, exhaustion markers) and to refine monitoring protocols. Only through such vigilance and research can the risk–benefit balance of DMF be optimized in clinical settings. Supplementation with substances with antioxidant activity Studies have shown that supplementation with substances with antioxidant activity, such as vitamins C and E, polyphenols and flavonoids, can effectively support natural defense mechanisms and reduce ROS-induced damage. The use of thees exogenous antioxidants allows support in the removal of ROS, reducing oxidative stress while supporting mitochondrial function and slowing neurodegenerative processes [ 16 ]. Vitamin E, also known as tocopherol, is one of the most effective lipid antioxidants. It works by neutralizing free radicals that attack polyunsaturated fatty acids in cell and mitochondrial membranes. Lipid peroxidation leads to loss of membrane integrity, disruption of membrane potential, and damage to membrane proteins, triggering a further degenerative cascade [ 108 ]. Many studies to date have confirmed the crucial role of vitamin E in maintaining nervous system health. Firstly, as reported by Cavalier et al. [ 109 ] among others, the functional deficits accompanying hereditary vitamin E deficiency (AVED) are neurological in nature [ 94 ]. Second, as proved by Bourre and Clement [ 110 ], the biological half-life of vitamin E in the brain is significantly longer compare to the other tissues. This suggests the existence of mechanisms regulating its reserves that are specific to the central nervous system [ 110 ]. his is also related to the fact that the concentration of α-tocopherol varies significantly in different areas of the brain, indicating the presence of active processes controlling its spatial distribution [ 111 ]. Another important consideration is the level of tocopherol transporter protein (TTP), which is significantly elevated in patients with neurodegenerative diseases associated with oxidative stress, such as Alzheimer’s disease. Moreover, TTP shows selective expression in cerebellar astrocytes, and its levels are regulated by both oxidative stress and vitamin E level [ 112 ]. In the central nervous system (CNS), the highest levels of TTP expression are observed in the cerebellum, particularly in astrocytes. Thees proteins are playing a key role in ensuring and regulating the transport of antioxidants, including vitamin E, into surrounding neurons [ 113 ]. Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) is a hydrophilic antioxidant that acts in the water spaces of cells and neutralizes ROS such as O₂- and H₂O₂. Under hypoxia, vitamin C plays an important role in reducing oxidative stress in the cytoplasm and on the surfaces of cell membranes. Moreover, vitamin C participates in the regeneration of other antioxidants, including vitamin E, restoring its active form after ROS neutralization. In clinical studies, vitamin C has demonstrated the ability to reduce markers of oxidative stress in neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s. The use of vitamin C in the treatment of hypoxic neurodegeneration may promote neuronal defense mechanisms, reduce cell death and improve mitochondrial function [ 114 ]. It is worth noting, however, that most ascorbate is found in neurons, not in glial cells. This phenomenon was first described more than forty years ago in histochemical studies. The differences in ascorbate concentration between neurons and glial cells are significant. For example, in the brain of a newborn rat, areas characterized by a high density of glial cells show ascorbate concentrations that are only about 10–20% of the levels observed in regions of high neuronal density [ 115 ]. Vitamin A, mainly in the form of retinol and its derivatives, has a dual function as an antioxidant and regulator of gene transcription. Its antioxidant role is to neutralize free radicals, especially in lipid-rich tissues such as the brain. Vitamin A also influences the mechanisms of neurogenesis, promoting the regeneration of damaged neurons. Under hypoxic conditions, vitamin A and its metabolites can protect neurons from degeneration by regulating the cellular response to oxidative stress through signaling pathways such as Nrf2. Activation of these pathways leads to the expression of antioxidant enzymes such as catalase and glutathione peroxidase, enhancing cellular defense mechanisms [ 116 , 117 ]. The effects of antioxidant vitamins are often synergistic. For example, vitamin E protects cell membranes, while vitamin C regenerates its active form in the cytosol. Vitamin A promotes the stability of lipid membranes and regulates the transcription of genes responsible for antioxidant reactions. The combination of supplementation with these vitamins can increase the effectiveness of therapy by minimizing ROS damage in neurons and promoting mitochondrial repair functions. In clinical practice, the use of antioxidant vitamins in hypoxic neurodegeneration aims not only to reduce oxidative stress, but also to promote the regenerative capacity of neurons [ 16 , 118 ]. The future of therapy may be based on combining vitamins with other bioactive compounds, for a more comprehensive approach to mitochondrial protection and ROS neutralization. Despite numerous evidence pointing to the potential benefits of antioxidant supplements, their clinical effectiveness is largely limited by bioavailability issues. Many of these compounds are characterized by low solubility in water or fats, rapid metabolism in the liver, and active excretion, leading to short-lived and insufficient concentrations in target tissues, including the brain [ 119 , 120 ]. Furthermore, under conditions of HI, blood flow to nervous tissue is reduced, further decreasing the delivery of exogenous antioxidants to neurons and glial cells [ 121 ]. Consequently, even at relatively high oral doses, the concentration of these compounds in mitochondria may remain suboptimal, limiting their ability to neutralize ROS and protect organelles from oxidative stress. Therefore, research on antioxidant therapy is increasingly focusing on strategies to improve bioavailability, such as encapsulation in nanoparticles, liposomes, polymer carriers, or chemical modification of compounds to increase their permeability across the BBB [ 122 , 123 ]. These approaches not only increase the concentration of antioxidants in target tissues, but also enable their targeted delivery to mitochondria, which is crucial in the context of neuroprotection in hypoxic-ischemic neurodegeneration. Therapies targeting mitochondrial signaling pathways Mitochondrial signaling pathways play a key role in the regulation of cellular functions, especially under stress conditions such as hypoxia and ischemia. As previously described in HI neurodegeneration, mitochondria are crucial for many pathological processes, including the production of reactive oxygen species, activation of apoptotic pathways or regulation of cellular metabolism. Therefore, acting on specific points in these pathways may seem an appropriate avenue for research into therapies for neurodegenerative diseases [ 124 ]. Potential mechnisms may involve hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF) complexes, mechanistic target of rapamycin (mTOR), tumor necrosis factor receptor-associated protein (TRAP) or apoptotic signalling pathways. HIF-1α pathway The HIF-1α (hypoxia-inducible factor 1-alpha) pathway plays a key role in the adaptation of cells to low oxygen conditions. It is a regulator of the cellular response to hypoxia, activating the expression of genes that help cells adapt to oxygen deprivation [ 125 ]. Under normoxia (normal oxygen levels), HIF-1α is rapidly degraded by proteasomes. However, under hypoxia, HIF-1α degradation is inhibited, leading to its accumulation and activation. HIF-1α moves to the cell nucleus, where it binds to HIF-1β, forming an active transcriptional complex. This complex binds to hypoxia response elements (HREs) in the promoters of target genes, initiating their transcription [ 124 ]. HIF-1α regulates the expression of many genes involved in neuroprotection, angiogenesis, glucose metabolism and cell survival. For example, HIF-1α increases the expression of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), which promotes angiogenesis and improves oxygen delivery to damaged tissues. In addition, HIF-1α induces the expression of genes encoding anti-apoptotic proteins, such as B-cell lymphoma 2 (Bcl-2), which protect neurons from cell death [ 126 , 127 ]. HIF-1α plays a key role in reprogramming cellular metabolism in response to hypoxia. It induces the expression of genes encoding glycolytic enzymes and glucose transporters, which increases the ability of cells to produce energy under low oxygen conditions. HIF-1α also up-regulates the expression of pyruvate kinase (PDK1), which inhibits pyruvate dehydrogenase activity, reducing ATP production by mitochondria and reducing oxidative stress [ 128 ]. Modulation of HIF-1α activity represents a promising therapeutic strategy for treating HI neurodegeneration. HIF-1α inhibitors could be used to reduce excessive angiogenesis and cell proliferation in pathological conditions such as cancer. HIF-1α activators, on the other hand, can promote neuronal survival and tissue regeneration in oxygen-deficient conditions. Cell death pathways Mitochondria play a central role in integrating signals leading to cell death following HI. Early events after reperfusion such as massive increase in mitochondrial reactive oxygen species (mROS), rapid influx of Ca²⁺ into mitochondria, and loss of membrane potential trigger molecular cascades that can lead to several distinct modules of cell death, with a clear involvement of mitochondrial organelles. Mechanistically, the most important links between mitochondria and cell death include: the opening of the mPTP and the resulting loss of ΔΨm, the release of pro-apoptotic factors (cytochrome c, apoptosis inducing factor - AIF) from mitochondria, delivery of mitochondrial damage-associated molecular patterns (mtDAMP) (e.g., mtDNA) stimulating inflammasomes, and mitochondrial quality disorders (ineffective mitophagy, pathological fragmentation) promoting the accumulation of dysfunctional mitochondria. These processes are mechanistically linked to the clinical manifestations following HI [ 129 , 130 ]. In models of HI, the mitochondrial intrinsic pathway of apoptosis is widely recognized as key in the pathomechanism of neuronal damage. Excessive production of ROS and disturbances in the balance between anti-apoptotic members of the Bcl-2 family (e.g., Bcl-2, Bcl-xL, Mcl-1) and proapoptotic BAX activate the permeability of the outer mitochondrial membrane, leading to the release of cytochrome-c and the activation of caspase-9 and caspase-3 [ 131 , 132 ]. Under hypoxic-ischemic conditions, especially in the adolescent brain, BAX-dependent mitochondrial permeabilization is a key pathway of cell death. Given that the expression levels of Bcl-2 and Bcl-xL are modulated after birth and that their increased levels (e.g., in a transgene model overexpressing Bcl-xL) reduce the extent of damage in HI models, the regulation of this balance shows neuroprotective potential [ 132 ]. Neurotrophic factors such as insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1), Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF), and hexarellin also exhibit protective effects, partly by restoring the normal ratio between Bcl-2-like proteins, which translates into inhibition of caspase activation and reduction of neuronal damage [ 132 ]. Necrosis and necroptosis reflect the more “drastic” consequences of severe mitochondrial dysfunction. The complete collapse of oxidative phosphorylation and a rapid decline in ATP promote necrosis. Necroptoza, on the other hand, is regulated by the receptor-interacting serine/threonine-protein kinase 1 (RIPK1) → receptor-interacting serine/threonine kinase 3 (RIPK3) → mixed lineage kinase domain-like protein (MLKL). It is activated in conditions where apoptosis is inhibited by the lack of caspase activity (e.g., caspase-8 inhibition) and interacts with mitochondrial disorders (increased mROS, impaired mitophagy). In models of cerebral ischemia, inhibition of RIPK1/RIPK3 provides significant protection, highlighting the clinical importance of necroptoza in the pathogenesis of HI [ 133 ]. Pyroptosis is an inflammasome-mediated, caspase-1-dependent form of cell death. It is linked to mitochondria indirectly via mtROS and released mtDAMPs, which stimulate NLR family pyrin domain containing 3 (NLRP3). Inflammasome activation leads to caspase-1 activation, pore formation by Gasdermin D (GSDMD), and IL-1β/IL-18 release, causing inflammation and secondary tissue damage. In ischemia-reperfusion models, there is evidence for NLRP3 involvement in injury progression, suggesting that mtROS reduction and mtDAMP neutralization may limit pyroptosis [ 134 ]. Autophagy-associated death (including pathological mitophagy/autosis) plays a dual role: moderate mitophagy removes damaged mitochondria and is protective, while excessive or dysfunctional mitophagy can lead to mitochondrial mass loss, ATP deficiency, and cell death. The balance between mitochondrial biogenesis and mitophagy determines the cell’s ability to recover function after HI; disturbances in this balance promote the transition from adaptation to death [ 135 ]. From a pharmacological point of view, mitochondria offer several points of intervention: mPTP blockade in the critical reperfusion window, reduction of RET-dependent mROS production in complex I, targeting Bcl-2/Bax signaling, modulation of RIPK pathways, and control of inflammasome activation. Unfortunately, attempts at clinical translation (e.g., mPTP inhibitors) have encountered limitations related to the therapeutic window, bioavailability, and patient heterogeneity-highlighting the need for precise patient selection and a multidimensional therapeutic approach [ 136 ]. A key mechanism for this process is the regulated in development and DNA damage 1 (REDD1) protein, which is induced by hypoxia and binds to the 14-3-3 protein, leading to the release of the hamartin/tuberin (TSC1/TSC2) complex from the inhibitory effects of 14-3-3. This in turn activates TSC1)/TSC2, which inhibits mTORC1 activity, reducing the phosphorylation of its targets, such as S6K1 and 4E-BP1 [ 143 , 144 ]. In addition, hypoxia can lead to phosphorylation of HIF-1α by ataxia telangiectasia mutated (ATM) kinase, which also contributes to decreased mTORC1 activity [ 145 ]. Under hypoxia, mTOR activation can promote cell survival by regulating protein synthesis and energy metabolism. mTORC1 is activated in response to stress signals, leading to increased protein synthesis and cell growth, as well as the regulation of autophagy, a process that helps remove damaged organelles and proteins. Autophagy is particularly important under hypoxia, as it helps cells survive under stress by recycling cellular components [ 146 ]. Modulation of mTOR activity represents a promising therapeutic strategy for treating hypoxic-ischemic neurodegeneration. Inhibitors of mTOR, such as rapamycin, can be used to regulate cellular processes and protect neurons from hypoxia-induced damage. In addition, research into specific mTORC1 and mTORC2 inhibitors may provide new therapeutic strategies to precisely modulate the activity of these complexes to protect neurons [ 145 , 147 ]. Although preliminary studies show promising results, translating these findings into clinical practice faces numerous challenges. One of the studies being conducted in this direction are this one using rapamycin in neurodegeneration caused by Alzheimer’s disease performed by Spilman et al. [ 148 ]. Another example is the study performed by Wang et al. [ 149 ] related to the regulation of the Nek6 protein on autophagy regulation through mTOR signaling pathways after neurodegeneration caused by cerebral ischemia [ 133 ]. However further research is needed to understand the mechanisms of action of mTOR and to evaluate the safety and efficacy of therapies targeting this pathway in clinical trials. Nonetheless, the mTOR pathway offers new opportunities for the treatment of hypoxic-ischemic neurodegeneration. TRAP1 pathway TRAP1 (tumor necrosis factor receptor-associated protein 1), also known as HSP75 (heat shock protein 75), is a mitochondrial chaperone protein belonging to the HSP90 family of proteins. TRAP1 plays a key role in regulating mitochondrial dynamics, redox homeostasis and protection against oxidative stress and apoptosis [ 150 ]. This protein acts as a molecular chaperone, promoting protein folding and maintaining mitochondrial integrity, even under cellular stress conditions. Under hypoxia, TRAP1 helps regulate the balance between mitochondrial fusion and fission by interacting with DRP1, a key regulator of mitochondrial fission. TRAP1 affects the expression of proteins associated with mitochondrial dynamics, such as Mitochondrial fission 1 protein (Fis1), Mfn1 and Mfn2, which is crucial for maintaining mitochondrial structure and function [ 150 ]. Under hypoxia, increased production of ROS can lead to damage to proteins, lipids and DNA. TRAP1 helps neutralize ROS and protect cells from oxidative damage [ 151 ]. In addition, TRAP1 regulates the stress response in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), which is crucial for maintaining cellular homeostasis [ 152 ]. TRAP1 also plays a role in regulating apoptosis. Under stress conditions, TRAP1 can inhibit the release of cytochrome c from mitochondria, which prevents the activation of caspases and the initiation of apoptosis. Studies have shown that modulation of TRAP1 activity can protect neurons from hypoxia-induced cell death, which is important for protecting the brain from ischemia-induced damage [ 151 ]. This represents a promising therapeutic target for the treatment of hypoxic-ischemic neurodegeneration. TRAP1 inhibitors can be used to modulate mitochondrial dynamics and protect cells from oxidative stress and apoptosis. In addition, research on specific TRAP1 inhibitors may provide new therapeutic strategies to precisely modulate the activity of this protein to protect neurons. Inhibition of mPTP Mitochondrial permeability transition pore plays a key role in regulating mitochondrial membrane permeability, which is important in the context of hypoxic-ischemic neurodegeneration. The mPTP is a multicomponent protein complex that, in response to cellular stress such as excessive calcium concentration or oxidative stress, can open, leading to loss of mitochondrial membrane potential, impaired ATP production and cell death [ 153 ]. Mechanism of action of mPTP The opening of mPTP leads to a rapid and uncontrolled flow of ions and small molecules across the inner mitochondrial membrane, resulting in mitochondrial depolarization and interruption of ATP production. This phenomenon is a key step in the mechanisms leading to apoptosis and cell necrosis [ 154 ]. Under conditions of hypoxia and ischemia, excessive calcium concentrations and increased ROS production can induce the opening of mPTP, leading to nerve cell damage [ 136 ]. Thus, cyclosporin A protects mitochondria from depolarization, loss of mitochondrial membrane potential, and interruption of ATP production. Cyclosporin A also helps reduce oxidative stress, which is one of the main mechanisms leading to nerve cell damage from HI. By preventing the opening of the mPTP, cyclosporin A reduces the production of ROS in the mitochondria, which protects cells from oxidative damage. Studies have shown that cyclosporin A can protect neurons from HI induced cell death [ 159 – 161 ]. Although preclinical studies show promising results, further research is needed to understand the mechanisms of action of mPTP and to evaluate the safety and efficacy of therapies targeting this pathway in clinical trials. Nonetheless, inhibition of mPTP opening offers new opportunities for the treatment of hypoxic-ischemic neurodegeneration, with the potential to improve clinical outcomes for patients [ 159 ]. Mitochondrial transplantation Mitochondrial replacement therapy (mitochondrial transplantation, mitotransfer) is a modern neuroprotective strategy, especially in models of HI brain damage. In preclinical studies, healthy mitochondria were typically isolated from autologous cells or animal donors and then delivered to damaged neurons or glial cells in vitro (by direct addition to cultures) or in vivo (by intracerebral, intraperitoneal) [ 162 – 164 ]. Once incorporated into target cells, mitochondria integrate into the endogenous mitochondrial system, restoring energy homeostasis by increasing ATP production and stabilizing mitochondrial membrane potential. At the same time, they reduce the excessive production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and inhibit the activation of apoptotic pathways, such as the caspase cascade, which limits cell death [ 165 ]. In addition, exogenous mitochondria interact with microglia and oligodendrocyte progenitors, modulating the immune phenotype of microglia and promoting myelination, which contributes to improved neuronal function and synaptic network restoration [ 166 ]. Mechanistically, mitotransfer works by increasing the NAD⁺/NADH ratio, activating SIRT1/PGC-1α pathways, and reducing respiratory chain complex dysfunction, leading to improved oxidative metabolism and reduced oxidative stress [ 167 ]. Despite promising results, mitochondrial replacement therapy still faces a number of significant preclinical and translational challenges. A key issue remains the source of mitochondria, which can be autologous, allogeneic, or exogenous; each of these options carries risks of immune response, genetic and metabolic incompatibility, and limitations in the availability and number of mitochondria for administration [ 168 , 169 ]. Optimizing isolation and storage methods is equally important, as mitochondria are susceptible to membrane damage and loss of function during prolonged storage—studies indicate that their activity declines after one hour on ice, and cryopreservation requires appropriate protective agents to maintain structural and functional integrity [ 169 ]. Another challenge is the effective and precise delivery of mitochondria to target cells. Preclinical studies have used direct tissue injections and systemic administration, but also innovative carriers such as extracellular vesicles (EVs) or MSCs loaded with mitochondria [ 170 ]. However, the BBB is a significant limitation in neurological applications and requires the development of new, safe, and efficient delivery strategies [ 171 ]. In addition, data on the long-term stability of transplanted mitochondria, their integration with the endogenous mitochondrial system, and their impact on the functioning of cellular recipients remain limited. Furthermore, the immune response that mitochondria from other sources may elicit is not fully understood. Reports indicate both a lack of pro-inflammatory response after autologous transplants and the immunogenic potential of allogeneic mitochondria, particularly in the context of DAMP [ 169 ]. Finally, before translation to the clinic, it is necessary to determine the optimal dose, frequency of therapy, and timing of administration to maximize therapeutic effects and minimize the risk of adverse events [ 172 ]. Nanocarriers of therapeutic molecules Therapeutic molecules can often have difficulty reaching mitochondria due to the multi-layered structure of the cell and the specific biological barriers of the organism itself. To this end, a lot of research is being conducted on nanomaterials that can selectively target to mitochondria, and represent a promising tool for precise and effective delivery of therapies directly to their site of action. The process of targeting mitochondria with therapeutic molecules can be achieved through several strategies. One of the most commonly described strategies is one that uses the mitochondrial membrane potential as a directional signal. Another described in publications mechanism for selective targeting of mitochondria is using lipid components that respond to the mitochondrial membrane [ 173 ]. One of the nanoparticles that exploit the potential of the mitochondrial membrane is PLGA-PEG-TPP. PLGA-PEG nanoparticles with a triphenylphosphonium group (TPP) are drug carriers designed for targeted delivery of therapeutic substances to mitochondria [ 174 ]. The PLGA (poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid)) nanoparticle is a biodegradable and biocompatible polymer with a polyethylene glycol (PEG) molecule embedded on its surface. PEG increases the solubility and stability of nanoparticles in plasma and helps avoid phagocytosis. A terminal lipophilic triphenylphosphonium cation (TPP⁺) was attached to this molecule. The combination of nanoparticles with the TPP group - allows them to selectively accumulate in mitochondria due to the negative potential of the mitochondrial membrane. In addition, the appropriate size and hydrophilic-lipophilic properties of these nanoparticles allow them to cross the BBB, making them promising drug carriers for the treatment of neurodegenerative diseases [ 174 , 175 ]. One of the molecules using specific components of the mitochondrial membrane as an attachment site described in the literature is the hemi-gramicidin S (hemi-GS) peptide. This peptide is a synthetic derivative of the natural antibiotic gramicidin S, specifically designed for targeted transport of therapeutic substances to the IMM. Unlike classical mitochondria-targeted compounds such as TPP⁺, the action of hemi-GS does not depend on the mitochondrial membrane potential. This makes them particularly useful in cells where the mitochondrial membrane potential is disrupted. The structure of hemi-GS is an amphipathic cyclic peptide containing hydrophobic and cationic groups, which allows it to build selectively into lipid membranes. Hemi-GS peptides have a high affinity for cardiolipin, a phospholipid found almost exclusively in the inner mitochondrial membrane. Once bound to cardiolipin, hemi-GS integrates into the IMM, enabling the delivery of drugs, fluorescent probes or antioxidants [ 173 , 176 ]. With appropriate chemical modification, these peptides can be conjugated to a variety of therapeutic or imaging payloads and used as precise tools for mitochondrial research or targeted therapy. The examples presented here represent some of the possibilities from the carriers of therapeutic molecules targeting mitochondria selectively described in the literature. In the case of neurodegenerative events, when designing and testing such molecules, in addition to their selective action on relevant structures in the cell, one must also keep in mind the ability of these molecules to penetrate the BBB). Therefore, further research is needed to evaluate efficacy of such nanocarriers and safety in a clinical context. Molecules modulating cell protection and injury response after reperfusion At the end, it is worth mentioning few selected molecules and kinases that modulate cell protection in ischemic-reperfusion conditions. Although most of the available data come from studies conducted on cardiomyocytes, their role in perinatal neurodegeneration remains poorly characterized. Particularly interesting regulators include STAT3, melusin, NLRP3 inflammasome, and connexin 43 (Cx43), whose activity are directly related to mitochondrial function. STAT3, in addition to its classic transcriptional role, performs non-genomic functions in mitochondria, limiting reverse electron transport, stabilizing membrane potential, and reducing excessive ROS emission, which delays the opening of the mPTP [ 177 ]. Melusina acts as a β1-integrin adaptor and supports metabolic tuning, improving substrate flexibility and protecting mitochondria from calcium overload, which increases the resistance of cells to oxidative and bioenergetic stress [ 178 ]. In turn, the NLRP3 inflammasome, activated by mitochondrial signals such as mROS or mtDNA, integrates redox stress with the inflammatory response. Its excessive activity intensifies pyroptosis and secondary damage, although under physiological conditions it can stabilize electron flow in the respiratory chain [ 179 ]. The mitochondrial pool of Cx43 also plays an important role, regulating potassium ion uptake, bioenergetics, and ROS signaling, with its phosphorylation by CK1 kinase proving crucial for the activation of preconditioning mechanisms [ 180 , 181 ]. Although the majority of these observations come from cardiomyocyte studies, their inclusion in the present discussion is justified for several reasons. First, mitochondria across highly metabolic tissues including the heart and developing brain share conserved signaling pathways governing calcium handling, mPTP regulation, redox balance, and stress-induced preconditioning. Recent integrative reviews (2020–2024) emphasize that cardio-protective mechanisms such as STAT3-dependent mitochondrial signaling, NLRP3-driven redox-inflammatory coupling, or Cx43-mediated ion regulation are not cardiomyocyte-specific, but represent universal mitochondrial stress-response modules that are also detectable in neurons and glial cells, although less extensively studied [ 182 – 184 ]. Second, perinatal HI activates molecular cascades overlapping with those observed in reperfused myocardium particularly those related to oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, and inflammatory amplification suggesting that data from cardiology can provide biologically relevant hypotheses for neuroprotection research. Third, the scarcity of direct studies in the developing brain highlights the need to draw on well-established mitochondrial frameworks to identify potential therapeutic targets. For this reason, the molecules discussed here are presented as promising but still insufficiently explored candidates whose role in neuronal and glial responses to HI requires systematic investigation. Their characterization may guide future studies aimed at developing mitochondrial-targeted strategies for preventing or mitigating neurodegeneration induced by perinatal HI. Conclusion Hypoxic-ischemic neurodegeneration remains a complex phenomenon in which mitochondria play a complex role, both as primary targets of damage and as active modulators of pathological processes. Disorders of their function include bioenergetic dysregulation, excessive production of ROS, fragmentation of the mitochondrial network, and defects in the mechanisms of fusion, division, and mitophagy. These processes result in an intensifying cycle of oxidative stress, mitochondrial DNA damage, calcium homeostasis disorders, and apoptosis initiation, which promotes the progression of neurodegeneration. Preclinical studies have shown that pharmacological modulation of mitochondrial dynamics (e.g., inhibition of Drp1, activation of Mfn2), stimulation of the Nrf2/ARE pathway, or promotion of mitochondrial biogenesis can effectively reduce neuronal damage. However, their clinical translation is hampered by problems with bioavailability, insufficient selectivity towards mitochondria, and potential side effects in tissues not affected by neurodegenerative processes. In addition, traditional antioxidants show low clinical efficacy, highlighting the need to develop mitochondria-targeted carriers, such as lipid nanoparticles or systems conjugated with mitochondrial peptides. Current data suggest that effective therapeutic strategies should integrate various approaches - modulation of mitochondrial dynamics, bioenergetic stabilization, reduction of inflammatory responses, and support for the regenerative mechanisms of neurons and glial cells. At the same time, it is crucial to develop biomarkers of mitochondrial function and oxidative stress that will enable precise monitoring of treatment efficacy. In summary, mitochondria are central to the pathogenesis and potential treatment of hypoxic-ischemic neurodegeneration. Further progress requires the integration of basic and clinical research and the development of precision drug delivery technologies that will enable the effective exploitation of the potential of mitochondrial therapies in medical practice.
