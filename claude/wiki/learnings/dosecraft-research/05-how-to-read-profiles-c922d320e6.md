# 05-how-to-read-profiles

Every compound in the DoseCraft Library follows the same structured template. Understanding this format lets you compare any two compounds at a glance and make informed decisions about your protocol.

Each profile in the Compound Library contains these standardized fields. Here is what each one means and why it matters:

| Field | What It Tells You |
|---|---|
| Classification | The compound's primary functional category (e.g., systemic repair, metabolic optimizer, immune modulator). Tells you what it does at the highest level. |
| Source | Where the compound originates — natural protein fragment, synthetic analog, mitochondrial-derived, etc. Helps you understand its biological context. |
| Mechanism of Action | The specific biochemical pathways the compound activates or suppresses. The "how it works" at the molecular level. |
| Dosing Protocol | Standard dose, injury/acute dose, and maintenance dose with exact quantities, frequencies, and durations. |
| Root Causes Targeted | Which of the three root causes (Inflammation, Insulin Resistance, ATP Shortage) this compound primarily addresses. The core of protocol design. |

| Field | What It Tells You |
|---|---|
| Evidence Lane Rating |
Clinical

Experimental
The quality tier of supporting data. Clinical = published studies. Expert = practitioner protocols. Experimental = early-stage or animal data. |
| Cycling | Whether the compound requires cycling (on/off periods) or can be run continuously. Prevents receptor downregulation and maintains efficacy. |
| Administration | Route (SubQ, oral, topical), injection site guidance, needle gauge, and timing relative to meals or training. |
| Stacking Notes | Which named stacks include this compound, synergies with other compounds, and sequencing considerations. |
| Warnings | Contraindications, common misconceptions, scam alerts (e.g., oral formulations), and interactions to watch. |

Not all evidence is created equal. The DoseCraft Protocol Bible uses a three-lane evidence system to help you understand exactly how much confidence to place in each compound's claims. Here is how to interpret each lane and what it means for your decision-making.

This compound has been evaluated in published, peer-reviewed human clinical trials. Data exists from controlled studies with defined endpoints, dosing protocols, and safety monitoring.

This compound is widely used by experienced clinical practitioners with documented patient outcomes, but formal clinical trials may be limited or absent. Protocols derive from practitioner experience across hundreds or thousands of patients.

Evidence is primarily from animal studies, in vitro research, or very early human data. The compound shows strong mechanistic promise but lacks the depth of clinical or practitioner validation.

When evaluating a compound, use this four-factor framework to gauge your personal confidence level:

| Factor | High Confidence | Lower Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| Evidence Lane | Clinical lane with multiple published trials | Experimental lane with animal data only |
| Safety History | Decades of human use; FDA-approved in any country; well-characterized side effect profile | Novel compound; limited human exposure data; safety profile based on short-term trials |
| Mechanism Clarity | Clear, well-understood biochemical pathway; mechanism confirmed at molecular level in humans | Proposed mechanism based on in vitro models; multiple competing theories of action |
| Practitioner Adoption | Widely used by leading practitioners with consistent reported outcomes across diverse patient populations | Used by a small number of practitioners; limited outcome reporting; protocols vary widely |

A compound with Clinical evidence, decades of safety data, clear mechanism, and wide practitioner adoption (e.g., Thymosin Alpha-1) warrants high confidence. A compound with Experimental evidence and limited human data (e.g., SS-31 for longevity) warrants cautious exploration under practitioner guidance.

The compound profile format is designed for rapid comparison. Here is how to use it to select the right compound(s) for a specific goal, followed by a complete worked example.

Follow this 5-step decision process whenever you need to select a compound or stack for a specific objective:

Is your primary problem inflammation (#1), insulin resistance (#2), or ATP shortage (#3)? Use the Root Cause Matrix to filter compounds that target your dominant issue.

Among compounds targeting your root cause, which have the strongest evidence? Prioritize Clinical over Expert over Experimental when multiple options exist for the same goal.

Check the Stacking Notes field. Do any of your candidate compounds appear in the same named stack? Compounds designed to work together will deliver superior results to isolated use.

Consider administration route (can you self-inject?), cycling requirements, cost, and availability. The best compound is the one you will actually use consistently.

Check for contraindications, scam alerts (e.g., oral formulations of injectable-only peptides), and interactions with other compounds in your stack. Eliminate any compound with a warning that applies to your situation.
