Abstract Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) is characterized by aggressive metastatic behavior driven through epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT). CD1d, a non-classical MHC molecule, modulates immune responses via Natural Killer T (NKT) cells and is implicated in tumor progression. This study examines the effect of CD1d inhibition on EMT in TNBC. We demonstrate that Zerumbone (ZER, 20 µM), a phytochemical, downregulates CD1d, reducing EMT markers, cell migration, and adhesion in MDA-MB-468 and MDA-MB-231 TNBC cell lines. Conversely, CD1d upregulation by α-galactosylceramide (AGC, 2 µM) and thymosin α-1 (TA, 2 µM) promotes EMT, enhances migration and adhesion. Immunofluorescence and Western blot analyses confirmed ZER suppresses CD1d, vimentin, N-cadherin, and FASN, while increasing E-cadherin, indicating mesenchymal-to-epithelial transition (MET). AGC and TA elevate CD1d and EMT markers. Molecular docking and dynamics simulations show ZER’s stable binding to CD1d (-8.13 kcal/mol), supporting the in vitro data and it is predicted to act as a potential CD1d antagonist. These findings suggest that CD1d inhibition may represent a potential strategy to attenuate EMT associated phenotypes in TNBC. Introduction Breast cancer (BCa) remains one of the leading causes of cancer-related morbidity and mortality among women worldwide [ 1 , 2 ]. Globally, it accounts for approximately 12% of all new cancer cases diagnosed among women [ 1 ]. In the United States, breast cancer incidence has shown a gradual increase of about 0.5% per year [ 3 ]. Despite advances in conventional treatments, such as surgery, chemotherapy, and radiotherapy, their therapeutic outcomes are often compromised by adverse effects, tumor relapse, and the development of resistance [ 4 ]. A significant challenge in breast cancer management lies in the intratumoral heterogeneity and the complex interplay of tumor microenvironment (TME) factors such as cancer stem cells, drug resistance, and immune evasion, which collectively contribute to poor prognosis and therapeutic failure [ 5 ]. EMT is a critical process that enhances cancer cell motility, invasiveness, and metastatic potential [ 6 ]. Therefore, strategies aimed at reversing EMT or halting its progression are of significant therapeutic interest. TNBC, a highly aggressive subtype of BCa, lacks expression of estrogen receptor (ER), progesterone receptor (PR), and HER2. It exhibits pronounced metastatic behavior and a high degree of cellular plasticity mediated through EMT [ 7 ]. Recent studies have emphasized the interplay between tumor cells and immune regulation in TNBC progression [ 8 , 9 ]. In this context, NKT cells, a specialized subset of T cells, have emerged as critical immunoregulatory agents. They recognize lipid antigens presented by CD1d, a non-classical MHC class I-like molecule [ 10 ]. CD1d expression is pivotal in shaping the NKT cell-mediated immune response and is increasingly implicated in tumor immune evasion and progression. Depending on the TME, CD1d-NKT interactions may either suppress or promote tumor growth [ 11 ]. Recently investigations have increasingly focused on boosting CD1d mediated NKT cell activity within the TME through CAR NKT approaches which have shown improved breast cancer prognosis [ 12 – 14 ]. Other approaches targeting CD1d on antigen-presenting cells (APCs) to modulate NKT immune responses have also shown promise. Some of the alternatives of AGC like 7DW8-5, a more stable analog are currently under investigations against cancer [ 15 ]. However, the potential limitations associated with these strategies are targeting more on immune cells rather than on tumor cells. Although CD1d expression and lipid antigen presentation classically occur through APCs like dendritic cells, macrophages, and B cells, several cancer cells, including TNBC cells, also express CD1d. The role of CD1d expressed on tumor cells appears to differ across cancer types, but its specific function in TNBC has not yet been studied in detail. However, with some previous findings, CD1d can be crucial and seems to play a direct role in tumor progression [ 16 , 17 ]. In this study we investigated the intrinsic role of CD1d in TNBC cellular proliferation and metastasis. Targeting CD1d to modulate the tumor-immune interface and interrupt EMT-related signaling could represent a promising avenue for TNBC therapy. Naturally occurring phytochemicals are gaining recognition for their potential anticancer activities due to their low toxicity and multifaceted mechanisms, including immunomodulatory, anti-inflammatory, and anti-metastatic effects [ 18 , 19 ]. Among them, zerumbone (ZER) a sesquiterpene derived from Zingiber zerumbet has demonstrated potent pharmacological activity against various cancers [ 20 ]. In contrast, compounds such as α-galactosylceramide (AGC), a glycolipid derived from marine sponge Agelas mauritianus , and thymosin α−1 (TA), a thymic peptide, enhance NKT cell activation and immune stimulation [ 21 , 22 ]. The current study explores the role of CD1d in regulating EMT and metastatic behavior in TNBC. Specifically, it examines the effect of CD1d inhibition by ZER and its comparison with CD1d activation using AGC and TA. Our findings suggest how ZER-mediated CD1d suppression attenuates EMT markers, migration, and adhesion, indicating a MET in TNBC cells and potentially reversing aggressive tumor features. These findings support further investigation of CD1d as a promising therapeutic target for EMT-driven metastasis in TNBC. Materials and methods Cell lines and culture conditions TNBC cell lines MDA-MB-468 and MDA-MB-231 were obtained from ATCC and maintained in DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS (fetal bovine serum) and 1% Pen-Strep antibiotics. Cells were grown and maintained at 37 °C in a humidified CO 2 incubator containing 5% CO 2 . For all experiments, the treatment concentrations of the biomolecules used were ZER (20 µM), AGC (2 µM), and TA (2 µM), along with the control (DMSO). Chemicals and reagents ZER, AGC, GAPDH, and β-actin antibody, (3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)−2,5-diphenyl tetrazolium bromide (MTT), were purchased from Sigma Aldrich. TA was purchased from CPC Scientific. CD1d monoclonal antibody was obtained from ThermoFisher. PVDF membrane and enhanced chemiluminescence (ECL) reagents were purchased from Bio-Rad. FBS was obtained from Invitrogen-Life Technologies. All other chemicals used were of the highest purity grade. Clonogenic assay The TNBC cancer cells were plated in twelve-well plates at a cell density of 500 cells/well and treated with biomolecules (Control, ZER, AGC, and TA) as mentioned in Sect. 2.1 . The cells were then incubated for 1 week. The medium was replaced with fresh media every 2 days, and at the end of the experiment, the cells were washed using PBS. Colonies were fixed with methanol and stained with 0.5% crystal violet solution. The plate containing colonies was imaged using a Chemidoc (Bio-Rad) imager. Colonies were quantified using ImageJ software and plotted as a graph. Transwell migration assay In a 24-well plate, (1 × 10 5 cells/well) TNBC cells were seeded in serum-free media, into the upper chamber of a transwell with respective wells containing treatment controls, ZER, AGC, and TA. The lower chamber was provided with 10% serum-containing media as a chemoattractant, followed by incubation for 24 h in a CO 2 incubator. Then, the upper chamber was removed, and the cells were carefully extracted from it using a cotton swab, followed by fixation of the cells trapped on the membrane with 4% paraformaldehyde for 20 min. Crystal violet (0.1%) solution was poured onto the membrane and incubated for 10 min at room temperature. The excess stains were washed with water and left to air dry. Images were captured in a Chemidoc imager. Crystal violet-stained cells were analyzed for the integrated intensity using ImageJ software and represented in the form of graphs. Knockdown of CD1d through SiRNA transfection MDA-MB-231 and MDA-MB-468 cells were seeded to to reach approximately 70% confluence and then transfected with MISSION esiRNA targeting CD1d (EHU146871-50UG, Sigma-Aldrich, USA) at a final concentration of 40 nM using Lipofectamin 3000 (Invitrogen, USA) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. A non-targeting siRNA (MISSION siRNA Universal Negative Control 1, Sigma-Aldrich, USA) was used as the control. After 48 h, cells were processed for immunofluorescence staining. Knockdown efficiency was analyzed by the fluorescence imaging. Immunofluorescence imaging Cells were seeded and allowed to grow to 70% confluency in chamber slides, and then treated with biomolecules (Control, ZER, AGC, and TA) for 24 h, as described in Sect. 2.1 . At the end of the treatment, the media was removed and fixed with 0.2 mL of 4% paraformaldehyde, which was added gently from the sides of the well. Cells were allowed to be fixed for 15 min at room temperature. After fixation the cells were washed three times with 1x PBS and permeabilized with 0.1% Triton X 100 for 15 min at room temperature, followed by 3 times 1x PBS washing. F-actin used is primarily conjugated with a green fluorophore. The cells were washed three times in 1x PBS. Next, the cells were incubated in a DAPI (1 µg/mL) solution for 5–7 min. The wells were imaged under a fluorescence microscope using specific fluorescence filters. The images were taken at 200X and 400X magnification. Molecular docking and MD simulation studies Molecular docking analysis was performed to check the potential binding of the compounds with CD1d. The PDB structure of CD1d (PDB id: 1ZT4 ) was retrieved from the RCSB PDB database ( https://www.rcsb.org/ ). AUTODOCK Vina CMD, an open-source program for performing docking analysis, was used. Heteroatoms from the CD1d structure were removed, and hydrogen was added using Avogadro and UCSF Chimera. The grid box was constructed using the MGL tools, and the docking coordinates were calculated as (Center X = 30, Y = 20.37, and Z = 31). The 3D and 2D structures obtained after the docking analysis were visualized using the BIOVIDIA Discovery Studio ( https://www.3ds.com/products/biovia/discovery-studio ). Parameterization, ligand structure generation, and GROMACS input files were prepared using the CGENFF ( https://cgenff.com/ ). All studies were conducted up to 100 nanoseconds (ns) of runtime. All the libraries and dependencies used for the visualization and post-simulation analysis were downloaded using Python 23 packages. TCGA cohort expression correlation, survival analysis, and EMT status TCGA BRCA RNA-seq patient data were procured from UCSC Xena with STAR-FPKM ( n = 1226). Statistical analysis All experiments were performed in triplicate and data are presented as means ± standard deviation (SD). We focused on structural stability, flexibility, and ligand retention within the binding sites. Both AGC and ZER complex stabilities were predicted individually with CD1d alone, and then with CD1-αGalcer (agonist) complex. Figure 6 A represents the root mean square deviation (RMSD) plot for the CD1d-ZER complex, CD1d-AGC complex, both AGC and ZER complexed with CD1-αGalcer (agonist) complex, and the CD1d backbone serving as a control, which serves as a baseline for comparison. RMSD fluctuations indicate structural adjustments within the protein-ligand complex, with AGC displaying higher variability due to its dynamic interactions with the CD1d binding pockets. Consistent RMSD values for ZER complexed with CD1d suggest a more rigid interaction. When included, the unliganded CD1d (control) typically exhibited an RMSD value that fluctuated more broadly, highlighting the stabilizing effect of ligand binding on the overall protein motions. Despite these differences, both ligand-bound complexes remained well within a stable RMSD window of 1.5 nm, suggesting effective binding and no major unfolding events. Figure 6 B shows the per-residue flexibility over the 100 ns MD simulations for the CD1d-ZER, CD1d-AGC, as well as the AGC and ZER bound with CD1-αGalcer (agonist) complex. In all the complexes, more residues fluctuate below 0.3 nm, indicating moderate flexibility across the proteins. The key residues lining the CD1d binding groove (for example, Phe118, Val49, Leu412, Phe417, and Phe418) typically show lower RMSF values than more peripheral regions. This reduced mobility arises because these residues form direct contacts, such as hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, and electrostatic interactions with the bound AGC and ZER. Figure 6 C represents the radius of gyration (Rg) plot showing the compactness of the CD1d alone and in complexes (CD1d-αGalcercomplex) with AGC and ZER, with slightly higher variations for the AGC. This CD1d-AGC tends to show a slightly high radius of gyration fluctuation, indicating a mere expansion or change in the protein’s binding pockets. Typically, ligand-bound forms show marginally reduced or more stable Rg compared to the native state, suggesting that ligand interactions help maintain a more defined stable conformation. The Rg reflects the overall structural integrity, suggesting that AGC induces only minor conformational changes Fig. 6 C further shows that AGC maintains structural integrity throughout the simulation period. At the same time, the ZER exhibits a slight deviation, but it is within the safe range, indicating a reduced yet more substantial stability in its binding. Figure 6 D indicates the formation of more consistent H-bonds during the 100 ns run, further supporting the higher binding affinities. The formation and persistence of H-bonds highlight the stabilizing forces within the CD1d-AGC and CD1d-ZER complex. The hydrogen bonding pattern correlates with the binding efficiency of AGC and ZER to CD1d. The dynamic H-bond network was dominated by interactions with His68, Ile69, Phe77, and Leu148, aligning with computational predictions of stable CD1d-complexes. Patients with high CD1D expression exhibited significantly reduced overall survival (log-rank ( p = 3.53e-02) Discussion NKT cell-based cancer immunotherapy is an advanced and promising approach that has shown valuable outcomes in cancer patients [ 14 , 25 ]. NKT cells play a crucial role in regulating immune activities during pathological conditions by bridging the innate and adaptive immune systems [ 26 – 28 ]. However, tumors can modulate the number and function of NKT cells, altering the TME or the glycolipid antigens to escape immune surveillance and cytotoxicity [ 29 – 31 ]. The introduction of AGC has significantly impacted NKT cell-based cancer therapy by enhancing the number and activity of these cells. Despite these improvements, challenges remain, including the unresponsiveness or diminished responses of AGC in some instances, as well as the immunosuppressive functions present in the TME. Similarly, TA, a peptide hormone released by the thymus gland, regulates T cell development and plays a crucial role in immune response in diseases, including cancer [ 32 , 33 ]. TA exhibits anti-cancer activities by inducing various immune cells, including T cells, Natural Killer cells, macrophages, and dendritic cells, as evidenced by the literature [ 34 ]. The major limitations of TA against cancer therapy in clinical studies were reported for its short half-life and nonspecific tumor targeting [ 34 – 36 ]. CD1d is primarily known for its role in lipid antigen-presentation to NKT cells, however its intrinsic regulatory role in tumor cells remains underexplored [ 17 , 37 ]. A wide variety of tumor cells express CD1d; however, their expression levels differ among various tumors [ 11 , 38 ]. In breast cancer, the significance of CD1d regulating TNBC survival, and EMT has not been fully established. Though there is an important study done by Hix et al., 2011 highlighting the significance of CD1d in the breast cancer however, their main focus was on examining CD1d mediated immune evasion through NKT cells [ 16 ]. Here in this study, we are trying to examine the unidentified intrinsic role of CD1d on TNBC cells beyond regulating NKT cells activation. Besides directly contributing immune evasion and antigen presentation there could be another role of CD1d in promoting TNBC. Our study has demonstrated the differential CD1d expression pattern in MDA-MB-231 and MDA-MB-468 TNBC cells, correlated with the proliferative and migratory potential, suggesting its association with tumor aggressiveness. CD1d knockdown study markedly reduced in CD1d expression and cell viability supporting a role in TNBC cell survval. In addition, this study is the first to identify an alternative role of CD1d in TNBC cells possibly mediating survival and EMT signaling using CD1d modulators (AGC, TA and ZER). We found CD1d expression was enhanced following treatment with AGC and TA, suggesting that both agents act as CD1d agonists capable of augmenting tumorigenic processes in vitro. Interestingly, this observation provides an insight about how CD1d upregulation on TNBC may favor TNBC growth in the absence of NKT cells, rather than immune mediated elimination of tumor cells [ 39 – 41 ]. Although both agents enhance CD1d levels to different extents, they trigger the TNBC EMT pathway. The differential impacts could be due to the specific biological property of the cells. AGC is known to bind the CD1d molecule through the lipid antigen presentation pathway; however, TA does not have any direct evidence of binding to CD1d, but it can induce through the Erk/MAPK signaling pathway [ 17 , 37 , 42 ]. ZER, a bioactive phytochemical is also known to modulate immune cells and has the potential to regulate innate and adaptive immune responses [ 43 , 44 ]. In our previous study, we showed that ZER has the potential to reduce CD1d expression in breast cancer cells. Therefore, we have investigated whether suppression of CD1d through ZER treatment could substantially reduce TNBC proliferation, adhesion, and EMT. We found that ZER downregulated CD1d and led to a significant reduction in cell adhesion, wound healing and the migration potentials of TNBC cells. ZER was found to reduce vinculin and F-actin in both cell lines, suggesting its ability to disrupt the architecture of cancer cells and compromise further growth [ 45 , 46 ]. These evidences suggest that ZER may be used as a potential candidate for CD1d modulator with anti-cancer property [ 17 , 47 ]. The mechanistic link between CD1d and EMT signaling in TNBC likely involves the regulation of lipid antigen presentation pathways and cytoskeletal remodeling [ 48 , 49 ]. CD1d, being a lipid-presenting molecule, can influence the composition of membrane microdomains and lipid rafts, thereby affecting the localization and activity of EMT-associated molecules such as FASN, E-cadherin, and vimentin [ 50 – 54 ]. CD1d-expressing cells, such as adipocytes, macrophages, and dendritic cells, interact with NKT cells to regulate immune responses in a tissue microenvironment, which is a key factor in the development of EMT, however its direct association in tumor metastasis are not reported [ 55 ]. Our siRNA data demonstrated that silencing CD1d resulted in a reduction of vimentin expression and decreased migratory behavior in TNBC cells, suggesting that CD1d may modulate EMT pathway via cytoskeletal remodeling (Supplementary Fig. 1A, B &amp; C). Thus the role of CD1d in EMT is complex, however its loss or gain can regulate metastasis and immune evasion, depending on its expression on type of tumor cells as well as antigen-presenting cells. Molecular docking and molecular dynamics (MD) simulation analyses supported our in vitro findings. The molecular docking analysis, conducted using AutoDock Vina, revealed distinct differences in the binding interactions between CD1d and the two compounds, ZER and AGC. Both exhibited a stronger binding affinity, as evidenced by their numerous hydrogen bonds with critical residues, including Lysine 178, Leucine 174, Lysine 182, and Arginine 6. This continuous interaction with all these residues determines that both ligands stabilize the CD1d, thereby contributing to the downstream signaling pathways [ 56 ]. Coming to the MD simulations, it provided a more convincing insight into the dynamics and the determination of the stability of the receptor-ligand complexes. The root mean square deviation plots showed that both the ligands, ZER and AGC, implicated a stable binding with CD1d during the 100ns run. This behavior can be attributed to the ligand’s adaptation within the binding pockets and its interactions with the important residues. The root mean square fluctuations showed an increased flexibility towards the N-terminal regions, which are not related to the ligand binding, therefore affirming the stability of the binding site interactions. These Rg and RMSD analyses also justify these findings [ 57 ]. The ligand RMSD revealed more consistency in structural confirmations for AGC than for ZER, which exhibited gradual deviations. Conversely, the Rg corroborated these observations, indicating that AGC promoted more compactness than ZER; however, both showed considerable binding with CD1d. Energy calculations offered additional evidence for the stronger interactions of AGC &amp; ZER. The coulombic and Lennard-Jones interaction energies demonstrated higher electrostatic and van der Waals interactions for AGC, indicative of robust binding within the CD1d active sites. The minimum distance analysis revealed that ZER maintained closer proximity to the active sites, albeit with transient interactions, whereas AGC exhibited severe contact points. H-bond analysis underscored the significance of these interactions in stabilizing both complexes [ 58 ]. AGC showed consistency in the multiple hydrogen bond formations throughout a 100ns run, particularly with Lysine178 and Lysine182. These overall findings suggest that AGC function more effectively as agonists, whereas ZER may be useful as optional antagonists, showing stable interactions. These findings provide an overall understanding of ligand-CD1d interactions and lay the groundwork for future research in the field of therapeutic targeting of CD1d. Similarly, computational analysis of the TCGA-BRCA RNA-seq patient data revealed that CD1D expression is associated with EMT activation, thereby reducing overall survival and increasing the aggressiveness of TNBC. The outcomes of our study reveal an important link between CD1d expression and the tumorigenic potential of TNBC, specifically in terms of cell proliferation, adhesion, and migration. Together these finding expands the understanding of CD1d beyond its canonical role in NKT cell activation, revealing its intrinsic contribution to tumor cell plasticity (Fig. 9 ). Furthermore, the identification of ZER as a potential CD1d-modulating compound introduces a new avenue for therapeutic targeting. While our in silico docking and MD simulations indicate that ZER interacts with CD1d at key binding residues, additional biochemical validation is required to confirm this interaction. Nevertheless, the observed reduction in EMT markers and impaired cell adhesion following ZER treatment support its potential utility as a natural compound capable of attenuating CD1d-associated tumor progression in vitro. Additionally, the potential drugs AGC and TA, which have been used in clinical studies, may have a positive impact on immune cells; however, their long-term effects on tumor progression are not promising and warrant further investigation. Collectively, these findings highlight a previously underexplored role of CD1d in regulating EMT associated phenotypes in TNBC and suggest that targeting CD1d expression or its downstream pathways should be explored further as a potential therapeutic strategy. CD1d modulators regulating EMT markers in TNBC cells Although our findings suggest a possible involvement of CD1d in tumorigenesis, beyond its known role in lipid antigen presentation, there are certain limitations exist in the study. All the experiments conducted were primarily in vitro models limited to two TNBC cell lines, which may not completely represent all TNBC cells. In addition, the complexity of tumor–immune interactions in vivo is crucial, considering CD1d’s primary role in presenting lipid antigens through APCs and regulating NKT cells. The lack of an animal study limits our ability to evaluate the role of CD1d in tumor progression and metastasis under physiological conditions. We have taken ZER, AGC, and TA as modulators of CD1d; however, their direct physical interactions on the cell surface have been confirmed only through by in silico approach. To enhance understanding of the effect of AGC interaction with CD1d, the L363 antibody, as well as 7DW8-5, can be used as a more stable α-GalCer analog. The siRNA data shown in our study is just to validate the possible role of CD1d in the TNBC EMT process. Future work involving CD1d knockdown or knockout models, as well as co-culture studies with NKT cells, will confirm the mechanistic depth and provide insight into the immunoregulatory role. Moreover, the difference in the levels of Thymosin α1 and α-GalCer as agonists in TNBC cells suggests further exploration to identify their potential as immunomodulatory therapeutics. Together, these limitations of the study provide important directions for ongoing and future research to validate CD1d’s intrinsic and extrinsic roles in the context of TNBC.
