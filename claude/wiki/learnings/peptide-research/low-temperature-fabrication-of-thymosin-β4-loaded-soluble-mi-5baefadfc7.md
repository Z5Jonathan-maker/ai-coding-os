ABSTRACT Skin wound treatment is hindered by the poor penetration of large therapeutics and a lack of treatments that effectively regulate immune environments. While microneedles (MNs) can bypass the skin, they destabilize sensitive peptides, including thymosin β4 (Tβ4), whose immunomodulatory targets are not well understood, limiting their clinical use. This study introduces a novel fabrication method for Tβ4‐loaded soluble MNs at low temperatures using chitosan and sucrose, avoiding the denaturing conditions of traditional MN production. These MNs exhibit a uniform shape, high drug capacity (248.15 ± 1.37 µg/patch), quick dissolution within an hour, excellent biocompatibility, and significantly enhanced wound healing in mice. This study examines the mechanism by which Tβ4 accelerates wound healing, identifying the downregulated immune regulators Spp1, Vsig4, and IL22rɑ2 through RNA‐seq and DEG analysis. In vitro qPCR, western blot, and surface plasmon resonance (SPR) experiments demonstrate that Tβ4 specifically binds to the downregulated immune regulators Vsig4 (K D = 3.56 × 10 −6 m ) and IL22rɑ2 (K D = 9.69 × 10 −6 m ). This article explores how Tβ4 influences the wound immune microenvironment to aid healing, identifies its specific molecular targets, and moves beyond its general roles to offer new opportunities for drug development. Approximately four million cutaneous wounds are reported annually in affluent countries, and cases in developing nations continue to rise [ 2 ]. Beyond their impact on psychological, social, and physical health, wounds also diminish productivity and impose high treatment costs, creating a substantial financial strain on healthcare sectors, thereby emphasizing the urgent need for efficient wound treatment. Current standard therapies, including hydrogels, growth factor applications, and skin grafts, encounter considerable limitations. First, poor penetration remains a major issue, as the stratum corneum—the outermost barrier of the skin—obstructs more than 90% of macromolecular drugs exceeding 500 Da. Second, low bioavailability is a concern: topical peptides degrade rapidly in wound exudate, while systemic delivery often leads to off‐target effects. Third, inadequate immunomodulation persists, as most treatments fail to dynamically regulate the transition between the inflammatory and proliferation phases of wound healing. In response to these challenges, thymosin β4 (Tβ4), a peptide consisting of 43 amino acids with a molecular weight of 4963 Da, has emerged as a promising regenerative agent due to its multifaceted roles in accelerating angiogenesis [ 3 ], promoting vascular endothelial cell migration [ 4 ], inhibiting apoptosis [ 5 ], and reducing inflammation [ 6 ]. Tβ4 is found in most eukaryotic cells, with the exception of red blood cells [ 7 ]. As a highly conserved member of the Tβ family [ 8 ], Tβ4 aids wound healing by acting differentially during various stages of the process. In the hemostasis phase, it helps form blood clots by binding to actin, collagen, and fibrin. During inflammation [ 9 ], Tβ4 reduces inflammation by blocking the nuclear factor (NF)‐κB pathway and lowering inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin (IL)‐1 and tumor necrosis factor (TNF)‐α. It also prevents cell death by decreasing caspase 3 expression and adjusting the Bax/Bcl‐2 ratio to promote cell survival [ 10 ]. Tβ4 also reduces oxidative stress by inhibiting inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) and cyclooxygenase‐2 (COX‐2), thus reducing reactive oxygen species, nitric oxide, and prostaglandin 2α—all of which speed up healing [ 11 ]. During the proliferative phase, Tβ4 enhances angiogenesis by activating the PI3K/Akt and Notch/NF‐κB pathways [ 12 ], aids the migration of endothelial progenitor cells and prevents their apoptosis, thus supporting neovascularization [ 13 ]. In the tissue remodeling phase, Tβ4 boosts wound repair proteins, reduces myofibroblast formation, and increases MMP‐2 and MMP‐9 expression to minimize scarring. The complement and immune systems are crucial during wound healing, but the precise mechanisms through which they act in this context remain unclear—including Tβ4's regulatory effects on the immune microenvironment and its direct impact on immune cells such as T cells and neutrophils. Soluble MNs, which have recently emerged as a promising approach to transdermal administration, can effectively deliver macromolecule drugs into the skin [ 14 , 15 ]. Tβ4‐exosome‐based microneedles (MNs) have also been reported as a therapeutic strategy for wound healing [ 16 ]. In this approach, extracellular vesicles derived from T𝛽4‐overexpressing adipose‐derived stem cells were engineered and then integrated into MN patches. However, the extraction and purification process of T𝛽4‐modified adipose‐derived stem cells is extremely precise and technically demanding. Moreover, this treatment method faces challenges in clinical application due to the limited sources, difficulty in quality control, and the high costs of exosomes, which may hinder their clinical development. Modern approaches have facilitated the efficient synthesis of Tβ4 at lower costs [ 17 ]. Herein, we developed a low‐temperature drying method (at 4°C and 65% humidity) to prepare Tβ4 MNs for wound healing applications, as illustrated in Figure 1 . This study aimed to address the conventional challenges associated with the fabrication of soluble MNs for biomacromolecular drug applications, thereby enhancing their transdermal delivery and potentially leading to the development of innovative products to aid wound healing. We introduced a low‐temperature drying method (at 4°C and 65% humidity) for the formulation of peptide MNs. Tβ4 was loaded into CS and sucrose‐based MNs to promote skin permeability, which successfully promoted wound healing in mouse models. To explore the mechanism of Tβ4's regulatory effects on the immune microenvironment, RNA‐seq and DEG analyses were performed. We identified the downregulation of V‐set and immunoglobulin domain‐containing 4 (Vsig4), a member of the immunoglobulin superfamily involved in regulating T‐cell activity, as well as interleukin 22 receptor alpha 2 (IL22Rα2). These immune‐related molecules were identified as specific binding targets of Tβ4. Overall, our MN system offers an effective platform for Tβ4 delivery, thereby enhancing its translational potential in clinical wound management and advancing the understanding of its mechanisms in wound healing. They each contained a 10 × 10 MN array (Figure 2b ). The height, base axis, and center‐center width of each needle were 1000, 350, and 800 µm, respectively. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) imaging revealed conical tips (Figure 2c ). The drug‐loaded MNs exhibited uniform morphology and favorable appearance properties, with none appearing hollow. The tips were prepared intact, without any defects or breakage, and all possessed sharp points. Current MN fabrication often relies on heat‐based curing at high temperatures (typically &gt; 60°C) that can risk inducing protein denaturation. The findings are as follows: Under the selected protocol (4°C/65% RH/48 h), no significant alterations were detected in the HPLC chromatogram at the characteristic retention time of Tβ4 (7.9 min) after one week of storage at 4°C, thereby confirming the stability of Tβ4 integrity. In contrast, the alternative protocol (37°C/65% RH/48 h) resulted in broadening of the main Tβ4 peak and the emergence of distinct impurity peaks at retention times of 7.9 and 9.4 min after one week of storage at 37°C, indicating peptide degradation (Figure S1 ). Lyophilization at −80°C represents another technique that is commonly used to prepare MNs; however, it has strict requirements for preparation, and the material is prone to deformation at room temperature. The UV‐cured molding method requires the use of photosensitive polymers, which may restrict the application of certain materials with better biocompatibility that are not photosensitive (e.g., certain natural polymers). The equipment required for UV curing can also be prohibitively costly. By contrast, our method uses relatively mild conditions (4°C and 65% humidity), as well as simple and inexpensive equipment (a humidity chamber). It has previously been reported that Tβ4‐modified adipose‐derived stem cell extracellular vesicle MNs can be fabricated using 2‐hydroxy‐2‐methylpropiophenone (HMPP), gelatin methacryloyl (GelMA), and polyethylene glycol diacrylate (PEGDA) materials, through a process that also requires costly photoinitiators and photocuring [ 16 ]. In contrast, the CS and sucrose used in our preparation method are common materials for MN fabrication that are generally inexpensive and simple to produce on a large scale. 2.2 Drug‐Loading and Distribution Using Microscopy We used doxorubicin hydrochloride labeled with red fluorescence as a model drug, loaded onto the MN tips, and fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC) labeled with green fluorescence loaded onto the bases, to investigate drug‐loading and distribution within the MNs via inverted fluorescence and laser‐scanning confocal microscopy (Figure 2d ). The results indicated that the red fluorescent dye was uniformly distributed on the needle tips, while the green fluorescent dye was located on the bases—thus having achieved the targeted drug distribution. 2.3 Drug‐Excipient Interactions Fourier‐transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) analysis was conducted on Tβ4, Tβ4 MN, and blank MN samples to study drug‐excipient interactions (Figure 2e ). For the blank MNs, peaks at 3319, 2950, 1599, 1401, 1219, 1091, 918, and 850 cm −1 indicated bond vibrations that included O─H, C─H, C═O, and C─O. For the Tβ4 samples, peaks at 1637, 1521, 1390, and 1237 cm −1 corresponded to C═O, N─H, C─H, and C─O bond vibrations. Our analysis of the Tβ4 MNs revealed that their characteristic peaks were similar to those of the blank MNs. No new peaks were detected, and no previous ones were lost, as the only apparent changes involved peak intensities. This suggests that drug loading did not alter the chemical structures of the blank MNs. The reduced peak intensity and red shift of the O─H stretching peak at 3279 cm −1 indicated that hydrogen bond formation was involved, implying that the drug was primarily loaded onto the MNs through physical interactions. We therefore concluded that there was no interaction between the Tβ4 proteins and MN excipients during the process. 2.4 Mechanical and Hardness Testing We assessed the mechanical strength of our MN array to evaluate its ability to penetrate skin. Figure 3a shows the force‐displacement curves from our mechanical compression tests, which revealed that most of the MNs remained elastic, without plastic deformation. A sudden drop in the curve at 47.0 N indicated needle tip fracture, meaning that each needle was able to endure up to 0.47 N of force before breaking. These results demonstrate that they were able to rapidly and continuously release most of the Tβ4 drug within 45 min. This represents a significantly higher transdermal rate vs the 5 h release time that has been reported for Tβ4 ethanol nanocapsule gels [ 19 ]. Although Tβ4 hydrogels exhibit prolonged release kinetics, their large molecular weights may limit transdermal penetration, with only a fraction of the drug permeating the skin. By contrast, Tβ4 MNs facilitate precise transdermal delivery directly into the wound bed, achieving therapeutic efficacy with significantly lower treatment durations. 2.7 In Vivo Penetration and Degradation Next, we assessed how Tβ4 MNs dissolved on the skins of normal mice vs wound model ones. Optical and SEM images showed that, on normal murine skin, the MN tips showed no changes over the first 5 min. Approximately 20% of the needle body had dissolved within 30 min, 50% had dissolved by 1 h, 67% by 2 h, and nearly all by 3 h—although the base remained intact (Figure 4c–d ). Similar images of the experimental MNs in the murine wound model showed that approximately 25% of the needle dissolved within 1 min, 33% within 5 min, 50% within 30 min, and complete dissolution occurred within 1 h (Figure 4e, f ). The MNs dissolved from the base upward, with the tips disappearing completely after 1 h and the bases remaining intact. The dissolution rate of the MNs was faster when applied to wound sites vs normal skin, which we attributed to the higher concentration of interstitial fluid and relatively moist environments at such sites. Although the release rate of our MNs was relatively fast, their multi‐pathway therapeutic mechanism allows them to achieve long‐lasting therapeutic effects nonetheless. We hypothesize that this is because downstream signaling pathways are likely activated upon target binding, potentially initiating a cascade reaction that facilitates wound healing. 2.8 In Vitro Biocompatibility Biocompatibility serves as a critical parameter for assessing the quality of biomaterials. Blank control group, blank MN group, Tβ4 group, and rhIII‐collagen were used as controls to support comparison. Wound area (%) was defined as: residual wound area at day ‘X’ / wound area at day ‘0’ × 100%. The lack of a statistically significant difference in wound healing rates between the control group and the blank microneedle (MN) group indicates that the blank microneedle matrix did not exert any influence on the wound healing process. The substantial differences we consistently noted in terms of gap size, collagen deposition, and increased expression levels of VEGF, IL‐10, and α‐SMA between Tβ4 MN groups vs the other ones demonstrate that Tβ4 MNs promote wound healing by enhancing collagen deposition, angiogenesis, and tissue regeneration, while modulating inflammatory responses. 2.12 Transcriptional Profiles of Tβ4 MN‐Treated Wounds To study the gene alterations that took place following Tβ4‐MN treatment, we investigated the differentially expressed genes (DEGs), using cutoff criteria of |log 2 F − C| &gt; 2 and p value &lt; 0.05, between the control and Tβ4‐MN groups. These were plotted into a volcano plot using high‐throughput mRNA sequencing, ultimately allowing us to identify 416 DEGs following Tβ4‐MN induction. Among them, 174 (41.83%) genes were up‐regulated, while 242 (58.17%) were significantly down‐regulated (Figure 9a ). Notably, our heatmap revealed a significant upregulation of Krtap family genes (e.g., Krtap19–9b, Krtap31–1, and Krtap31–2) in the Tβ4‐MN group, while a number of genes related to biological tissue functions (e.g., Mmp8 and Mmp12) exhibited downregulation (Figure 9b ). These findings are consistent with those of previous related studies. To intuitively display the DEGs that resulted from the Tβ4‐MNs intervention, gene ontology (GO) terms and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway analysis were used to reveal further associations. GO analysis was performed via Fisher's exact test to assign categories of biological processes (BPs), cellular components (CCs), and molecular function (MF) to the DEGs (Figure 9c ). The BP category was mainly enriched in genes related to the activation and mobilization of granulocytes and leukocytes. In the CC category, the GO terms were mainly enriched in intermediate filaments, keratin filaments, contractile elements, and myofibrillar structures. The MF category was mainly enriched in actin interaction, oxidoreductase capability, peroxidase activity, and transferase enzyme activity. Our KEGG pathway analysis revealed that the DEGs were enriched in pathways related to wound healing, including complement and coagulation cascades, activation and chemotaxis of immune cells, cytoskeletal remodeling, and the regulation of oxidoreductase activity. Among these newly identified Tβ4‐related genes, our results show that reduced expression levels of Spp1, Vsig4, and IL22rɑ2 facilitate wound healing by modulating inflammation, immune responses, and extracellular matrix remodeling. Mechanistically, Spp1 alleviates inflammatory damage by attenuating the excessive activation of macrophages and T cells, and inhibits collagen synthesis in fibroblasts to prevent pathological fibrosis. Its expression may be directly influenced by physical stimulation from MNs. Western blot analysis validated that Tβ4 MNs downregulated the expression levels of Spp1, Vsig4, and IL22rɑ2 (Figure 9e ). Our qPCR analysis confirmed the trend we observed via Western blot (Figure 9f ). Our SPR assay revealed that Tβ4 binds to Spp1, IL22rɑ2, and Vsig4 in a dose‐dependent manner, with affinity constant (K D ) values of 1.665 × 10 −4 , 9.689 × 10 −6 , and 3.559 × 10 −6 m , respectively (Figure 9g ). These results supported the specific binding of Tβ4 to IL22rɑ2 and Vsig4. These findings suggest that Vsig4 and IL22rɑ2 may represent novel Tβ4 targets that promote wound healing. Notably, the complement cascade regulation via Vsig4 represents, to the best of our knowledge, a previously unreported mechanism of wound healing. Tβ4 mediates wound healing through direct binding interactions with Vsig4 and IL22rɑ2, both of which are proteins expressed at low physiological levels. These binding targets may facilitate wound healing by modulating inflammatory responses, immune cell functions, extracellular matrix remodeling, and tissue regeneration. Notably, Vsig4 is involved in the complement and coagulation cascade (C3‐Vsig4) to exert its effects. Several studies have demonstrated that Vsig4 inhibits the activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome and IL‐1β secretion [ 24 ]. we hypothesize that during wound healing, C3 induces Vsig4 to suppress NLRP3 inflammasome activation and IL‐1β secretion, thereby mitigating the detrimental effects of inflammatory responses on wound healing. Similarly, given the binding of Tβ4 to IL22rɑ2 and its downregulation, we speculate that this might amplify the stimulatory impact of IL‐22 on immune cells, thereby enhancing the secretion of pro‐inflammatory cytokines such as IL‐6 and TNF‐α. This process facilitates the transition from the inflammatory phase to the proliferative one, which promotes wound repair. It is plausible that Under conditions of IL22rɑ2 underexpression, IL‐22 preferentially binds to its functional receptor, IL22RA1, activating a number of signaling pathways including STAT3. This activation promotes the proliferation and migration of keratinocytes, fibroblasts, and epithelial cells, along with the upregulation of antimicrobial peptides such as β‐defensins, thus expediting wound healing [ 25 ]. Unlike previous reports showing that Tβ4‐derived exosomes promote angiogenesis via the PI3K/AKT/mTOR/HIF‐1α pathway [ 26 ], alleviate inflammation by regulating macrophage M1/M2 polarization, reverse cellular senescence, and enhance cell proliferation and migration through the PTEN/PI3K/AKT pathway [ 16 ], In contrast, the present study represents the first investigation to elucidate a non‐classical regulatory pathway mediated by thymosin beta‐4 (Tβ4) in wound healing. This pathway is distinguished by several core characteristics: it is driven by the low expression of Spp1, Vsig4, and IL22rα2, forming a cross‐boundary regulatory network that is defined by “immune microenvironment remodeling—multicellular coordinated repair.” Notably, this pathway operates independently of the classical mechanisms associated with angiogenesis or cellular senescence that have been the focus of prior research. Specifically, the dual regulatory axes identified in this study demonstrate distinct functional divisions and synergistic interactions: the Vsig4/C3b axis precisely modulates the balance between immune clearance efficiency and anti‐fibrotic responses. This resolves the central issue in wound healing, namely “excessive immune activation leading to tissue fibrosis,” a regulatory function of immune homeostasis that has not been previously reported in the mechanisms described in References 19 and 21. Simultaneously, the activation of the IL‐22/STAT3 pathway not only directly stimulates the proliferation and migration of epithelial cells but also augments the wound's anti‐infective capabilities by inducing the expression of antimicrobial peptides. This process effectively facilitates the transition of wounds from the inflammatory phase to the proliferative phase. This “immune regulation—epithelial repair—anti‐infection” triadic effect represents a significant departure from the unidirectional regulatory models of previous studies, which primarily concentrated on either cell proliferation or angiogenesis. These findings challenge the current thinking regarding the mechanism of action of Tβ4 exosomes, which has predominantly focused on angiogenesis and macrophage polarization, and thus provide novel molecular targets and regulatory perspectives for wound healing. 3 Conclusion In this study, we developed a new therapeutic platform combining advanced biomaterials with biological insights to improve peptide delivery for wound healing. Our low‐temperature process for creating Tβ4‐loaded soluble MNs offers a stable and scalable alternative to traditional methods. This platform ensures high drug loading, quick dissolution, and precise transdermal delivery, leading to accelerated wound closure, better collagen deposition, and improved angiogenesis in a full‐thickness mouse model. Importantly, our work enhances the understanding of Tβ4 in tissue repair by identifying Vsig4 and IL22ra2 as novel high‐affinity targets. This study demonstrated that thymosin beta‐4 (Tβ4) selectively binds to and downregulates the expression of both Vsig4 and IL22ra2. Based on these observations, we propose a plausible mechanistic hypothesis: Tβ4 may modulate NLRP3 inflammasome activity through the Vsig4/C3 axis and augment STAT3 signaling via the IL22ra2/IL‐22 axis, thereby synergistically facilitating wound healing. This dual mechanism shifts the focus beyond angiogenesis and macrophage polarization, highlighting Tβ4's role in maintaining immune balance during wound healing. Fundamentally, the Vsig4/C3 and IL22ra2/IL‐22 pathways present new targets for immunomodulatory therapy beyond wound care. Future efforts will aim to scale manufacturing to ensure reproducibility, sterility, and stability. Key next steps include validation in complex porcine models and eventual translation into human clinical trials, advancing this promising regenerative medicine technology from laboratory research to clinical use. In future studies, we will employ genetic approaches (CRISPR/Cas9‐mediated knockout or siRNA silencing of Vsig4 and IL22ra2) to further validate the causal relationships. These planned investigations will determine whether ablation of these targets abolishes or attenuates the therapeutic effects of Tβ4, thereby definitively confirming their functional roles in the observed wound healing process. 4 Experimental Section 4.1 Fabrication of Tβ4 MNs The MNs were molded using silicone molds consisting of 10 × 10 arrays with needle heights of 1000 µm, spacings of 800 µm, and base diameters of 350 µm. An aqueous solution of CS (50%), sucrose (1%), and Tβ4 (80 mg/mL) was poured into each mold (100 µL) to form the MN tips, before the mold was placed in a vacuum chamber maintained at 4°C and 65% relative humidity (RH) for 3 min of degassing. Any surface bubbles were removed using pipette tips, after which the mold was re‐inserted into the same vacuum chamber for another 2 min of degassing, to ensure that the solution fully filled all of the MN cavities to the tips. To form the bases, the excess solution was discarded before a second aqueous solution (200 µL) of CS (50%) and sucrose (1%) was poured into each mold, before being dried in a desiccator at 4°C and 65% humidity for 48 h. 4.2 Morphology Characterization A DiMicro Optics AM7915MZT L handheld optical microscope (Weidi Optics Co, Ltd, WuXi, China) was used to obtain high‐resolution images of the fabricated Tβ4 MNs. The morphology of the MN tips was also observed via SEM on a SU8100 instrument (Hitachi, Ltd., Tokyo, Japan). 4.3 Drug Loading and Distribution Using Microscopy Doxorubicin hydrochloride and FITC were added as model drugs to the tip and base matrix solutions, respectively, prior to casting into the molds to fabricate the MNs. Fluorescent images were obtained using a DLYMLLS I×73 inverted fluorescence microscope (Olympus Corporation, Tokyo, Japan) and an LSM 980 laser scanning confocal microscope (Carl Zeiss Microscopy GmbH, Jena, Germany) to analyze the loading and distribution of the drugs within the MNs. 4.4 Drug‐Excipient Interactions FTIR was performed on an INVENIO‐S instrument (Bruker Corporation, Billerica, MA, USA) to characterize the structural characteristics of the Tβ4 MNs. The FTIR spectra were collected in transmission mode, over a range of 4000− 400 cm −1 . 4.5 Mechanical and Hardness Testing A load cell was assembled with a displacement‐force testing station on an MTS C42.502y universal testing machine (MTS Systems Corporation, Eden Prairie, MN, USA), which was then used to estimate the mechanical strength of our MN patch. Each tested patch was placed onto a rigid stainless‐steel platform. The station‘s sensor probe was then moved downward (0.05 mm/s) against the patch. The measurement was recorded from the moment the probe touched the tips until the force reading reached 70 N. 4.6 Aluminum Foil Penetration To assess the penetration ability of the Tβ4 MNs, we used one layer of aluminum foil to mimic skin depth. We inserted and removed the Tβ4 MNs from this foil layer, then disassembled them to count the resultant holes via optical microscopy. 4.7 In Vitro Penetration The in vitro puncturing performance of the Tβ4 MNs was evaluated using porcine skin purchased from Beijing Hebang Xingye Scientific Instrument Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China). The MNs were applied to full‐thickness porcine skin for 10 min, followed by 20 µL of 0.4% Trypan Blue solution for 10 min. After removing excess dye, the resultant blue dots were counted using an AM7915MZTL optical microscope (Weidi Optics Co, Ltd, WuXi, China). Puncture performance was determined by comparing the number of blue dots to the total number of MNs. 4.8 Determination of Drug Loading for Tβ4 MNs MN molds filled with the needle tip solution were placed into a release medium of pH 7.4 phosphate‐buffered saline (PBS; 6 mL), sonicated for 3 min, then sampled and injected into a U3000 high‐performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) system (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). The obtained values were substituted into a calibration curve (y = 1.039x − 9.0989 [R 2 = 0.9906]) to determine the drug loading characteristics of the MNs. All experiments were repeated in triplicate. A C18 chromatographic column (4.6 × 250 mm, 5 µm) was used, with a mobile phase A of 0.1% trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) in water and a mobile phase B of 0.1% TFA in acetonitrile. The gradient elution program was as follows: between 0–40 min, the proportion of mobile phase B increased linearly from 20–40%. The flow rate was set at 1.0 mL/min, and the detection wavelength was 210 nm. The column temperature was maintained at 25°C, and the injection volume was 100 µL. 4.9 In Vitro Tβ4 MN Release For each MN patch, 5 mL of the release medium was aliquoted into a small beaker. The mold, filled with the needle tip solution, was placed into the medium, before the beaker was placed into a thermostatic shaker set to a temperature of 37°C and rotation speed of 100 rpm. Samples (400 µL each) were removed at 15 and 30 s; 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 30, and 45 min; and 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, and 7 h. Upon removal, each sample was quickly replenished with the same volume of release medium. Each experiment was repeated in triplicate using three MN patches, and HPLC was used to determine the drug content in each removed sample. The cumulative release amount (CRA) and cumulative release rate (CRR) for the drug were then calculated using the formula(1): (1) C R A = C i · V + ∑ i = 1 n − 1 C i − 1 · V i where V represents the volume of the release medium, Vi represents the volume of each sample taken, Ci represents the drug concentration in the release medium at the ii‐th sampling time, and Ci – 1 represents the drug concentration in the release medium at the (i−1)‐th sampling time. 4.10 In Vivo Penetration and Degradation To investigate the in vivo penetration abilities of our Tβ4 MNs, the fabricated patches were pressed moderately onto the dorsal skin of C57 mice and secured with medical tape for set durations (0, 1, 5, 30, 60, 120, and 180 min). Portions of each affixed patch were removed at the indicated time points and examined via SEM to analyze degradation. For wound site penetration, the same procedures were repeated on wounded dorsal skin, with patches affixed for set durations (0, 1, 5, 30, and 60 min). SEM imaging was once again used to observe their dissolution over time as portions were removed. 4.11 In Vitro Biocompatibility The in vitro toxicity of our Tβ4 MN patch was assessed using cultured HaCaT cells. MN patches were immersed in a complete medium containing Dulbecco's Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM, high glucose) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) for 2 h, after which liquid samples were collected to assess biocompatibility. The CCK‑8 assay (CR2407009; Wuhan Servicebio Technology Co., Ltd., Wuhan, China) was used to test cell activity. Approximately 5000 cells were seeded into 96‐well plates (100 µL of extracted liquid per well) for 24 h, with six parallel controls for each group. A 10 µL aliquot of the working reagent was then added to each well, before the plate was incubated at 37°C for 2 h. 4.12 Punctured Skin Recovery All animal experimental procedures were approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of the Experimental Animal Center of the Academy of Military Medical Sciences (Beijing, China) (protocol code: IACUC‐DWZX‐2025‐568). Anesthesia was induced via the intraperitoneal injection of 150 µL of 4% chloral hydrate. After abdominal fur removal, Tβ4 MN patches were applied to the exposed abdominal skin. The patches were removed after 10 min, before the skin condition was photographed and recorded (the 0 min time point). Each rat's skin was then observed again at 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, and 30 min, as well as at 1 and 2 h following the patch removal, and photographed using a handheld AM7915MZTL optical microscope (DiMicro Optics) to document any local reactions such as erythema, swelling, congestion, exudation, degeneration, or necrosis. 4.13 In Vivo Wound‐Healing Experiments All animal experimental procedures were approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of the Experimental Animal Center of the Academy of Military Medical Sciences (Beijing, China) (protocol code: IACUC‐DWZX‐2025‐568). Fourty healthy eight‐week‐old male C57BL mice, weighing 18–22 g, were purchased from Beijing Vital River Laboratory Animal Technology Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China). The animals were randomly divided into four groups: control, blank MN Tβ4 MN, topical Tβ4 (2.5 mg/mL Tβ4 aqueous solution), and rhIII‐collagen (rhIII collagen, D‐(+)‐trehalose dihydrate, glycerol, carbomer, butylene glycol, triethanolamine, and water; Falandu Technology Co., Ltd.), with n = 8 per group. They were anesthetized using 4% chloral hydrate (100 µL/10 g) delivered through intraperitoneal injection. An 8 mm full‐thickness cutaneous wound was then excised onto the back of each mouse, at the midline. A silicone ring was sutured to the wound‘s edge to prevent contraction. The wounds were then covered with our Tβ4 MN patch, a topical Tβ4 solution, an rhIII‐collagen gel, blank MN or left uncovered (control group). Each group included eight replicates. During the 14‐day MN administration period, the animals were allowed free movement. Digital photographs were taken daily, and wound areas were measured using ImageJ version (National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, MD, USA). 4.14 Histology and Immunofluorescence Experiments After 15 days, the mice were euthanized, and the wound‐site skin tissue was excised with a scalpel and fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 48 h. The samples were then dehydrated and embedded in paraffin. A microtome was used to obtain 5 µm‐thick serial sections that were stained with H&amp;E, Masson‘s trichrome, and immunofluorescent stains. Observation of the re‐epithelialization degree and collagen fibers was performed using H&amp;E and Masson's trichrome staining. The sections were then incubated with primary anti‐VEGF (1:100; Servicebio), anti‐α‐smooth muscle actin (1:300; Servicebio), and anti‐IL‐10 (1:100; Servicebio) antibodies overnight at 4°C. They were then incubated with secondary antibodies (Servicebio) for 1 h at room temperature. Fluorescence images were acquired using a NIKON ECLIPSE C1 microscope (Nikon Corporation, Tokyo, Japan) with an excitation wavelength of 550 nm and emission wavelength of 590 nm, and analyzed using Image J software (NIH). Differential expression analysis was performed using DESeq2 (Genentech, Inc., South San Francisco, CA, USA). DEGs were defined as those with p &lt; 0.05 and |log 2 (‐fold change)| &gt; 2. Hierarchical cluster analysis of the DEGs was performed using R (R Foundation for Statistical Computing) to demonstrate the genetic expression pattern across samples and experimental groups. Using the hypergeometric distribution, we conducted GO and KEGG database enrichment analyses for all of the identified DEGs. Terms and pathways with adjusted p values of ≤ 0.05 in both the GO and KEGG analyses were considered to be significantly enriched. 4.16 Western Blot for Transcriptome Analysis Tβ4 MN‐treated skin tissues were lysed with pre‐cooled radioimmunoprecipitation assay buffer containing a mixture of protease and phosphate inhibitors.
