THE FUNDAMENTALS
Why Cycling Matters
Not all peptides are created equal. Some compounds cause receptor downregulation with
chronic use, while others can be run indefinitely without diminishing returns. Understanding
the difference is the key to long-term optimization.
"You don't cycle health. You build it every single day. The question is which tools need rest
periods and which ones are daily infrastructure."
— Leading peptide practitioner
Two Categories of Compounds
Receptor Downregulation — The Core Concept
When certain receptors are chronically stimulated, your body reduces their number or sensitivity
— a protective mechanism called downregulation. The result: the same dose produces weaker
effects over time. Cycling allows receptors to reset, restoring full sensitivity when you resume the
compound.
However, not every compound triggers this response. Peptides that work through structural
repair pathways (like VEGF upregulation or collagen synthesis) often maintain efficacy indefinitely
because they are supporting natural biological processes, not overstimulating receptors.

PRACTICAL STRATEGY
Stacking Timeline Tips
How you introduce compounds matters as much as which ones you choose. These rules
prevent confusion, ensure accurate tracking, and maximize your ability to attribute effects to
specific peptides.
"A short pencil is better than a long memory. If you aren't writing it down, you aren't
optimizing — you're guessing."
— Leading clinical practitioner
1 Don't Start Everything at Once
Stagger introductions by at least 2 weeks between new compounds. If you start five peptides on
Day 1, you have no idea which one is responsible for any effect — positive or negative.
Sequential introduction is the only way to build reliable data about your individual response.
2 Start with BPC-157 Alone
BPC-157 is the ideal first compound — it has the broadest safety profile, no cycling requirement,
and establishes a systemic repair baseline. Run it solo for 2 weeks before adding your next
compound. This gives you a clean reading on its individual effects.
3 Track Each Compound Separately Before Stacking
Log daily metrics (energy, pain, sleep, mood) with each new introduction. After 2 weeks of
stable data, add the next compound. This creates a staircase of evidence — you know exactly
what each peptide contributes to your protocol.
4 The "5 On / 2 Off" Question
You'll see this protocol everywhere — 5 days on, 2 days off. The truth: it depends entirely on the
specific peptide and its receptor dynamics. "Forever" compounds like BPC-157 gain nothing
from weekend breaks. Cycled compounds need their full off-period, not micro-breaks. Follow
the compound-specific protocol from the master table, not a one-size-fits-all rule.
