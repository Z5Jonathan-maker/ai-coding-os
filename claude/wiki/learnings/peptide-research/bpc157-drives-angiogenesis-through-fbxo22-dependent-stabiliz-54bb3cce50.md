Background The body-protective polypeptide BPC157 is a natural therapeutic agent that has demonstrated robust efficacy in promoting repair across multiple organ systems, indicating its significant clinical potential. However, its clinical translation has been substantially hindered by a lack of understanding of its precise molecular mechanisms of action. Given its prominent proangiogenic effects, deciphering the mechanistic basis of its vascular regenerative actions is critically important. Methods To investigate the molecular pathway underlying the proangiogenic effects of BPC157, we employed a combination of molecular, biochemical, and cellular approaches. These include techniques to identify protein‒protein interactions, assess ubiquitination status, and evaluate key cellular functions. The specific role of the proline residue at position 3 of BPC157 was also experimentally validated. Results We revealed that intracellular BPC157 engages the E3 ubiquitin ligase adaptor protein FBXO22 through its proline residue at position 3. This interaction results in the formation of a protein complex that effectively suppresses the ubiquitination and subsequent proteasomal degradation of the transcription factor BACH1, leading to significant stabilization of BACH1 protein levels. The consequent accumulation of BACH1 was shown to enhance critical processes in vascular regeneration, namely, the proliferation and tube-forming capacity of vascular endothelial cells. Conclusions This study establishes a novel and critical molecular mechanism for the pharmacological actions of BPC157, centered on the BPC157-FBXO22-BACH1 axis. These findings provide a solid mechanistic foundation for the future development of BPC157-based or sequential therapeutic agents targeted at enhancing vascular repair. Furthermore, this work offers robust scientific evidence that can inform improved strategies for the prevention and treatment of tissue injury. The development of novel therapeutics to address such complex tissue damage is therefore of high medical importance. Body-protective compound (BPC) 157 is a 15 amino acid peptide (Gly-Glu-Pro-Pro-Pro-Gly-Lys-Pro-Ala-Asp-Asp-Ala-Gly-Leu-Val; MW 1419 Da) originally isolated from human gastric juice [ 1 ]. BPC157 exerts a significant protective effect on various tissues and organs, such as the esophagus, stomach, duodenum [ 2 ], colorectal mucosa [ 2 ], liver, pancreas [ 3 ], muscle [ 4 ], cornea [ 5 ], heart [ 6 ] and nerves [ 7 ]. In addition to its cytoprotective properties against stress, BPC157 exerts anti-inflammatory effects and preserves epithelial integrity [ 5 ]. Although its precise mechanisms remain incompletely characterized, BPC157 displays exceptional pharmacodynamic potency at nanomolar doses and remarkable biostability—resisting hydrolysis, enzymatic degradation, and gastric fluid while maintaining activity at room temperature. These broad biological effects combined with their favorable stability profile position BPC157 as a highly promising therapeutic candidate for severe tissue injury, potentially offering advantages over conventional cytokine-based wound therapies. We previously established a solid-phase synthesis protocol for BPC157 [ 8 ], which was patented in China (ZL201110114806. X) and developed the “HuBPP Body-Protective Polypeptide Injection”. As the first global therapeutic exploration of BPC157, HuBPP has demonstrated efficacy in accelerating multitype wound healing, mitigating visceral organ stress responses, and providing neuroprotection [ 9 ]. Preclinical safety evaluations revealed no toxicity in acute studies with SD rats and beagles following intravenous/intramuscular administration [ 10 ]. Pharmacokinetic (PK) profiling in both species revealed linear dose‒exposure relationships, with [³H] BPC157 rapidly metabolized into small peptides and ultimately tritium-labeled proline for normal amino acid catabolism/excretion [ 11 ]. This comprehensive PK and tissue distribution dataset—the first of its kind—informs clinical trial design and underscores the systemic therapeutic potential of BPC157. Despite these advances and their significant therapeutic potential, the molecular mechanisms underlying the potent cytoprotective effects of BPC157 remain largely undefined, limiting its full clinical translation. Elucidating the mechanism underlying the effects of BPC157 is therefore crucial to bridge foundational research for practical therapeutic applications. Tissue repair is a complex, dynamic process that relies on tightly coordinated cellular events to achieve functional regeneration. Angiogenesis plays a pivotal role by enhancing the delivery of oxygen and nutrients to granulation tissue at the injury site, thereby accelerating the reparative response [ 12 ]. In our investigations, we observed that robust angiogenesis, characterized by grossly visible neovascularization and a marked increase in CD31-positive luminal structures by immunohistochemistry, was a prominent histological feature of healing in animal models of tissue injury treated with BPC157. Mechanistically, we demonstrated that intracellular BPC157 interacts with the E3 ubiquitin ligase adaptor FBXO22 via its proline residue at position 3. This interaction results in the formation of a complex that suppresses the ubiquitination and subsequent degradation of the transcription factor BACH1. Consequently, BACH1 stabilization leads to the upregulation of key growth factors and their receptors, driving vascular endothelial cell proliferation and tube formation to promote angiogenic repair. This study provides critical molecular insights into the pharmacological actions of BPC157, establishes a solid mechanistic foundation for the development of sequential therapeutic agents that target vascular repair, and offers robust scientific evidence for advancing strategies to prevent and treat complex tissue injuries. Results BPC157 accelerates tissue injury repair and angiogenesis During the pioneering developmental phase of the “HuBPP Body Protective Polypeptide Injection,” our research team executed a series of systematic pharmacodynamic evaluations. We constructed standardized experimental models encompassing murine full-thickness burn injuries [ 13 ], rat dermal incisional wounds [ 13 ], and rodent skeletal muscle contusion models (Figure S1). The experimental protocol involved daily administration of varying BPC157 dosages (5–40 µg/kg) via subcutaneous injection for 7 consecutive days, with parallel saline control groups receiving equivalent volumes. Quantitative histomorphometry analysis demonstrated the significant therapeutic efficacy of BPC157 in accelerating tissue regeneration across all three model systems, particularly in terms of new collagen fiber formation rates [ 13 ]and interfiber spacing recovery (0.62 ± 0.21 μm vs 0.18 ± 0.15 μm, p &lt; 0.005) (Figure S1). Notably, comparative angiogenic profiling through CD31 immunohistochemistry revealed a dose-dependent increase in microvascular density in the BPC157-treated rodent skeletal muscle contusion model groups (muscle injury model: 1.7 ± 0.4 vessels vs. 6.2 ± 3.2 in the BPC157-treated group, p &lt; 0.001) (Fig. 1 A, B). This neovascularization phenomenon was consistently observed in murine full-thickness burn injuries (Fig. 1 C, D) and rat dermal incisional wounds (Fig. 1 E, F), suggesting that BPC157 plays a fundamental role in promoting endothelial cell proliferation and vascular network reconstitution during tissue repair processes. Proline at the third position is the key binding site of BPC157 for FBXO22 After confirming the critical role of FBXO22, we further investigated the interaction mechanism between BPC157 and FBXO22. Molecular docking simulations using MOE predicted a stable binding conformation (S score = -10.1), with electrostatic interactions dominating the binding interface. Hydrogen bonding was observed between residues G1, E2, P3, K7, and V15 of BPC157 and E31, R58, and E183 of FBXO22 (Fig. 4 A). To identify key binding residues, we performed alanine-scanning mutagenesis across BPC157. Alanine (Ala), the smallest chiral amino acid, serves as an ideal probe for alanine-scanning mutagenesis (Figure S2). Systematic substitution of each residue in the sequence with Ala enables the identification of critical amino acids governing protein bioactivity. We first determined the binding affinity between BPC157 and FBXO22 via surface plasmon resonance (SPR). BPC157 specifically bound FBXO22 with a dissociation constant KD of 1.0 × 10⁻⁷, which was significantly greater than that of the scrambled peptide control (KD = 2.6 × 10⁻⁴) (Fig. 4 B). A library of alanine-substituted BPC157 mutants was chemically synthesized (Fig. 4 C), and SPR analysis of all 14 mutant peptides revealed reduced binding affinities (10⁻⁷ to 10⁻⁵ range) (Fig. 4 D). Strikingly, substitution of proline at position 3 (P3A) abolished binding, yielding a KD = 56.7 (Fig. 4 D). A comprehensive understanding of pharmacological mechanisms is therefore essential for developing personalized treatment regimens. Elucidating drug‒target interactions, signaling pathway regulation, and biological effect generation enables precise characterization of drug action modes and therapeutic outcomes, providing a scientific foundation for optimized disease management. While studies on the therapeutic effects, pharmacokinetics, and toxicology of BPC157 have been completed [ 10 , 11 ], its molecular mechanisms remain elusive. BPC157 exhibits broad tissue repair capacity, suggesting that its actions may not depend on cell type-specific regulation (e.g., myocytes or epithelial cells) but rather target universal injury responses. Wound healing—a tightly coordinated cascade involving keratinocytes, macrophages, fibroblasts, and endothelial cells—requires robust angiogenesis during the proliferative phase to establish a nutrient/oxygen supply via granulation tissue vascularization [ 12 , 15 ]. Notably, BPC157 accelerates burn wound healing after flame exposure [ 4 ], and our prior studies in thermal/incisional injury models implicate its proangiogenic activity as a central reparative mechanism [ 9 ]. Given the pivotal role of angiogenesis in tissue regeneration and the demonstrated vascular effects of BPC157, deciphering its molecular regulation of endothelial cell proliferation is critical for understanding its pantissue reparative capacity. Therefore, identifying the molecular targets mediating BPC157-induced HUVEC proliferation, migration, and differentiation is imperative. While Ming-Jer Hsieh et al. reported that BPC157 promotes angiogenesis via VEGFR2 upregulation [ 16 ], how BPC157 interacts with VEGFR2, whether it physically binds, and the mechanism underlying VEGFR2 upregulation remain unclear. In this study, coimmunoprecipitation-mass spectrometry (co-IP-MS) identified FBXO22 as a direct intracellular interactor of BPC157. Molecular docking (MOE), alanine-substituted peptide library screening, and surface plasmon resonance (SPR) revealed that BPC157 binds FBXO22 through its proline residue at position 3 (P3). Functional assays confirmed P3’s critical role: the P3A mutant failed to promote HUVEC proliferation in CCK-8 assays. Our prior pharmacokinetic studies revealed rapid metabolism of BPC157 into small peptide fragments, ultimately yielding single amino acid residues (predominantly proline) in plasma, urine, and feces [ 11 ]. We speculate that the BPC157-FBXO22 interaction via P3 delays enzymatic degradation—peptide catabolism typically initiates with endopeptidases, followed by stepwise proteolysis into amino acids. This P3-dependent binding may protect BPC157 from rapid cleavage, explaining its ultimate proline metabolic fate. The BPC157-FBXO22 complex is essential for mediating the proangiogenic effects of BPC157. Mechanistically, the binding of BPC157 to FBXO22 induces steric hindrance that suppresses FBXO22-mediated ubiquitination and degradation of its substrate BACH1. This stabilizes the transcription factor BACH1, which subsequently upregulates angiogenesis-related factors, including PDGFB, EGFR, FGF2, and FGFR1, ultimately driving vascular regeneration. Conversely, BACH1 knockdown not only inhibited BPC157-induced HUVEC proliferation and tube formation but also blocked the expression of these proangiogenic factors, confirming that BACH1 is the pivotal downstream effector of the BPC157-FBXO22 axis. Notably, BACH1 is traditionally recognized as an angiogenesis inhibitor that regulates HO-1 or VEGF transcription; however, our data show that BACH1 stabilization promotes angiogenesis, which may be attributed to cell type specificity or context-dependent downstream targets (e.g., PDGFR instead of HO-1) [ 17 , 18 ]. Based on a comprehensive analysis of the literature, we propose that BACH1 is a typical “context-dependent transcription factor”, and its function is strongly regulated by the intracellular redox state: ROS levels and heme metabolism significantly influence the expression and activity of BACH1. Specifically, in ischemic/high-ROS environments, BACH1 tends to be degraded and acts to suppress angiogenesis by inhibiting the Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway and recruiting HDAC1, among other mechanisms. In contrast, under antioxidant/low-ROS conditions (such as in certain tumor microenvironments or after treatment with BPC157 in our study), BACH1 becomes stabilized and can switch to promoting angiogenesis by directly activating target genes like VEGF. This dual role aligns well with BACH1’s function as a redox-sensing molecule. Our preliminary experiments showed that BPC157 protects cells from hydrogen peroxide-induced oxidative damage (Supplementary Figure S3A), suggesting antioxidant activity. We subsequently established a cellular hypoxia model using cobalt chloride (CoCl₂) and validated the effects of BPC157 under these conditions. The results showed that although BPC157 exhibits antioxidant effects, it was insufficient to counteract the severe cellular hypoxic damage induced by CoCl₂. After treatment with CoCl₂, BPC157-treated HUVECs failed to form tube-like structures (Supplementary Figure S3B), which is consistent with our analysis that the redox state is a key determinant of BACH1’s functional direction. Therefore, we hypothesize that BPC157 may reduce intracellular ROS levels through an antioxidant mechanism similar to that reported in J Clin Invest [ 19 ], thereby stabilizing BACH1 protein and enabling its pro-angiogenic function. Although this mechanism requires further validation, it is consistent with existing evidence that “BACH1 promotes angiogenesis under antioxidant conditions.” In conclusion, this study systematically elucidated the intracellular molecular mechanism of BPC157 through multimethod validation, addressing a critical knowledge gap in its pharmacological profile. We delineated the molecular cascade linking BPC157 engagement to upregulated proangiogenic factor expression, thereby establishing a comprehensive mechanistic framework for its therapeutic actions. This work lays a solid foundation for the development of sequential therapeutic agents that target vascular repair and provides robust scientific evidence to optimize strategies for the prevention and treatment of tissue injury across various contexts. Materials and methods Rat model of soft tissue injury Animal studies and procedures were carried out in accordance with an approved protocol from the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) at the Fourth Military Medical University (IACUC-20220505). Male Sprague–Dawley (SD) rats weighing 200 ± 20 g were obtained from the Animal Experiment Center of the Fourth Military Medical University (Xi’an, China) and housed in a specific pathogen-free animal facility. Twenty-five rats were randomly divided into five groups: the control group, model group, and three BPC 157-treated groups (10 µg/kg, 20 µg/kg, and 40 µg/kg). The previously described method for the induction of soft tissue injury was used here with some minor modifications [ 20 ]. First, all the rats were anesthetized via an intraperitoneal injection of 2% pentobarbital sodium (75 mg/kg), and the hair of the right posterior leg was removed. The soft tissue injury model was established by mechanical stress. To hit the middle calf muscle of each rat, a stainless-steel hammer weighing 300 g was dropped from a height of 50 cm five consecutive times. The standard of success for these models is visible swelling and subcutaneous ecchymosis at the impact site. All animals with fractures or skin ruptures were removed from the study. In the control group, the hair was removed while the rats were anesthetized. In the BPC157-treated groups, BPC157 was continuously intramuscularly administered for 7 days after modeling. The rats were dissected on the 1st, 4th, and 7th days after BPC157 treatment, and injured muscle tissues were collected. The tissues were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde and then embedded in paraffin. Immunohistochemistry and immunofluorescence Serial Sect. (4 μm) of the paraffin-embedded samples were conventionally dewaxed and hydrated with gradient ethanol. For histological examination, H&amp;E staining was carried out. For immunohistochemical staining, the tissue sections were inactivated by endogenous peroxidase with 3% hydrogen peroxide for 10 min and blocked with goat serum for 30 min. Then, the sections were coated with an anti-CD31 antibody (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Dallas, TX) at 4 °C overnight, followed by incubation with an HRP-labeled secondary antibody (Gene Tech, Shanghai, China), and the sections were visualized with a DAB system (Gene Tech). Cell culture and stimulation Human umbilical vein endothelial (HUVEC) cells were purchased from Shanghai Zhong Qiao Xin Zhou Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). The cells were cultured in DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS and antibiotics (100 µg ml-1 streptomycin and 100 units ml-1 penicillin, Gibco). The cells at the proper confluence were subsequently treated with BPC157 for the indicated times. BPC157 was added at concentrations of 5, 10, 20, and 40 µg ml-1 for the indicated periods. Stimulated cells were collected for RNA and protein extraction. Cell viability assay Cell proliferation was detected via a CCK-8 assay (Biosharp, China) or a CellTiter-Glo assay (Promega) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The experiment was performed in triplicate at a minimum. Tube formation assay The extracellular matrix gel-induced tube formation assay was used as an in vitro measurement of angiogenesis. Briefly, a 24-well culture plate was coated with 200 µl (9.1 mg/ml)/well Matrigel prepared from Engelbreth-Holm-Swarm mouse tumors (Corning, MA, USA) and allowed to stand for 30 min at 37 °C to form a gel layer. After gel formation, 1 × 10 5 HUVECs pretreated with or without BPC 157 for 24 h were seeded into each well with 0.5 ml of M199 supplemented with 16% fetal bovine serum and 20% EGM-2. The plate was immediately incubated at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere with 5% CO 2 for 4 h. Only tubes formed with perfect proximity were counted in four defined fields of each well under an inverted microscope. Western blot analysis Western blotting was performed as previously described via the following antibodies: FBXO22, BACH1, KLF4, LKB1, SNAL1, PTEN, KDM4A, BAG3, and GAPDH (all from Proteintech, Rosemout, IL). The intensities of the bands were quantified via Image Lab, version 5.2.1 (Bio-Rad Laboratories). RNA extraction and RT‒qPCR Total RNA from cells was isolated via TRIzol (Takara, Otsu, Japan) and subsequently purified with chloroform/isopropanol/ethanol, and cDNA was synthesized via the PrimeScript RT reagent Kit (Takara) according to the manufacturer’s instructions and used as the template for qRT‒PCR. qRT‒PCR was performed via SYBR Premix Ex Taq II (Takara) on a Chromo 4 continuous fluorescence detector with a PTC-200 DNA Engine Cycler (Bio-Rad Laboratories, Hercules, CA). All reactions were run in triplicate for at least three independent experiments. Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) For the ligand‒receptor affinity ELISA, the human FBXO22 protein served as an antigen to coat the 96-well plate at 4 °C for 24 h. After coating, each well was then incubated with biotin-BPC 157 and the first antibody, goat-anti-human-IgG-Fc-biotin (Abcam), followed by the second antibody, streptavidin-HRP. Then, TMB chromogen solution (CWBIO) was added, and the absorbance at 450 nm was measured via a SpectraMax plate reader (Molecular Devices). Surface plasmon resonance (SPR) spectroscopy The kinetics of the binding of the FBXO22 protein to either full-length BPC157 or single-site alanine-mutated BPC157 were assessed via an SPR assay on a Biacore T200 SPR system (Cytiva). A CM5 sensor chip (Cytiva) was used to immobilize recombinant human FBXO22. Moreover, full-length BPC157 or single-site alanine-mutated BPC157 was dissolved in HBS-EP Buffer (Cytiva) at concentrations ranging from 1.89 nM to 31.25 nM through serial 2-fold dilutions. The data were processed via Biacore T200 Evaluation Software v3.2 (Cytiva) and 1:1 binding model analysis. The equilibrium dissociation constant ( K D ) was derived via the following formula: K D = k off (dissociation rate, s −1 )/ k on (association rate, M −1 s −1 ). Co‑immunoprecipitation (Co‑IP) analysis Co-IP was performed according to an established method with minimal modifications. In brief, HUVECs were lysed in Co-IP buffer containing 20 mM Tris-HCl (pH 7.5), 100 mM NaCl, 0.5 mM EDTA, 0.5% NP-40, 0.5 mM PMSF, and 0.5% protease inhibitor cocktail on ice for 20 min. The lysates were incubated with Biotin-BPC157 at 4 °C for 12 h, followed by incubation with anti-FBXO22 antibody at 4 °C for 12 h. Dynabeads Protein G (Dynal Biotech: Invitrogen) equilibrated in Co-IP buffer was added to the mixture, which was gently mixed for another 1 h. Blots were blocked with phosphate-buffered saline with 0.1% Tween 20 and then bound with antibodies for Biotin, after which an enhanced chemiluminescence (ECL) reaction was performed for blot development. Band intensities were quantified by densitometry. Statistical analysis The data are shown as the mean and standard deviation. The significance of the results obtained from both groups was evaluated with Student’s unpaired t test and one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA). All the statistical analyses were performed with GraphPad Prism 10.0 (GraphPad, Inc.). A difference with a two-tailed P value less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant. P &lt; 0.01 was considered a highly significant difference between the values.
