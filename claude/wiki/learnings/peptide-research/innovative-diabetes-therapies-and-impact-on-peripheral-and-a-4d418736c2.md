Abstract As a leading complication of diabetes mellitus, diabetic neuropathy (DN) represents a major public health challenge due to its high prevalence and impact on patients’ quality of life. The most common form, diabetic peripheral neuropathy (DPN), is characterized by progressive sensory loss, neuropathic pain, and autonomic dysfunction, all of which can significantly increase the risk of serious complications, such as foot ulcers and amputations. Traditionally, therapeutic strategies for DN have been largely limited to symptomatic management. However, recent advancements in diabetes therapy have opened promising avenues for disease-modifying interventions. In particular, incretin-based therapies and sodium-glucose co-transporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors have attracted increasing interest not only for their glucose-lowering effects, but also for their broader metabolic, renal, and cardiovascular benefits. In this narrative review, we synthesize emerging evidence on the potential role of these innovative therapies in the management of DN. Preclinical models, clinical trials and real-world observational studies strongly support the hypothesis that glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) and SGLT2 inhibitors may confer neuroprotective benefits. Beyond these established classes, novel agents such as dual and triple receptor agonists are currently being investigated. Although clinical data on their effects in DN are still limited, the simultaneous activation of multiple metabolic pathways suggests the potential for synergistic neuroprotective effects through enhanced regulation of glucose and lipid metabolism, attenuation of systemic inflammation and oxidative stress, improvement of mitochondrial function and reduction of neuronal damage. Although innovative diabetes therapies are still in early stages of development, they reflect a rapidly evolving landscape in the management of DN in the future. Introduction Recent epidemiological data confirm that diabetes mellitus (DM), projected at 643 million cases in 2030, is among the major and fastest-growing global health issues [ 1 ]. Consequently, the prevention and treatment of diabetic complications represent a huge challenge for healthcare systems. The most prevalent complication is diabetic neuropathy (DN), a neurodegenerative disorder of the peripheral nervous system. Precise estimates of global prevalence and incidence of all forms of DN are mostly missing due to a lack of population surveys [ 2 , 3 ]. Still, the projections show that DN will ultimately develop in up to 50% of diabetes cases during the course of the disease [ 4 ]. The mere fact that more than 18 million people suffer from diabetic foot ulcers every year [ 5 ] is enough to illustrate the burden that DN and its consequences place on both patients and healthcare professionals. The disease primarily affects sensory neurons and can be broadly classified into diffuse and focal forms [ 6 ]. The most common type is distal symmetric polyneuropathy (DSPN), a type of diffuse diabetic peripheral neuropathy (DPN) that accounts for approximately 75% of all DN cases [ 7 ]. Diabetic autonomic neuropathy (DAN) affects autonomic nerves and can cause severe impairment of various organ systems [ 8 ]. In addition, DN can exhibit atypical forms such as mononeuropathy and radiculopathy/polyradiculopathy [ 6 ]. Pathophysiological mechanisms involved in the etiopathogenesis of DN are complex, but hyperglycemia, impaired insulin signaling, insulin resistance, and dyslipidemia play the major roles. These factors act on neurons, glial cells and vascular endothelial cells via a variety of signaling pathways, leading to damage to DNA, mitochondria and the endoplasmic reticulum, which ultimately causes apoptosis and loss of neurotrophic signals [ 4 , 9 ]. Acknowledging diversity in clinical manifestations, frequency of undiagnosed cases and limitations of therapeutic approaches in DN, efforts were recently put into the development of expert consensus recommendations for screening, diagnosis, and management of DN [ 10 ]. Recommended treatment for DN relies on optimization of diabetes control, minimization of cardiovascular risk and direct pharmacological and non-pharmacological targeting of causes and symptoms of DN. Similarly, to prevent or slow DN, the American Diabetes Association (ADA) recommends early and intensive glycemic control, as well as optimization of weight, blood pressure and lipid profile, along with regular assessment and treatment of neuropathic pain [ 11 ]. Given the complexity of the etiopathogenesis of DN, the efficacy of therapeutic interventions may vary. Therefore, a thorough insight into the effectiveness of currently available therapeutics, as well as the development of new strategies, is of utmost importance. In recent years, incretin-based therapies and sodium-glucose co-transporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors have gained increasing attention for their innovative mechanisms of action and growing therapeutic potential in diabetes care [ 12 – 14 ]. This review summarizes the current advances in the management of DN by these innovative treatments. This article is based on previously conducted studies and does not contain any new studies with human participants or animals performed by any of the authors. We provide an overview of the novel therapeutic approaches and emerging disease-modifying agents, discuss their mechanisms of action and potential effects in DN, and highlight gaps in the existing knowledge in this field. Sodium-glucose Co-Transporter 2 Inhibitors SGLT2 inhibitors, including empagliflozin, canagliflozin, and dapagliflozin, are a relatively recent class of oral antidiabetic agents that lower blood glucose by inhibiting renal glucose reabsorption and promoting glucosuria independently of insulin secretion. Initially developed to improve glycemic control in type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM), these agents have demonstrated additional benefits such as weight loss, blood pressure reduction, and importantly, cardiovascular and renal protection. Clinical trials have shown that canagliflozin reduces major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) [ 15 ], while empagliflozin decreases cardiovascular mortality by 38% and heart failure hospitalizations by 32% in patients with T2DM at high cardiovascular risk [ 16 ]. Although achieving and maintaining good glycemic control is fundamental in diabetes management, many patients develop chronic hyperglycemia over time, which predisposes them to microvascular complications, including DPN, one of the most common complications in T2DM. DPN manifests with sensory loss, neuropathic pain, and autonomic dysfunction, severely impairing quality of life. Until recently, therapeutic options to modify the course of DPN beyond symptom management were limited [ 17 , 18 ]. Large-scale observational studies have demonstrated a significant correlation between elevated glycated hemoglobin (HbA 1c ) levels and increased neuropathy risk, underscoring the importance of stringent glycemic control in preventing DPN [ 19 ]. Beyond glycemic effects, emerging evidence suggests that SGLT2 inhibitors may exert direct neuroprotective actions. Preclinical studies indicate that SGLT2 inhibition with dapagliflozin exerts a protective effect on the peripheral and cardiac nervous system in diabetic models. Diabetes markedly reduced cardiac nerve density (0.0122 ± 0.005 mm 2 /mm 2 vs. 0.022 ± 0.006 mm 2 /mm 2 in non-diabetic controls, p = 0.004), as well as atrioventricular node and renal innervation. Treatment with dapagliflozin attenuated this loss (cardiac: 0.019 ± 0.004 mm 2 /mm 2 , renal: 0.022 ± 0.003 mm 2 /mm 2 ), preserving nerve structure close to baseline values. These findings suggest a direct neuroprotective action of dapagliflozin, independent of glycemic control, potentially contributing to the cardiovascular and renal benefits observed with SGLT2 inhibitors in clinical trials [ 20 ]. Consistent with these findings, empagliflozin has been shown to improve nerve conduction velocity (NCV) from 22.5 ± 2.3 m/s to 35.8 ± 3.1 m/s ( p &lt; 0.01), increase intraepidermal nerve fiber density by 45%, and reduce oxidative stress markers such as malondialdehyde by over 50%. These effects appear to occur independently of glycemic changes, potentially via AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) activation [ 21 ]. Clinically, treatment with SGLT2 inhibitors has been associated with significant improvements in cardiac autonomic neuropathy, evidenced by enhanced heart rate variability and reduced vasovagal syncope episodes in patients with T2DM [ 22 ]. Additionally, a 3-year prospective study involving 1274 patients with modestly controlled T2DM reported that treatment with dapagliflozin or canagliflozin was linked to significant neuroprotective effects. Patients receiving SGLT2 inhibitors demonstrated preservation of corneal nerve fiber length (2.56 ± 0.57 mm/mm 2 vs. 2.13 ± 0.52 mm/mm 2 ; p &lt; 0.001) and nerve branch density (48.6 ± 11.2 vs. 42.1 ± 9.8 branches/mm 2 ; p &lt; 0.01) compared to non-users. Furthermore, motor nerve conduction velocity (MNCV) of the tibial nerve remained stable (41.2 ± 3.8 m/s at baseline vs. 41.5 ± 4.0 m/s at follow-up; p = ns) in the SGLT2 inhibitor group, while it declined significantly in controls. Intraepidermal nerve fiber density was also better preserved, with a mean reduction of only 4.8% versus 10.2% in controls. These findings suggest that SGLT2 inhibitors confer neuroprotective benefits by preserving both small and large nerve fiber integrity, potentially slowing the progression of DPN and improving clinical outcomes in patients with T2DM [ 23 ]. Supporting these findings, a recent 6-month open-label, randomized controlled trial in patients with T2DM and distal peripheral neuropathy evaluated the effects of dapagliflozin in addition to standard oral antidiabetic therapy. Corneal nerve morphology (CNFD, CNBD, CNFL) was assessed via corneal confocal microscopy, and intraepidermal nerve fiber density (IENFD) from skin biopsies. Dapagliflozin significantly improved corneal nerve fiber density, branch density, and fiber length, whereas no significant changes were observed in the standard therapy group. Both groups showed an increase in IENFD, with no difference between arms. These results suggest a potential neuroprotective effect of dapagliflozin on small nerve fibers, particularly in the cornea, independent of changes in glycemic control or metabolic parameters [ 24 ] (Table 1 ). Large-scale clinical trials are warranted to confirm their efficacy and safety in the management of diabetic neuropathy. Glucagon-like Peptide-1 Receptor Agonists Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) have become a cornerstone in the management of T2DM due to their consistent efficacy in improving glycemic control, reducing body weight, and modulating inflammation [ 26 ]. Several landmark cardiovascular outcome trials have highlighted these benefits alongside meaningful reductions in microvascular complications, which are pivotal in the progression of diabetic complications. For example, the SUSTAIN-6 trial, which enrolled 3297 patients at high cardiovascular risk, demonstrated that once-weekly semaglutide reduced HbA 1c by approximately 1.4% and body weight by 3 to 5 kg. Notably, it conferred a 36% relative risk reduction in new or worsening nephropathy (HR 0.64; 95% CI 0.46–0.88), particularly impacting outcomes such as new-onset persistent macroalbuminuria and progression to renal replacement therapy. However, an unexpected numerical increase in retinopathy complications was observed (HR 1.76; 95% CI 1.11–2.78) [ 27 ]. Similarly, the LEADER trial, involving over 9300 patients treated with liraglutide, reported a 1.0% reduction in HbA 1c , 2 to 3 kg of weight loss, and a significant 22% decrease in nephropathy events (HR 0.78; 95% CI 0.67–0.92). This study also documented improvements in inflammatory biomarkers such as C-reactive protein, suggesting anti-inflammatory effects of GLP-1 RAs [ 28 ]. The REWIND trial, which enrolled nearly 10,000 participants, demonstrated more modest reductions in HbA 1c (0.6 to 0.7%) and weight (around 2 kg) with dulaglutide, yet still achieved a 15% reduction in microvascular complications (HR 0.85; 95% CI 0.77–0.93) [ 29 ]. The EXSCEL trial with exenatide showed similar glycemic and weight benefits, accompanied by trends toward better microvascular outcomes [ 30 ]. Ongoing research continues to expand the understanding of GLP-1 RAs. Trials within the SUSTAIN series, such as SUSTAIN-7 and SUSTAIN-8, are focusing on the effects of semaglutide on various patient populations, including those with renal issues, further elucidating its renal and cardiovascular benefits. Additionally, the LEADER trial is undergoing additional long-term follow-up analyses on liraglutide data, which may provide valuable insights into its long-term effects on neuropathy and other microvascular complications. Given the close pathophysiological links among diabetic microvascular complications, including nephropathy, retinopathy, and neuropathy with endothelial dysfunction, increased arterial stiffness, chronic inflammation, oxidative stress, atherogenic dyslipidemia, and hyperglycemia-induced metabolic disturbances [ 31 , 32 ], it is plausible that the protective effects of GLP-1 RAs extend beyond kidneys and eyes to encompass peripheral nerves. This hypothesis is supported by a growing body of preclinical research demonstrating that GLP-1 RAs like semaglutide and liraglutide reduce oxidative stress and inflammation in animal models of diabetic neuropathy, promote nerve regeneration, and improve nerve conduction velocity [ 33 , 34 ]. More recent clinical evidence indicates that GLP-1 RAs can reverse nerve morphological abnormalities in patients with DPN, providing compelling support for their neuroprotective potential. Although data remain limited, emerging studies, including a recent meta-analysis, suggest these agents may also improve neuropathic symptoms and enhance nerve function [ 35 ]. Real-world evidence further strengthens the neuroprotective potential of GLP-1 RAs. A large retrospective propensity-matched cohort study by Siddeeque and colleagues, which included over 200,000 adults with obesity, found that treatment with GLP-1 RAs was associated with significant reductions in the risk of major neurodegenerative diseases. Specifically, the relative risks were reduced by approximately 37% for Alzheimer’s disease, 41% for Lewy body dementia, and 56% for vascular dementia. Although the overall reduction in Parkinson’s disease risk did not reach statistical significance, semaglutide users exhibited a notable protective effect with a relative risk of 0.574. Moreover, GLP-1 RAs therapy correlated with a substantial 47.5% decrease in all-cause mortality [ 36 ] (Table 2 ). These findings point to benefits that surpass glycemic control, potentially involving neurovascular and anti-inflammatory mechanisms relevant to neurodegenerative conditions and diabetic neuropathy. In summary, GLP-1 RAs show remarkable potential for addressing multiple core mechanisms underlying diabetic complications, including hyperglycemia, obesity, and systemic inflammation. Their proven benefits in reducing nephropathy and other microvascular complications, coupled with emerging preclinical and observational evidence of neuroprotection, support the rationale for their further investigation as therapeutic agents for diabetic neuropathy. Further targeted investigations are warranted to substantiate these findings and clarify their therapeutic implications in diabetic neuropathy. Dual Receptor Agonists Dual agonists in diabetes are medications designed to activate two different gut hormone receptors, most commonly GLP-1 and glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP), or sometimes GLP-1 and glucagon receptors. These dual agonists, such as tirzepatide (a GIP/GLP-1 dual agonist), are emerging as promising treatments for T2DM due to their superior effects on blood glucose control and weight loss compared to traditional single agonists. These agents offer an alternative to enhance the incretin effect, which is often diminished in people with diabetes, and can help improve glycemic control while also providing benefits such as lowering HbA 1c , promoting weight loss, improving cardiovascular health, supporting a healthy lipoprotein profile, and improving metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH) [ 38 ]. In terms of DPN, there is growing evidence that GLP-1 RAs provide neuroprotective effects, which may help limit or slow the progression of neuropathy in some patients. The SURPASS trials are ongoing studies evaluating the efficacy of tirzepatide in improving glycemic control and its potential effects on diabetic complications, including DN. Tirzepatide is a new medication for diabetes that has received approval from the Food and Drug Administration (FDA). The term "twincretin" was introduced by Finan and colleagues to describe the combined effects of these two hormones on insulin secretion [ 39 ]. Around 15–20 min after a meal, levels of GLP-1 and GIP in the blood rise and stimulate receptors on pancreatic cells, leading to a glucose-dependent insulin response to process carbohydrates and fats. This review chapter highlights tirzepatide’s significant clinical promise for diabetes and related metabolic disorders. In the SURPASS-1 trial, tirzepatide’s efficacy and safety in T2DM were shown, with improvements in blood glucose control. No specific direct evidence or study outcomes were reported regarding improvement of established DN, though improved glycemic control is known to help slow progression of diabetic complications [ 40 ]. The SURMOUNT-1 and SURPASS-5 trials summarize weight loss and glycemic benefits, again, both associated with reduced risk and progression of DN, but this study does not present direct clinical trial evidence of tirzepatide improving established neuropathy [ 41 , 42 ]. There is adequate evidence that tirzepatide provides a superior net health benefit for T2DM. Although this article does not mention direct trial results focused on DN, it supports superior metabolic outcomes, which can influence neuropathy risk [ 43 ]. Medications affecting GIP and GLP-1 receptors, such as tirzepatide, might have neuroprotective effects, potentially aiding in conditions involving neuronal degeneration, like Parkinson’s or Alzheimer’s disease [ 44 – 46 ]. There are some recent studies that suggest that tirzepatide may help reduce the risk of developing DPN. According to a large database analysis, patients taking tirzepatide had a significantly lower risk for DPN over 2 years. Additionally, research indicates that tirzepatide may alleviate oxidative stress and inflammation, which are key factors in DN. These findings were presented at the Peripheral Nerve Society (PNS) 2024 Annual Meeting [ 47 ]. Using data from the TrinetX Global Health Research Network, electronic health records of patients with T2DM from 83 healthcare organizations were analyzed. A total of 988,074 patients were taking no diabetes medications; 328,574 were taking insulin; 387,130 were taking metformin; 27,776 were taking semaglutide; 27,514 were taking empagliflozin; 21,972 were taking dulaglutide; 4,225 were taking liraglutide; and 2,146 were taking tirzepatide. Patients were score–matched based on age, sex, race, body mass index (BMI), glucose (≥ 140 mg/dl), and HbA 1c (≥ 7%) and followed for the outcome of new-onset DPN at three time points: 1 month, 1 year, and 2 years. Results showed that although tirzepatide reduced the risk for DPN by − 0.6%, − 1.1%, and − 1.8% at 1 month, 1 year, and 2 years, respectively, insulin actually increased the risk at each time point (+ 0.2%, + 1.4%, + 1.4%). This translated to a relative risk (RR) of 3% of developing DPN at 2 years for tirzepatide compared with an RR of 6.3% for those on insulin [ 47 ]. The exact mechanisms are still being studied, and more research is needed to fully understand its protective effects. The research by Fontanella et al. [ 48 ] demonstrated that tirzepatide can improve high glucose-induced neurodegeneration and counter neuronal insulin resistance. Tirzepatide treatment activated the CREB/BDNF signaling cascade, essential for neuronal growth, while also preventing the downregulation of glucose transporters caused by high glucose levels. The study also revealed that tirzepatide induces specific miRNA expression, reversing epigenetic modifications caused by hyperglycemia and further promoting neuronal survival and differentiation. These findings suggest that tirzepatide could have therapeutic potential for treating diabetes-related neuropathy and neurodegenerative disorders. As for dual agonists of GLP1 and GIP, for agents that combine GLP1 and glucagon receptor agonists, particularly glucagon receptor agonists, there is limited direct evidence on their effects on DPN. Mazdutide is also administered by subcutaneous injection once a week. In a Chinese phase II trial, mazdutide reduced HbA 1c in a dose-dependent manner by 1.41% to 1.67%, while the placebo group’s HbA 1c increased by 0.03% [ 49 ]. Survodutide was shown to significantly reduce HbA 1c compared to placebo in a phase II study, with dosing given either once or twice weekly via subcutaneous injection [ 50 ]. Cagrilintide, a long-acting amylin analogue, is being developed alongside semaglutide 2.4 mg (forming the combination known as CagriSema) for treating T2DM. This combination is given as a weekly subcutaneous injection. In a phase 2 clinical trial, CagriSema lowered HbA 1c by 2.2% ( p = 0.075) compared to semaglutide 2.4 mg alone [ 51 ]. For now, there are no data on the efficacy of these therapeutics in the treatment of DN. Orforglipron is an oral, non-peptide, small-molecule GLP-1 receptor agonist. Unlike peptide GLP-1 receptor agonists, orforglipron causes less receptor desensitization by enhancing cyclic AMP signaling. In a phase II trial for T2DM, orforglipron significantly reduced HbA 1c by 2.1%, compared with reductions of 1.1% from dulaglutide and 0.43% from placebo [ 52 ]. In another phase II trial, twice-daily danuglipron resulted in a dose-dependent HbA 1c reduction of 0.49% to 1.18%, while the placebo group saw a decrease of just 0.02% [ 53 ]. GLP-1 RAs have been proven to improve glycemic control when added to insulin therapy, especially basal insulin, and may be especially helpful in more advanced T2DM. In the SUSTAIN-5 trial, adding semaglutide at a dose of 0.5 mg or 1 mg per week to basal insulin led to HbA 1c reductions of 1% and 1.8%, respectively [ 54 ]. Compared to traditional opinions on insulin, it may be that insulin is not the best choice for all patients with DPN, and some of these newer medications may be better choices [ 55 , 56 ]. However, research is ongoing, and the clinical impact of dual agonists on DPN is still under investigation. Triple Receptor Agonists Incretin-based therapies that simultaneously target GLP-1, GIP, and glucagon receptors represent a novel and promising class of agents, offering substantial weight loss, improved metabolic control, and potential benefits that may extend beyond glucoregulation. Retatrutide is a novel triple hormone receptor agonist with promising results in phase II clinical trials for obesity and T2DM treatment (Table 3 ). In a 48-week study, participants receiving retatrutide experienced substantial weight loss, with reductions of 22.8% and 24.2% at 8 mg and 12 mg doses, respectively [ 57 ]. In addition to body weight, retatrutide efficiently reduced HbA 1c levels in patients with T2DM [ 40 ]. Furthermore, upcoming retatrutide trials are planned to assess its safety and efficacy more comprehensively, including potential implications for metabolic and neuropathic outcomes. A recent study showed promising results for retatrutide in treating metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD), in terms of significant reduction in liver fat, with up to 82.4% decrease at 24 weeks with higher doses [ 58 ]. Overall, retatrutide appears to be a promising candidate for obesity and diabetes treatment, warranting further investigation. While improved glycemic control remains a cornerstone in preventing microvascular complications, available evidence suggests that obesity, hypertension, and hypertriglyceridemia are also associated with the development of DN [ 59 ]. Thus, triple receptor agonist treatment may exert additional disease-modifying effects by modulating these specific risk factors. For instance, in the clinical trial for obesity treatment, retatrutide also led to greater reductions in both systolic and diastolic blood pressure than placebo [ 57 ]. Similarly, in the T2DM trial, reductions in systolic blood pressure were more pronounced with retatrutide than with either dulaglutide or placebo [ 40 ]. Finally, in the MASLD clinical trial, liver fat reduction was closely related to improvements in cardiometabolic biomarkers, including body weight, insulin sensitivity, and triglyceride levels [ 58 ]. Direct mechanistic evidence supporting the efficacy of triple receptor agonists in DN is currently lacking. To date, most neuroprotective insights into incretin-based therapies have originated from studies of GLP-1 RAs in DN models and GIP receptor agonists in models of neurodegenerative disease (Fig. 1 ). Specifically, GLP-1 and GIP receptor activation has been shown to attenuate oxidative stress and neuroinflammation [ 60 ], both of which play central roles in peripheral nerve demyelination and the development of neuropathic pain [ 61 ]. In parallel, glucagon receptor co-activation may enhance lipid oxidation and mitochondrial function, potentially reducing lipotoxicity, another contributor to DN progression [ 62 ]. Collectively, these mechanisms may complement processes that enhance myelination and support Schwann cell survival, mediated by GLP-1-RAs [ 63 – 65 ]. Yet, the potential neuroprotective effects of triple receptor agonists have been explored in a few preclinical studies [ 66 ]. Based on the currently available, albeit limited data, efocipegtrutide (HM15211), another GLP-1/GIP/glucagon receptor triple agonist, seems to have beneficial effects in animal models of neurodegenerative diseases [ 67 , 68 ]. Emerging Agents In addition to being the most common, DPN is a multifaceted complication of diabetes. The majority of available DPN treatments focus on symptom relief rather than disease modification [ 69 ]. However, many innovative therapies, ranging from molecularly targeted drugs to regenerative cell and gene therapies, are under investigation [ 70 ]. Even with strict glycemic control, many patients with T2DM continue to experience DPN progression. Conventional cardiovascular drugs (such as aspirin and antioxidants) mostly address related conditions and are not effective for DPN itself. Pain management options often lose efficacy over time, requiring higher doses or combination therapies. Recent trials (COMBO-DN, OPTION-DM) show that combination therapies do not markedly outperform high-dose single therapies [ 71 , 72 ]. Discussion A growing body of research indicates that diabetes treatment is evolving toward therapeutic strategies aimed at modifying disease progression. SGLT2 inhibitors have changed the management of T2DM by extending their benefits beyond glucose lowering to include cardiovascular, renal, and metabolic protection. Increasing evidence suggests that these effects may also play a role in protecting the nervous system. The possible neuroprotective actions of SGLT2 inhibitors are likely multifactorial. By reducing oxidative stress, systemic inflammation, and metabolic dysregulation, these agents may counteract key mechanisms involved in the development of DPN. Their positive effects on endothelial function and microvascular perfusion may help maintain nerve fiber integrity through better oxygen and nutrient delivery. In addition, modulation of mitochondrial activity and cellular energy balance could promote neural repair and regeneration. Interestingly, some of these benefits appear to occur independently of glucose control, suggesting that SGLT2 inhibitors may directly influence cellular mechanisms such as AMPK activation and improved mitochondrial function. However, despite these promising findings, current evidence remains limited and heterogeneous. Differences in study design, patient characteristics, and treatment duration make it difficult to draw firm conclusions. Well-designed studies specifically focused on neuropathy outcomes are needed to confirm these effects and clarify their clinical significance. In parallel, GLP-1 RAs represent another drug class with potential neuroprotective effects. These agents act on multiple metabolic pathways, improving insulin sensitivity, reducing body weight, and lowering systemic inflammation, all of which contribute to nerve damage in diabetes. Their positive impact on endothelial function and microvascular perfusion may further support the delivery of oxygen and nutrients to peripheral nerves, promoting structural maintenance and repair. Preclinical studies suggest that GLP-1 RAs can reduce oxidative stress, enhance axonal regeneration, and improve nerve conduction. Although these mechanisms are biologically plausible, clinical data specifically addressing neuropathy remain scarce. Most available studies were designed to assess cardiovascular or renal outcomes, with neuropathy endpoints evaluated only secondarily. Therefore, despite encouraging early evidence, the role of GLP-1 RAs in slowing or preventing DPN progression remains uncertain. Future research with dedicated, long-term clinical trials is needed to determine their potential as disease-modifying therapies in DN. Evidence supporting neuroprotection provided by dual agonists in DN is limited, with no randomized controlled trials specifically assessing neuropathy outcomes. Gold standard measures, such as nerve conduction velocity, intraepidermal nerve fiber density, or corneal confocal microscopy, which were performed in previously described SGLT2 inhibitor studies, are rarely evaluated. Follow-up periods have been too short to detect meaningful neuronal changes, and electrophysiological testing is typically absent. Although observational data suggest a potential reduction in DN incidence with dual agonists, these findings are vulnerable to confounding. Therefore, it remains unclear whether neuroprotection arises directly from GIP receptor activation or through improved metabolic control. Hence, long-term randomized clinical trials are needed to clarify these effects. Recent reports on incretin-based therapies that simultaneously target GLP-1, GIP, and glucagon receptors suggest a potential step forward in the management of T2DM and associated complications. In particular, retatrutide has demonstrated sustained weight loss and significant HbA 1c reduction, exceeding the effects of the available incretin-based therapeutics. Furthermore, retatrutide treatment modulated metabolic alterations that contribute to the development of both macrovascular and microvascular complications of diabetes, such as hypertension and dyslipidemia. Although current clinical trials have not specifically addressed DN as an outcome, the observed improvement of cardiometabolic risk factors may potentially slow its progression. Early preclinical data with other triple agonists, such as efocipegtrutide, further support their potential neuroprotective effect. In preclinical studies, retatrutide was recently shown as the most effective incretin-based agent in db/db mouse model of diabetic kidney disease [ 97 ]. Specifically, the group receiving retatrutide had significantly improved levels of kidney function parameters, as well as lower expression of inflammatory and fibrotic biomarkers in renal tissue. Taken altogether, retatrutide seems as a promising therapeutic candidate for microvascular complications of diabetes, but future research should aim to elucidate its specific effects on DN. Future Directions Innovative antidiabetic therapies have attracted growing interest not only for their effects on glycemic control, but also for their capacity to delay the onset and progression of chronic diabetes complications. Yet, several unanswered questions remain. Although SGLT2 inhibitors have shown improvements in neurophysiological parameters of peripheral neuropathy, they do not appear to significantly affect the overall prevalence of the condition. Also, available evidence does not support the superiority of any specific SGLT2 inhibitor in the context of neuropathy-related outcomes. However, certain SGLT2 inhibitors, such as canagliflozin, may be associated with an increased risk of lower limb amputation (LLA), which is more pronounced in patients with preexisting peripheral arterial disease (PAD) and DN, or prior history of amputation [ 98 ]. A recent meta-analysis, including over 300,000 patients with T2DM worldwide, of whom over 50,000 subjects had PAD, examined whether SGLT2 inhibitors increase the risk of LLA compared to other glucose-lowering therapies. The authors found no significant increase in the LLA for SGLT2 inhibitors compared to GLP-1 RAs, while they were associated with a lower risk compared to dipeptidyl peptidase-4 (DPP-4) inhibitors, but a higher risk compared to sulphonylureas [ 99 ]. Thus, future large-scale comparative studies with longer follow-up are needed to accurately estimate the amputation risk associated with specific antidiabetic therapy across different categories of patients with T2DM. Clinical data specifically addressing the effects of incretin-based therapeutics on DN remain limited, despite compelling preclinical evidence of their favorable metabolic effects, which support their further evaluation. Of note, a possible association between certain GLP-1 RAs, such as semaglutide, and increased incidence of nonarteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (NAION) has been suggested [ 100 ]. However, besides several case reports and observational studies, no large clinical trial specifically linking GLP-1 RAs to either increased risk or protection from NAION has been published. More recently, a registry-based study in the United States, including over 37 million patients, reported a modest increase in the rate of NAION among semaglutide users. The authors found no significant difference in the risk of NAION among new semaglutide users when compared to users of other GLP-1 RAs or non-GLP-1 RAs therapies, except empagliflozin [ 93 ]. Although further research is essential to investigate this potential causal relationship, caution is advised when prescribing GLP-1 RAs to patients with preexisting ocular risk factors [ 101 ]. The mentioned findings emphasize the need for long-term clinical trials, employing standardized neuropathy assessment tools, to clarify the impact of innovative antidiabetic therapies on DN and to determine whether the observed changes could be translated into substantial clinical benefits. These studies should include nerve conduction assessment, quantitative sensory testing, and neuropathy-specific self-reported outcomes tools related to patients’ personal experience of symptoms and quality of life. Furthermore, the assessment could be supported by advanced imaging techniques and biochemical markers of neuronal injury, such as neurofilament light chain [ 102 ] and neuron-specific enolase [ 103 ], among others [ 104 ]. Future longitudinal studies involving patients with established DN and extended follow-up periods are of particular interest to accurately evaluate the status of this complication during treatment with innovative antidiabetic therapies. Finally, further studies are warranted to clarify their effects on diabetic autonomic neuropathy, given their potential impact on cardiac autonomic function [ 105 , 106 ]. Conclusions In recent years, the treatment paradigm for DN has gradually been shifting from symptomatic management toward addressing underlying risk factors and promoting neuronal regeneration. Emerging evidence suggests that innovative diabetes therapies, particularly GLP-1 RAs and SGLT2 inhibitors, may offer neuroprotective benefits beyond glycemic control, through their favorable effects on metabolic regulation, inflammation, oxidative stress and neuronal survival. Although the clinical data are still evolving, early findings are encouraging and support the exploration of these agents in the years to come. Furthermore, the development of dual and triple receptor agonists paves the way for more comprehensive DN treatment strategies by simultaneously targeting multiple metabolic pathways. In addition, a growing body of research is increasingly focusing on effective neuropathic pain management alongside regenerative approaches. Altogether, these developments represent promising new directions in the management of DN, with the potential to improve both symptoms and quality of life of the patients. Future research through well-designed, long-term clinical trials, employing standardized neuropathy assessment tools, is essential to validate these therapeutic approaches and translate them into meaningful clinical outcomes.
