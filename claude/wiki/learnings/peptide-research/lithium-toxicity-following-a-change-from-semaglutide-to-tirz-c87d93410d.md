Introduction Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) are increasingly prescribed for weight management. Although generally well-tolerated, these agents may alter the pharmacokinetics of concurrently administered medications. Case Presentation A 23-year-old male with schizoaffective disorder, bipolar type, developed lithium toxicity shortly after switching from semaglutide to tirzepatide. While taking semaglutide, the patient was started on lithium and titrated to 1800 mg nightly, corresponding to a level of 0.9 mEq/L. Four days after his first dose of tirzepatide, he reported early symptoms of potential lithium toxicity, which progressed significantly after the second dose. Symptoms corresponded to a 15-hour post-dose level of 1.7 mEq/L, despite no changes in renal function, hydration status, or other concurrent medications. Management with intravenous fluids was sufficient, and lithium dose reduction prevented future toxicity. Discussion Application of the Drug Interaction Probability Scale suggests a probable interaction between tirzepatide and lithium. The interaction might be mediated through delayed gastric emptying or other pharmacokinetic changes. Limited reports of GLP-1-modulating agents associated with lithium toxicity have been published and may be an underrecognized phenomenon. To date, no pharmacokinetic studies have been published evaluating this potential drug-drug interaction. Although many other medications seem to be at risk for decreased absorption, tirzepatide may enhance the absorption of lithium. Conclusion Clinicians should be vigilant when initiating or switching GLP-1-modulating agents in patients receiving lithium. Pharmacokinetic studies are needed to clarify the mechanism of this interaction. Several medications, including nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, angiotensin II receptor blockers, and thiazide diuretics, are well-known to increase lithium levels. 1 Data implicate glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) as also having the potential to elevate lithium levels, but this is limited to just a case report level of information. 2 , 3 This class of medication is known to cause nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, which may precipitate lithium toxicity, but it is possible that changes in lithium pharmacokinetics may be involved. Most pharmacokinetic studies of other medications combined with GLP-1 RAs suggest that absorption is minimally affected or may be decreased. 4 However, some medications may be prone to increased absorption. For lithium, when combined with the GLP-1 RA, semaglutide, enhanced absorption has been reported. 2 In a single case, a patient was found to have a lithium level of 2.4 mEq/L 1 week after starting semaglutide 0.25 mg weekly. Semaglutide would be restarted, but the patient required a 50% lithium dose reduction to maintain similar pre-semaglutide levels. Additionally, a report of 3 cases described possible changes in lithium clearance after the introduction of semaglutide. 3 In 2 cases, the patients presented to the hospital and were found to have lithium toxicity. In the third case, lithium was reduced from 2100 to 1800, and the lithium level increased from 1.1 to 1.2 mEq/L after the introduction of semaglutide. Ultimately, the lithium dose would be decreased to 1200 mg (a 42% decrease from the original dose), with the corresponding lithium level decreasing to 0.8 mEq/L (a 27% reduction from baseline). To date, there have been no cases describing lithium toxicity after a change from semaglutide to tirzepatide, nor cases with tirzepatide initiation. Tirzepatide, like semaglutide, is a GLP-1 RA but also is a glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP) RA. It is not known whether this dual mechanism confers any added risk for lithium interaction. However, tirzepatide has been shown to produce significantly greater weight loss than semaglutide as demonstrated in a recent randomized controlled trial. 5 Additional research is needed to explore the potential interaction between both GLP-1 and GLP-1/GIP RAs to prevent lithium toxicity. A case of lithium toxicity is reported after the change from semaglutide to tirzepatide. Case Report A 23-year-old male with a history of schizoaffective disorder, bipolar type, obesity (class III), and multiple psychiatric hospitalizations related to medication nonadherence was admitted to an inpatient psychiatric unit for management of acute manic symptoms. Before admission, the patient was prescribed lurasidone 80 mg daily and quetiapine 50 mg at bedtime; however, he reported nonadherence to these medications. Additionally, the patient was prescribed weekly subcutaneous semaglutide for weight management several months before hospitalization. At semaglutide initiation, his weight was 150 kg with a body mass index of 51.3 kg/m 2 . Despite intermittent gaps in semaglutide use, the medication was successfully titrated to a dose of 2.4 mg weekly. During this hospitalization, lurasidone and quetiapine were discontinued, and oral aripiprazole was started. He would later transition to aripiprazole monohydrate 400 mg intramuscular injection every month. As the patient reported adherence to semaglutide 2.4 mg weekly, it was continued throughout the hospitalization. Lithium (as extended-release) was also initiated and titrated to 1800 mg at bedtime, achieving a 12-hour serum lithium concentration of 0.9 mEq/L at discharge. Two months after discharge, a repeat lithium level measured 15 hours postdose was 0.9 mEq/L. Four months after discharge, the patient maintained mood stability and absence of psychotic symptoms. Additionally, at 4 months posthospitalization, because of a change in insurance coverage, semaglutide was discontinued and replaced with subcutaneous tirzepatide 5 mg weekly, with a weight of 140 kg at the time. Four days after the first tirzepatide injection, the patient reported polydipsia and intermittent blurred vision. Seven days after administration of the second 5 mg dose of tirzepatide, the patient presented to the emergency department with worsening dizziness, blurred vision, speech apraxia, nausea, and weakness. His weight at this time was 138 kg. A lithium level drawn 15 hours after the last dose was found to be 1.7 mEq/L. Serum creatinine at presentation was 1.03 mg/dL, unchanged from baseline. The serum sodium level was found to be within normal limits (139 mEq/L). The patient reported no recent changes in daily habits or medication exposures, including caffeine intake or use of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, aside from the change to tirzepatide. He denied vomiting or diarrhea. Conservative management with intravenous fluids resulted in symptom stabilization without progression of toxicity. The extended-release lithium dose was reduced the following day to 1350 mg at bedtime. Two weeks later, a 12-hour trough lithium level was 0.7 mEq/L. Tirzepatide was withheld during these 2 weeks due to concerns about a possible drug interaction. Tirzepatide 5 mg weekly was resumed 3 days after the lithium level of 0.7 mEq/L was achieved. Five days later, with the continued tirzepatide, a lithium level drawn 13 hours postdose was 1.0 mEq/L. After an additional 16 days, the patient reported fatigue attributed to lithium and skipped 1 dose with symptom improvement; he expressed a desire to discontinue lithium. However, the patient agreed to continue lithium extended-release at a dose of 1200 mg at bedtime after a discussion with his psychiatrist and recognizing the clinical benefits from the medication. One week after this adjustment, the lithium level measured 15 hours postdose was 0.6 mEq/L. This level was considered appropriate for maintenance treatment. Through an additional 4 months of follow-up, the patient maintained good mood stability on the same regimen without the emergence of symptoms associated with lithium toxicity. Recently, the patient and his primary care physician discussed increasing the dose of tirzepatide, given that the patient’s weight (138 kg) remained unchanged despite 5 mg weekly dose. Close monitoring of lithium levels has been planned. Discussion This case describes a patient with previously stable lithium levels who developed lithium toxicity after a change from semaglutide, a GLP-1 RA, to tirzepatide, a GIP/GLP-1 RA. The patient maintained stable lithium levels while receiving semaglutide; however, serum lithium concentrations increased significantly—prompting an emergency department visit—soon after switching to tirzepatide. Notably, a smaller rise in lithium levels was also observed upon reinitiating tirzepatide after a 3-week interruption. The temporal association between tirzepatide initiation and the subsequent rise in serum lithium concentrations with correlating symptoms of toxicity suggests a potential drug-drug interaction (DDI). Use of the Drug Interaction Probability Scale suggests a probable (score 7; where &gt;8 = highly probable, 5–8 = probable, 2–4 = possible, &lt;2 = doubtful) interaction between tirzepatide and lithium. 6 Given the rapid rise in the use of GLP-1 and GIP/GLP-1 RAs and their growing accessibility, understanding the potential for drug-drug interactions is crucial. Most studies have emphasized pharmacokinetic changes via the mechanism of delayed gastric emptying exerted by GLP-1 and GIP/GLP-1 RAs. This phenomenon has been discussed in multiple reviews reporting reductions in maximum concentration (Cmax) and delays in time to maximum concentration (Tmax) of several coadministered drugs with GLP-1 RAs. 4 , 7 Yet, many studies reported that the total exposure (ie, areas under the curve) or clinical effectiveness of medications, such as acetaminophen, HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors, and angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, were not changed or were not clinically significant. The area under the curve for metformin after the addition of oral semaglutide increased by 32% in one study, but remained largely unchanged in another. 8 , 9 Caution has been advised for medications with narrow therapeutic indices, 7 a warning specifically communicated in the prescribing information for tirzepatide. 10 Limited pharmacokinetic influence of certain GLP-1 RAs on digoxin, warfarin, or tacrolimus is reassuring, while oral semaglutide has been observed to increase total exposure to levothyroxine by 33%. 4 , 7 Decreased absorption of oral hormonal contraceptives has been documented, such that patients receiving tirzepatide should use an alternative form of contraception. 7 Unfortunately, no pharmacokinetic studies have been published regarding the interaction between lithium and GLP-1 RAs or GIP/GLP-1 RAs. In fact, very limited data are available regarding pharmacokinetic changes between GLP-1 or GIP/GLP-1 RAs and any psychiatric medication. One recent randomized controlled trial assessing the impact of semaglutide on weight in patients prescribed clozapine reported that serum levels were not significantly different from baseline to the study endpoint (310.68 vs 289.47 ng/mL). 11 Lithium is rapidly absorbed in the upper gastrointestinal tract with peak serum concentrations typically reached within a few hours, depending on the formulation. 12 However, data on the precise location of absorption within the gastrointestinal tract remain unclear, and data on how gastric motility affects lithium pharmacokinetics are similarly limited. One animal study found that propantheline, a potent anticholinergic, reduced Cmax and prolonged the plateau phase of lithium concentration by 272%. 13 However, the areas under the curve with and without propantheline did not differ. Similarly, a small human study documented coadministration of lithium “slow release” with metoclopramide resulted in a more rapid Tmax and higher Cmax, whereas coadministration with propantheline delayed Tmax and lowered Cmax, with levels rising gradually over 12 hours. The authors hypothesized that these results were secondary to increased or decreased gastrointestinal transit time via metoclopramide and propantheline, respectively. 14 It is unclear how this might apply to lithium in the setting of a GLP-1 RA or GIP/GLP-1 RA or if delays in absorption with extended-release lithium could create an accumulation effect with repeated dosing. Notably, in this case and the 4 other published cases involving semaglutide-associated lithium toxicity, patients were prescribed extended-release lithium with toxicity developing in a similar timeframe. 2 , 3 Until more is known, it would be safer to err on the side of caution and have awareness that GLP-1 or GIP/GLP-1 RAs might lead to increased lithium levels. Loss of solely adipose tissue would not be expected to result in major changes to lithium’s volume of distribution, and levels would not necessarily be expected to increase. This is because lithium is not distributed outside the total body water compartment, and adipose tissue contains little water. Conversely, loss of lean muscle, a concern described with chronic use of GLP-1 modulating agents, can result in elevated lithium levels. 15 Additionally, obese patients have greater renal clearance of lithium, which results in higher doses needed to achieve the same therapeutic level, as compared with patients who do not have obesity. 16 , 17 Thus, significant weight loss secondary to a GLP-1 or GIP/GLP-1 RA may reduce lithium dose requirements to maintain a similar level, leading to lithium toxicity without dose adjustments. 18 Changes in volume of distribution or in obesity-related lithium clearance seen longitudinally are irrelevant to this case as weight did not change despite an elevated lithium level within 2 weeks of starting tirzepatide. However, these concepts also lend to a potential need for increased monitoring of lithium levels in patients chronically exposed to a GLP-1 or GIP/GLP-1 RA. Lithium is excreted almost exclusively by the kidneys. Its narrow therapeutic window makes it highly susceptible to fluctuations in renal clearance and volume status. 18 GLP-1 RAs are known to cause gastrointestinal adverse effects, such as nausea, vomiting, and decreased oral intake, which can lead to volume depletion, reduced renal perfusion, and a subsequent decline in glomerular filtration rate. This can impair lithium clearance and predispose to toxicity. However, these adverse effects were not observed in this patient. Emerging evidence suggests that GLP-1 RAs may also alter renal hemodynamics. Some studies indicate these agents promote natriuresis and reduce proximal tubular sodium reabsorption, potentially increasing the glomerular filtration rate and enhancing renal clearance. 7 Because lithium is reabsorbed in the proximal tubule, this mechanism would theoretically favor increased lithium excretion rather than accumulation, making it an unlikely explanation for the altered levels observed in our patient. Limitations of this report include the slight variability in the timing of lithium levels. However, based on the eLi 12 equation, 19 it is not predicted that up to the 3-hour difference seen with some levels would dramatically impact the clinical interpretation of the presented case. Future studies should be conducted that can control for factors impacting lithium levels, including laboratory timing. Second, the exact conversions of GLP-1 RA modulating agents are only estimates based on glycemic control studies in patients with diabetes. A 5-mg dose of tirzepatide has been described as comparable, in this regard, to 2 mg of semaglutide. 20 , 21 It is not known if the patient would have avoided lithium toxicity, at least initially, if switched to 2.5 mg of tirzepatide. Yet, this reinforces the consideration of obtaining lithium levels not only when initiating a GLP-1-modulating agent or with dose changes, but also when switching between agents. This case contributes to the literature about a potentially underrecognized interaction. Based on several additional cases involving lithium toxicity, authors locally have been proponents of lithium therapeutic drug monitoring following the initiation of a GLP-1 or GIP/GLP-1 RA, after each dose change, and when switching to a different agent in the class. More information is also required to determine what increased lithium level monitoring, if at all, is needed longitudinally due to any significant weight loss or with GLP-1 or GIP/GLP-1 RA. Additional monitoring may also be prudent when a GLP-1 or GIP/GLP-1 RA agent is discontinued. Finally, as both the side effects (eg, diarrhea and vomiting) and potential pharmacokinetic alteration from a GLP-1-modulating agent may increase lithium levels, there is a need to reinforce education with patients on the signs and symptoms of lithium toxicity. Conclusion This case underscores the importance of monitoring lithium levels in patients who are starting or titrating GIPs/GLP-1RAs, despite previously stable lithium levels. Clinicians should counsel patients regarding signs of lithium toxicity and closely monitor serum levels during initiation or when changing GLP-1-modulating therapy. Further research and continued reporting of this potential interaction are warranted to assess the impact of GLP-1 and GIP/GLP-1 RAs on lithium pharmacokinetics. Until more is known, closer monitoring of lithium levels is warranted when initiating, changing, or increasing the dose of a GLP-1 or GIP/GLP-1 RA.
