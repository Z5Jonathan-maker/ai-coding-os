THE RED FLAGS
7 Warning Signs — Part 1
1 No Third-Party COA (Certificate of Analysis)
A COA from their own lab means nothing. Any vendor can print a document with numbers on it.
The only COA that matters comes from an independent, accredited third-party laboratory —
one the vendor does not own, operate, or fund. If they can't produce one, they're hiding
something. If they get defensive when you ask, that's your second red flag.
EXPERT
2 Prices Too Good to Be True
Quality peptides cost real money to synthesize properly. Solid-phase peptide synthesis, HPLC
purification, lyophilization, sterile fill, and third-party testing are not cheap. If a vendor is selling
BPC-157 at half the market price, they are cutting corners somewhere — and those corners are
purity, sterility, or potency. You don't want discounts on injectables.
EXPERT
3 No HPLC Purity Data
High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is the gold standard for verifying peptide
identity and purity. A legitimate COA must show >98% purity with a chromatogram — the
actual graph, not just a number. The chromatogram shows the peaks: one dominant peak (your
peptide) and minimal noise (impurities). No chromatogram = no proof.
CLINICAL
4 Selling "Blends" Instead of Individual Compounds
Peptide blends are stealing your money. When a vendor sells a "BPC + TB-500 Blend," you have
no way to verify the dose of each compound, no way to adjust ratios, and no way to isolate
which compound is producing results or side effects. Blends exist to mask underdosing and
inflate margins. Serious practitioners buy individual compounds and mix themselves.

THE RED FLAGS
7 Warning Signs — Part 2
THE COMPOUND RISK
These red flags don't exist in isolation. A vendor that fails on one is likely failing on several. If they don't test
for endotoxins, they probably aren't doing batch-specific HPLC either. One red flag should make you
cautious. Two should make you walk away. Three or more means you're dealing with a vendor that has
no business selling injectables.
5 No Batch-Specific Testing
Each manufacturing batch is different. Synthesis yields vary, purification efficiency fluctuates,
and contamination risks change run to run. A legitimate vendor provides a unique COA for every
batch with a batch number you can cross-reference to your vial. A single "generic" COA that
covers all products is worthless — it proves nothing about what's actually in your vial.
CLINICAL
6 Claims That Oral Peptides Work Systemically
Any vendor selling oral BPC-157 (capsules, tablets, sublingual drops) for systemic effects is
either lying or scientifically ignorant — both disqualifying. Peptides are chains of amino acids.
Your GI tract has proteolytic enzymes whose entire job is to break peptide bonds. Oral BPC-157
is destroyed before it reaches systemic circulation. If a vendor makes this claim, everything else
they say is suspect.
CLINICAL
 EXPERT
7 No Endotoxin Testing
This is the one that can put you in the hospital. Endotoxins are lipopolysaccharides from
bacterial cell walls — they trigger severe inflammatory responses, fever, and in extreme cases,
sepsis. The LAL (Limulus Amebocyte Lysate) test is the standard for detecting endotoxins in
injectables. If a vendor's COA doesn't include endotoxin levels, they are skipping the most
critical safety test for any injectable product. Bacterial contamination is the #1 safety risk in
peptide sourcing.

THE VERIFICATION PROCESS
How to Verify Any Vendor
Follow this step-by-step process before placing any order. A legitimate vendor will make every step easy.
A bad one will make excuses.
1 Request the COA Before Ordering
Email or message the vendor and ask for the Certificate of Analysis for the specific product and batch
you intend to buy. A legitimate vendor will provide this immediately — it should be a standard part of
their operation, not a special request.
2 Check the Lab Name
The COA must come from an independent, accredited third-party laboratory. Search for the lab
online. Verify they exist, have ISO 17025 accreditation (or equivalent), and actually perform peptide
analysis. If the lab name matches the vendor name — it's not third-party.
3 Verify the Batch Number
The batch number on the COA must match the batch number on your vial label. If there's no batch
number on either — or they don't match — the COA is meaningless. Some vendors use a single COA
for all inventory. That's not batch-specific testing.
4 Look for the HPLC Chromatogram
A number saying "99% purity" is not enough. You need the actual chromatogram graph showing a
single dominant peak with minimal impurity peaks. The chromatogram is the proof — the number is
just the summary. No graph = no verification.
5 Check Endotoxin Levels
The COA must include LAL (Limulus Amebocyte Lysate) endotoxin testing results. For injectables,
endotoxin levels must be <5 EU/kg body weight per dose. If endotoxin testing is absent from the
COA, the product has not been verified safe for injection.
6 Cross-Reference Pricing
Compare the vendor's pricing against 3-5 established vendors selling the same compound at the
same quantity. If they're significantly cheaper than the market average, ask why. Low price + no third-
party COA = high probability of a bad product.
The Vendor Verification Checklist
Third-party COA available on request — provided immediately, not after pressure>
Independent lab name verifiable online — ISO accredited, real facility>
Batch number matches vial to COA — every batch tested individually>
HPLC chromatogram included — actual graph, not just a purity percentage>
Endotoxin (LAL) test results present — confirmed safe for injection>
