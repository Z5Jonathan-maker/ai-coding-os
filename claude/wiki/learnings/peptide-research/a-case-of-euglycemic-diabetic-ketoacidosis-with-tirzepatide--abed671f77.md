# A Case of Euglycemic Diabetic Ketoacidosis With Tirzepatide Use and Severe Calorie Restriction

Abstract We describe a case of euglycemic diabetic ketoacidosis (EDKA) in a 30-year-old man with no known diabetes on tirzepatide, intermittent fasting (IF), and a low-carbohydrate diet for weight loss. While glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) and combined GLP-1/glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide receptor agonists (GLP-1/GIP RAs), like tirzepatide, are effective for weight loss and diabetes management, their unsupervised use, especially alongside ketosis-inducing diets, may lead to serious risks such as EDKA. This report emphasizes the need for medical supervision in weight management, particularly when combining medications such as tirzepatide with dietary interventions, like IF and low-carbohydrate diets. Introduction In recent years, intermittent fasting (IF) has emerged as a prominent dietary strategy for patients seeking weight loss, gaining attention because of its potential benefits in improving metabolic health. Due to its popularity in nonscientific media, IF is often being implemented without proper medical supervision. While studies indicate that IF may lead to significant weight reduction and improved glycemic control, it remains uncertain whether the effects are sustained and safe enough to be universally adopted by those seeking weight loss, without consideration of comorbidities or active medical treatments. Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) and combined GLP-1/glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide receptor agonists (GLP-1/GIP RAs), such as tirzepatide, have also gained popularity as effective therapies, not only for diabetes, but also for weight management. By decreasing gastric emptying and food intake, and increasing satiety and glucose-stimulated insulin secretion, they are also approved for obesity management in nondiabetic individuals. We discuss the interaction between tirzepatide treatment, IF, and a low-carbohydrate diet for the management of obesity, highlighting the risk of ketoacidosis in these individuals. Through an extensive literature review, we offer insights into the clinical implications of combining these interventions and hope to increase provider and patient awareness. Case Presentation A 30-year-old man with a history of class I obesity and no alcohol use presented following a motor vehicle accident after getting struck by a passing car and eventually ejected from his motorcycle. He reported a 15-pound (∼7 kg) weight loss over the preceding 3 weeks, which he attributed to tirzepatide 2.5 mg weekly prescribed by a health-care provider, combined with unsupervised IF (12-6 Pm daily) and an extremely low-carbohydrate ketogenic diet, including mostly protein-based foods with vegetables, with his only carbohydrate intake being fruit in the middle of this period. On admission, the patient exhibited extensive musculoskeletal injuries. Diagnostic Assessment Initial laboratory results revealed mild anion gap metabolic acidosis, bicarbonate of 24.4 mEq/L (SI: 24.4 mmol/L) (reference range, 24.0-30.0 mEq/L [SI: 24.0-30.0 mmol/L]), anion gap of 18.6 mEq/L (SI: 18.6 mmol/L) (reference range, ≤13.9 mEq/L [SI: ≤13.9 mmol/L]), undetectable serum alcohol, pH of 7.34 (reference range, 7.35-7.45), and lactate of 3.0 mmol/L (SI: 3.0 mmol/L) (reference range, 0.6-1.4 mmol/L [SI: 0.6-1.4 mmol/L]). Treatment As these findings were suggestive of euglycemic diabetic ketoacidosis (EDKA), the patient received supportive care with glucose containing intravenous fluids, bicarbonate drip, and a close monitoring of electrolyte levels and glucose. Insulin was not administered because of risk of hypoglycemia on presentation. Within 12 hours of this therapy, acid-base disorder was corrected. Of note, the patient's metabolic derangements did not require insulin administration. Outcome and Follow-up It remains unclear whether starvation, the strict low-carbohydrate diet, IF, tirzepatide, or their combination, triggered the ketoacidosis. The patient was educated regarding the potential complications of tirzepatide use and extreme carbohydrate restriction and was advised to seek regular medical supervision for safe weight-loss strategies. Discussion Obesity rates continue to rise at an unprecedented scale worldwide. The rise of consumption of health news from nonscientific media, including social media, has prompted many individuals to seek solutions on their own, often unsupervised by health-care providers. Individuals may choose to adopt a variety of diets, exercise regimens, supplements, over-the-counter medications, and prescription medications. Different types of diets, including IF, have recently gained popularity as individuals seek effective ways to manage their weight. During IF, individuals alternate between periods of regular eating and fasting, by severely restricting their food intake or consuming no food during periods of fasting. While this may lead to weight loss in some individuals, it is not without risks, particularly when undertaken without medical supervision. The popular types of IF diets include alternate-day fasting (ADF), time-restricted feeding (TRF), 4:3, and 5:2 diet. Although evidence regarding the effects of IF in individuals with diabetes or obesity remains preliminary and conflicting, and long-term outcomes are unknown, some beneficial effects have been observed in recent reports [ 1 ]. In a systematic review of 27 IF trials regarding overweight and obese patients, 5, including individuals with type 2 diabetes, reported weight reductions of 0.8% to 13.0% from baseline and improvements in glycemic control with IF. However, it remains unclear if the effect of IF on body weight would be sustained over time as studies are limited to 12 months or less [ 1 , 2 ]. Additionally, a recent meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials of overweight or obese, nondiabetic individuals examined different types of IF and their effect on weight loss and indicated that ADF could be a viable alternative to calorie restriction [ 3 ]. ADF has also been shown to improve insulin resistance as measured by the homeostatic model assessment of insulin resistance (HOMA-IR) index [ 4 ], while TRF has demonstrated improvements in the HOMA-IR index, fasting glucose levels, fasting insulin levels, and even in glycated hemoglobin A 1c (HbA 1c ) in other studies [ 5 ]. GLP-1 and combined GLP-1/GIP RAs were initially approved for the treatment of diabetes and later shown to be highly effective for weight loss in those without diabetes. GLP-1 RAs such as liraglutide, dulaglutide, and semaglutide are approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of obesity. In the landmark SURMOUNT-3 trial, weekly tirzepatide (a dual GLP-1/GIP RA) was associated with approximately 20% further weight loss in 72 weeks at doses of 10 or 15 mg weekly in nondiabetic adults with obesity or overweight with obesity-related complication, who had already lost 5% or more body weight through lifestyle changes [ 6 ]. Additionally, tirzepatide at all doses has demonstrated superiority to weekly semaglutide in improving HbA 1c levels after 40 weeks in type 2 diabetic patients [ 7 ]. While uncommon, GLP-1 RAs have been previously reported to increase the likelihood of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA). While the exact mechanism is unknown, it has been speculated that it may involve a profound reduction in appetite and caloric intake, rapid weight loss, and decreased carbohydrate availability induced by GLP-1 RAs, which can precipitate a state of marked insulin deficiency and increased lipolysis, leading to ketogenesis even in the presence of normal or mildly elevated blood glucose levels. This risk is heightened in individuals with underlying diabetes or those who experience acute illness, dehydration, or substantial caloric restriction while on these medications. In our patient, these dietary practices likely created a marked insulin-deficient state, superimposed on an underlying background of insulin resistance associated with obesity. Data from the FDA Adverse Event Reporting System in the United Kingdom, collected between 2004 and 2019, has documented this risk. Particularly, the risk of DKA is higher when these agents are not combined with insulin, which is usual in obese individuals without diabetes. Notably, ketoacidosis has been reported even without concomitant administration of sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitor (SGLT-2i), a well-known cause of EDKA. Semaglutide demonstrated a higher rate of DKA compared to non–GLP-1-related medications in patients using it for diabetes management. However, when GLP-1 RAs were administered along with insulin, their use was no longer associated with DKA [ 8 , 9 ]. Additionally, semaglutide for 1 week combined with a short-term ketogenic diet in a patient who had previously been on liraglutide for a year resulted in DKA [ 10 ]. Initiation of dulaglutide has also been associated with DKA, or a mixed presentation of DKA with hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS) in patients who had previously been on insulin, that was discontinued 1 day prior to symptom onset [ 11 , 12 ]. Other reports describe ketosis or EDKA following recent-onset, unsupervised liraglutide initiation, combined with a low-carbohydrate diet [ 13 ], and there are also cases of EDKA involving dulaglutide administration along with SGLT-2i treatment and an extremely restricted carbohydrate diet [ 14 ]. Last but not least, and supporting our present report, tirzepatide has been proposed in other reports as a potential causative agent for ketoacidosis states in nondiabetic individuals when used for weight loss [ 15 , 16 ] ( Table 2 ). While prior reports have described ketoacidosis associated with incretin-based therapies, those cases generally occurred in patients without significant dietary carbohydrate restriction. Although there have been rare case reports describing ketoacidosis in nondiabetic individuals after a few days of complete fasting, without any use of GLP-1 RAs [ 28 ], most of the evidence supports that the incidence of ketoacidosis is extremely low in nondiabetic individuals on a ketogenic diet. It should be noted that the risk is higher in people with diabetes, on a very-low-carbohydrate diet, and on SGLT-2is [ 29 ]. In our present case, the physiological stress response to trauma, which involves a surge in cortisol and catecholamines, may also have transiently disrupted glucose metabolism and could have played a role in accelerating the underlying metabolic dysfunction. In this scenario, we think that the combination of recent trauma, ongoing obesity with insulin-resistant state, as well as tirzepatide use, which has been already described as a causative agent of ketoacidosis in similar dietary changes, may have collectively increased the patient's susceptibility to developing EDKA. The combination of marked carbohydrate restriction, prolonged fasting intervals, and tirzepatide therapy is unique in the present report, and we hope that it will increase provider and patient awareness about side effects of these popular interventions. We propose that patients started on pharmacological treatment for obesity should be assessed monthly for 3 months and then at least every 3 months. Change in weight and in waist circumference, blood pressure, glucose levels, as well as the quantity and quality of the diet, amount of physical activity, and mental health should be closely monitored by a multidisciplinary team including dieticians, psychologists, and health coaches [ 30 ]. If discontinuation or dose reduction of sulfonylureas and/or insulin is desired, especially if the patient has type 2 diabetes mellitus and is interested in following a ketogenic diet, they need to be carefully monitored by the health-care provider or providers. Our case highlights the interaction between tirzepatide therapy and a very-low-carbohydrate diet. We propose that the combination of reduced carbohydrate availability, appetite suppression from tirzepatide, and relative insulinopenia led toward accelerated ketogenesis. It is important to raise awareness among medical providers and the lay public that the use of medications, especially in this class, for nonmedically supervised weight loss, can have serious and life-threatening side effects, particularly when combined with low-carbohydrate, ketosis-inducing diets and IF. A collaborative approach between providers, patients, nutritionists, and other health professionals would ensure better outcomes for weight management and overall medical care. Learning Points EDKA can occur in nondiabetic individuals using incretin-based therapies, like tirzepatide, for weight loss, especially when combined with IF and a very low-carbohydrate diet. Medical supervision is essential when combining weight loss medications with dietary interventions to avoid serious metabolic complications.
