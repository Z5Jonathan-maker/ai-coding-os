# Unleashing the Potential of Oral Deliverable Nanomedicine in the Treatment of Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), marked by chronic gastrointestinal tract inflammation, poses a significant global medical challenge. Current treatments for IBD, including corticosteroids, immunomodulators, and biologics, often require frequent systemic administration through parenteral delivery, leading to nonspecific drug distribution, suboptimal therapeutic outcomes, and adverse effects. There is a pressing need for a targeted drug delivery system to enhance drug efficacy and minimize its systemic impact. Nanotechnology emerges as a transformative solution, enabling precise oral drug delivery to inflamed intestinal tissues, reducing off-target effects, and enhancing therapeutic efficiency. The advantages include heightened bioavailability, sustained drug release, and improved cellular uptake. Additionally, the nano-based approach allows for the integration of theranostic elements, enabling simultaneous diagnosis and treatment. Recent preclinical advances in oral IBD treatments, particularly with nanoformulations such as functionalized polymeric and lipid nanoparticles, demonstrate remarkable cell-targeting ability and biosafety, promising to overcome the limitations of conventional therapies. These developments signify a paradigm shift toward personalized and effective oral IBD management. This review explores the potential of oral nanomedicine to enhance IBD treatment significantly, focusing specifically on cell-targeting oral drug delivery system for potential use in IBD management. We also examine emerging technologies such as theranostic nanoparticles and artificial intelligence, identifying avenues for the practical translation of nanomedicines into clinical applications. The incidence of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), including Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis (UC), has surpassed 3 million cases in the United States and Europe, and 6 million cases globally. 1 Effectively managing and treating the sizable population affected by IBD, a chronic disease, presents a substantial global medical challenge. 2 Research indicates that severe symptoms in IBD patients commonly are addressed through injections, including anti–tumor necrosis factor α (anti–TNF-α) antibodies, corticosteroids, and immunosuppressive drugs. 3 However, more than a third of patients exhibit inadequate responses, and even those initially responsive may experience reduced efficacy over time, along with increased risks of infections and associated ailments such as liver toxicity, cancer, depression, and lung inflammation. 4 These issues stem mainly from the nontargeted distribution of injected drugs and subsequent systemic side effects. Therefore, there is a critical need for a targeted drug delivery system (DDS) to enhance the safety and efficacy of therapeutics. Among IBD-targeted DDS, oral delivery offers distinct advantages compared with other methods (eg, intravenous, intramuscular, and rectal routes). 5 , 6 These include a higher likelihood of localizing the drug in the inflamed intestinal region, greater convenience, reduced invasiveness, and improved patient compliance. 7 However, optimal biodistribution and efficacy in oral drug delivery face significant challenges in the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, such as premature drug release, enzymatic biodegradation, and excessive retention by biological barriers. 8 Conventional oral drug formulations struggle with low permeability and poor enzymatic stability in the GI tract. The incorporation of nanomedicine in oral IBD treatment shows great promise, providing a targeted and efficient approach that requires concerted efforts for further advancement in the field. Preclinical studies have explored diverse mechanisms and strategies for oral, disease-targeted, drug delivery, including GI tissue targeting through size, charge, pH, pressure, and microbiome-dependent DDS, which have been reviewed extensively. 9 , 10 More advanced nanotechnology in oral - DDS involves sophisticated surface modifications enabling cell-specific or molecular targeting, such as ligand-receptor and antibody-antigen–dependent technologies. 11 , 12 Bio-inspired nanoparticles (NPs), such as exosomes, also are explored for homing to their originating cells. 13 Clinically, the lack of safe and effective targeted oral therapeutics for IBD has led researchers to explore cutting-edge technologies and incorporate them into developing oral nano DDS. State-of-the-art nanotechnology includes theranostic functions, in which NPs with dual diagnostic and therapeutic capabilities offer a multifaceted approach to addressing IBD complexities. 14 Researchers actively are integrating advanced imaging technologies such as magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) with NPs capable of delivering therapeutic agents to specific sites in the GI tract. 15 This integrated approach allows real-time monitoring of disease progression and treatment response, providing targeted and controlled drug delivery. Theranostic NPs hold great promise for improving diagnostic accuracy and therapeutic outcomes, offering a glimpse into the future of personalized and precision medicine for individuals with this chronic condition. 15 In the era of artificial intelligence (AI), sophisticated multifunctional nanomedicines designed using AI principles are emerging. These advancements align well with the objectives of personalized and precision medicine. 16 This review offers an overview of current and emerging NP-mediated oral nano DDS in preclinical IBD research. It sheds light on the ongoing evolution of these strategies and their substantial potential for practical translation into clinical applications. Recognizing that designing NPs involves more than a standalone consideration, it necessitates the integration of distinctive attributes of the loaded drug payload and the specific characteristics of the patient's disease. Although acknowledging this as a limitation in our current review, we aspire to emphasize the contemporary aspects of oral deliverable nano platforms. These platforms aim to precisely target specific cells by leveraging their unique cellular and molecular properties. Fundamental Aspects of Intestine-Targeted Oral Nano DDS pH variations within the GI tract represent a fundamental characteristic of the digestive system ( Figure 1 ). Nano DDS administered orally encounter the acidic environment of the stomach (pH 1–3) necessary for digestion, followed by mildly alkaline conditions in the duodenum and ileum (pH 6.6–7.5) for neutralization. The colon maintains a neutral pH (7–8) ( Figure 1 A ). 17 To navigate these pH challenges, polymers such as poly lactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) and plant-derived lipid NPs are used in constructing NPs that resist acidic pH, 18 , 19 ensuring targeted cargo delivery to the intestine. Surface modifications with proteins or peptides, although enhancing precise cell targeting, may require additional protection in the stomach, such as a hydrogel or a nano-in-micro DDS. 20 , 21 Figure 1 Fundamental aspects of intestine-targeted oral nano DDS. ( A ) Orally administered NPs encounter environmental changes (pH, mucus, microbiota, enzymes, and so forth) during transition through the GI tract. ( B ) Options of NP materials and targeting mechanisms for delivering medications to patients with IBD. Orally administered nano DDS must contend with various digestive enzymes within the GI tract, including salivary amylase in the oral cavity, along with pepsin and gastric lipase in the stomach, and trypsin in the small intestine. 22 To withstand these enzymatic challenges, polysaccharides such as chitosan and pectic polysaccharides commonly are used in the construction of drug carriers targeting the colon. 23 , 24 Unlike other segments of the GI tract, the colon harbors a significantly more abundant and diverse gut microbiota ( Figure 1 A ), predominantly anaerobic. This microbial community plays a pivotal role in influencing enzymatic behavior, thereby impacting the physiological functions of the colon. 25 The colonic microbiota secretes an array of hydrolytic and reductive enzymes, facilitating the degradation of polysaccharide-based NPs and the subsequent release of encapsulated drugs. Physically, the mucus layer in the GI tract acts as a crucial barrier, shielding the host from exogenous substances such as ingested toxins or medications. 26 This protective function is attributed to the intricate network of mucin fibers within the mucus, characterized by glycosylated, negatively charged segments. Additionally, these mucin fibers contain hydrophobic domains that attract particles with a strong affinity for hydrophobic substances. 27 In the digestive system, the mucus layer in the small intestine is thinner compared with its counterpart in the colon. This thinness is a reflection of its primary role in nutrient absorption. 28 On the contrary, the colon assumes a more pivotal role in water absorption and electrolyte balance. It acts as a protective barrier against the abrasive action of fecal material, effectively preventing mechanical damage to the colon's epithelial cells. 29 The ability of NPs to navigate the mucin network is influenced by factors such as size and surface charge, with smaller NPs demonstrating enhanced penetration capabilities. 30 Exploring surface modifications of NPs offers a potential avenue to improve their interaction with and traversal of mucin barriers. A noteworthy consideration arises when examining individuals with IBD. In this context, the integrity of the intestinal mucus layer becomes compromised, resulting in heightened intestinal permeability, often colloquially referred to as leaky gut ( Figure 1 B ). 31 This compromise entails significant alterations in the composition and thickness of the mucus layer. It involves reduced production of protective mucus, modifications in mucin glycosylation (the adding of sugar molecules to mucin proteins), and changes in the overall structure of the mucin network. Consequently, these modifications lead to an increased permeability of the intestinal lining, allowing the passage of substances that typically are restricted. 32 The compromised mucosal barrier in IBD has substantial implications, potentially enabling the infiltration of harmful agents such as bacteria and toxins, thereby triggering the characteristic inflammatory response. In these conditions, small to mid-sized NPs (&lt;200 nm) find greater ease in penetrating the compromised mucus layer, offering potential therapeutic avenues in IBD treatment. Beneath the intestinal mucus layer lies the epithelium, forming the intestine lining. 33 The small intestine's lining, characterized by villi and crypts, includes absorptive enterocytes, goblet cells, Paneth cells, and enteroendocrine cells. In contrast, the colon lacks pronounced villi and consists of simple tubular glands called colonic crypts, with cell types including absorptive colonocytes and goblet cells. 33 NPs can be tailored to target different locations based on the cell composition and structural differences. Consideration of the transit time through the colon is crucial for sustained drug release. In most IBD cases, NPs are designed to target the immune system, necessitating transcellular transport. 34 This involves transcytosis, wherein particles are taken up by cells at the apical membrane through endocytosis, transported through the cells, and released at the basolateral pole, entering the submucosal layer. In a healthy GI tract, NPs' transcellular transport is limited, but in IBD patients, because of a compromised intestinal lining, this process becomes prominent in NP-based DDS targeting. Molecular Targets for Designing Cell-Specific Nano DDS Molecular targets intricately contribute to the pathophysiological processes underlying inflammation in IBD, encompassing cytokines, cell adhesion molecules, transcription factors, nucleic acids, receptors, and other pivotal proteins associated with the progression of the disease. 3 When designing cell-specific–targeting NPs, researchers actively pinpoint the molecular targets expressed in specific cells. 35 The selection of molecular targets is contingent upon the distinct characteristics of a patient's IBD and the disease stage. Tailoring NPs to address the underlying molecular mechanisms fueling inflammation in individual cases is a key focus. These NPs can be crafted to target one or more categories, adopting a multifaceted approach to tackle the intricate nature of IBD pathogenesis. The cellular location (eg, cell surface, cytoplasmic/intracellular) of a molecular target may shift dynamically with the context and stage of inflammation. Some targets, such as cytokines and reactive oxygen species (ROS), may manifest in both extracellular and intracellular compartments. Successful NP design strategies must factor in these cellular nuances to deliver therapeutic agents proficiently to the intended sites. Macrophage-Targeting Nano DDS In the realm of intestinal inflammation, the depletion of mucous-gel layers and disruption of the epithelial barrier, arising from enterocyte damage and heightened immune cell influx to mucosal tissue, contribute to the selective accumulation and uptake of nanomedicines by immune cells. 49 Macrophages, serving as initial responders in the innate immune system to nanomaterials, emerge as a focal point for cell-targeting strategies in nano-based therapies for IBD. 50 Numerous molecular targets associated with macrophages, particularly saccharide receptors such as the lectin receptor macrophage galactose-type C-type lectin (MGL), mannose receptors, and dectin-1, have been explored for NP targeting. 11 , 12 , 36 , 37 The MGL receptor, a member of the C-type lectin family, is expressed prominently on specific immune cells, notably macrophages. Zeeshan et al 37 demonstrated enhanced targeting efficiency of NPs to macrophages by modifying their surfaces with galactose. Polymeric NPs conjugated with galactose, known as galactosylated (GAL)-PLGA, exhibited optimal characteristics and demonstrated biocompatibility. The uptake of GAL-PLGA by macrophages was hindered by D-galactose, confirming the involvement of MGL-2. In a mouse model with dextran sodium sulfate (DSS) colitis, GAL-PLGA NPs selectively accumulated in inflamed intestines, suggesting their potential as a promising platform for oral targeting of macrophages in the inflamed intestine. In a different approach, Xiong et al 36 developed oral galactosylated carboxymethyl chitosan polymeric nano micelles embedded in a chitosan-alginate hydrogel for delivering cyclosporine A. Galactosylated carboxymethyl chitosan NPs, with favorable attributes, demonstrated increased internalization by macrophages, indicating specific internalization through galactose-receptor–mediated endocytosis for targeted delivery to inflammatory macrophages. Galactose surface modification also was used to enhance the macrophage targeting ability of extracellular vesicles. Liu et al 21 designed a nested delivery system, Gal–interleukin 10 (IL10) extracellular vesicles (in chitosan alginate), to protect IL10 from stomach degradation and facilitate targeted delivery to inflammatory macrophages in the colonic lamina propria. In a study focusing on the polysaccharide lentinan (LNT), Lin et al 11 explored the development of oral LNT-based NPs targeting colonic macrophages. Cellular uptake evaluations demonstrated that LNT-NPs could target macrophages specifically, enhancing their uptake rate through β-glucan to Dectin-1 receptors. LNT-NPs showed excellent gastric stability and resistance to digestive enzymes. Encapsulation significantly improved drug targeting to colon lesions, and biodistribution studies revealed efficient accumulation of LNT-NPs in the inflamed colons of mice. As anticipated, drug-loaded LNT-NPs significantly alleviated inflammation by inhibiting the Toll-like receptor 4/Myeloid differentiation primary response protein 88/nuclear factor-κB signaling pathway. These advantages suggest that LNT-based NPs represent a promising oral DDS for the treatment of UC. Surface modification strategies also were explored for targeting activated macrophages. Li et al 38 fabricated polydopamine NPs with a surface dextran sulfate coating, demonstrating specific binding to the class A macrophage scavenger receptor on activated macrophages. In animal experiments, oral administration of these NPs mitigated colonic inflammation significantly. The more sophisticated DDS was devised by Han et al 12 using β-cyclodextrin and D-mannose surface modification, encapsulated in yeast cell wall microparticles. In vitro experiments demonstrated improved efficiency of NP internalization into macrophages, and mannose-receptor binding facilitated enhanced anti-inflammatory effects. Inspired by the glycocalyx (a surface layer that covers the cell membrane of gut bacteria) structure, Yoo et al 51 constructed a library of glycocalyx-mimicking NPs attached to bilirubin, effectively alleviating colitis symptoms in a mouse model of IBD. Beyond saccharide-modified NPs, Laroui et al 52 demonstrated that surface modification with the fragment antigen-binding region improved the in vitro macrophage-targeting kinetics of polymeric poly(lactic acid)-poly(ethylene glycol) NPs. In DSS-induced colitis mice, orally administered fragment antigen-binding–covered poly(lactic acid)-poly(ethylene glycol) delivered TNF-α small interfering RNA (siRNA) showed significantly higher efficacy in reducing TNF-α expression and enhanced anti-inflammatory activities compared with uncovered NPs. Targeting the Epithelium via Associated Molecular Target The intestinal epithelium plays a pivotal role in the gastrointestinal barrier, and its dysfunction is implicated in IBD. Strategies involving NPs with specific surface modifications, such as ligands or antibodies targeting epithelial cell receptors, have been developed to enhance their affinity for these cells. This approach seeks to deliver therapeutic agents directly to epithelial cells, promoting healing and maintaining barrier integrity. Peptide transporter 1 (PepT1), a proton-coupled oligopeptide transporter found primarily in the small intestine, shows heightened expression on colonic epithelial cells, linking it to the initiation and progression of IBD. Addressing this, Wu et al 39 devised an innovative PepT1-mediated medicinal nano system for the precise delivery of cyclosporine A to mitigate acute ulcerative colitis. Using the lysine–proline–valine (KPV) tripeptide as a high-affinity ligand to PepT1, the synthesized NPs used PLGA–KPV with a montmorillonite/chitosan coating to minimize cyclosporine A leakage. Significantly, these NPs selectively targeted colonic epithelial cells, accumulating in inflamed tissues for up to 36 hours. In a murine model of DSS-induced UC, drug-loaded PLGA–KPV/montmorillonite/chitosan NPs led to marked improvements in body weight, colon length, and disease activity index, suggesting their potential as an effective DDS. Similarly, the up-regulation of cluster of differentiation 98 (CD98) on colonic epithelial cells and macrophages is associated with disease advancement. 40 Addressing this, Xiao et al 41 introduced an innovative strategy by encapsulating CD98 siRNA into a chitosan-alginate hydrogel and grafting NPs with single-chain CD98 antibodies CD98) onto their surface. The resulting NPs exhibited a notable affinity for CD98-overexpressing cells. Xiao et al observed a significant reduction in CD98 levels in Colon-26 cells and RAW 264.7 macrophages when treated with these NPs. Furthermore, orally administered NPs demonstrated efficacy in alleviating colitis severity in both a T-cell transfer mouse model and a DSS-induced colitis model. This study underscores the potential of NPs with high affinity for specific cell types as a promising strategy for targeted drug delivery and IBD treatment. The folate receptor, expressed on the apical (luminal) surface of enterocytes, facilitates the uptake of dietary folate into these absorptive cells. Wang et al 53 used folic acid (FA), the ligand of the folate receptor, as part of a functionalized lipid NP for targeted delivery to enterocytes. The FA was attached in a nonconjugated fashion, enhancing NP absorption and the bioavailability of loaded siRNA. This approach effectively inhibited the expression of divalent metal-ion transporter 1 and attenuated iron loading in a mouse model, suggesting the potential use of FA-functionalized lipid NPs as a nano DDS for treating lesions at the duodenum, which are otherwise challenging to reach. Other Targeting Strategies Beyond the conventional focus on macrophages and enterocytes, exploring alternative cell targets presents a promising frontier in nano-based therapeutic strategies. Goblet cells contribute to mucosal defense, 54 Microfold (M) cells in Peyer's patches form a robust barrier armed with tight junctions, 44 and stem cells provide regenerative potential. 55 Understanding the distinctive roles of these cell types opens new possibilities for fine-tuned interventions. By harnessing the unique characteristics of goblet cells, the strategic location of M cells, and the regenerative potential of stem cells, researchers could tailor NP designs to enhance targeted therapeutic delivery, optimizing efficacy and minimizing off-target effects. Goblet cells are the second most abundant cells in the small intestine's epithelium, secreting mucins for the protective mucus gel layer. Despite being the second-largest population of intestinal epithelial cells, limited research has targeted goblet cells for enhanced oral delivery. Challenges include active mucus secretion counteracting effective drug delivery and cell uptake, along with the relatively low phagocytic and absorptive behavior of goblet cells. Studies identified CSKSSDYQC as a goblet cell–specific targeting ligand, 46 exploring its potential for NP targeting. Chen et al 56 investigated CSKSSDYQC peptide–functionalized N-trimethyl chitosan NPs for oral insulin delivery, highlighting the significant potential of NPs in targeting goblet cells for IBD treatment. M cells are specialized intestinal epithelial cells with a high capacity for transcytosis and trafficking of particulate substances. The unique features of M cells, such as the lack of continuous mucus coating, reduced membrane hydrolase activity, and sparse drug efflux transporters, make them favorable for NP absorption. 57 Targeting M cells involves receptors related to pathogen–host interactions and antigen processing, such as Toll-like receptors, 58 nucleotide oligomerization domain-like receptors, C-type lectin receptors, and α5β1 integrin receptors. 59 Ligands such as mono phosphoryl lipid A, bacterial flagellin, arginyl-glycyl-aspartic acid peptide, and arginyl-glycyl-aspartic acid peptidomimetics enhance NP interactions with M cells. 60 Additionally, Claudin 4, a tight junction protein on M cells, 61 and lectins, such as ulex europaeus agglutinin I, have been used as ligands to improve NP uptake. 62 Claudin-4 targeting with C-terminal of 30 amino acids of Clostridium perfringens peptide (a ligand) also showed enhanced interaction with M cells. 45 Studies using lectin-functionalized NPs demonstrated higher M-cell uptake in vitro and increased localization in Peyer’s patches in vivo, 63 emphasizing the potential of these strategies for oral NP delivery to treat IBD. Intestinal stem cells are crucial for tissue regeneration and repair, and NP targeting of these cells holds potential for accelerating tissue healing in IBD. 64 However, the unique challenges of targeting GI tract stem cells, including their low abundance and dynamic behavior, make it more difficult. Ju et al 47 demonstrated a proof of concept with grape exosome-like NPs (GELNs) successfully targeting intestinal stem cells, leading to tissue remodeling and protection against DSS-induced colitis. Notably, GELN treatment improved stem cell organoid formation, whereas inhibiting β-catenin signaling pathways reduced leucine-rich repeat containing G-protein–coupled receptor 5 + stem cell production. Visualization in leucine-rich repeat containing G-protein–coupled receptor 5–enhanced green fluorescent protein-internal ribosome entry site-Cre recombinase fused to a modified form of the estrogen receptor, variant 2 mouse tissue sections confirmed GELN presence in intestinal stem cells. The uptake mechanism, identified as micropinocytosis, was observed in the colon tumor 26- cell line. 47 These findings suggest that plant-derived exosomal NPs could be a promising option for stem cell–targeted nano DDS. It is worth mentioning that several molecular targets also are present in extracellular space during active IBD, these include ROS, TNF-α, and ILs (IL1, IL6, IL12, and IL23), NPs designed to target these molecules also may have the effect of accumulating at the extracellular matrix. Theranostic Nano DDS In the evolving landscape of IBD treatment, theranostic nano formulations have emerged as a new strategy, integrating diagnostic capabilities with precisely targeted therapeutic interventions. 65 Traditional clinical assessments of IBD rely on identifying signs of bowel inflammation, using contrast agents during diagnostic medical imaging techniques such as computed tomography and MRI. 15 These modalities are foundational in medical imaging, providing detailed insights into the complexities of the gastrointestinal tract. The fusion of nanotechnology with medical imaging techniques marks a significant leap forward in the realm of IBD theranostics. These innovative approaches provide insights into the intricacies of gastrointestinal disorders and pave the way for more targeted and effective therapeutic interventions. Challenges arise in computed tomography imaging owing to the nonspecific distribution of contrast agents in the GI tract, compromising precise diagnosis and subjecting IBD patients to accumulative radiation. 66 Addressing this challenge, Naha et al 67 introduced a novel solution: dextran-functionalized cerium oxide NPs. This nano contrast agent enhances targeting capabilities within intestinal macrophages, exhibiting specificity in inflamed areas while acting as artificial ROS scavengers to alleviate oxidative stress. Dextran-functionalized cerium oxide NPs, with demonstrated stability and biocompatibility in preclinical studies, mark a transformative advancement in the field of IBD theranostics. In recent years, iron oxide NPs (IONPs) have garnered significant interest, particularly in their role as oral contrast agents for MRI, progressing into phase 4 clinical trials. 68 The unique physical and biological features of IONPs, coupled with their versatile surface functionalization capabilities, position them as one of the key players in this transformative landscape. 15 Leveraging their magnetic properties, IONPs can be manipulated to target disease tissues using magnetic force, enabling precise and targeted drug delivery. 69 This targeted approach, coupled with the contrast-enhancement capabilities of IONPs, contributes to enhanced imaging and visualization of specific tissues or organs. Additionally, IONPs use various targeting methods, including passive and active approaches, to deliver loaded drugs to inflamed cells, significantly augmenting therapeutic efficacy. 70 As the field advances, the integration of nano theranostics with MRI holds the promise of more precise and personalized diagnostic and therapeutic interventions in the context of IBD. Apart from their use as contrast agents in medical imaging, molecular imaging techniques also have been used in the development of theranostic nanomedicine for treating IBD. Yan et al 71 contributed to this evolving landscape by engineering P-selectin binding peptide–coated PLGA NPs. These NPs were loaded with lipophilic dyes (1,1'-dioctadecyl-3,3,3',3'-tetramethylindocarbocyanine perchlorate and 1,1'-dioctadecyl-3,3,3',3'-tetramethylindodicarbocyanine, 4-chlorobenzenesulfonate salt) and incorporated anti-inflammatory natural products, betulinic acid and resveratrol, into the system. The betulinic acid/resveratrol–loaded NPs, with an average size of 164.18 nm and a negative zeta potential (-25.46 mV), displayed high entrapment efficiency and sustained drug release. P-selectin binding peptide–coated PLGA NPs efficiently penetrated RAW 264.7 cells and Colon-26 cells in vitro, exhibiting a preference for the inflamed colon. When combined with luminol, these NPs activated myeloperoxidase-dependent bioluminescence imaging, using the bioluminescence resonance energy transfer–fluorescence resonance energy transfer effect to visualize tissue inflammation. Importantly, the NPs exhibited significant efficacy in mitigating symptoms of UC in mouse models, preserving intestinal microbiota balance and preventing organ injuries. Although the current delivery route is intravenous, there is potential for the development of an oral deliverable nanoplatform for targeted IBD theranostic. Conclusion and Future Perspective The intricate landscape of cellular and molecular targets implicated in the pathophysiology of IBD sets the stage for innovative therapeutic exploration. Advanced oral nano-based drugs offer a promising avenue to increase the precision and efficacy of IBD treatment by precisely targeting specific cellular and molecular pathways. This approach spans from tailoring drug delivery to specific cells to seamlessly integrating diagnostic capabilities with precisely targeted therapeutic interventions. Currently, oral NPs designed based on molecular targets excel in effectively reaching and targeting abundant cells such as macrophages, enterocytes, colonocytes, and goblet cells. However, a notable gap exists in efficiently targeting intestinal stem cells. Plant-derived exosomal NPs, such as GELN, have shown their ability to target colonic stem cells via micropinocytosis. Investigating the chemical characteristics of GELN associated with stem cell–targeting functions is crucial for developing more efficient stem cell–targeting nano DDS. This understanding will pave the way for advancements in the field. IBD stands out as a complex, chronic condition necessitating inventive solutions. A fundamental element of IBD treatment is the strategic design of NPs for precise drug delivery. Researchers are exploring the use of AI to enhance the targeting capabilities of NPs for drug delivery. 72 AI algorithms analyze extensive data sets to identify optimal NPs characteristics, such as size, shape, and surface properties, for targeted delivery to specific tissues or cells. This approach aims to maximize the precision and efficiency of drug delivery while minimizing side effects. 73 The synergy between advanced imaging technologies and AI provides invaluable insights into NP distribution and their interactions within inflamed regions. 16 This dynamic feedback loop equips medical professionals with the necessary data to make real-time adjustments, optimizing drug release for maximum therapeutic impact. IBD often unfolds as a fluctuating symphony of disease activity. AI algorithms have the potential to predict symptom exacerbations by analyzing historical data and biomarkers. 74 In this context, NPs endowed with AI-enabled, drug-release systems offer adaptability to treatment regimens. These AI-empowered systems can recalibrate drug release schedules in response to changing disease dynamics, ensuring the persistent effectiveness of therapy. Several rapidly evolving fields highlight the role of AI in assisting nanomedicine, including predicting targeting sites, optimizing drug release profiles, and customizing attributes in the management of IBD. The gradual integration of AI-driven algorithms is anticipated to play pivotal roles in shaping the future of NP-based therapies for IBD management.
