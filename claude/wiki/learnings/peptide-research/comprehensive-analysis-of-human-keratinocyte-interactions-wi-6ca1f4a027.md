ABSTRACT In recent years, microbiome studies have revealed that Candida species are common colonizers of the human skin. The distribution of species, however, varies greatly. Although C. parapsilosis is more likely to resemble skin commensals, opinions are divided, and discrepancies are present regarding C. albicans that is also often associated with cutaneous candidiasis. Therefore, we aimed to thoroughly assess the nature of skin epithelial cell - Candida interactions. To study species-specific host responses, we examined internalization, cytokine and metabolic responses in different keratinocytes (HaCaT, HPV-KER) along with host cell damage following fungal stimuli. To rigorously examine yeast-keratinocyte interactions, we applied two distinct isolates of both C. albicans (SC5314, WO-1) and C. parapsilosis (GA1, CLIB214). Comparison of the two fungi’s virulence revealed that while C. albicans effectively adheres to human keratinocytes and causes subsequent damage, C. parapsilosis is unable to establish lasting physical contact and causes less harm. In terms of keratinocyte response, both cell lines showed significantly enhanced cellular (internalization), humoral (IL-6, IL-8) and metabolic responses (2-ketoglutaric acid, citric acid, threorine, hypotaurine) to C. albicans strains, while those towards C. parapsilosis remained relatively low or similar to the control condition. Under certain conditions strain preference was also detected. Of the two cell lines, HPV-KER was more sensitive, as besides interspecies differences, intraspecies differences were also measurable. These results suggest that C. albicans triggers an enhanced antifungal response, thus does not closely resemble skin commensals, like C. parapsilosis . Furthermore, HPV-KER might serve as a more applicable tool for studying keratinocyte antifungal responses. Introduction Since the 1980s, the number of diseases caused by human pathogenic fungi has been increasing [ 1 ]. Individuals with a weakened or immature immune system (e.g. infants, the elderly, patients treated with glucocorticosteroids or chemotherapy) are at greatest risk, as they develop systemic mycoses at high rates [ 1 , 2 ]. Even with appropriate antifungal drug treatment, systemic mycoses are frequently associated with a high mortality rate (20–90%) [ 2 , 3 ]. Such diseases are mainly caused by Candida , Aspergillus , Cryptococcus and Pneumocystis species, as well as fungi belonging to the subphylum Mucoromycotina [ 2 ]. Besides life-threatening infections, mycoses also appear in the form of superficial infections that are difficult to treat and often reoccur. These might affect the skin, their appendages and mucosal surfaces [ 2 ]. Fungal skin diseases affect at least 1 billion people [ 3 ]. They are primarily caused by dermatophytes [ 2 , 4 ], but a considerable amount of episodes is also caused by Candida species [ 5 ]. C. parapsilosis is considered as a common commensal of the healthy human skin [ 1 ]. Byrd et al. [ 6 ] listed it among the 10 most common eukaryotic colonizers of healthy dry skin surfaces, besides Malassezia restricta , Malassezia globosa and Aspergillus tubingensis . Although occasionally this species is capable of causing the infection of skin appendages (resulting in nail mycosis), it is generally considered as a natural skin commensal [ 1 , 5 , 7–9 ]. Contrarily to C. parapsilosis , C. albicans is frequently associated with cutaneous candidiasis, thus, the infection of various skin surfaces along with the mucosa of the oral cavity, gut and vagina [ 7 , 10–12 ]. Several studies report that C. albicans is less frequently identified as a skin commensal, if at all; thus its commensal status within the healthy adult skin microflora, is questionable [ 11 , 13 , 14 ]. Although a recent metagenomic study reported Candida on the skin of a healthy individual, no species level identification was performed [ 15 ]. Another study showed the predominance of C. parapsilosis (37.6%) on the healthy skin regardless of the age group, while C. albicans was identified in only 16% of all examined individuals [ 16 ]. Hence, this prompts a question: to what extent might this species exhibit similarities to skin commensals, if any? Our skin provides mechanical, enzymatic and immunological protection against invading pathogens. The latter includes Langerhans cells and CD8 + T-cells in the epidermis, and macrophages, dendritic cells, neutrophil granulocytes, mast cells, CD4 + T-cells, γδ T-cells and natural killer cells in the dermis [ 17 ]. A growing body of research demonstrates that keratinocytes in the epidermis also contribute to immunological functions via various mechanisms. Several microbes colonise the outer layers of the human skin, along with its appendices – such as hair follicles, where undifferentiated keratinocytes reside – and therefore are able to establish a direct contact with both differentiated and undifferentiated keratinocytes. Following a direct contact, both keratinocyte types are capable of inducing immune responses [ 18 , 19 ]. Keratinocytes are able to recognize pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMP) via pathogen recognition receptors (PRRs). Recognition leads to the activation of various transcription factors, triggering antimicrobial responses in the form of antimicrobial peptide and inflammatory cytokine secretion. Therefore, skin epithelial cells actively produce antimicrobial peptides such as β-defensins to eliminate pathogens, secrete cytokines such as IL-6, IL-1β, IL-10 IL-8 and CCL5 to recruit professional phagocytosing cells to the site of infection and produce nitric oxide (NO), to eliminate pathogens. In certain cases keratinocytes might also internalize invading microbes [ 20 ]. In this study, we aimed to thoroughly assess the nature of skin epithelial cell - Candida interactions. Specifically, to investigate human keratinocyte responses to C. albicans , a frequent cause of cutaneous candidiasis infections, and to another Candida species, C. parapsilosis that is more likely to exhibit behaviour akin to skin commensals. For the experiments we aimed to apply two distinct keratinocyte cell lines, HaCaT and HPV-KER. HaCaT is a commonly used spontaneously immortalized cell line, while HPV-KER is a cell line immortalized by the HPV (Human Papilloma Virus) E6/16 oncogene [ 21 ]. Materials and methods Applied Candida strains, their maintenance and preparations During the experiments C. albicans SC5314, WO-1 and C. parapsilosis GA1 and CLIB214 strains were used ( Table 1 ). Candida strains were maintained on YPD medium at 4 °C and were refreshed monthly. Prior to infection, Candida strains were inoculated into 2 ml of YPD liquid medium (0.5% yeast extract, 1% peptone, 1% glucose, 100 I.U./ml penicillin, and 100 µg/ml streptomycin) and incubated at 30 °C with shaking applied at 200 rpm. On the following day, 200 μl of suspension was added to fresh 2 ml YPD solution. On the day of the experiment, yeast suspensions were centrifuged (3000 rpm, 5 min), washed 2x with 1xPBS and the concentrations were set according to the applied multiplicity of infection doses (MOIs). Keratinocyte cell lines, maintenance, and preparations For the experiments, undifferentiated HPV-KER and HaCaT human keratinocyte cell lines were used kindly provided by the MTA-SZTE Dermatological Research Group. Prior to fungal stimuli, keratinocytes were collected using 1x TrypLE, washed with 1x PBS and seeded into the appropriate plates (12-, 24- or 96-well plates) for growth until full confluency. Adhesion of Candida cells to keratinocytes During the experiment, HaCaT and HPV-KER keratinocytes were co-incubated with C. parapsilosis GA1, CLIB214 and C. albicans SC5314, WO-1 cells at the MOI of 5:1 (pathogen: host) for 1 ( C. albicans ) and 3 hours ( C. parapsilosis ) at 37°C in the presence of 5% CO 2 . After co-incubation, supernatants were collected from each condition (500 μl) and the keratinocytes were washed twice (300 μl +200 μl) with PBS. During the process, all liquid suspensions were collected (a total of 1 ml per condition) in Eppendorf tubes, to later determine the amount of non-adherent fungal cells. To determine the amount of adhered fungal cells, keratinocytes were then treated with 1% TritonX-100 (300 μl) for 10 min, and after scraping, washed with 500 μl of FBS-PBS, then with 200 μl of 1x PBS. All liquid phases (a total of 1 ml per condition) were then collected and transferred to Eppendorf tubes. The collected samples were then suspended 5 times with a 29 G needle to disintegrate cellular aggregates and to burst intact keratinocytes. All of the collected suspensions, containing either non-adherent or adherent fungal cells, were diluted to an appropriate concentration, and plated onto YPD plates. After incubation at 30 °C for 2 days, the percentage of adhesion was determined by CFU analysis as follows: CFU of adhered fungal cells/CFU of non-adhered and adhered fungal cells * 100 (%). Candida elimination by keratinocytes Fungal cells were stained with 10 mg/ml Calcofluor White (Sigma-Aldrich, binds to chitin in cell wall), and host cells were labelled with 25 µl/ml WGA-AlexaFluor488 (Invitrogen, binds to N - acetylglucosamine in cell membrane) separately, prior to the experiment. For Calcofluor White staining, 1 mg/mL stock solution and 10 μg/mL working concentration was used. Following overnight culturing, yeast cells were washed 3 times with 1xPBS. After adjusting the cell concentration to 10 8 cells per mL, cells were stained with Calcofluor white for 10 minutes at room temperature. Following staining, yeast cells were washed 2 times with 1xPBS, and cell concentration was adjusted to 10 6 cells per mL prior to keratinocyte infection. For WGA-AlexaFluor488 staining, 1 mg/mL stock solution and 5 μg/mL working concentration was used. Pior to infection, keratinocytes (2x10 5 /well) were stained with WGA-AlexaFluor488, for 10 minutes at 37C, in the presence of 5% CO 2 . Following staining, cells were washed 3 times with 1xPBS. Stained keratinocytes were then infected with yeast cells at the ratio of 5:1 (fungal cells:host cells). The co-culture was incubated for 1 hour. After incubation, wells were washed 3 times with 1xPBS, and keratinocytes were harvested using 1xTryple after a 10-minute incubation. Tryple activity was inactivated by adding 10% FBS-PBS. The cells were collected via centrifugation (10 minutes at 600 RPM), and the supernatant was discarded. The pellet was resuspended in 100 µl 1xPBS for further analysis. Measurements were performed and percent internalization values were determined with the FlowSight® Imaging Flow Cytometer and evaluated with the IDEAS® 6.2 software. Between 9,000 and 10,000 individual keratinocytes were analysed per run. Keratinocyte cell damage by Candida strains To determine keratinocyte cell damage, the relative amount of lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) release was measured by the LDH Cytotoxicity Detection Kit (TaKaRa, catalogue number MK401) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. After fungal challenge, cell-free supernatants were collected and centrifuged (3000 RPM, 5 min) and following their appropriate dilution, 100 μl of the reaction mixture was added to 100 μl of each collected sample. Culture medium was used as negative control and supernatant of cells lysed with 1% Triton X-100 was used as positive control. The plates were incubated at room temperature, protected from light for 20 min, and the OD of each sample was measured at 492 nm using the SPECTROstar Nano Microplate Reader. The relative amount of LDH in the samples was calculated in percentage. The secretion of TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, IL-10, CCL5, IL-36 g, IL-12 p40, IL-12 p70, and IL-17a was determined. Keratinocyte RNA extraction procedure The experiment was performed in 12-well cell plates. A total of 3x 10 5 keratinocytes were seeded per well. Cells were infected with C. parapsilosis CLIB214 (MOI of 1:1) and C. albicans SC5314 (MOI of 1:25) for 12 hours. After the coincubation, supernatants were discarded, and keratinocytes were washed twice with 1x PBS. The host cells’ RNA was extracted with the FlowSight® Plus Mini Kit, according to the manufacturer’s suggestions. The extracted RNA samples were stored at −80°C. RNA quality and quantity were analysed using a Bioanalyzer instrument (Agilent). mRNA sequencing library preparation and mRNA sequencing (Illumina) was performed by specialists at Novogene according to their standard library preparation protocols. Fungal RNA extraction procedure The experiment was carried out in Petri dishes. In each condition, one Petri dish contained a keratinocyte cell line infected with a Candida strain. Candida strains without keratinocytes served as controls. 1x10 6 keratinocytes were seeded into Petri dishes and infected with 1x10 7 fungal cells for 4 h. After the coincubation, cells were scraped with a cell scraper and pipetted into a 15 ml Falcon tube, for centrifugation at 5.000 rpm for 5 min. The pellet was then resuspended in 500 μl of RNase-free distilled water and centrifuged (12.000 rpm, 3 min). The pellet was resuspended in 650 μl of RLT plus lysis buffer. Samples were then immersed in liquid nitrogen, then in pre-heated water (30 °C) in repeated cycles (total of 3 times per sample). Samples were then transferred to glass beads and vortexed at maximum speed for 10 min. Fungal RNA was then extracted using the RNeasy® Plus Mini Kit, according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Samples were stored at −80 °C. mRNA sequencing library preparation and mRNA sequencing was performed by specialists at Novogene according to their standard library preparation protocols. Library preparation involved poly(A) capture for mRNA enrichment, and sequencing was carried out on an Illumina NovaSeq X Plus platform using a 2 × 150 bp paired-end configuration with the NovaSeq PE150 workflow. The sequencing depth was targeted to achieve an average of ≥20 million read pairs per sample. Transcriptome analysis Initial quality control and preprocessing of human keratinocyte total RNA-seq samples were performed using TrimGalore (v0.6.4), which incorporates FastQC and Cutadapt to remove low-quality reads (phred &lt;20) and automatically detected Illumina adapter contamination (“AGATCGGAAGAGC”). Fungal sequences underwent quality assessment with standalone Cutadapt (v2.8), using manually defined 3' and 5' adapters (-aCGTATGCCGTCTTCTGCTTG$,-A GCATACGGCAGAAGACGAAC$, -g ^AATGATACGGCGACCACCGAGA, and -G ^TTACTATGCCGCTGGTGGCTCT). Processed human reads were aligned to the GRCh38 reference genome (release 96) from ENSEMBL using Hisat2 (v2.1.0). For fungal reads, the C. parapsilosis CDC317 and C. albicans SC5314 reference assemblies from the Candida Genome Database were used. Hisat2 parameters were configured for transcript assembly compatibility and non-deterministic alignment of identical reads. Subsequent read quantification was performed with featureCounts (v2.0.1) in the case of the fungal reads. Samtools (1.15) was used for processing and analysing the data, including tools for file format conversion and manipulation, sorting, querying, and statistics amongst other methods. Differential gene expression The keratinocyte alignment results in BAM format and the fungal count tables generated by featureCounts were loaded into R Studio. Differential gene expression in logarithmic fold change (LFC) was determined using DESeq2 (v1.34.0). For human samples, count tables were prepared with the GenomicAlignments (v1.30.0) package, using gene features extracted from a TxDb annotation created from the corresponding Ensembl reference GFF via the GenomicFeatures (v1.46.0) package in R. In both cases, features with extremely low expression (less than one read per million in at least three samples) were filtered out. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified with an absolute LFC &gt;1.5 and p-value &lt;0.05. Variance and volcano plots, as well as heatmaps of significant genes, were generated to visualize expression patterns. To gain further insight into signalling pathways and biological functions significantly impacted by the DEGs, overrepresentation analysis (ORA) and gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) were conducted on Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathways, Gene Ontology (GO) terms, and Molecular Signature Database (MSigDB) hallmark gene sets, using the clusterProfiler (v4.1.4) package in R. KEGG and GO ORA, as well as GO and hallmark GSEA analyses, were performed against a constant background using the human and yeast genome-wide annotation packages, org.Hs.eg.db (v3.14.0), and org.Sc.sgd.db (v3.14.0), filtered for genes actively expressed in keratinocyte and yeast samples, respectively. Dot plots and bar plots were created using the ggplot2 (v3.5.0) package to visualize the most enriched KEGG pathways and results in each GO category. For further data mining, semantic similarity (SS) was calculated between the enriched GO terms via the rrvgo (v1.5.4) package. Similarity values between terms were calculated after hierarchical clustering with complete linkage. A high threshold (0.9) was selected while cutting the tree to minimize collinearity between terms. To create a comprehensive visualization of activated GO terms and their dynamics, an activation z-score was integrated with the enrichment results. The z-score calculation was inspired by the methods introduced in Qiagen’s Ingenuity Pathway Analysis software suite [ 26 ], but followed a simplified version. Our model assumes that observed up-regulation in the gene expression correlates positively, and down-regulation negatively, with the activation of the given GO term. Z-scores were calculated as follows: z = x σ x = ∑ i x i N = N + − N − N where x = ∑ i x i = N + − N − represents the number of “increased”/”decreased” predictors, presuming that the mean equals 0, and the variance is 1. In the yeast activation plots, the representative terms of unique semantic clusters were visualized. ORA was also performed to identify GO terms that are statistically overrepresented amongst the lists of differentially expressed genes in the keratinocytes in response to the fungal pathogens. To provide more detailed insight into the dynamics that can be associated with these GO terms, redundant hits were clustered based on their semantic similarity and an activation z-score was calculated based on the DEGs to the representative terms. ORA effectively identifies pathways with a significantly higher number of DEGs than the proportion expected by chance but could miss coordinated changes in the gene expression below the significance threshold. Hence, we also complemented ORA with GSEA, to highlight more subtle but coordinated changes activating biological processes and molecular functions in the keratinocytes. Statistical analyses All graphs were prepared using the GraphPad Prism v.9.0.0 software. All experiments were performed at least 3 times, with at least 3 technical parallels in each condition. Results Adherence of Candida cells to keratinocytes To thoroughly assess the nature of keratinocyte - Candida interactions, we aimed to apply two different clinical isolates of both C. albicans (SC5314, WO-1) and C. parapsilosis (CLIB214, GA1) throughout the study. Previous studies have demonstrated that several Candida species are able to effectively adhere to the surface of various epithelial cells such as mucosal, renal, vaginal or bladder epithelial cells [ 27 , 28 ]. In general, microbial adhesion is a prerequisite of subsequent cellular and humoral responses. Thus, we examined the adherence of C. albicans and C. parapsilosis to both keratinocyte cell lines, during the period of 1 hour. When comparing all 4 Candida strains at an equivalent infection dose (MOI 1:), C. parapsilosis showed lower adhesion abilities (from 0.07% to 4.09%) to both keratinocyte cell lines compared to C. albicans (from 20.5 to 85%) ( Figure 1 ). This interspecies difference was more pronounced in the HPV-KER cell line. Furthermore, in the case of HPV-KER cells, intraspecies differences could also be detected, as CLIB214 cells (4.09 ± 1.30 %), adhered more effectively to keratinocytes than GA1 cells (0.67 ± 0.22 %), and SC5314 cells (85 ± 1.61%) to WO-1 cells (51.7 ± 3.25%). These results suggest that C. albicans strains possess greater adhesion properties to keratinocytes than C. parapsilosis strains. Furthermore, the HPV-KER cell line is able to differentiate between the distinct strains of both species. Candida elimination by keratinocytes Invading pathogens are recognized via a repertoire of pattern recognition receptors. As a result of recognition, subsequent cellular and humoral responses begin to manifest. Although phagocytosis is not a characteristic response of epithelial cells, previous studies have reported such events [ 20 ] that take place for the sake of better antigen presentation. To measure the efficacy of pathogen internalization we stimulated two keratinocyte cell lines with C. albicans and C. parapsilosis for 1 hour, using the MOI of 5:1. To detect internalization events, host cells were stained with WGA-AlexaFluor 488, while yeast cells were labelled with calcofluor white. Our results suggest that although internalization events were rare, both HaCaT ( Figure 2 ) and HPV-KER ( Figure 3 ) cells exhibited preference for C. albicans over C. parapsilosis . Internalization was confirmed as shown in the corresponding representative images in Figures 2 and 3 . Interestingly, while HaCaT cells did not differentiate between strains within the same species – showing no significant differences between C. parapsilosis GA1 and CLIB214, or C. albicans WO1 and SC5314), the HPV-KER cell line was able to distinguish between different isolates of each species. Degree of keratinocyte damage following Candida infection C. albicans is less frequently identified on healthy human skin surfaces and is responsible for a significant proportion of cutaneous candidiasis episodes causing substantial skin damage [ 11 ]. In contrast, C. parapsilosis is a frequent occupier of skin surfaces [ 1 , 6 ] and is rarely associated with skin diseases, thus dermal and epidermal damage. Here, we aimed to examine the extent of keratinocyte cell damage following C. albicans and C. parapsilosis presence. We stimulated human HaCaT and HPV-KER keratinocytes with both strains of C. parapsilosis and C. albicans at various doses and aimed to determine lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) enzyme activity. In the case of C. parapsilosis , the extent of LDH release caused by GA1 and CLIB214 cells, was comparable to the uninfected control, after 24 hours of coincubation ( Figure 4 ). In contrast, LDH release was higher after C. albicans exposure, although interestingly, only in the case of the SC5314 strain. This effect was more pronounced in the HPV-KER cell line. Gene expression changes within keratinocytes during keratinocyte-fungus interactions Next we aimed to assess the underlying molecular mechanisms of the observed cellular and metabolic responses. Therefore, we investigated the transcriptomic responses of keratinocytes after fungal stimuli. As more robust host responses were triggered by the CLIB214 strain of C. parapsilosis (adhesion, internalization) and the SC5314 strain of C. albicans (host cell damage, cytokine, chemokine responses, metabolome analyses), we examined specifically their effect on the transcriptome of both HaCaT and HPV-KER cells after 12 hours. As C. parapsilosis did not cause a significant host cell damage even after 24 hours of coincubation, in this case we applied the MOI of 1:1. However, under the same conditions C. albicans SC5314 treatment resulted in up to 45% of host cell death. Therefore, we first determined the appropriate fungal infection dose for 12 hours of coincubation. Of the applied MOIs, we choose the MOI of 1:25 for transcriptomic analyses, as at this dose the extent of host cell damage did not significantly exceed 30% in either keratinocyte cell lines (Supplementary Figure S1). Discussion Due to the widely applied microbiome analyses in the last decades, it became evident that Candida species reside within the human skin microflora. The distribution of species, however, varies greatly. Amongst the identified species, C. parapsilosis and C. albicans have also been reported. However, while the former species rarely, the latter species frequently causes infections in this niche, which raises questions about its suggested commensal nature. Therefore, in this study we aimed to examine host-microbial interactions in this niche, by focusing on the interplay between skin keratinocytes and the aforementioned species. Our results suggest that C. albicans elicits a more robust host response than C. parapsilosis , a characteristic particularly associated with the SC5314 strain used in this study. This is primarily due to the more virulent nature of C. albicans that is largely determined by its ability to form true hyphae [ 32 ]. This phenomenon is often associated with characteristic biochemical and enzymatic activities, as well as the production of toxins (e.g. candidalysin). This capability can vary significantly among the strains of this species [ 33 ]. The WO-1 strain used in this study was also unable to form true hyphae under the applied conditions, similar to the strains of C. parapsilosis . This could explain the moderate keratinocyte responses to the WO-1 strain. The inability of C. parapsilosis strains to produce true hyphae and the lack of candidalysin production (that is strongly associated with mucosal damage [ 34 ]) correlates with the low level of host cell damage. This correlates well with the observation that C. albicans causes the majority of cutaneous candidiasis, whereas C. parapsilosis causes skin disease less frequently [ 1 , 7 , 11 ]. Despite their poor hypha forming ability, WO-1 cells were still capable of effectively adhering to skin epithelial cells, similarly to SC5314 cells, and thereby establishing direct cell-cell interactions. Previous studies also confirmed that C. albicans exhibits a higher adhesion capacity to epidermal epithelial cells than C. parapsilosis [ 35 ]. The low levels of host cell damage caused by the WO-1 strain, akin to the strains of C. parapsilosis , could be explained by the fact that this strain has deficits associated with the lack of effective morphology switch [ 32 , 36 ]. This strain has also been reported to be less virulent than the SC5314 strain in an oral epithelial cell infection model by others [ 37 ]. Regarding the inflammatory responses of keratinocytes, C. albicans SC5314 elicited the most significant host response in terms of IL6 and IL8 release. During the study we also examined the secretion of several other inflammatory and anti-inflammatory markers (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, IL-10, CCL5, IL-36 g, IL-12 p40, IL-12 p70, and IL-17a); however, no significant changes were detected under the applied conditions (data not shown). The DUSP6 gene has a role in phosphoprotein phosphatase activity from molecular function side, and on the side of biological process it is part of the MAPK cascade. Differential activation of the MAPK pathway may be involved in immune activation and cytokine production between C. parapsilosis and C. albicans . Metabolomic changes in human keratinocytes during infection have previously been studied [ 38–42 ]. One study described the development of metabolic stress during S. aureus infection that is associated with enhanced glycolytic and proinflammatory processes [ 39 ]. In our study, in general, C. albicans presence caused greater metabolic alterations that occurred in a strain-dependent manner. The majority of metabolic responses occurred in the presence of the SC5314 strain, where 2-Hydroxyglutaric acid (signal transduction) [ 43 ], ribulose (facilitates the pentose-phosphate pathway for the synthesis of biomolecules and energy production processes), glycerol (enhancement of barrier functions, keratinocyte differentiation [ 44 ]), xylulose (glucose metabolism, oxidative stress [ 45 ]), and fucose (immunological functions and microenvironment [ 46 ]) levels elevated and fructose (promote keratinocyte differentiation [ 47 ]) levels decreased in the HaCaT cell line, while hypotaurine (antioxidant effect [ 48 ]), 2-ketoglutaric acid (barrier function [ 49 ]), ornithine (protective function of skin epithelial cells, epithelial cell metabolism [ 50 ]), citric acid (cytotixic effects [ 51 ]), threorine (adhesion, keratinocyte cell differentiation [ 52 ]) and arginine (immune function, wound healing [ 53 ]) levels increased significantly, and orotic acid (epithelial cell regeneration, epidermal barrier function, DNA repair mechanisms [ 42 ]) levels decreased significantly in the HPV-KER cell line. The other C. albicans strain, WO-1 caused significant alterations only in the HPV-KER cell line, where increased levels of aspartic acid (inflammation [ 54 ]), 2-ketoglutaric acid, malic acid (amino acid deficiencies [ 55 ]), hypotaurine, ornithine, citric acid, threorine and arginine and decreased levels of orotic acid were detected. C. parapsilosis presence, on the other hand, was less stimulatory. In HPV-KER keratinocytes, infection by the C. albicans WO-1 strain increased the production of aspartic acid that is involved in inflammatory processes [ 54 ], and both C. albicans strains enhanced the production of 2-ketoglutaric acid that plays a role in barrier function [ 49 ]. In these cells, higher secretion of metabolites associated with cytotoxic effects (citric acid [ 51 ]) or general amino acid deficiencies (malic acid [ 55 ]) were characterized. Hypotaurine has antioxidant properties [ 48 ], citric acid has apoptotic and cytotoxic effects [ 51 ], while 2-ketoglutaric acid enhances barrier function through cell proliferation [ 49 ]. Arginine modulates immune function, wound healing, hormone secretion, vascular tone, insulin sensitivity and endothelial function [ 53 ]. Threonine showed elevated secretion levels following infection with C. albicans strains, indicating the presence of adhesion processes and keratinocyte cell differentiation. The elevated ornithine levels under all conditions is related to the protective function of skin epithelial cells as a precursor of collagen synthesis and plays an important role in epithelial cell metabolism, in particular in the synthesis of polyamines [ 50 ]. Orotic acid plays an important role in triggering the metabolism of folic acid and vitamin B12 [ 42 ]. The decreased orotic acid production during Candida infection may indicate a downregulation of metabolic processes involved in epithelial cell regeneration, epidermal barrier function, DNA repair mechanisms and collagen production [ 42 , 56 ]. These findings further support the idea that C. albicans presence is more stimulatory than C. parapsilosis . In conclusion, following Candida infection, human keratinocytes show altered secretion of metabolites that regulate skin cell barrier function, differentiation, signal transduction, glycolysis, pentose phosphate pathway and the citric acid cycle. RNA sequencing results showed that HPV-KER cells are more sensitive to yeast infection compared to HaCaT, and C. albicans SC5314 induces a stronger response than C. parapsilosis CLIB214, both in HaCaT and HPV-KER cells at the level of biological processes and molecular functions. The cytokine/chemokine activation, increased signalling, the presence of inflammatory processes and the response to hypoxia induced by the SC5314 strain clearly demonstrate that due to the more pathogenic nature of C. albicans , cells need to mount a more robust response to protect themselves than during infection with C. parapsilosis [ 57–60 ]. RNA sequencing of fungal cells revealed that C. albicans strains prioritize metal ion homoeostasis and oxidative stress responses following interaction with PHV-KER keratinocyte cells. Other studies have also revealed that a robust stress response contributes to survival and virulence in C. albicans by adapting the fungus to altered conditions and protecting it against host-induced stress [ 28 ]. A further study details that C. albicans can utilize multiple sources of iron during a fungal-cell interaction and that iron uptake pathways are integrated with other pathogenic patterns, such as hyphal-associated adhesin and invasin, and thus coupled with adhesion and hyphal formation processes [ 61 ]. The catabolic processes seen in C. albicans strain SC5314 emphasize that metabolic adaptation affects the pathogenicity of C. albicans . This may occur at several levels such as promotion of nutrient assimilation, cell wall remodelling, stress resistance, and expression of virulence factors [ 62 , 63 ]. Although both C. albicans strains possess the Candida toxin encoding gene ( ECE1 ) in their genomes, ECE1 expression levels were found not to be significantly elevated in the presence of keratinocyte cells in either case (data not shown). However, basal ECE1 levels were higher in the SC5314 strain than in the WO-1 strain. Moreover, hyphal-specific processes were more active in the SC5314 strain than in the WO-1 strain. As the active transcription of the ECE1 depends on the morphology switch in C. albicans , the observed differences could be explained by the yeast-locked morphology of WO-1 that could also contribute to the high variability in host responses to the hereby applied C. albicans strains. The undifferentiated HaCaT and HPV-KER human keratinocyte cell lines were used in this study. Lewis et al. [ 64 ], demonstrated constitutive NF-κB activation in the HaCaT cell line compared to primary cells. On the other hand, HPV-KER is characterized by inducible NF-κB activation. Previously, Erdei et al., (2018) described an inducible negative regulator (TNIP1) of the NF-κB signalling pathway in HPV-KER cells. Based on this information, the differences between the cell lines and the hypersensitivity of the HPV-KER cell line compared to HaCaT cells is possibly due to the differences in NF-κB inducibility. The current study therefore highlights that we should not rely solely on the experimental results of one model but to apply multiple models to investigate a phenomenon. As the sensitivity of HPV-KER cells was greater than that of HaCaT cells in terms of distinguishing between not only the different species but also between the different strains of a species, we propose HPV-KER as another appropriate if not a more suitable model for studying the different pathomechanisms of different fungal pathogens. HPV-KER has previously been employed in research on keratinocyte-pathogen interactions, including studies on Propionibacterium acnes [ 65 , 66 ] Although several studies [ 67 , 68 ] discuss the ability of keratinocytes to produce antimicrobial peptides following bacterial and viral infection [ 69 ], in this study, we did not detect secretion of any of the major antimicrobial peptides (hBD-1, hBD-2, hBD-3 and LL-37) following either C. parapsilosis or C. albicans infection (data not shown). This discrepancy between the data in the literature and our results could be explained by the different origin of keratinocyte cell lines from different sources or by the undifferentiated keratinocyte status. Taken together, in this study we aimed to assess to what extent does C. albicans and C. parapsilosis resemble skin commensals, thus thoroughly examined the nature of skin epithelial cell - Candida interactions. The hereby gained findings imply that C. albicans induces a heightened antifungal response as reflected by the inflammatory cytokine, metabolic, and transcriptomic responses, besides causing considerable host damage – with high inter-strain differences-, thereby greatly differing from natural skin commensals such as C. parapsilosis . Although simplified 2D models (e.g. cell lines) of infections provide fundamental ideas about the nature of host – pathogen interactions, due to their limitations (e.g. genomic alterations due to immortalization, oversimplification of complex biological interactions, loss of cellular heterogeneity) experimental validation of the acquired findings is still required in more advanced models. Therefore, the hereby gained results are ought to be confirmed later in setups containing either primary cells, reconstituted tissue models or in vivo . Furthermore, as inter-strain variances are also often high, it is also highly recommended to perform a large-scale screen of multiple strains of each species to assess host keratinocyte- Candida interactions in more depth. Additionally, HPV-KER cells may be considered as a more applicable tool for investigating keratinocyte antifungal responses.
